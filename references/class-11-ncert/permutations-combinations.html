    Permutations and Combinations (Class 11, Chapter 7)

    A written tutorial covering the standard NCERT Class 11 chapter on counting. The chapter answers a single recurring question: “In how many ways can this be done?” The two big tools are the permutation (when order matters) and the combination (when it doesn’t), and both rest on the Fundamental Principle of Counting.

    1. Why we need this chapter

    Suppose a 4-digit suitcase lock has each digit chosen freely from 0–9. You forgot the code. How many combinations would you have to try in the worst case? Trying randomly is slow; what we want is a way to count the possibilities without listing them.

    Or: you have 2 shirts and 3 trousers. How many shirt-trouser outfits are possible? You can list (S₁,P₁), (S₁,P₂), (S₁,P₃), (S₂,P₁), (S₂,P₂), (S₂,P₃) — six pairs. But what if you had 12 shirts and 17 trousers? Listing breaks down. We need a principle.

    2. Fundamental Principle of Counting (FPC)

    2.1 Multiplication rule (AND)

    If a first job can be done in m ways and, for each such way, a second job can be done in n ways, then both jobs together can be done in m · n ways.

    The shirt-trouser problem: 2 ways to choose the shirt × 3 ways to choose the trouser = 6 outfits. ✓

    The 4-digit lock: each slot has 10 independent choices, so 10 × 10 × 10 × 10 = 10⁴ = 10 000 codes.

    The rule generalises: for k independent stages with n₁, n₂, …, n_k ways respectively, the total number of ways is n₁ · n₂ · … · n_k.

    2.2 Addition rule (OR)

    If a job can be done in one of two mutually exclusive ways — either method A (with m choices) or method B (with n choices) — then the total number of ways is m + n.

    Example: a college lets you take either one of 5 science electives or one of 3 humanities electives. You take exactly one elective. Total options = 5 + 3 = 8.

    Rule of thumb: “AND” between stages → multiply; “OR” between alternatives → add.

    2.3 Quick illustration

    A school student wants to pack a school bag (2 available), a tiffin box (3 available), and a water bottle (2 available) — one of each. How many combinations?

    By FPC (multiplication): 2 · 3 · 2 = 12 distinct loadouts.

    3. Factorial notation

    Many counting answers are products of consecutive integers. Notation:

    n! = 1 · 2 · 3 · … · n      (read "n factorial")
    n n!
    0 1 (by convention — see below)
    1 1
    2 2
    3 6
    4 24
    5 120
    6 720
    7 5 040
    8 40 320

    Key recursion: n! = n · (n − 1)!. So 8! = 8 · 7!, (n+1)! = (n+1) · n!, and so on.

    Why 0! = 1

    Forced by the recursion 1! = 1 · 0!. For this to give 1! = 1, we need 0! = 1. (It’s a convention chosen so formulas behave at boundaries — not a theorem.)

    Practical simplification

    Almost every factorial-heavy expression is simplified by cancelling a smaller factorial out of a larger one rather than computing both numerically:

    10! / 7! = 10 · 9 · 8 · 7! / 7! = 10 · 9 · 8 = 720

    4. Permutations

    4.1 What “permutation” means

    A permutation is an ordered arrangement. The arrangements AB and BA are different permutations of the same two letters. Order matters.

    4.2 The formula nPr (no repetition)

    How many ways can we arrange r distinct objects chosen from n distinct objects, in a line, with no repetition?

    Fill r blank slots one by one:

    • 1st slot: any of n objects → n choices
    • 2nd slot: any of the remaining n − 1 → n − 1 choices
    • 3rd slot: n − 2 choices
    • …
    • rth slot: n − r + 1 choices

    By FPC,

    nPr = n · (n − 1) · (n − 2) · … · (n − r + 1)
    = n! / (n − r)!

    4.3 Special cases

    Case Formula Explanation
    r = n (all objects taken at once) nPn = n! arrange all n distinct objects in a row
    r = 0 nP0 = 1 one “empty arrangement”
    With repetition allowed nʳ each of the r slots independently has n choices

    4.4 Worked examples

    E1. Arrange 4 letters of ROSE taken all at once. nPn = 4! = 24 words (most without meaning).

    E2. Number of 3-digit numbers from {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} with no digit repeated: 5P3 = 5·4·3 = 60.

    E3. Number of 3-digit numbers using digits 0–9 with repetition allowed: hundreds slot can’t be 0 so 9 choices, tens 10 choices, units 10 choices → 9 · 10 · 10 = 900.

    4.5 Permutations with identical objects

    If among n objects, p are alike of one kind, q alike of another kind, r alike of yet another, …, then the number of distinct arrangements taken all at once is

    n! / (p! · q! · r! · …)

    E4. Number of distinct words formed by rearranging the letters of PERMUTATIONS.

    The word has 12 letters; only T repeats (it appears twice). All other letters are distinct.

    total = 12! / 2!

    E5. Some standard sub-questions on the same word:

    • Words starting with P and ending with S: fix P and S at the ends. The middle 10 letters (which include the two Ts) get permuted: 10!/2! arrangements.

    • Words with all vowels together: the 5 vowels E, U, A, I, O are clumped into a single block. Now we permute (block) + 7 consonants = 8 objects, with T repeating: 8!/2! arrangements. Inside the block, the 5 distinct vowels can themselves be ordered in 5! ways. Total (8!/2!) · 5!.

    • There are always 4 letters between P and S: by sliding P and S along the 12-letter word with exactly 4 letters between them, you find 7 starting positions; P and S themselves can swap (2 orderings); the 4 letters between them are chosen and arranged from the remaining 10 in 10P4 ways; the remaining 6 letters fill the rest in 6!/2! ways (the two Ts are still indistinguishable). Combine via FPC.

    4.6 Circular permutations (just the idea)

    Arranging n distinct objects around a circle counts only the relative order, so we fix one object and permute the remaining n − 1:

    circular permutations of n distinct objects = (n − 1)!

    If clockwise and anticlockwise readings are considered the same (necklace problem), divide by 2: (n − 1)! / 2.

    5. Combinations

    5.1 The key shift: order doesn’t matter

    A combination is an unordered selection. From {V, R, D} (Virat, Rohit, Dhawan), the teams of size 2 are {V,R}, {V,D}, {R,D} — three teams. The corresponding permutations would be six (each team in two orders). The combination ignores the order.

    Concept Order matters? Example
    Permutation yes arranging books on a shelf, codes for a lock
    Combination no choosing a committee, picking a hand of cards, handshakes

    5.2 The formula nCr

    nCr = n! / (r! · (n − r)!)

    Derivation: each combination of r objects can be arranged in r! orders, so it accounts for r! permutations. Hence

    nPr = nCr · r!     ⇒     nCr = nPr / r! = n! / (r! · (n − r)!)

    Other notations: C(n, r) and the binomial coefficient (n choose r) written as a vertical pair (n; r).

    5.3 Properties of nCr

    Identity Statement
    Symmetry nCr = nC(n−r)
    Boundary nC0 = nCn = 1
    Single nC1 = nC(n−1) = n
    Pascal’s identity nCr + nC(r−1) = (n+1)Cr
    Sum across row nC0 + nC1 + … + nCn = 2ⁿ
    Equality nCx = nCy ⇒ x = y or x + y = n

    5.4 Worked examples

    E6. A committee of 3 chosen from 10 people: 10C3 = 10·9·8 / (3·2·1) = 120.

    E7. Cricket team of 11 from 17 players, with exactly 4 bowlers from the 5 available bowlers (the other 12 are batters).

    • Choose 4 bowlers from 5: 5C4 = 5.
    • Choose 7 batters from 12: 12C7 = 792.
    • Multiply (FPC, AND): 5 · 792 = 3 960.

    E8. 5 black + 6 red balls in a bag. Choose 2 black and 3 red.

    • 2 of the 5 black: 5C2 = 10.
    • 3 of the 6 red: 6C3 = 20.
    • 10 · 20 = 200 selections.

    E9. Programme of 5 courses from 9 available, of which 2 are compulsory.

    The 2 compulsory courses are fixed. We need to choose the remaining 5 − 2 = 3 courses from the remaining 9 − 2 = 7 electives. Answer = 7C3 = 35.

    E10. In how many ways can a 3-letter word (with or without meaning) be formed from the letters of DAUGHTER such that it contains 2 vowels and 1 consonant?

    DAUGHTER has vowels A, U, E (3) and consonants D, G, H, T, R (5).

    • Choose 2 vowels from 3: 3C2 = 3.
    • Choose 1 consonant from 5: 5C1 = 5.
    • Arrange the 3 chosen letters: 3! = 6.
    • Total = 3 · 5 · 6 = 90.

    (The nCr · r! step is exactly nPr. Notice how permutation and combination cooperate: combination chooses which letters; the r! orders them.)

    5.5 Diagnosing “permutation or combination?”

    Read the problem and ask: “If I list two outcomes that have the same objects in different orders, are they actually the same outcome?”

    • Same outcome ⇒ unordered ⇒ combination. Selecting a team, choosing a committee, picking balls from a bag, drawing a hand of cards, choosing a sub-collection.
    • Different outcome ⇒ ordered ⇒ permutation. Ranking, seating, forming numbers/words, deciding a code, assigning roles (President, Secretary, Treasurer).

    A useful real-world cue: a handshake between Rohit and Virat is the same event as a handshake between Virat and Rohit — combination. A batting order with Rohit then Virat differs from Virat then Rohit — permutation.

    6. Common solution patterns

    6.1 “Together / not together”

    To force objects to stay together, treat the bunch as one super-object, count arrangements of the super-object with the rest, then multiply by the internal arrangements of the bunch.

    To force objects to not be together, count all arrangements and subtract the “together” count, or use the gap method: arrange the other objects first, then place the restricted ones in the gaps.

    6.2 “At least / at most”

    “At least k” usually means summing several cases (k, k+1, …) — addition rule. Or you compute the complementary count and subtract from the total.

    6.3 Fixed positions

    Fix the constrained letters/objects in their slots first, then count the remaining free arrangements. (Used in all “starts with… and ends with…” problems.)

    6.4 Restricted digits / leading zeros

    When forming numbers, the leading digit usually cannot be 0. Count it separately, then count the rest.

    7. Worked-problem set (NCERT-flavoured)

    P1. Find the number of 4-digit numbers using digits 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 if no digit is repeated.

    5P4 = 5·4·3·2 = 120.

    P2. How many 4-digit even numbers can be formed from 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 with no repetition?

    The units digit must be even: 0, 2, 4.

    • Case A: units = 0. Remaining 3 slots filled from {1,2,3,4,5} in 5P3 = 60 ways.
    • Case B: units ∈ {2, 4}. Two sub-choices for the units digit. The thousands slot can be any of {1,2,3,4,5} \ {units digit} (must be non-zero), so 4 choices. The hundreds and tens slots together: pick 2 from the remaining 4 digits (which now includes 0), in 4P2 = 12 ways. Sub-total 2 · 4 · 12 = 96.
    • Total 60 + 96 = 156.

    P3. How many ways can the letters of EQUATION be arranged so that all vowels are together?

    EQUATION has 8 distinct letters with 5 vowels (E, U, A, I, O) and 3 consonants (Q, T, N).

    • Treat the 5 vowels as a block. Now (block) + 3 consonants = 4 objects, all distinct: 4! = 24.
    • The block’s internal order: 5! = 120.
    • Total 24 · 120 = 2880.

    P4. From a class of 25, a team of 11 is to be chosen, including a captain. How many ways?

    Choose 11 from 25: 25C11. Then choose the captain from those 11: 11. Or equivalently, choose the captain first (25 ways) then choose 10 more from the remaining 24 (24C10). Both give the same answer.

    P5. How many diagonals does a polygon with n sides have?

    A diagonal joins two non-adjacent vertices. Pairs of vertices: nC2. Of those, n are sides of the polygon. So diagonals = nC2 − n = n(n−3)/2.

    P6. In how many ways can 3 boys and 3 girls sit in a row so that no two girls are adjacent?

    Seat the 3 boys first: 3! = 6. They create 4 gaps (_B_B_B_) into which the girls go, one per gap, choosing 3 of the 4 gaps: 4P3 = 24. Total 6 · 24 = 144.

    P7. In how many ways can a committee of 5 be formed from 6 men and 4 women if it must contain at least 2 women?

    Cases by number of women: - 2W + 3M: 4C2 · 6C3 = 6 · 20 = 120 - 3W + 2M: 4C3 · 6C2 = 4 · 15 = 60 - 4W + 1M: 4C4 · 6C1 = 1 · 6 = 6

    Total 120 + 60 + 6 = 186.

    8. Common pitfalls

    • Confusing permutation with combination — always ask “does order matter?” before reaching for a formula.
    • Repetition allowed vs not — the formula changes (nʳ vs nPr). The phrase “no digit is repeated” or “all distinct” is the cue.
    • Identical objects — if some letters/objects look the same, divide by the factorials of their repetitions, otherwise you over-count.
    • Leading zero — when the problem asks for n-digit numbers, the leading slot has fewer choices than the others.
    • AT LEAST / AT MOST — these almost always need a sum of cases (addition rule) or a complement.
    • Forgetting the internal arrangement of a glued-together block — after treating a group as one object, multiply by the group’s own internal k!.
    • Counting handshakes as n² instead of nC2 — every handshake is shared between two people, hence each pair contributes once.
    • “Selecting” vs “Arranging” — “select 3 boys” is nC3; “select and seat 3 boys in a row” is nP3 or nC3 · 3!.

    9. Quick reference card

    n!                = 1 · 2 · … · n,   0! = 1
nPr               = n! / (n − r)!         (ordered, no repetition)
nCr               = n! / (r! · (n − r)!)  (unordered, no repetition)
nPr               = nCr · r!
nCr               = nC(n − r)
nC0 + nC1 + … +nCn = 2ⁿ
nCr + nC(r−1)     = (n+1)Cr               (Pascal)

Repetition allowed (ordered fillings) : nʳ
Distinct arrangements with identical objects : n! / (p! q! r! …)
Circular arrangements of n distinct objects : (n − 1)!

    Decision tree for word problems:

                          Does order matter?
                       /              \
                      yes              no
                       |                |
                  permutation       combination
                       |                |
              repetition allowed?       |
                /          \         use nCr
              yes           no
               |             |
              nʳ           use nPr

    10. Looking ahead

    Permutations and combinations are the gateway to several richer topics:

    • Binomial Theorem (the very next chapter): the coefficients nCr reappear as (x + y)ⁿ = Σ nCr xⁿ⁻ʳ yʳ. The combinatorial identity nCr + nC(r−1) = (n+1)Cr becomes Pascal’s triangle.
    • Probability. Once you can count outcomes, probability = favourable / total becomes a counting exercise. The whole of Class 11 probability builds on this chapter.
    • Multinomial coefficients. The “letters of a word” formula n!/(p!q!r!…) generalises to splits of n objects into groups of given sizes.
    • Inclusion–exclusion. For “at least one” type problems with overlapping events, you alternately add and subtract, each term being a sum of nCr choices.
    • Combinatorics on graphs, JEE Advanced level set-counting, and competitive olympiad problems all draw on the same FPC reasoning, just stacked many levels deep.

    The single habit to internalise: every counting problem reduces to (a) describe the choice as a sequence of stages, (b) ask whether each stage is independent or restricted by previous stages, (c) decide whether order matters at each stage. Once that’s clear, the formulas are just shorthand.
