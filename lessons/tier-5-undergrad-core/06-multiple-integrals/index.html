06 · Multiple Integrals

Integration over regions in 2D and 3D — volumes and probabilities

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T4 L08 — Definite Integral and 05 · Partial Derivatives?
  2. Comfortable with \int_a^b f(x) \, dx and the FTC?
  3. Recall area, volume formulas?

Touch

Pick a double integral over a rectangle. The widget computes it as two ordinary 1D integrals — inner first, then outer.

viewof miSel = Inputs.select(["∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ xy dx dy", "∫₀¹ ∫₀² x dx dy", "∫₀² ∫₀² (x+y) dx dy"], {value: "∫₀¹ ∫₀¹ xy dx dy", label: "Integral:"})

Wonder

  1. Why is \iint_R 1 \, dA = area of R? \iiint_V 1 \, dV = volume?
  2. Order of integration: does \int \int f \, dx \, dy = \int \int f \, dy \, dx?
  3. How would you integrate over a circle, not a rectangle?

Discover

Problem 1

Compute \int_0^1 \int_0^1 xy \, dy \, dx (inner: y from 0 to 1, treating x as a constant).

Hint 1 — a nudge Inner: \int_0^1 xy \, dy = x [y^2/2]_0^1 = x/2. Outer: \int_0^1 x/2 \, dx = 1/4.
Full solution

Result: 1/4.

Iterated: \int \int = \int [\int \, dy] dx. Inner first treats outer variable as constant; outer integrates the resulting 1D function.

Problem 2

Compute \int_0^2 \int_0^3 (x + y) \, dx \, dy.

Full solution

Inner: \int_0^3 (x + y) \, dx = [x^2/2 + xy]_0^3 = 9/2 + 3y.

Outer: \int_0^2 (9/2 + 3y) \, dy = 9 + 6 = 15.

Order can usually be swapped (Fubini’s theorem): $(x + y) , dy , dx = $ same.

Problem 3

Compute the area of the unit disk x^2 + y^2 \le 1 using a double integral.

Hint 1 — a nudge \iint_D 1 \, dA. Use polar coordinates: x = r \cos \theta, y = r \sin \theta, dA = r \, dr \, d\theta.
Full solution

\iint_D 1 \, dA = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^1 r \, dr \, d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} (1/2) \, d\theta = \pi.

Confirms the circle area formula.

In polar, r goes from 0 to 1 and \theta from 0 to 2\pi. The extra r factor in dA = r \, dr \, d\theta comes from the Jacobian of the change of variables.

Problem 4

A triple integral \iiint_V f \, dV over a 3D region. Compute \iiint_V 1 \, dV where V is the box [0, 2] \times [0, 3] \times [0, 4].

Full solution

\int_0^4 \int_0^3 \int_0^2 1 \, dx \, dy \, dz = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 = 24. (The volume of the box.)

For a constant integrand, \iiint 1 \, dV = volume of V. For varying f, you get a weighted volume — useful for mass (density), charge (charge density), expected values (probability density).

Problem 5

The expected value of a 2D random variable with joint density f(x, y) is E[X] = \iint x f(x, y) \, dA. Compute E[X] for f(x, y) = 4 x y on [0, 1]^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge E[X] = \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x \cdot 4 x y \, dy \, dx.
Full solution

\int_0^1 \int_0^1 4 x^2 y \, dy \, dx = \int_0^1 4 x^2 \cdot 1/2 \, dx = \int_0^1 2 x^2 \, dx = 2/3.

E[X] = 2/3. Expectations of multivariate distributions are multiple integrals — foundational in probability.

The fact that you can compute a double integral by doing two single integrals — and in either order — feels obvious, but it is not. Guido Fubini proved the rigorous version in 1907, building on Lebesgue’s new theory of integration: for any function whose absolute value has a finite integral over the rectangle, the iterated integrals exist and agree. Without Fubini’s theorem, the move from \iint to \int \int is a leap of faith. With it, every multivariate calculation in physics, statistics, and ML rests on solid ground.

A multiple integral is just adding up little pieces of a region — and you compute it by sweeping one direction at a time. The choice of coordinates (rectangular, polar, spherical) is the choice of how to slice the region; the Jacobian factor is the bookkeeping that keeps each slice the right size. Pick coordinates that match your region’s symmetry, and intractable integrals become one-liners.

Name

Double integral over region R: \iint_R f(x, y) \, dA = \int \int f(x, y) \, dy \, dx (iterated; order swappable for nice f, by Fubini’s theorem).

Triple integral over V: \iiint_V f \, dV, three iterated integrals.

Geometric meaning: $ , dA = $ area of R. $ , dV = $ volume of V. With weighting f: total of “f per unit area / volume” — mass, probability, charge.

Polar coordinates in 2D: x = r \cos \theta, y = r \sin \theta, dA = r \, dr \, d\theta.

Cylindrical (3D): like polar plus z. Spherical: (\rho, \theta, \phi), dV = \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\theta \, d\phi.

Change of variables: \iint f \, dA = \iint f \cdot |J| \, du \, dv where J is the Jacobian of the coordinate change.

Iterated integration: inner first, outer last; Polar / cylindrical / spherical for symmetric regions; Multiple integrals compute mass, probability, expected values.

Apply

Worked example

Compute \iint_R xy \, dA where R is the triangle with vertices (0, 0), (1, 0), (1, 1).

Triangle: 0 \le x \le 1, 0 \le y \le x.

\int_0^1 \int_0^x xy \, dy \, dx = \int_0^1 x [y^2/2]_0^x \, dx = \int_0^1 x^3 / 2 \, dx = 1/8.

Practice — Warm

W1. \int_0^1 \int_0^2 1 \, dx \, dy?

Full solution 2.

W2. \int_0^1 \int_0^1 x \, dy \, dx?

Full solution \int_0^1 x \cdot 1 \, dx = 1/2.

W3. Polar area element?

Full solution dA = r \, dr \, d\theta.

Practice — Working

P1. \int_0^2 \int_0^3 xy \, dy \, dx.

Full solution Inner: 9x/2. Outer: 9.

P2. Volume of the upper half of the unit ball (x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \le 1, z \ge 0).

Hint 1 — a nudge Spherical: \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^{\pi/2} \int_0^1 \rho^2 \sin \phi \, d\rho \, d\phi \, d\theta.
Full solution

2\pi \cdot 1 \cdot 1/3 = 2\pi/3.

(Half of the full ball volume 4\pi/3.)

P3. \iint_R 1 \, dA over the rectangle [0, 5] \times [0, 3].

Full solution 15 (area).

Practice — Stretch

S1. The Gaussian integral. \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2} \, dx = \sqrt{\pi}. The trick: square it as a 2D integral, switch to polar.

Hint 1 — a nudge \left(\int e^{-x^2} dx\right)^2 = \iint e^{-(x^2 + y^2)} \, dA. In polar: \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\infty e^{-r^2} r \, dr \, d\theta.
Full solution

Polar inner: \int_0^\infty e^{-r^2} r \, dr. Substitute u = r^2: \int_0^\infty e^{-u}/2 \, du = 1/2.

Outer: \int_0^{2\pi} 1/2 \, d\theta = \pi.

So \left(\int e^{-x^2} dx\right)^2 = \pi \Rightarrow \int e^{-x^2} dx = \sqrt{\pi} ✓.

This trick — squaring the integral as a 2D integral and using polar — is one of the most beautiful results in calculus, due to Poisson. Foundational to probability (normal distribution).

S2. Bayesian inference. A posterior probability p(\theta | D) \propto p(D | \theta) p(\theta) — the normalising constant is a high-dimensional integral \int p(D | \theta) p(\theta) \, d\theta. Why are these often intractable?

Full solution

For high-dimensional \theta (modern ML models have millions of parameters), the integral has no closed form and grid-based numerical integration scales as N^d — exponentially expensive in dimension. Hence ML uses:

  • Monte Carlo (sample-based estimation; T4 L08 S2).
  • Variational inference (replace p(\theta | D) with tractable approximation).
  • Markov Chain Monte Carlo (generate samples to approximate the integral).
The whole subfield of probabilistic ML exists to deal with intractable multivariate integrals.

Common confusions

  1. “Order of integration always matters.” For nice (continuous, absolutely integrable) functions on rectangles, Fubini says it doesn’t. For pathological cases or non-rectangular regions, be careful with limits.
  2. “Polar always uses r.” r is the radius. The Jacobian factor r in dA = r \, dr \, d\theta is essential — easy to forget.
  3. “Multiple integrals are just lots of 1D integrals.” Mostly yes for nice regions, but coordinate changes (polar, spherical) and complex regions complicate things.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A multiple integral is the higher-dimensional definite integral — it adds up f over an area or volume, slice by slice.
  2. Iterate inner-first, outer-last, and Fubini’s theorem says the order can usually be swapped for nice integrands.
  3. Change coordinates to match the region’s symmetry — polar for disks, spherical for balls — and don’t forget the Jacobian factor (the r in r \, dr \, d\theta).

Where this matters

AI / Machine Learning. Bayesian inference (S2) requires multivariate integration. Probabilistic generative models, Bayesian neural networks, and many statistical methods rest on these.

Physics & engineering. Mass, charge, energy, fluid flow are all multiple integrals over regions.

Statistics. The Gaussian integral (S1) gives the normalisation of the normal distribution; multivariate normal extends this with matrix inversions.
