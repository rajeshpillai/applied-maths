14 · Conditional Probability and Independence

What changes when you know more — and Bayes’ rule

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done 13 · Basic Probability?
  2. Comfortable with sample spaces and P(E) = \text{favourable}/\text{total}?
  3. Comfortable with inclusion-exclusion and complement rules?

Touch

Pick the disease prevalence and a test’s accuracy. The widget computes P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive test}) — the conditional probability that you actually have the disease, given a positive result. The answer is often shockingly low.

viewof prev = Inputs.range([0.001, 0.10], {value: 0.01, step: 0.001, label: "Disease prevalence (P(D)):"})
viewof sens = Inputs.range([0.80, 1.0], {value: 0.99, step: 0.01, label: "Test sensitivity (P(+|D)):"})
viewof spec = Inputs.range([0.80, 1.0], {value: 0.95, step: 0.01, label: "Test specificity (P(−|¬D)):"})
{
  const pD = prev;
  const pNotD = 1 - pD;
  const pPosGivenD = sens;
  const pPosGivenNotD = 1 - spec;
  const pPos = pPosGivenD * pD + pPosGivenNotD * pNotD;
  const pDGivenPos = (pPosGivenD * pD) / pPos;
  return html`<div style="margin: 1rem 0;">
    <div style="text-align:center; font-family:monospace; font-size:1.2rem;">
      P(D | +) = ${(pPosGivenD * pD).toFixed(4)} / ${pPos.toFixed(4)}
      = <strong style="color:#c2185b;">${(pDGivenPos * 100).toFixed(1)}%</strong>
    </div>
    <div style="text-align:center; font-size:0.95rem; margin-top:0.5rem; color:#666;">
      With a ${(prev*100).toFixed(1)}% prevalence and a ${(sens*100).toFixed(0)}%-sensitive,
      ${(spec*100).toFixed(0)}%-specific test, only ${(pDGivenPos * 100).toFixed(1)}%
      of positive results indicate true disease.
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

A positive test on a “99% accurate” screening for a 1% prevalent disease gives only 17% probability of actually being sick. Set the sliders. Watch the surprise. This is Bayes’ theorem at work.

Wonder

  1. P(disease | positive) — why isn’t it 99%?
  2. What changes if disease prevalence is 50% instead of 1%?
  3. Two events A, B are independent if knowing A doesn’t change the probability of B. Examples?

Discover

Problem 1

Two cards are drawn (without replacement) from a 52-card deck. P(second is red given first is red)?

Hint 1 — a nudge After removing one red card, 51 cards remain — 25 red.
Full solution

P(second red | first red) = 25/51 ≈ 0.490.

(Without conditioning, P(red) = 26/52 = 0.5. The condition lowers it slightly because one red was already taken.)

Notation: P(A | B) means “probability of A given B” — the conditional probability.

Problem 2

Define P(A | B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}. Verify with the card problem.

Hint 1 — a nudge P(A \cap B) = P(both red) = \dfrac{26}{52} \cdot \dfrac{25}{51}. P(B) = P(first red) = 26/52.
Full solution

P(A \cap B) = \dfrac{26}{52} \cdot \dfrac{25}{51} = \dfrac{650}{2652}. P(B) = \dfrac{26}{52}. \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \dfrac{25}{51} ✓.

The definition formalises “given B, restrict the sample space to B and re-normalise.”

Problem 3

Two events A, B are independent if P(A | B) = P(A) (equivalently, P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)).

Are coin tosses independent? Are card draws without replacement independent?

Full solution

Coin tosses: independent. The first toss tells you nothing about the second.

Card draws (no replacement): not independent — Problem 1 showed P(red | red) = 25/51, not P(red) = 26/52. The first draw affects the second.

Card draws with replacement: independent. After replacing the card, the deck is restored.

Problem 4

Apply the definition: P(A | B) · P(B) = P(A ∩ B). And by symmetry, P(B | A) · P(A) = P(A ∩ B). Equate: P(A|B) P(B) = P(B|A) P(A), giving: P(A | B) = \dfrac{P(B | A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}. This is Bayes’ theorem.

Full solution

Direct algebra from the definition. The theorem is named after Thomas Bayes (1701–1761).

Importance: lets us “flip” a conditional probability. Often P(B | A) is easy to know (sensitivity of a test, frequency of a symptom given a disease) but P(A | B) is what we want to know (probability of disease given a positive test, probability of a hypothesis given data).

Problem 5

A disease has prevalence 1% (P(D) = 0.01). A test has sensitivity 99% (P(+|D) = 0.99) and specificity 95% (P(−|¬D) = 0.95, so P(+|¬D) = 0.05). You test positive. What is P(D | +)?

Hint 1 — a nudge Apply Bayes: P(D|+) = \dfrac{P(+|D) P(D)}{P(+)}. P(+) = P(+|D) P(D) + P(+|\neg D) P(\neg D) = 0.99 \cdot 0.01 + 0.05 \cdot 0.99 \approx 0.059.
Full solution

P(D|+) = \dfrac{0.99 \cdot 0.01}{0.0594} \approx 0.167 — only 17%.

The reason: with 1% prevalence and a 5% false-positive rate, most positives come from healthy people (because there are 99× more of them). Even with a “99% accurate” test, the rare disease and the common false-positive together make positive results unreliable.

This is the base rate fallacy — and it explains why screening tests for rare conditions need follow-up confirmation.

Thomas Bayes (1701–1761), an English Presbyterian minister, never published the theorem now bearing his name. After his death, his friend Richard Price found the manuscript among his papers and submitted it to the Royal Society as “An Essay towards solving a Problem in the Doctrine of Chances” (1763). The essay was largely ignored for two centuries — then resurrected in the 20th century to become the backbone of medical statistics, machine learning, and the spam filter that protects your inbox today.

Conditioning shrinks the sample space. “Given B” simply throws away every outcome where B didn’t happen, then re-normalises the rest. Bayes’ theorem is the algebraic price of switching which event you stand inside.

Name

Conditional probability: P(A | B) = \dfrac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}, provided P(B) > 0.

Multiplication rule: P(A \cap B) = P(A | B) \cdot P(B) = P(B | A) \cdot P(A).

Independence: A and B are independent iff P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B), \quad \text{equivalently} \quad P(A | B) = P(A).

Bayes’ theorem: P(A | B) = \dfrac{P(B | A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}.

Law of total probability: if \{B_1, \ldots, B_k\} partitions the sample space, P(A) = \sum_{i} P(A | B_i) P(B_i).

Conditional probability restricts to the conditioning event; Independence: knowing one tells you nothing about the other; Bayes’ theorem flips P(A|B) into P(B|A) — the inference rule.

Apply

Worked example

A factory has two machines. M1 produces 60% of parts, with 2% defective. M2 produces 40%, with 5% defective. A defective part is found. What is the probability it came from M1?

A competent solver applies Bayes’ theorem:

  1. Setup: P(M_1) = 0.6, P(M_2) = 0.4, P(D|M_1) = 0.02, P(D|M_2) = 0.05.
  2. Total: P(D) = 0.02 \cdot 0.6 + 0.05 \cdot 0.4 = 0.012 + 0.02 = 0.032.
  3. Bayes: P(M_1 | D) = \dfrac{0.02 \cdot 0.6}{0.032} = \dfrac{0.012}{0.032} = 0.375.

Answer: 37.5%. Despite M1 producing more parts overall, M2 contributes more to defective parts (proportionally). Posterior probabilities flip your intuition.

Practice — Warm

W1. P(rolling 6 | rolling even) on a die?

Full solution P(6 ∩ even)/P(even) = (1/6)/(3/6) = 1/3.

W2. Two coin tosses. Independent? P(2nd H | 1st H)?

Full solution Independent. P(2nd H | 1st H) = P(2nd H) = 1/2.

W3. Bayes: P(A|B) = ? given P(A) = 0.3, P(B|A) = 0.4, P(B) = 0.5.

Full solution P(A|B) = (0.4 \cdot 0.3)/0.5 = 0.24.

Practice — Working

P1. A bag has 4 red and 6 blue marbles. Draw two without replacement. P(both red)?

Hint 1 — a nudge P(R_1 \cap R_2) = P(R_1) \cdot P(R_2 | R_1) = (4/10)(3/9).
Full solution = 12/90 = 2/15 \approx 0.133.

P2. Email spam filter: P(spam) = 0.4. P(contains “free” | spam) = 0.7. P(contains “free” | not spam) = 0.05. An email contains “free.” P(spam | “free”)?

Hint 1 — a nudge Bayes. P(“free”) = 0.7·0.4 + 0.05·0.6 = 0.31.
Full solution

P(\text{spam} | \text{"free"}) = \dfrac{0.7 \cdot 0.4}{0.31} \approx 0.903.

About 90%. Modern spam filters (Naive Bayes, originally) use Bayes’ theorem on many keywords simultaneously.

P3. A and B are independent with P(A) = 0.3, P(B) = 0.5. P(A or B)?

Hint 1 — a nudge Inclusion-exclusion: P(A) + P(B) - P(A) P(B).
Full solution 0.3 + 0.5 - 0.15 = 0.65.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Monty Hall problem. Three doors. Behind one: a car. Behind the other two: goats. You pick door A. The host (who knows where the car is) opens door B revealing a goat and offers you the chance to switch to door C. Should you switch?

Hint 1 — a nudge P(car behind A) = 1/3. P(car behind B or C) = 2/3. After host opens B (always a goat), where is that 2/3 concentrated?
Full solution

Switch. P(car behind A) is still 1/3 (the host’s reveal didn’t change it — he was forced to open a goat door). The remaining 2/3 probability is now entirely on door C.

So P(win | stay) = 1/3, P(win | switch) = 2/3. Switching doubles your chances.

This problem gained fame in the 1990s when Marilyn vos Savant correctly explained it in Parade magazine and was deluged with angry letters from PhDs (including Paul Erdős!) insisting she was wrong. She wasn’t. Conditional probability defies intuition.

S2. Naive Bayes classifier. With features x_1, \ldots, x_n and class C, assume features are conditionally independent given C: P(C | x_1, \ldots, x_n) \propto P(C) \prod_i P(x_i | C). Why is this useful — and why “naive”?

Full solution

Useful: turns a high-dimensional joint probability into a product of one-dimensional conditionals — vastly simpler to estimate and compute.

Naive: real features are usually not conditionally independent (in spam filtering, “free” and “money” co-occur more than independence predicts). Yet Naive Bayes works surprisingly well in practice — for many tasks, the assumption errs only on probability magnitudes, not on which class wins.

Naive Bayes is one of the oldest and still useful classifiers, especially as a baseline. It exemplifies a common ML strategy: make a simplifying assumption, derive a tractable model, and let empirical performance vindicate it.

Common confusions

  1. “P(A|B) = P(B|A).” Almost never. Bayes’ theorem relates them: P(A|B) = P(B|A) \cdot P(A)/P(B). Confusing the two is the prosecutor’s fallacy (P(evidence|innocent) ≠ P(innocent|evidence)).
  2. “Independent events have nothing to do with each other.” They can both occur — they’re just probabilistically uncorrelated. Don’t confuse with mutually exclusive (which means they cannot both occur).
  3. “P(B|A) is always smaller than P(B).” Not necessarily — it can be larger or smaller, depending on whether A makes B more or less likely.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Conditional probability restricts the sample space to “given B” and re-normalises — that’s the entire definition; everything else is bookkeeping.
  2. Bayes’ theorem is how you flip a conditional. What you can measure (P(\text{symptom} \mid \text{disease})) is rarely what you want (P(\text{disease} \mid \text{symptom})); Bayes is the bridge.
  3. Independence is a fragile gift. If A and B are independent, joint probability factors — P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B). Most real-world events are not independent, which is why “naive” assumptions get you in trouble.

Where this matters

AI / Machine Learning. Naive Bayes (S2) is a classic classifier. Bayesian inference is an entire approach to ML — every model is a posterior probability updated from data. Bayesian deep learning is an active research area.

Encryption. Side-channel attacks use Bayes’ theorem on partial observations to recover keys. Probabilistic security proofs are foundational.

Medicine & policy. Diagnostic test interpretation (S5) requires Bayes thinking. Public-health policy on screening is shaped by base- rate considerations.

Law & forensics. The prosecutor’s fallacy (confusion 1) has caused multiple high-profile miscarriages of justice. Sound legal reasoning needs Bayes thinking.
