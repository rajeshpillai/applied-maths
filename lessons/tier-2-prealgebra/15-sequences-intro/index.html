15 · Sequences (Intro)

Numbers in a deliberate order — and the two patterns that govern much of the world

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T1 L11 — Variables and 07 · Exponents?
  2. Can you compute 2^5 = 32?
  3. Have you ever heard “compound interest” or “Moore’s Law”? They are geometric sequences — the topic of this lesson.

Touch

Pick a starting value and a rule. The widget shows the first 10 terms of the resulting sequence.

viewof seqType = Inputs.select(["arithmetic (add d)", "geometric (multiply r)"], {value: "arithmetic (add d)", label: "Type:"})
viewof seqStart = Inputs.range([-5, 10], {value: 2, step: 1, label: "First term a₁:"})
viewof seqStep = Inputs.range([-5, 5], {value: 3, step: 0.5, label: "Step (d or r):"})
{
  const terms = [];
  let cur = seqStart;
  for (let i = 0; i < 10; i++) {
    terms.push(cur);
    cur = (seqType === "arithmetic (add d)") ? cur + seqStep : cur * seqStep;
  }
  const formula = (seqType === "arithmetic (add d)")
    ? `aₙ = ${seqStart} + (n−1) × ${seqStep}`
    : `aₙ = ${seqStart} × ${seqStep}^(n−1)`;
  return html`<div style="margin: 1rem 0;">
    <div style="font-family:monospace; text-align:center; font-size:1.2rem;">
      ${terms.map((t, i) => `<span style="color:${i === 0 ? '#c2185b' : '#1f2933'};font-weight:${i === 0 ? 'bold' : 'normal'};">${typeof t === 'number' && Math.abs(t) > 9999 ? t.toExponential(2) : (Number.isInteger(t) ? t : t.toFixed(2))}</span>`).join(', ')}, ...
    </div>
    <div style="font-family:monospace; text-align:center; margin-top:0.6rem; color:#666;">${formula}</div>

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers. Arithmetic sequences add the same amount each step. Geometric sequences multiply by the same factor each step. The first is linear; the second is exponential.

Wonder

  1. Pick “arithmetic (add d)” with start 2 and d = 3. Read the terms aloud. Each is bigger than the last by how much?
  2. Now switch to “geometric (multiply r)” with start 2 and r = 3. Read the terms. How fast do they grow?
  3. Set arithmetic with d = 0.5. Now geometric with r = 0.5. What happens?

Discover

Problem 1

The sequence 3, 7, 11, 15, \ldots — what kind of sequence is it, and what’s the next term?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each step, what is added?
Full solution

Common difference d = 4. Arithmetic sequence. Next term: 19.

Formula: a_n = a_1 + (n - 1)d = 3 + (n - 1) \cdot 4. The 10th term: a_{10} = 3 + 9 \cdot 4 = 39.

Problem 2

The sequence 2, 6, 18, 54, \ldots — what kind, and what’s the next?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each step, what is multiplied?
Full solution

Common ratio r = 3. Geometric sequence. Next term: 162.

Formula: a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{n-1} = 2 \cdot 3^{n-1}.

Problem 3

You deposit Rs. 10000 in a savings account at 5% interest, compounded annually. After year 1: Rs. 10500. After year 2: 5% on the new balance, so Rs. 10500 × 1.05 = Rs. 11025. After year 3: 11025 × 1.05.

Is the balance over years an arithmetic or geometric sequence?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each year, you multiply by the same factor (1.05).
Full solution

Geometric, with r = 1.05. After n years: balance = 10000 \cdot 1.05^n.

After 10 years: 10000 \cdot 1.05^{10} \approx 16289 rupees. After 30 years: 10000 \cdot 1.05^{30} \approx 43219 rupees.

Compound interest is exponential, not linear. Doubling time at 5%: about 14 years. (The “Rule of 72” estimates doubling time as 72/rate.)

Problem 4

A geometric sequence with r > 1 grows fast. With r < 1 (but positive), it shrinks. Compute the first 5 terms of a geometric sequence with a_1 = 100 and r = 0.5.

Full solution

100, 50, 25, 12.5, 6.25, \ldots Each term is half the previous.

This is the pattern of radioactive decay (half the substance remains after each “half-life”), of drug elimination from the body (approximately), and of any damped oscillation.

If you continue forever, the terms approach 0 — they have a limit. The infinite sum is bounded too: 100 + 50 + 25 + \cdots = 200. We meet infinite series properly in Tier 3.

Problem 5

An arithmetic sequence has a_3 = 11 and a_7 = 23. Find a_1 and d.

Hint 1 — a nudge Going from a_3 to a_7 is 4 steps, each of size d. So a_7 - a_3 = 4d.
Full solution

23 - 11 = 12 = 4d \Rightarrow d = 3. a_3 = a_1 + 2d \Rightarrow 11 = a_1 + 6 \Rightarrow a_1 = 5.

Check: sequence is 5, 8, 11, 14, 17, 20, 23, \ldots. a_3 = 11 ✓, a_7 = 23 ✓.

Two pieces of information about an arithmetic (or geometric) sequence are enough to pin down all terms. This is the same “two equations, two unknowns” idea from Lesson 6.

The famous Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, \ldots) debuted in Europe in 1202 in the Liber Abaci of Leonardo of Pisa — known to history as Fibonacci. He posed a deliberately playful problem: if a pair of newborn rabbits matures in one month, then breeds a new pair every month after, and no rabbit ever dies, how many pairs are alive after a year? The month-by-month answer is the sequence that bears his name. The same book also championed the Hindu–Arabic digits (0–9) over Roman numerals — arguably its more consequential contribution.

An arithmetic sequence walks; a geometric sequence runs. Walking adds the same step every time — distance grows in proportion to time. Running multiplies — distance explodes (or collapses) at a rate that quickly dwarfs anything walking can do. That single difference is why compound interest looks like a miracle and why exponential growth ends every “this can continue forever” intuition.

Name

A sequence is an ordered list of numbers a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots. The number in position n is a_n, the n-th term.

Arithmetic sequence: common difference d. Each term is the previous plus d. a_n = a_1 + (n - 1) d.

Geometric sequence: common ratio r. Each term is the previous times r. a_n = a_1 \cdot r^{n - 1}.

Sum of an arithmetic sequence (n terms): S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}(a_1 + a_n) = \dfrac{n}{2}(2 a_1 + (n-1)d).

Sum of a geometric sequence (n terms, r \neq 1): S_n = a_1 \cdot \dfrac{r^n - 1}{r - 1}.

Arithmetic — add a fixed step; the formula is linear in n. Geometric — multiply by a fixed factor; the formula is exponential in n. Two terms determine the whole sequence; sums have closed forms.

Apply

Worked example

Find the sum of the first 100 positive integers: 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 100.

A competent solver recognises this as an arithmetic sequence with a_1 = 1, d = 1, n = 100, a_n = 100.

S = \dfrac{100}{2}(1 + 100) = 50 \cdot 101 = 5050.

Answer: 5050. Legend has it that the young Carl Friedrich Gauss (later one of the greatest mathematicians) was set this exact problem by a teacher who hoped to keep him quiet for an hour. Gauss returned the answer in minutes by pairing terms (1 + 100, 2 + 99, \ldots, 50 + 51 — fifty pairs each summing to 101) and computing 50 \cdot 101 = 5050. The same trick yields the general formula.

Practice — Warm

W1. Identify each as arithmetic or geometric, and give the next two terms:

  1. 5, 8, 11, 14, \ldots
  2. 3, 6, 12, 24, \ldots
  3. 20, 17, 14, 11, \ldots
Full solution
  1. Arithmetic, d = 3: 17, 20.
  2. Geometric, r = 2: 48, 96.
  3. Arithmetic, d = -3: 8, 5.

W2. For arithmetic sequence a_1 = 4, d = 5, find a_{10}.

Full solution a_{10} = 4 + 9 \cdot 5 = 49.

W3. For geometric sequence a_1 = 3, r = 2, find a_5.

Full solution a_5 = 3 \cdot 2^4 = 48.

Practice — Working

P1. Sum of the first 50 positive even integers (2 + 4 + 6 + \cdots + 100).

Full solution Arithmetic with a_1 = 2, d = 2, n = 50, a_n = 100. S = \dfrac{50}{2}(2 + 100) = 25 \cdot 102 = 2550.

P2. Find the 8th term of the sequence 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \ldots.

Full solution

Geometric, a_1 = 1, r = 2. a_8 = 1 \cdot 2^7 = 128.

This is the doubling sequence — the same one from the chess-board problem (T2 L07 Stretch S1).

P3. A car worth Rs. 800,000 depreciates by 20% each year. What is its value after 5 years?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each year multiplies by (1 - 0.20) = 0.80. Geometric sequence with r = 0.8.
Full solution

a_5 = 800000 \cdot 0.8^5 = 800000 \cdot 0.32768 \approx 262{,}144 rupees.

Depreciation, drug elimination, atmospheric absorption — many real-world quantities decay geometrically.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A neural network’s learning rate is often decayed geometrically: each epoch, the rate is multiplied by 0.95 (say). Starting at 0.01, what is the learning rate after 50 epochs? After 100 epochs?

Hint 1 — a nudge \text{rate}_n = 0.01 \cdot 0.95^n.
Full solution

After 50: 0.01 \cdot 0.95^{50} \approx 0.01 \cdot 0.0769 \approx 7.7 \times 10^{-4}. After 100: 0.01 \cdot 0.95^{100} \approx 0.01 \cdot 0.0059 \approx 5.9 \times 10^{-5}.

By epoch 100 the rate has shrunk to about 0.6% of its starting value — still positive, but very small. This annealing of the learning rate is one of the most common training tricks in deep learning, and it is exactly a geometric sequence.

S2. The Fibonacci sequence is neither arithmetic nor geometric: each term is the sum of the two previous. Starting from 1, 1: 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, \ldots. Compute the next 5 terms. What is unusual about this rule compared to arithmetic/geometric?

Full solution

Continuing: 34, 55, 89, 144, 233, \ldots.

Unusual: the rule is recursive: a_n = a_{n-1} + a_{n-2} — each term depends on two previous terms, not just one. This places Fibonacci outside the simple arithmetic/geometric world.

Yet a closed form does exist: Binet’s formula a_n = \dfrac{\phi^n - (1 - \phi)^n}{\sqrt{5}}, \qquad \phi = \dfrac{1 + \sqrt{5}}{2}.

The number \phi \approx 1.618 is the golden ratio — and a_{n+1} / a_n \to \phi as n grows. So Fibonacci is asymptotically geometric with ratio \phi, even though it isn’t exactly so.

The Fibonacci sequence appears in nature (sunflower spirals, pinecone patterns), in art (the golden rectangle), in algorithms (Fibonacci heaps, Euclidean algorithm complexity), and in finance (technical analysis “Fibonacci retracements”). A whole journal, The Fibonacci Quarterly, is dedicated to it. Recursive sequences are a major topic of Tier 5+ discrete mathematics.

Common confusions

  1. “Arithmetic = numbers; geometric = shapes.” No — both are types of sequence. Arithmetic adds a fixed amount; geometric multiplies by a fixed factor. The names are historical.
  2. “a_1 is term zero.” Convention varies. Most school texts start with a_1; computer scientists often use a_0. Be consistent within one problem.
  3. “All sequences are arithmetic or geometric.” No — most sequences are neither (e.g., Fibonacci, the prime numbers, the sequence of perfect squares 1, 4, 9, 16, \ldots — which is quadratic).

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A sequence is an ordered list of numbers — and most of the world’s interesting growth follows one of two simple rules.
  2. An arithmetic sequence adds a fixed step; a geometric sequence multiplies by a fixed factor. Linear vs exponential, in one distinction.
  3. Two terms are enough to pin the whole sequence down — and both types have closed-form sum formulas, so you never need to add term by term.

Where this matters

AI / Machine Learning. Learning-rate annealing (S1), exponential moving averages, batch-size warmup schedules — all are sequences in the training loop. Many model-architecture choices (hidden layer widths, attention head counts) are also sequences.

Finance. Compound interest is the most consequential geometric sequence in human life — it underlies retirement planning, debt accumulation, mortgage amortisation, inflation, and economic growth.

Physics & biology. Radioactive decay, population growth (under unlimited resources), pharmacokinetics, sound attenuation — geometric sequences and their continuous counterpart, the exponential function (Tier 3+), are everywhere.
