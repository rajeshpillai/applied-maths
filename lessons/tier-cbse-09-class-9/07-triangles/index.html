Ch 7 — Triangles (Congruence and Properties)

SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, RHS — the laws of identical triangles

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you state the angle-sum theorem of a triangle? (If no, revisit cbse9.lines-and-angles.)
  2. Can you write a two-column proof?
  3. Can you recognise an isosceles triangle (two sides equal)?
  4. Do you know what perpendicular means? (If no, revisit t1.shapes-angles.)

Three or four yes? Read on.

Touch

Cut three strips of cardboard: 6 cm, 8 cm, 10 cm. Form a triangle by joining them at their endpoints. Now have a partner cut a second set of three strips with the same lengths. Form a triangle on the other side of the table.

Lift one triangle and place it on the other. They overlap exactly — even if you flip one over.

viewof a = Inputs.range([2, 10], {value: 6, step: 1, label: "side a"})
viewof b = Inputs.range([2, 10], {value: 8, step: 1, label: "side b"})
viewof angC = Inputs.range([20, 160], {value: 90, step: 5, label: "angle C (°)"})
{
  // Construct triangle by SAS: sides a, b with included angle C
  const θ = (angC * Math.PI) / 180;
  const c2 = a*a + b*b - 2*a*b*Math.cos(θ);
  const c = Math.sqrt(c2);
  const A = (Math.acos((b*b + c2 - a*a)/(2*b*c)) * 180/Math.PI);
  const B = 180 - angC - A;
  return html`<div style="font-family: monospace; font-size: 1rem; padding: 0.4rem 0;">
    Two sides ${a} and ${b}, included angle ${angC}° → third side ≈ ${c.toFixed(3)}<br/>
    Other two angles ≈ ${A.toFixed(1)}° and ${B.toFixed(1)}°<br/>
    <span style="color:#080">Same three inputs always give the same triangle.</span>
  </div>`;
}

Drag the sliders. The same three inputs fix one triangle. Two triangles built from the same recipe are guaranteed identical.

Wonder

  1. If two triangles share just one equal side, are they identical? What if they share two equal sides?
  2. To uniquely build a triangle, how many measurements do you need — three? more? Which three?
  3. Could you have two different triangles with the same three angles? With the same three sides?
  4. Why does the carpenter’s rule “diagonals brace a square” work — why does adding a diagonal to a four-sided frame stop it from wobbling?
TipA whisper, no answer yet

Q3 hides the difference between congruence (identical) and similarity (same shape, different size). This year is congruence; similarity comes next year.

Discover

Problem 1 (SSS)

You and a friend each draw a triangle with sides 5, 7, 8. Without seeing each other’s drawings, would you trust that the two triangles are identical (after possibly flipping)?

Hint 1 — a nudge There is only one way to fit three given sides together (up to a flip). Try with strips of paper.
Full solution Yes. Three sides — and nothing else — uniquely determine a triangle up to flipping. This is the SSS congruence rule.

Problem 2 (SAS)

A bridge truss is built with two diagonal members of 3 m and 4 m, meeting at a 60^\circ angle. A second truss is built the same way. Are both trusses interchangeable parts?

Hint 1 — a nudge Two sides and the angle between them — does that pin down the third side?
Full solution Yes, by SAS (side–angle–side). Two sides and the included angle fix the triangle uniquely.

Problem 3 (ASA)

A surveyor measures an angle from one bank of a river (60^\circ), walks 50 m along the bank, and measures a second angle from there (45^\circ). Can he reconstruct the river-crossing triangle without ever crossing the river?

Hint 1 — a nudge Two angles plus the side between them.
Full solution Yes, by ASA (angle–side–angle). Two angles and the included side fix the triangle uniquely. (And once two angles are known, the third is forced by the angle-sum theorem.)

Problem 4 (Isosceles theorem)

In \triangle ABC, AB = AC. Drop the bisector AD from A onto BC (so \angle BAD = \angle CAD). Prove \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD, and deduce \angle B = \angle C.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD. Which sides and angles match?
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. AB = AC. Given.
2. \angle BAD = \angle CAD. AD is the bisector.
3. AD = AD. Common side.
4. \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD. SAS, from 1, 2, 3.
5. \angle B = \angle C. Corresponding parts of congruent triangles (CPCT). \blacksquare
The angles opposite equal sides of a triangle are equal.

Problem 5 (Converse)

Now suppose only that \angle B = \angle C in \triangle ABC. Use ASA with the bisector to prove AB = AC.

Hint 1 — a nudge Drop the bisector AD as before. Try ASA on \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. \angle B = \angle C. Given.
2. \angle BAD = \angle CAD. AD is the angle bisector.
3. AD = AD. Common.
4. \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD. ASA.
5. AB = AC. CPCT. \blacksquare
The sides opposite equal angles are equal (converse).

Problem 6 (RHS)

Two right triangles have hypotenuse 13 cm and one leg 5 cm. Are they congruent?

Hint 1 — a nudge You can’t use SSA in general — it’s the one rule that fails (the “AAS” imitator). But for right triangles, the right angle helps.
Full solution Yes, by RHS (right angle, hypotenuse, side). The third side is forced by Pythagoras: \sqrt{13^2 - 5^2} = 12. So both triangles have sides 5, 12, 13 and hence are congruent by SSS.

You discovered: SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, RHS — five rules, each pinning down a unique triangle from a small set of measurements. CPCT lets you read off any unknown part once you have a congruence.

The Indian śulbasūtras (~800 BCE — “rules of the cord”) contain the oldest known recipe for laying out a perfect right angle on the ground: stretch a rope into a closed loop knotted into 12 equal segments, and pull it taut as a 3, 4, 5 triangle. The right angle sits at the corner between the sides of length 3 and 4. Vedic priests used this to align fire-altars centuries before Pythagoras stated the algebraic identity.

A small handful of measurements (three, in the right combination) is enough to force a triangle into existence — and once forced, every other side and angle is determined. Congruence is the language for saying “two triangles are not just similar, they are the same”.

Name

Two triangles are congruent (notation \triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF) if all corresponding sides and corresponding angles are equal. The five tests for congruence:

  • SSS — three sides equal.
  • SAS — two sides and the included angle equal.
  • ASA — two angles and the included side equal.
  • AAS — two angles and any non-included side equal (follows from ASA, because the third angle is forced).
  • RHS — for right triangles only: hypotenuse and one other side equal.

Once two triangles are congruent, CPCT (corresponding parts of congruent triangles) is the rule that lets you conclude any other side or angle is equal.

Isosceles triangle theorem (and converse). In a triangle, two sides are equal if and only if their opposite angles are equal.

Triangle inequality. The sum of any two sides of a triangle is greater than the third side.

A short verse:

Three sides, or two-and-included, or angle-side-angle — Each is a recipe; the triangle obeys it once. Right-angled? Then hypotenuse-and-side suffice. Equal sides? Equal angles. Each implies the other.

Apply

Worked example

In \triangle PQR, PQ = PR. The bisector of \angle P meets QR at M. Prove PM \perp QR and that M is the midpoint of QR.

A competent solver:

  1. Compare \triangle PQM and \triangle PRM.
  2. PQ = PR (given), \angle QPM = \angle RPM (bisector definition), PM = PM (common). So SAS ⟹ \triangle PQM \cong \triangle PRM.
  3. CPCT gives QM = RM (so M is midpoint) and \angle PMQ = \angle PMR.
  4. The two angles at M are also a linear pair (they sit on QR), so \angle PMQ + \angle PMR = 180^\circ. With both equal, \angle PMQ = 90^\circ, hence PM \perp QR.

Conclusion: PM is both the perpendicular and the median from P. \blacksquare

Practice — Warm

W1. State which congruence rule (SSS / SAS / ASA / AAS / RHS) is suggested by each of these descriptions.

  1. “Two sides and the angle between them are equal.”

  2. “Two angles and a corresponding side are equal.”

  3. “Two right triangles share hypotenuse and a leg.”

Full solution
  1. SAS. (b) AAS or ASA depending on which side. (c) RHS.

W2. In \triangle ABC, AB = AC and \angle A = 40^\circ. Find \angle B and \angle C.

Full solution \angle B = \angle C. 40 + 2x = 180 \Rightarrow x = 70^\circ. So \angle B = \angle C = 70^\circ.

W3. Sides of a triangle are 4, 5, 9. Possible?

Hint 1 — a nudge Triangle inequality: each pair sums to more than the third side.
Full solution 4 + 5 = 9 — equal, not greater. So the “triangle” collapses to a line. Not a valid triangle.

Practice — Working

P1. AB and CD are two segments bisecting each other at O. Prove \triangle AOC \cong \triangle BOD.

Hint 1 — a nudge AO = OB, CO = OD (bisecting). Where is the angle?
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. AO = OB. AB bisected at O.
2. CO = OD. CD bisected at O.
3. \angle AOC = \angle BOD. Vertically opposite angles.
4. \triangle AOC \cong \triangle BOD. SAS. \blacksquare

P2. In \triangle ABC, AB = AC. Two points D on AB and E on AC are chosen so AD = AE. Prove BE = CD.

Hint 1 — a nudge Look at \triangle ABE and \triangle ACD.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. AB = AC. Given.
2. AE = AD. Given.
3. \angle A = \angle A. Common.
4. \triangle ABE \cong \triangle ACD. SAS.
5. BE = CD. CPCT. \blacksquare

P3. AD is a median of \triangle ABC (so D is the midpoint of BC). AD is extended to a point E such that DE = AD. Prove \triangle ADB \cong \triangle EDC, and conclude that AB = EC and AB \parallel EC.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two segments meet at D; what kind of angles form there?
Hint 2 — the technique You have one pair of equal sides (DB = DC, AD = DE). You need an angle between those sides — vertically opposite angles at D. SAS.
Full solution

In \triangle ADB and \triangle EDC:

Statement Reason
1. AD = ED. Given (E is on the extension of AD with DE = AD).
2. BD = DC. D is the midpoint of BC (AD is a median).
3. \angle ADB = \angle EDC. Vertically opposite angles at D.
4. \triangle ADB \cong \triangle EDC. SAS (steps 1, 3, 2).
5. AB = EC. CPCT.
6. \angle DAB = \angle DEC. CPCT.
7. AB \parallel EC. Step 6: alternate interior angles equal \Rightarrow lines parallel (transversal AE). \blacksquare
This is a classic NCERT result — it tells you that doubling a median produces a parallelogram ABEC. We come back to it in Lesson 8: Quadrilaterals.

Practice — Stretch

S1. In \triangle ABC, the bisector of \angle A meets BC at D. If AB = AC, prove D is the midpoint of BC and AD \perp BC.

Hint 1 — a nudge This is the worked example, generalised. Use SAS on \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD.
Full solution Mirror the worked example exactly. SAS gives the congruence; CPCT gives BD = DC (midpoint) and \angle ADB = \angle ADC, which together with the linear pair forces both to be 90^\circ. \blacksquare

S2. Show that the perpendicular from any vertex of an equilateral triangle to the opposite side bisects that side.

Hint 1 — a nudge Equilateral is “extra-isosceles”. Use RHS.
Full solution Drop AD \perp BC. In right triangles \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD: AB = AC (equilateral), AD = AD (common), \angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90^\circ. So \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD by RHS. CPCT gives BD = DC. \blacksquare

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). In \triangle ABC, if \angle A = \angle B, which sides are equal? (a) AB = BC (b) BC = CA (c) CA = AB (d) all three.

Full solution (b) BC = CA — the sides opposite equal angles are equal. Side BC is opposite A; side CA is opposite B.

2-mark (short answer). Two sides of a triangle are 7 cm and 9 cm. Between which two integer values must the third side lie?

Hint 1 — a nudge Triangle inequality: third side < sum, > difference.
Full solution |7 - 9| < x < 7 + 9, so 2 < x < 16. Integer values: 3, 4, \ldots, 15.

3-mark (short answer II). ABCD is a quadrilateral in which AB = AD and CB = CD. Prove that AC bisects both \angle A and \angle C.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare \triangle ABC and \triangle ADC.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. AB = AD. Given.
2. CB = CD. Given.
3. AC = AC. Common.
4. \triangle ABC \cong \triangle ADC. SSS.
5. \angle BAC = \angle DAC and \angle BCA = \angle DCA. CPCT.
6. So AC bisects \angle A and \angle C. \blacksquare

5-mark (long answer). Prove the isosceles triangle theorem: in \triangle ABC, if AB = AC, then \angle B = \angle C. Use a clear figure and a two-column proof.

Hint 1 — a nudge Drop the bisector from A. Prove the two halves congruent by SAS.
Full solution Reproduce Discover Problem 4. Construction: angle bisector from A meets BC at D. Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD: AB = AC (given), \angle BAD = \angle CAD (bisector), AD = AD (common). By SAS, \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD. By CPCT, \angle B = \angle C. \blacksquare

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Class 9 Exemplar Ch. 7, Problem 7.2 (SAS recognition, similar to W1). Previous-year pointer: CBSE Class 9 sample paper “prove AC bisects in a kite” (similar to 3-mark).

Common confusions

  1. “SSA is also a congruence rule.” No — two sides and a non-included angle can describe two different triangles (the “ambiguous case”). Only when the angle is right (RHS) does it work.
  2. “AAA proves congruence.” No — three equal angles only force similarity (same shape), not congruence (same size). A small equilateral triangle and a large one share AAA but are different sizes.
  3. “CPCT can be used before proving congruence.” It cannot. CPCT is a consequence of congruence; you must establish the congruence first, then quote CPCT to extract a side or angle equality.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Three matching measurements (in the right pattern) force two triangles to be identical. SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS, RHS — five recipes, no others.
  2. CPCT is a payoff, not a tool. First establish congruence, then read off any equal side or angle for free.
  3. Equal sides and equal angles always come in pairs. Opposite-side to opposite-angle, in both directions — that is the isosceles theorem and its converse.

Where this matters

Game development. Mesh deformation in 3D engines preserves triangle topology (which vertices are connected) but changes geometry. When a 3D character moves, the rig sends each triangle to a congruent copy in a new position — that’s why characters don’t suddenly grow extra limbs.

Research. The congruence axioms became one of Hilbert’s groups in his 1899 axiomatisation. They survive almost unchanged into modern Tarski-style geometry, the version most amenable to automated theorem provers.
