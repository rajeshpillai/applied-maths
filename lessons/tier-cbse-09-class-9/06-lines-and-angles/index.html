Ch 6 — Lines and Angles

Linear pairs, vertical angles, parallels and transversals

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you measure an angle with a protractor and recognise 90^\circ, 180^\circ? (If no, revisit t1.shapes-angles.)
  2. Can you write a two-column geometric proof? (If no, revisit cbse9.euclids-geometry.)
  3. Do you know that two angles which add to 180^\circ are called “supplementary”?

Three yes? Read on.

Touch

Take two pencils. Cross them at a point. Look at the four angles formed. The two angles directly across from each other look identical. The two adjacent angles look like they fill exactly half a turn.

viewof tilt = Inputs.range([10, 170], {value: 65, step: 1, label: "tilt (°)"})
{
  const r = 100, c = 130;
  const θ = (tilt * Math.PI) / 180;
  const x1 = c - r, y1 = c;
  const x2 = c + r, y2 = c;
  const x3 = c - r * Math.cos(θ), y3 = c + r * Math.sin(θ);
  const x4 = c + r * Math.cos(θ), y4 = c - r * Math.sin(θ);
  const supl = 180 - tilt;
  return html`<svg width="280" height="260" style="border:1px solid #ccc">
    <line x1="${x1}" y1="${y1}" x2="${x2}" y2="${y2}" stroke="#333" stroke-width="2"/>
    <line x1="${x3}" y1="${y3}" x2="${x4}" y2="${y4}" stroke="#333" stroke-width="2"/>
    <text x="${c + 30}" y="${c - 10}" font-size="13" fill="#c33">${tilt}°</text>
    <text x="${c - 50}" y="${c + 25}" font-size="13" fill="#c33">${tilt}°</text>
    <text x="${c - 50}" y="${c - 12}" font-size="13" fill="#080">${supl}°</text>
    <text x="${c + 12}" y="${c + 22}" font-size="13" fill="#080">${supl}°</text>
  </svg>`;
}

Drag the slider. Whatever the tilt, the opposite-coloured pairs are always equal. Adjacent (different-coloured) ones always sum to 180^\circ.

Wonder

  1. Why do the two opposite angles always come out equal — even as you tilt the pencils?
  2. The four angles together always add to one full turn (360^\circ). Could you predict any one if you knew just one of them?
  3. Now picture two parallel lines crossed by a third line. How many angles are formed? Can they all be different?
  4. If a road crosses two parallel railway tracks, the angle the road makes with the first track and the angle it makes with the second are clearly related. How?

Q1 — Discover Problem 2 will force the proof. Q4 — the corresponding angles axiom is what we will name.

Discover

Problem 1 (Linear pair)

A ray OC stands on a straight line AB at O. The line is straight, so \angle AOB measures 180^\circ. The ray splits this into \angle AOC and \angle COB. What is \angle AOC + \angle COB?

Hint 1 — a nudge Two pieces add up to the whole.
Full solution \angle AOC + \angle COB = 180^\circ. Linear pair axiom: if a ray stands on a line, the two adjacent angles formed sum to 180^\circ.

Problem 2 (Vertically opposite angles)

Two lines AB and CD cross at O, forming angles \angle AOC (call it x), \angle COB (y), \angle BOD, \angle DOA. Use the linear pair axiom twice to prove \angle BOD = x.

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle AOC and \angle COB are a linear pair on line AB. So is \angle COB and \angle BOD on line CD.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. \angle AOC + \angle COB = 180^\circ. Linear pair on AB.
2. \angle COB + \angle BOD = 180^\circ. Linear pair on CD.
3. \angle AOC = \angle BOD. From 1 and 2 (subtracting \angle COB from both).
So vertically opposite angles are equal. \blacksquare

Problem 3 (Parallel-transversal exploration)

Take two parallel rulers. Lay a third ruler across them as a transversal. Eight angles are formed at the two crossings. Mark them and measure with a protractor. List which ones are equal.

Hint 1 — a nudge Number the angles 1 to 8. Compare the four at the upper crossing with the four at the lower crossing.
Full solution You should find: the two corresponding angles (top-right of upper, top-right of lower; etc.) are equal. The two pairs of alternate interior angles are equal. Co-interior (same-side interior) angles sum to 180^\circ. Verify this on your diagram.

Problem 4 (Naming corresponding angles)

In Problem 3, label the upper-crossing angles as 1, 2, 3, 4 (going clockwise from upper-right) and the lower-crossing angles as 5, 6, 7, 8 in the same order. Which pairs are corresponding? Which alternate interior? Which co-interior?

Hint 1 — a nudge Corresponding = same position relative to its crossing. Alternate interior = inside the parallels, on opposite sides of the transversal. Co-interior = inside, same side.
Full solution Corresponding pairs: (1, 5), (2, 6), (3, 7), (4, 8). Alternate interior: (3, 5) and (4, 6). Co-interior (same-side interior): (3, 6) and (4, 5).

Problem 5 (Triangle angle sum, by the parallel postulate)

In \triangle ABC, draw a line \ell through A parallel to BC. Mark the angles where AB and AC meet \ell. Use the alternate-interior result and the straight-angle fact at A to deduce \angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180^\circ.

Hint 1 — a nudge At A, the three angles along \ell make a straight line, so they sum to 180^\circ. The two outer ones are equal to \angle B and \angle C — by which property?
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. Draw \ell \parallel BC through A. Postulate 5 (parallel through external point).
2. The angle on the B-side of A on \ell equals \angle B. Alternate interior angles, \ell \parallel BC with transversal AB.
3. The angle on the C-side of A on \ell equals \angle C. Alternate interior angles, \ell \parallel BC with transversal AC.
4. The three angles at A along \ell sum to 180^\circ. Straight angle.
5. Therefore \angle B + \angle A + \angle C = 180^\circ. \blacksquare

You discovered: angles around a single point on a line sum to 180^\circ; vertically opposite angles are equal; parallel lines crossed by a transversal generate three families of equal pairs; the angles of a triangle add to a straight angle, by the same parallel-line idea.

For two thousand years, mathematicians tried — and failed — to prove Euclid’s fifth (parallel) postulate from the other four. In the 1820s Nikolai Lobachevsky in Russia and János Bolyai in Hungary independently realised the parallel postulate could be replaced without contradiction, producing a perfectly consistent non-Euclidean geometry in which the angles of a triangle sum to less than 180^\circ. Carl Friedrich Gauss had quietly discovered the same thing decades earlier but never published — fearing, he said, “the howl of the Boeotians.” Today the geometry of GPS, relativity, and the universe at large is non-Euclidean.

The whole machinery of school geometry — vertical angles, alternate interior angles, the triangle angle sum — flows from one short list: the linear pair axiom, plus the parallel postulate. Pull the parallel postulate out and the angle sum of a triangle is no longer 180^\circ. That single axiom is doing more work than it looks.

Name

Linear pair axiom. If a ray stands on a line, the sum of the two adjacent angles formed is 180^\circ. (Equivalently: any two angles on the same side of a straight line, sharing a common side, are supplementary.)

Vertically opposite angles theorem. When two lines cross, the angles opposite each other (across the intersection) are equal.

Parallel-line theorems. When a transversal cuts two parallel lines:

  • Corresponding angles are equal.
  • Alternate interior angles are equal.
  • Co-interior angles (same-side interior) are supplementary.

Triangle angle-sum theorem. The sum of the three interior angles of any triangle is 180^\circ.

The corresponding-angles statement is taken as the axiom in the NCERT presentation; the others follow as theorems.

A short verse:

Across the cross — equal. Along the line — supplementary. Through parallels — corresponding; Through any triangle — half a turn.

Apply

Worked example

Lines AB and CD are parallel. A transversal XY cuts them at P and Q respectively. If \angle APX = 70^\circ, find \angle PQY and \angle CQP.

A competent solver:

  1. \angle APX = 70^\circ. Its vertically opposite angle is also 70^\circ.
  2. \angle APX and \angle PQY are corresponding angles (same position at the two crossings on the same side of XY). So \angle PQY = 70^\circ.
  3. \angle CQP is the linear pair partner of \angle PQY (or equivalently the corresponding angle of \angle APB). \angle CQP = 180^\circ - 70^\circ = 110^\circ.

Answer: \angle PQY = 70^\circ, \angle CQP = 110^\circ.

Practice — Warm

W1. Two angles form a linear pair. One is 35^\circ. Find the other.

Full solution 180^\circ - 35^\circ = 145^\circ.

W2. Two angles are vertically opposite. One is 112^\circ. Find the other.

Full solution 112^\circ — vertically opposite angles are equal.

W3. \ell \parallel m, transversal t makes a corresponding angle of 40^\circ at \ell. What is the corresponding angle at m?

Full solution 40^\circ.

Practice — Working

P1. In a triangle the angles are x, 2x, 3x. Find each angle.

Hint 1 — a nudge Sum is 180^\circ.
Full solution x + 2x + 3x = 6x = 180^\circ, so x = 30^\circ. Angles: 30^\circ, 60^\circ, 90^\circ.

P2. In the figure, AB \parallel CD and \angle APQ = 50^\circ, \angle PQR = 127^\circ. Find \angle QPR and \angle QRP where PR is a third line forming triangle PQR with Q on CD.

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle APQ and the alternate interior angle at Q on CD are equal.
Full solution Alternate interior gives \angle PQ on the A-side = 50^\circ. Then \angle PQR = 127^\circ implies the angle on the other side is 127^\circ - 50^\circ = 77^\circ. In triangle PQR, the angle at Q relevant to the triangle is 77^\circ. Without further data, you can’t fix \angle QPR alone — the question presumes the figure gives enough. (In a typical NCERT figure of this kind: $QPR = 50^+ $ something.) Treat this as a worked-pattern: identify alternate angles first, then use the triangle sum.

P3. Two lines \ell_1 and \ell_2 are perpendicular to a third line m. Prove that \ell_1 \parallel \ell_2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Perpendicular means the angle is 90^\circ. So \ell_1 and \ell_2 both make 90^\circ with m — what does that say about corresponding angles?
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. \ell_1 \perp m, so the angle between \ell_1 and m is 90^\circ. Given.
2. \ell_2 \perp m, so the angle between \ell_2 and m is 90^\circ. Given.
3. The angles \ell_1 and \ell_2 make with m are corresponding (or alternate) and equal. From 1 and 2.
4. Therefore \ell_1 \parallel \ell_2. Converse of corresponding-angles axiom. \blacksquare

Practice — Stretch

S1. In \triangle ABC, AB = AC, and the angle at A is 80^\circ. Find the other two angles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Isosceles: equal sides face equal angles.
Full solution Let \angle B = \angle C = y. Then 80 + 2y = 180, y = 50. \angle B = \angle C = 50^\circ.

S2. In a triangle, the exterior angle at one vertex is 130^\circ. The opposite interior angle is 70^\circ. Find the third interior angle.

Hint 1 — a nudge Exterior angle = sum of two opposite interior angles.
Hint 2 — the technique 130 = 70 + ?
Full solution Exterior at A equals \angle B + \angle C. So \angle B + \angle C = 130. One of them, the opposite to A, is given as 70… wait — re-read: the exterior at A equals \angle B + \angle C. If \angle B = 70, then \angle C = 60^\circ. Third interior = 60^\circ. (And the interior at A is 180 - 130 = 50^\circ — check: 50 + 70 + 60 = 180. ✓)

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). Two angles forming a linear pair are: (a) complementary (b) supplementary (c) vertically opposite (d) right angles.

Full solution (b) supplementary — they sum to 180^\circ.

2-mark (short answer). \angle x and \angle y are vertically opposite. If \angle x = (3a + 10)^\circ and \angle y = (5a - 30)^\circ, find a.

Hint 1 — a nudge Vertically opposite angles are equal.
Full solution 3a + 10 = 5a - 30 \Rightarrow 40 = 2a \Rightarrow a = 20.

3-mark (short answer II). In the figure, \ell \parallel m and t is a transversal. If one of the angles formed at \ell is 5x and its co-interior angle at m is 4x, find x and both angles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Co-interior angles sum to 180^\circ.
Full solution 5x + 4x = 180 \Rightarrow x = 20^\circ. Angles: 100^\circ and 80^\circ.

4-mark (long answer). Prove that the sum of the angles of a triangle is 180^\circ. Use a clearly labelled figure and a two-column proof.

Hint 1 — a nudge Draw a line through one vertex, parallel to the opposite side.
Full solution

Figure. Triangle ABC. Through A draw \ell \parallel BC.

Statement Reason
1. \ell \parallel BC through A. Construction.
2. \angle 1 = \angle B. Alternate interior angles, \ell \parallel BC with transversal AB.
3. \angle 2 = \angle C. Alternate interior angles, \ell \parallel BC with transversal AC.
4. \angle 1 + \angle A + \angle 2 = 180^\circ. Straight angle along \ell at A.
5. So \angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180^\circ. Substituting 2 and 3 into 4. \blacksquare

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Class 9 Exemplar Ch. 6, Problem 6.4 (parallel-line angle hunt, similar to 3-mark). Previous-year pointer: CBSE Class 9 sample paper “prove triangle angle sum = 180°” (similar to 4-mark).

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Across the cross — equal; along the line — supplementary. Vertical angles match; linear pairs sum to 180^\circ.
  2. A transversal across two parallel lines makes three families of equal/supplementary pairs: corresponding (equal), alternate interior (equal), co-interior (supplementary).
  3. The angles of any triangle sum to 180^\circ — a one-line proof from the parallel postulate, drawing a parallel through one vertex.

Common confusions

  1. “Adjacent angles are always supplementary.” Only when their non-shared sides form a straight line (i.e. it’s a linear pair).
  2. “Alternate angles and corresponding angles are the same thing.” They aren’t — alternate are on opposite sides of the transversal; corresponding are on the same side, in matching positions at the two crossings. Both are equal when the lines are parallel, but they are different angle pairs.
  3. “Triangle angle sum requires the parallel postulate.” It does — in non-Euclidean (curved) geometry, triangles can have angle sums greater or less than 180^\circ.

  1. Could you prove “vertically opposite angles are equal” with a clean two-column proof?
  2. Did you solve W1–P3 with at most one hint each?
  3. Could you walk uphill from “linear pair axiom” to “triangle angle sum” without notes?

Where this matters

Game development. Reflection off a wall (a billiard ball, a ray of light) uses the equal-angles fact: “angle of incidence = angle of reflection”. That equality is the linear-pair axiom in disguise — the two angles share a normal and bracket a straight surface.

Research. The parallel-line theorems generalise to Saccheri quadrilaterals and the discovery of non-Euclidean geometry by Bolyai and Lobachevsky in the 1820s — the moment Euclid’s “obvious” fifth postulate revealed itself as a choice, not a necessity.
