Ch 9 — Circles

Chords, arcs, angles, and the cyclic quadrilateral

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you state the angle-sum theorem of a triangle? (If no, revisit cbse9.lines-and-angles.)
  2. Can you prove triangles congruent by SSS / SAS / ASA / RHS? (If no, revisit cbse9.triangles.)
  3. Do you know “radius”, “diameter”, “circumference”? (If no, revisit t1.shapes-angles.)

Three yes? Read on.

Touch

Take a circular plate. Place a long ruler across the plate so it crosses two points on the rim. Mark those points A and B — that’s a chord. Find the midpoint of AB, then drop a perpendicular from the centre O of the plate down to the chord. Where does it land?

viewof chordPos = Inputs.range([-80, 80], {value: 30, step: 5, label: "chord offset"})
{
  const cx = 150, cy = 130, R = 100;
  const yOff = chordPos;
  if (Math.abs(yOff) > R - 1) return html`<div>chord misses circle</div>`;
  const halfLen = Math.sqrt(R*R - yOff*yOff);
  const ax = cx - halfLen, ay = cy + yOff;
  const bx = cx + halfLen, by = cy + yOff;
  const mx = (ax+bx)/2, my = (ay+by)/2;
  return html`<svg width="300" height="280" style="border:1px solid #ccc">
    <circle cx="${cx}" cy="${cy}" r="${R}" fill="none" stroke="#333" stroke-width="1.5"/>
    <line x1="${ax}" y1="${ay}" x2="${bx}" y2="${by}" stroke="#36a" stroke-width="2"/>
    <line x1="${cx}" y1="${cy}" x2="${mx}" y2="${my}" stroke="#a30" stroke-dasharray="3,3"/>
    <circle cx="${cx}" cy="${cy}" r="3" fill="#000"/>
    <circle cx="${mx}" cy="${my}" r="3" fill="#a30"/>
    <text x="${cx + 6}" y="${cy - 6}" font-size="13">O</text>
    <text x="${mx + 6}" y="${my - 6}" font-size="13">M</text>
    <text x="${ax - 14}" y="${ay + 4}" font-size="13">A</text>
    <text x="${bx + 4}" y="${by + 4}" font-size="13">B</text>
  </svg>`;
}

Drag the slider. The dashed line from O always hits the chord at its midpoint M at 90^\circ. Move the chord — the perpendicular from centre never misses the midpoint.

Wonder

  1. The radius from O to a chord’s midpoint is always perpendicular to the chord. Why must that be? Could a circle ever cheat this?
  2. Cut two chords of equal length on the same circle. Are they equally far from the centre? What if you cut one short and one long?
  3. Pick two points A, B on a circle. Pick a third point P anywhere on the major arc between them. The angle \angle APB has a value. Slide P along the major arc — does the value change?
  4. A four-cornered figure with all four corners on the circle — what special relationship do its opposite angles share?
TipA whisper, no answer yet

Q3 is the angle in the same segment theorem (also called the inscribed-angle theorem in this special form). Q4 is cyclic quadrilateral — opposite angles sum to 180^\circ.

Discover

Problem 1 (Equal chords ⟺ equal distances)

In a circle with centre O, two chords AB and CD are drawn. M is the midpoint of AB and N is the midpoint of CD. Suppose AB = CD. Prove OM = ON.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use right-angled triangles \triangle OMB and \triangle OND, with the radius as hypotenuse.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. OM \perp AB and ON \perp CD. Radius to midpoint of chord is perpendicular.
2. OB = OD. Both are radii.
3. MB = \tfrac{1}{2} AB, ND = \tfrac{1}{2} CD, and AB = CD. Given + midpoint.
4. So MB = ND. From 3.
5. \triangle OMB \cong \triangle OND. RHS (right angle, hypotenuse, side).
6. OM = ON. CPCT. \blacksquare

Problem 2 (Angle subtended at centre vs at arc)

In a circle with centre O, an arc AB subtends \angle AOB at the centre. Pick any point P on the major arc. Use the radii OA, OB, OP and the isosceles triangles \triangle OPA, \triangle OPB to show \angle APB = \tfrac{1}{2} \angle AOB.

Hint 1 — a nudge Each radius makes an isosceles triangle with the chord through P. Exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two opposite interior angles.
Full solution

Join PO and extend it to meet the circle at Q (the diameter through P). Look at \triangle OPA: it is isosceles (OP = OA), so \angle OAP = \angle OPA. The exterior angle \angle AOQ at O equals \angle OAP + \angle OPA = 2 \angle OPA. Similarly \angle BOQ = 2 \angle OPB. Adding (or subtracting, depending on which side of PO the points lie), \angle AOB = 2(\angle OPA + \angle OPB) = 2 \angle APB. \blacksquare

The angle subtended by an arc at the centre is twice the angle it subtends at any point on the major arc.

Problem 3 (Same-segment angles equal)

Two points P_1 and P_2 lie on the major arc of AB. Use the result of Problem 2 to conclude \angle AP_1 B = \angle AP_2 B.

Full solution Both equal \tfrac{1}{2} \angle AOB (the central angle of AB is the same angle for the same arc). So they equal each other. Angles in the same segment are equal. \blacksquare

Problem 4 (Angle in a semicircle)

If AB is a diameter and P is any point on the circle (not A or B), what is \angle APB?

Hint 1 — a nudge The arc AB is now half the circle. The central angle is what?
Full solution \angle AOB = 180^\circ (since AB is a diameter and O is on it). By the central-vs-arc theorem, \angle APB = \tfrac{1}{2}(180^\circ) = 90^\circ. The angle in a semicircle is a right angle. (This is Thales’ theorem, the oldest named theorem in geometry, ~600 BCE.)

Thales of Miletus (~624 – ~546 BCE) is the first person in recorded history credited with proving a geometric statement rather than just observing it. The legend has him deducing the height of the Egyptian pyramids by comparing their shadow to a stick’s shadow at the same hour — a similar-triangles argument that exploits the very inscribed-angle ideas you are deriving here. Two and a half millennia later, his theorem still survives every geometry curriculum on Earth.

Problem 5 (Cyclic quadrilateral)

ABCD is a quadrilateral with all four vertices on a circle (a cyclic quadrilateral). Show \angle A + \angle C = 180^\circ.

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle A stands on the major arc of BCD; \angle C stands on the opposite arc.
Full solution \angle A is the inscribed angle on arc BCD, so it equals half the central angle on arc BCD. Similarly \angle C is half the central angle on arc BAD. The two arcs together make the whole circle, so the two central angles sum to 360^\circ, hence the two inscribed angles sum to \tfrac{1}{2}(360^\circ) = 180^\circ. Opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral are supplementary. \blacksquare

You discovered: chords pair up by length and distance from centre; central angle is twice the inscribed angle for the same arc; angles in the same segment are equal; the angle in a semicircle is right; and opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral sum to 180^\circ.

The whole chapter is one theorem in disguise: the central angle is twice the inscribed angle on the same arc. Equal chords ↔︎ equal distances, same-segment angles, the semicircle right angle, the cyclic quadrilateral — every result you proved is a special case or consequence of that single fact.

Name

In a circle with centre O:

  • Chord, radius, diameter, arc, sector, segment — standard vocabulary from the figure.
  • Theorem 1. The perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord. (Conversely, the line from the centre to the midpoint of a chord is perpendicular to it.)
  • Theorem 2. Equal chords of a circle are equidistant from the centre, and conversely.
  • Theorem 3. The angle subtended by an arc at the centre is twice the angle it subtends at any point on the major arc.
  • Theorem 4 (same segment). Angles in the same segment are equal.
  • Theorem 5 (Thales). The angle in a semicircle is a right angle.
  • Theorem 6 (cyclic quadrilateral). The sum of either pair of opposite angles of a cyclic quadrilateral is 180^\circ.

A short verse:

Drop from the centre — perpendicular and midpoint, both at once. Equal chords, equal distances; equal arcs, equal angles. Centre angle is double the rim; On a diameter, every angle is right.

Apply

Worked example

AB is a chord of a circle of radius 5 cm. The chord is at a perpendicular distance of 3 cm from the centre. Find the length of AB.

A competent solver:

  1. Drop the perpendicular from O to AB, meeting at M. By Theorem 1, M is the midpoint of AB, and OM = 3, OA = 5.
  2. Triangle OMA is right-angled at M. By Pythagoras, AM^2 + OM^2 = OA^2 \Rightarrow AM^2 = 25 - 9 = 16 \Rightarrow AM = 4.
  3. AB = 2 \cdot AM = 8 cm.

Answer: AB = 8 cm.

Practice — Warm

W1. A chord of length 24 cm is at a perpendicular distance of 5 cm from the centre. Find the radius.

Hint 1 — a nudge Same setup as the worked example, going the other way.
Full solution Half-chord = 12. Radius = \sqrt{12^2 + 5^2} = 13 cm.

W2. \angle AOB = 80^\circ at the centre of a circle. What is the inscribed angle \angle APB at any point P on the major arc?

Full solution \tfrac{1}{2}(80) = 40^\circ.

W3. ABCD is cyclic with \angle A = 92^\circ. Find \angle C.

Full solution \angle C = 180 - 92 = 88^\circ.

Practice — Working

P1. Two chords AB and CD of a circle are equal in length. Show that the arcs AB and CD are equal.

Hint 1 — a nudge Equal chords subtend equal angles at the centre. Equal central angles mean equal arcs.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. OA = OC and OB = OD. All radii.
2. AB = CD. Given.
3. \triangle OAB \cong \triangle OCD. SSS.
4. \angle AOB = \angle COD. CPCT.
5. So arc AB = arc CD. Equal central angles cut equal arcs. \blacksquare

P2. ABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral. The bisectors of \angle A and \angle C meet at point X. Show X lies on the line BD (or state the relationship between the bisectors).

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle A + \angle C = 180^\circ, so half each sums to 90^\circ.
Full solution Half-angles sum to 90^\circ, so the two bisectors are perpendicular at their intersection. The full proof of “X lies on BD” requires additional configuration — the cleaner statement is that the bisectors are perpendicular. Treat this as practice in extracting facts.

P3. A diameter of a circle is 26 cm. A chord of the circle is 24 cm long. Find its perpendicular distance from the centre.

Full solution Radius = 13. Half-chord = 12. Distance from centre = \sqrt{13^2 - 12^2} = \sqrt{25} = 5 cm.

Practice — Stretch

S1. AB is a diameter and CD is any other chord of a circle. The perpendicular from A to CD has foot P; the perpendicular from B to CD has foot Q. Prove CP = QD.

Hint 1 — a nudge Drop a perpendicular from the centre O to CD — call the foot M. M is the midpoint of CD, and OM is the average of AP and BQ (parallel lines, like rungs of a ladder).
Full solution AP, OM, BQ are all perpendicular to CD, so they are parallel. O is the midpoint of AB (centre on diameter), so OM is the average of AP and BQ — meaning M is the midpoint of PQ. But M is also the midpoint of CD (perpendicular from centre). So CP = CM - PM = MD - MQ = QD. \blacksquare

S2. A circle has two parallel chords of lengths 6 cm and 8 cm. The chords are on opposite sides of the centre, 7 cm apart. Find the radius of the circle.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let the perpendicular distances from the centre be d_1 and d_2 (d_1 + d_2 = 7). Use Pythagoras to relate each chord to the radius.
Full solution Let radius be r. Then r^2 = d_1^2 + 3^2 = d_1^2 + 9 and r^2 = d_2^2 + 4^2 = d_2^2 + 16. So d_2^2 = d_1^2 - 7. Together with d_1 + d_2 = 7: substitute d_2 = 7 - d_1, get (7 - d_1)^2 = d_1^2 - 7 \Rightarrow 49 - 14 d_1 = -7 \Rightarrow d_1 = 4, so d_2 = 3. r = \sqrt{16 + 9} = 5 cm.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). The angle in a semicircle is: (a) 45^\circ (b) 60^\circ (c) 90^\circ (d) 180^\circ.

Full solution (c) 90^\circ — Thales’ theorem.

2-mark (short answer). A chord of a circle of radius 10 cm subtends a right angle at the centre. Find the length of the chord.

Hint 1 — a nudge The chord and two radii form a right triangle with the right angle at the centre.
Full solution Length = \sqrt{10^2 + 10^2} = 10\sqrt{2} cm.

3-mark (short answer II). ABCD is a cyclic quadrilateral with \angle A = 100^\circ and \angle B = 70^\circ. Find \angle C and \angle D.

Full solution Opposite angles supplementary: \angle C = 180 - 100 = 80^\circ, \angle D = 180 - 70 = 110^\circ. Check: 100 + 70 + 80 + 110 = 360 ✓.

5-mark (long answer). Prove that the perpendicular from the centre of a circle to a chord bisects the chord.

Hint 1 — a nudge Right triangles \triangle OMA and \triangle OMB, with hypotenuse = radius.
Full solution
Statement Reason
1. OM \perp AB, M on AB. Given.
2. OA = OB. Radii.
3. OM = OM. Common.
4. \angle OMA = \angle OMB = 90^\circ. Given.
5. \triangle OMA \cong \triangle OMB. RHS.
6. AM = MB. CPCT. So M is the midpoint of AB. \blacksquare

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Class 9 Exemplar Ch. 9, Problem 9.3 (chord distance, similar to W1). Previous-year pointer: CBSE Class 9 sample paper “prove perpendicular from centre bisects chord” (similar to 5-mark).

Common confusions

  1. “The angle subtended at the centre equals the angle subtended at the rim.” No — centre angle is twice the rim angle for the same arc.
  2. “Every quadrilateral inscribed in a circle has all four angles equal.” No — only opposite angles are supplementary. They need not be equal individually.
  3. “A chord is the same as a diameter.” A diameter is the longest chord; not every chord is a diameter.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. The perpendicular from the centre to a chord cuts it in half. That single move converts almost every chord problem into a right triangle and Pythagoras.
  2. Centre angle is double the rim angle on the same arc. Memorise this one sentence and most circle theorems fall out of it.
  3. A cyclic quadrilateral pairs its opposite angles to 180^\circ. That is why the rim of a circle keeps the four corners “balanced”.

Self-Assessment

Be honest with yourself.

  1. Could you prove “perpendicular from centre bisects chord” without notes?
  2. Did you solve W1–P3 with at most one hint each?
  3. Could you state the inscribed-angle theorem and its consequences (same segment, semicircle, cyclic quadrilateral) cold?
  • 3 yes: move on to Heron’s Formula.
  • 2 yes: redo S1 and S2.
  • 0–1 yes: re-read Discover 2–5. The inscribed-angle theorem is the engine of this chapter.

Where this matters

Game development. Camera-frustum culling — deciding which objects are visible — uses the angle subtended by an object at the camera. The inscribed-angle theorem is the formal version of “things on the same horizon look the same width regardless of where you are on the horizon”.

Research. Thales’ theorem (Discover Problem 4) is the oldest recorded named theorem, attributed to Thales of Miletus around 600 BCE. It is also the key step in deriving the equation of a circle from x^2 + y^2 = r^2 — the first place algebra and geometry truly fuse.
