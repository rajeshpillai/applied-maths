Ch 9 — Some Applications of Trigonometry (Heights & Distances)

Stand on the ground, look up, and trigonometry tells you the height

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state \sin\theta, \cos\theta, \tan\theta for a right triangle? See cbse10.trigonometry-intro.
  2. Do you remember the special-angle values at 30°, 45°, 60°?
  3. Can you draw a right triangle from a verbal description (“a tower of height h casts a shadow of length s”)?
  4. Can you simplify \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} to \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}?

Three yeses, you are ready.

Touch

Stand outside on a sunny day. Stand a metre stick vertically. Measure the length of its shadow. Note the time and the date.

The triangle formed by the metre stick, the shadow, and the line from the top of the stick to the tip of the shadow — is a right triangle. The angle between the ground and the line to the sun is the angle of elevation of the sun.

viewof H = Inputs.range([2, 50], {value: 10, step: 1, label: "Object height H (m)"})
viewof angle = Inputs.range([10, 80], {value: 45, step: 5, label: "Angle of elevation θ"})
{
  const w = 460, h = 220, padX = 30, padY = 20;
  const rad = angle * Math.PI / 180;
  const D = H / Math.tan(rad);  // ground distance
  // scale to fit
  const maxX = Math.max(D, 30);
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([0, maxX]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([0, 60]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");
  // ground
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", padX).attr("x2", w - padX)
    .attr("y1", yS(0)).attr("y2", yS(0)).attr("stroke", "#888");
  // pole
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(D)).attr("x2", xS(D))
    .attr("y1", yS(0)).attr("y2", yS(H)).attr("stroke", "#26a")
    .attr("stroke-width", 3);
  // line of sight
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(0)).attr("y1", yS(0))
    .attr("x2", xS(D)).attr("y2", yS(H)).attr("stroke", "#c33")
    .attr("stroke-dasharray", "4,3");
  // labels
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY + 14).attr("font-size", 13)
    .text(`H = ${H} m,  θ = ${angle}°,  ground distance D = H/tan θ = ${D.toFixed(2)} m`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX + 10).attr("y", yS(0) - 4).attr("font-size", 11).text("θ");
  return svg.node();
}

For each combination of height and angle, the ground distance changes deterministically. One measurement, one angle, full geometry.

Wonder

  1. If the angle of elevation is 45°, what is the relationship between the height and the ground distance?
  2. As the angle of elevation grows from 30° to 60°, does the ground distance get bigger or smaller (with the same height)? Why?
  3. What is the angle of depression, and how is it related to angle of elevation?
  4. If you measured a shadow but did not know the angle of the sun, could you still find the height of an object by comparing it to a stick of known height nearby?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Equal — \tan 45° = 1. (2) Smaller — \tan grows with the angle so D = H/\tan\theta shrinks. (3) Equal — they are alternate angles when sight is from the higher point. (4) Yes — that is the similar-triangle shortcut from Ch 6 (P2 there).

Discover

Problem 1 — A tower

The angle of elevation of the top of a tower from a point on the ground 30 m away from the foot is 30°. Find the height of the tower.

Hint 1 — a nudge \tan 30° = H / 30.
Full solution H = 30 \tan 30° = 30 \cdot \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = 10\sqrt{3} \approx 17.32 m.

Problem 2 — A taller tower

A tower stands vertically. From a point 20 m from its foot, the angle of elevation of the top is 60°. Find the height.

Full solution H = 20 \tan 60° = 20 \sqrt{3} \approx 34.64 m.

Problem 3 — Shadows at two times

A vertical pole has shadow lengths L_1 at angle \theta_1 and L_2 at angle \theta_2. If \theta_1 = 60° and \theta_2 = 30°, what is the ratio L_2 : L_1?

Hint 1 — a nudge L = H / \tan\theta = H \cot\theta.
Full solution L_1 = H \cot 60° = H/\sqrt{3}. L_2 = H \cot 30° = H\sqrt{3}. Ratio L_2 : L_1 = \sqrt{3} : 1/\sqrt{3} = 3 : 1.

Problem 4 — Two angles, one tower

From two points on the ground, on the same side and in line with the foot of a tower, the angles of elevation of the top are 30° and 60°. The two points are 40 m apart. The closer point has elevation 60°. Find the height of the tower.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let height = H, distance from closer point to base = x. Then \tan 60° = H/x and \tan 30° = H/(x + 40).
Hint 2 — the technique x = H/\sqrt{3} from the first. Substitute into the second.
Full solution \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{H}{H/\sqrt{3} + 40} \Rightarrow H/\sqrt{3} + 40 = H\sqrt{3} \Rightarrow 40 = H(\sqrt{3} - 1/\sqrt{3}) = H \cdot \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \Rightarrow H = 20\sqrt{3} \approx 34.64 m.

Problem 5 — Angle of depression

From the top of a 50 m lighthouse, the angle of depression of a boat in the sea is 30°. How far is the boat from the foot of the lighthouse?

Hint 1 — a nudge “Angle of depression” from the top equals “angle of elevation” from the boat (alternate interior angles, with horizontal at top parallel to ground).
Full solution \tan 30° = 50 / D \Rightarrow D = 50 / \tan 30° = 50\sqrt{3} \approx 86.60 m.

Problem 6 — Two ships

A man on a cliff of height 100 m sees two boats in the sea, on the same side. Their angles of depression are 30° and 45°. Find the distance between the two boats.

Hint 1 — a nudge D_1 at 45°: \tan 45° = 100/D_1 \Rightarrow D_1 = 100. D_2 at 30°: \tan 30° = 100/D_2 \Rightarrow D_2 = 100\sqrt{3}.
Full solution Distance = D_2 - D_1 = 100\sqrt{3} - 100 = 100(\sqrt{3} - 1) \approx 73.21 m.

Eratosthenes measures the Earth (~240 BCE). A Greek librarian at Alexandria heard that at noon on the summer solstice in Syene (modern Aswan), the sun was directly overhead — a vertical stick cast no shadow. On the same day in Alexandria, \sim 800 km north, a vertical stick cast a shadow at an angle of about 7.2° — a 50th of a full circle. The sun’s rays are effectively parallel, so 7.2° must be the arc on Earth’s surface between the two cities. Therefore Earth’s circumference is roughly 50 \times 800 \approx 40{,}000 km — within 2\% of the modern value. One stick, one angle, one planet measured.

The recipe is always the same: sketch first, identify the right triangle, write \tan\theta = \text{vertical} / \text{horizontal}, then substitute special-angle values. Two angles in the diagram means two equations; two unknowns become solvable. The mathematics is trivial; the mistake is almost always the sketch.

Name

Angle of elevation. The angle between the horizontal ground and your line of sight up to a higher object.

Angle of depression. The angle between the horizontal at your level and your line of sight down to a lower object.

Solving height-and-distance problems is a four-step recipe:

  1. Sketch the situation as a right triangle (or two).
  2. Identify which side is the height, which is the ground distance, and which angles are known.
  3. Use the appropriate trig ratio: \tan\theta = \dfrac{\text{vertical}} {\text{horizontal}} is the most common.
  4. Substitute special-angle values from the table; simplify.

A key fact: angle of depression from a high point equals angle of elevation from the corresponding low point (alternate interior angles between the two horizontals).

A verse:

Look up — that’s elevation. Look down — depression’s the name. Tan of theta is height over ground; the rest is the same game. Sketch first, label twice, then trigonometry takes the lead; Substitute special angles — and the answer is your reward indeed.

Apply

Worked example

A boy is standing on the ground and flying a kite with a string of length 100 m at an angle of elevation of 60°. Another boy is standing on the roof of a 10 m high building, and is flying his kite at an elevation of 45°. Both boys are on the same side of their kites. If the kites coincide (are at the same point in the sky), find the length of the second boy’s string.

For the first boy: kite height above the ground = 100 \sin 60° = 50\sqrt{3} m. Horizontal distance from boy to point below kite = 100 \cos 60° = 50 m.

The kite is at the same point for both. The second boy is 10 m up.

Vertical from second boy to kite: 50\sqrt{3} - 10 m. At elevation 45°, vertical = horizontal, so the second boy’s string makes a 45°-45°-90° triangle with vertical leg 50\sqrt{3} - 10.

String length = (50\sqrt{3} - 10) \cdot \sqrt{2} = 10\sqrt{2}(5\sqrt{3} - 1) m.

Numerically: \approx 10 \cdot 1.414 \cdot (8.660 - 1) = 14.14 \cdot 7.66 \approx 108.3 m.

Practice — Warm

W1. A pole 6 m high casts a shadow 2\sqrt{3} m long. Find the angle of elevation of the sun.

Full solution \tan\theta = 6/(2\sqrt{3}) = \sqrt{3}, so \theta = 60°.

W2. From the top of a building, the angle of depression of a car on the ground is 30°. The car is 30\sqrt{3} m from the foot. Find the building’s height.

Full solution \tan 30° = H / (30\sqrt{3}) \Rightarrow H = 30\sqrt{3}/\sqrt{3} = 30 m.

W3. A ladder 10 m long leans against a wall, making an angle of 60° with the ground. How high up the wall does it reach?

Full solution \sin 60° = H/10 \Rightarrow H = 5\sqrt{3} \approx 8.66 m.

Practice — Working

P1. From the top of a 50 m high tower, the angles of depression of two cars on the ground (on the same side and in line) are 45° and 60°. Find the distance between the two cars.

Hint 1 — a nudge D_1 at depression 60°: closer car. D_2 at 45°: farther.
Full solution D_1 = 50 / \tan 60° = 50/\sqrt{3}. D_2 = 50 / \tan 45° = 50. Distance between = D_2 - D_1 = 50 - 50/\sqrt{3} = 50(1 - 1/\sqrt{3}) = 50 \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3} - 1}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{50(\sqrt{3} - 1)}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{50\sqrt{3}(\sqrt{3} - 1)}{3} = \dfrac{50(3 - \sqrt{3})}{3} \approx 21.13 m.

P2. A vertical tower stands on a horizontal plane and is surmounted by a vertical flagstaff of height h. At a point on the plane, the angles of elevation of the bottom and top of the flagstaff are \alpha and \beta. Show that the height of the tower is \dfrac{h \tan\alpha}{\tan\beta - \tan\alpha}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let tower height = H, distance from observer = x. Then \tan\alpha = H/x and \tan\beta = (H + h)/x.
Full solution x = H/\tan\alpha. Substitute: \tan\beta = (H + h) \tan\alpha / H. H \tan\beta = H \tan\alpha + h \tan\alpha. H(\tan\beta - \tan\alpha) = h \tan\alpha. H = \dfrac{h \tan\alpha}{\tan\beta - \tan\alpha}. \square

P3. The angle of elevation of a cloud from a point 60 m above the surface of a lake is 30°, and the angle of depression of its reflection in the lake is 60°. Find the height of the cloud above the lake.

Hint 1 — a nudge Reflection is at depth equal to the cloud’s height below the lake.
Full solution Let cloud height above lake = h, horizontal distance = x. Observer is 60 m above lake. Vertical distance from observer to cloud = h - 60; to reflection = h + 60. \tan 30° = (h - 60)/x \Rightarrow x = (h - 60)\sqrt{3}. \tan 60° = (h + 60)/x \Rightarrow x = (h + 60)/\sqrt{3}. Equate: (h - 60)\sqrt{3} = (h + 60)/\sqrt{3} \Rightarrow 3(h - 60) = h + 60 \Rightarrow 2h = 240 \Rightarrow h = 120 m.

Practice — Stretch

S1. The angle of elevation of the top of a tower from a point on the ground is 30°. On walking 40 m towards the tower, the angle becomes 60°. Find the height of the tower.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two unknowns (height, original distance), two equations.
Full solution Let H = height, x = distance after walking 40 m. \tan 60° = H/x \Rightarrow x = H/\sqrt{3}. \tan 30° = H/(x + 40) \Rightarrow x + 40 = H\sqrt{3}. H/\sqrt{3} + 40 = H\sqrt{3} \Rightarrow 40 = H\sqrt{3} - H/\sqrt{3} = H(2/\sqrt{3}). H = 20\sqrt{3} \approx 34.64 m.

S2. A bird sits on the top of a tree 80 m high. From a point on the ground, the angle of elevation of the bird is 45°. The bird flies away horizontally and after 2 seconds, the angle becomes 30°. Find the speed of the bird in m/s.

Hint 1 — a nudge At elevation 45°: ground distance = 80 m. At elevation 30°: ground distance = 80\sqrt{3} m.
Full solution Distance flown = 80\sqrt{3} - 80 = 80(\sqrt{3} - 1) \approx 58.56 m in 2 seconds. Speed \approx 29.28 m/s. Exact: 40(\sqrt{3} - 1) m/s.

Board-Pattern Practice

Q1 (1 mark — MCQ). The angle of elevation of the top of a tower from a point on the ground at a distance 30 m is 60°. The height of the tower is: (a) 30 m (b) 30\sqrt{3} m (c) \dfrac{30}{\sqrt{3}} m (d) 60 m.

Full solution H = 30 \tan 60° = 30\sqrt{3}. (b).

Q2 (1 mark — fill-in). If the shadow of a vertical pole is \sqrt{3} times its height, the angle of elevation of the sun is ____.

Full solution \tan\theta = H / (H\sqrt{3}) = 1/\sqrt{3} \Rightarrow \theta = 30°.

Q3 (2 marks). From a point on the ground, the angles of elevation of the bottom and the top of a transmission tower fixed at the top of a 20 m high building are 45° and 60° respectively. Find the height of the tower.

Hint 1 — a nudge Distance from observer to base = 20 / \tan 45° = 20 m. Then total height to top of tower = 20 \tan 60° = 20\sqrt{3}.
Full solution Tower height = 20\sqrt{3} - 20 = 20(\sqrt{3} - 1) \approx 14.64 m.

Q4 (2 marks). The angle of elevation of the top of a hill from the foot of a tower is 60° and the angle of elevation of the top of the tower from the foot of the hill is 30°. If the tower is 50 m high, find the height of the hill.

Hint 1 — a nudge Distance between foot of tower and foot of hill = D. From foot of hill: \tan 30° = 50/D \Rightarrow D = 50\sqrt{3}. From foot of tower: \tan 60° = H/D \Rightarrow H = D\sqrt{3}.
Full solution D = 50\sqrt{3}. H = 50\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{3} = 150 m. Hill: 150 m.

Q5 (3 marks). As observed from the top of a 75 m lighthouse, the angles of depression of two ships are 30° and 45°. If one ship is exactly behind the other (on the same side of the lighthouse), find the distance between them.

Full solution D_1 at 45°: D_1 = 75. D_2 at 30°: D_2 = 75\sqrt{3}. Distance = D_2 - D_1 = 75(\sqrt{3} - 1) \approx 54.9 m.

Q6 (3 marks). A man on the deck of a ship 16 m above sea level observes that the angle of elevation of the top of a cliff is 45°. The angle of depression of the base of the cliff is 30°. Find the height of the cliff and the distance from the ship.

Hint 1 — a nudge Distance to base of cliff = 16 / \tan 30° = 16\sqrt{3}. Cliff height above deck = 16\sqrt{3} \cdot \tan 45° = 16\sqrt{3}. Total cliff height = deck height + above-deck = 16 + 16\sqrt{3}.
Full solution Distance from ship: 16\sqrt{3} \approx 27.71 m. Cliff height: 16(1 + \sqrt{3}) \approx 43.71 m.

Q7 (5 marks — long answer). A person standing on the bank of a river observes that the angle of elevation of the top of a tree on the opposite bank is 60°. When he moves 40 m away from the bank, he finds the angle of elevation to be 30°. Find: (a) the height of the tree, and (b) the width of the river.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let tree height = H, river width = W. Two equations.
Full solution

At first position (on bank): \tan 60° = H/W \Rightarrow H = W\sqrt{3}. After walking 40 m back: \tan 30° = H/(W + 40) \Rightarrow H = (W + 40)/ \sqrt{3}. Equate: W\sqrt{3} = (W + 40)/\sqrt{3} \Rightarrow 3W = W + 40 \Rightarrow 2W = 40 \Rightarrow W = 20 m. H = 20\sqrt{3} \approx 34.64 m.

(a) Height of tree: 20\sqrt{3} m. (b) Width of river: 20 m.

Common confusions

  1. “Angle of depression is measured from vertical.” No — it is measured from the horizontal at the higher point, looking down. So it equals (alternate-interior) the angle of elevation from the lower point.
  2. “\tan 30° = \sqrt{3}.” No: \tan 30° = 1/\sqrt{3}. \tan 60° = \sqrt{3}. Memorise the table; one swap-mistake costs ₹2-5.
  3. “All ground distances are positive, so I can drop the absolute values.” True for the magnitude. But signed distance matters when you compute “distance between two points on the same side” vs “on opposite sides” — sketch carefully before solving.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Sketch the triangle first. Every wrong answer in this chapter begins with a sketch error, not an arithmetic one.
  2. Angle of depression equals angle of elevation across the horizontal. This single fact converts every “looking down” problem into a “looking up” problem.
  3. Two angles, two equations, two unknowns. When the problem gives two viewpoints, the height-and-distance algebra is forced.

Where this matters

Game development. Aiming a projectile in a 3D game uses the same right-triangle reasoning. The “lead a target” computation in first-person shooters: predict where the target will be, then compute the angle of elevation to send the projectile there.

Research. Surveying — measuring real-world land — uses these exact techniques. The Great Trigonometric Survey of India (1802–1871) measured the height of Mount Everest, then named “Peak XV”, using height-and-distance problems exactly like Q4 and Q7, scaled to hundreds of kilometres.
