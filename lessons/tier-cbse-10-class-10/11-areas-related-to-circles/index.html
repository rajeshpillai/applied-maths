Ch 11 — Areas Related to Circles

Slice the pizza, count the crumbs — sectors, segments, and the slim crescents

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state the area and circumference of a circle of radius r? See t1.area-perimeter-volume.
  2. Can you compute \dfrac{60}{360} as a fraction (in lowest terms)?
  3. Do you know that \pi \approx 22/7 \approx 3.14159?
  4. Can you compute the area of a triangle with two sides and included angle (or just base × height ÷ 2)?

Three yeses, you are ready.

Touch

A pizza, cut from the centre. One slice. The two straight edges of the slice are radii of the circle; the curved edge is an arc; the angle between the two radii is the sector angle.

viewof rad = Inputs.range([1, 10], {value: 5, step: 0.5, label: "Radius r (cm)"})
viewof secAngle = Inputs.range([10, 360], {value: 60, step: 10, label: "Sector angle θ (°)"})
{
  const w = 320, h = 320, cx = w / 2, cy = h / 2;
  const r = rad * 25;
  const θ = secAngle * Math.PI / 180;
  const x1 = cx + r * Math.cos(0), y1 = cy + r * Math.sin(0);
  const x2 = cx + r * Math.cos(θ), y2 = cy + r * Math.sin(θ);
  const largeArc = θ > Math.PI ? 1 : 0;
  const path = `M ${cx},${cy} L ${x1},${y1} A ${r},${r} 0 ${largeArc},1 ${x2},${y2} Z`;
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");
  // full circle outline
  svg.append("circle").attr("cx", cx).attr("cy", cy).attr("r", r)
    .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#bbb");
  // sector
  svg.append("path").attr("d", path).attr("fill", "rgba(38,102,170,0.25)")
    .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 2);
  // text
  const area = Math.PI * rad * rad * (secAngle / 360);
  const arcLen = 2 * Math.PI * rad * (secAngle / 360);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", 10).attr("y", 16).attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`Sector area = (θ/360°) · πr² = ${area.toFixed(2)} cm²`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", 10).attr("y", 32).attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`Arc length  = (θ/360°) · 2πr = ${arcLen.toFixed(2)} cm`);
  return svg.node();
}

A sector is a fraction of the whole circle. The fraction is \theta/360°. Drag — when \theta = 360°, you get the whole circle.

Wonder

  1. The sector occupies \theta/360° of the full circle. What does that say about its area, compared to \pi r^2? Its arc length, compared to 2\pi r?
  2. If you cut a circle into a sector and the rest, the rest has a name. What might it be?
  3. A segment is the region between a chord and the arc on one side. How is its area related to the sector area?
  4. Suppose a square has a circle inscribed in it. The four corners of the square outside the circle — what would you call those, and how do you find their area?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Both scale linearly with \theta/360°. (2) The “minor sector” plus “major sector” make the whole. (3) Segment = sector - triangle (the triangle being formed by the two radii and the chord). (4) “Corners” = square - inscribed circle.

Discover

Problem 1 — Area and circumference of a sector

A circle has radius 7 cm. A sector with angle 60° is cut. Find its area and arc length. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Sector area = \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot \pi r^2. Arc = \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot 2\pi r.
Full solution Area = \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 49 = \dfrac{77}{3} \approx 25.67 \text{ cm}^2. Arc = \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot 2 \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 7 = \dfrac{22}{3} \approx 7.33 cm.

Problem 2 — Segment area

The same circle, the same 60° sector. Find the area of the segment (the part between the chord and the arc).

Hint 1 — a nudge Segment area = sector area - triangle area. The triangle has two sides of 7 and included angle 60°.
Full solution Triangle area = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 7 \cdot 7 \cdot \sin 60° = \frac{49 \sqrt{3}}{4} \approx 21.22 \text{ cm}^2. Segment area \approx 25.67 - 21.22 = 4.45 \text{ cm}^2.

Problem 3 — General formula for sector area

Derive a formula for the area of a sector of radius r and angle \theta (in degrees).

Full solution \text{Sector area} = \dfrac{\theta}{360°} \cdot \pi r^2.

Problem 4 — A square minus a quadrant

A square of side 14 cm has a quarter-circle (quadrant) of radius 14 cm cut out from one corner. Find the remaining area. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Square area = 14^2. Quadrant area = \frac{1}{4} \pi r^2.
Full solution 196 - \dfrac{1}{4} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 196 = 196 - 154 = 42 \text{ cm}^2.

Problem 5 — A combination

Inside a square of side 14 cm, four quadrants of radius 7 cm are drawn at the four corners (each centred at a corner). Find the area of the remaining region. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Four quadrants of radius 7 together have the area of one full circle of radius 7.
Full solution Square = 196. Four quadrants = \pi \cdot 49 = \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 49 = 154. Remaining = 42 \text{ cm}^2.

Problem 6 — Two semicircles

On the diameter of a circle of radius r, a smaller circle is drawn whose diameter is the radius of the big circle. (So the small circle has radius r/2.) Find the area inside the big circle but outside the small one.

Full solution \pi r^2 - \pi (r/2)^2 = \pi r^2 (1 - 1/4) = \dfrac{3\pi r^2}{4}.

Archimedes computes \pi by exhaustion (~250 BCE). In Measurement of a Circle, Archimedes inscribed and circumscribed regular polygons of 96 sides around a circle, computed their perimeters, and proved that \dfrac{223}{71} \;<\; \pi \;<\; \dfrac{22}{7}. The lower bound is 3.1408\ldots, the upper bound is 3.1428\ldots, and \pi = 3.14159\ldots — a 2,300-year-old sandwich that is still respectable. The board-paper convention \pi = 22/7 that you use in every problem of this chapter is exactly Archimedes’ upper bound.

Every formula in this chapter is one fact: a sector is the fraction \theta/360° of the whole circle, and both its area and its arc length scale by that fraction. From that one idea you get sector area, arc length, segment area (sector - triangle), and the annulus formula (big circle - small circle). The bookkeeping is mechanical once you see the fraction.

Name

For a circle of radius r:

  • Circumference: C = 2\pi r.
  • Area: A = \pi r^2.

For a sector subtending angle \theta (in degrees) at the centre:

  • Arc length: \ell = \dfrac{\theta}{360°} \cdot 2\pi r.
  • Sector area: A_{\text{sec}} = \dfrac{\theta}{360°} \cdot \pi r^2.

A segment is the region between a chord and one of the arcs it cuts off.

  • Minor segment area = (sector area with same \theta) - (triangle area with the two radii and included angle \theta).
  • The triangle area with two sides r and included angle \theta is \frac{1}{2} r^2 \sin\theta.

A circular ring (annulus) between radii R (outer) and r (inner) has area \pi(R^2 - r^2).

A verse:

Slice of pi-r-squared by theta over three-sixty’s call — That’s a sector. Arc? Same fraction of two-pi-r, that’s all. Segment? Sector minus the triangle on the chord — Ring? Big-r squared minus small-r squared, by pi underscored.

Apply

Worked example

A circular flower bed has radius 4 m. A path of width 1 m goes around it on the outside. Find the area of the path. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Outer radius = 4 + 1 = 5 m. Inner = 4 m.

Path area = annulus = \pi(5^2 - 4^2) = \pi \cdot 9 = \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 9 = \dfrac{198}{7} \approx 28.29 \text{ m}^2.

Practice — Warm

W1. A sector of a circle has area 54 \text{ cm}^2 and radius 9 cm. Find the angle of the sector.

Full solution 54 = \dfrac{\theta}{360} \pi \cdot 81 \Rightarrow \theta = \dfrac{54 \cdot 360}{81 \pi} = \dfrac{240}{\pi} \approx 76.39°. With \pi = 22/7: \theta = \dfrac{240 \cdot 7}{22} = \dfrac{1680}{22} \approx 76.36°.

W2. Find the perimeter of a sector with radius 7 cm and angle 90°.

Hint 1 — a nudge Perimeter = 2 radii + arc.
Full solution 2 \cdot 7 + \dfrac{90}{360} \cdot 2 \pi \cdot 7 = 14 + \dfrac{1}{4} \cdot 14\pi = 14 + \dfrac{7\pi}{2} = 14 + 11 = 25 cm (with \pi = 22/7).

W3. A circle of radius 14 cm. Find the length of an arc subtending 72° at the centre.

Full solution \dfrac{72}{360} \cdot 2 \pi \cdot 14 = \dfrac{1}{5} \cdot 2 \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 14 = \dfrac{1}{5} \cdot 88 = 17.6 cm.

Practice — Working

P1. Find the area of a segment of a circle of radius 14 cm with sector angle 90°. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Sector area - triangle area. Triangle is right-angled with legs 14, 14.
Full solution Sector = \dfrac{1}{4} \pi \cdot 196 = 154. Triangle = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 14 \cdot 14 = 98. Segment = \mathbf{56 \text{ cm}^2}.

P2. A horse is tied to a peg at one corner of a square grass field of side 15 m by means of a 5 m long rope. Find the area of that part of the field in which the horse can graze.

Hint 1 — a nudge The horse traces a quarter-circle (since the corner restricts grazing to a 90° wedge) of radius 5.
Full solution Area = \dfrac{1}{4} \pi r^2 = \dfrac{1}{4} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 25 = \dfrac{275}{14} \approx 19.64 \text{ m}^2.

P3. An umbrella has 8 ribs equally spaced. Assuming the umbrella to be a flat circle of radius 45 cm, find the area between two consecutive ribs.

Hint 1 — a nudge Each gap is a sector of angle 360°/8 = 45°.
Full solution \dfrac{45}{360} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 45^2 = \dfrac{1}{8} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 2025 = \dfrac{22275}{28} \approx 795.54 \text{ cm}^2.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A square has side 4 cm. Inside, a circle of largest possible size is drawn (inscribed). Outside the square, a circle of smallest possible size that contains the square (circumscribed) is drawn. Find the ratio of their areas.

Hint 1 — a nudge Inscribed radius = side/2 = 2. Circumscribed radius = diagonal/2 = 2 \sqrt{2}.
Full solution Inscribed area = 4\pi. Circumscribed area = \pi (2\sqrt{2})^2 = 8\pi. Ratio = 4\pi : 8\pi = 1 : 2.

S2. Two circles touch externally. The sum of their areas is 130 \pi \text{ cm}^2 and the distance between their centres is 14 cm. Find the radii.

Hint 1 — a nudge r_1 + r_2 = 14 and \pi(r_1^2 + r_2^2) = 130\pi.
Full solution r_1 + r_2 = 14, r_1^2 + r_2^2 = 130. So (r_1 + r_2)^2 = 196 = r_1^2 + r_2^2 + 2 r_1 r_2 = 130 + 2 r_1 r_2, giving r_1 r_2 = 33. r_1, r_2 are roots of t^2 - 14t + 33 = 0 → t = 11 or t = 3. Radii: 11 cm and 3 cm.

Board-Pattern Practice

Q1 (1 mark — MCQ). The area of a sector of a circle of radius 14 cm with angle 90° is: (a) 77 \text{ cm}^2 (b) 154 \text{ cm}^2 (c) 98 \text{ cm}^2 (d) 49 \text{ cm}^2.

Full solution \dfrac{90}{360} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 196 = \dfrac{1}{4} \cdot 22 \cdot 28 = 154. (b).

Q2 (1 mark — fill-in). The length of an arc of a circle of radius 21 cm subtending an angle of 60° at the centre is ____ cm.

Full solution \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot 2 \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 21 = \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot 132 = 22 cm.

Q3 (2 marks). Find the area of a quadrant of a circle whose circumference is 22 cm.

Hint 1 — a nudge Find r first.
Full solution 2\pi r = 22 \Rightarrow r = \dfrac{11}{\pi} = \dfrac{77}{22} = 3.5 cm. Quadrant area = \frac{1}{4}\pi r^2 = \dfrac{1}{4} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 12.25 = \dfrac{77}{8} = 9.625 \text{ cm}^2.

Q4 (2 marks). A chord of a circle of radius 10 cm subtends a right angle at the centre. Find the area of the minor segment.

Full solution Sector = \frac{1}{4} \pi \cdot 100 = 25 \pi. Triangle = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 10 \cdot 10 = 50. Segment = 25\pi - 50 \approx 25(3.14) - 50 = 78.5 - 50 = 28.5 \text{ cm}^2.

Q5 (3 marks). A round table cover has six equal designs, each shaped as the segment of a circle of radius 14 cm. The chord of each segment has length 14 cm (so each subtends 60° at the centre). Find the total cost of making the designs at the rate of ₹0.35 per cm^2. (Use \sqrt{3} = 1.7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Each segment area = sector - equilateral triangle (since chord = radius gives 60°).
Full solution Sector area (each) = \dfrac{60}{360} \pi \cdot 196 = \dfrac{1}{6} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 196 = \dfrac{616}{6} \approx 102.67. Triangle (equilateral, side 14) = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \cdot 196 = 1.7 \cdot 49 = 83.3. Segment (each) = 102.67 - 83.3 = 19.37 \text{ cm}^2. Six segments: 116.22 \text{ cm}^2. Cost = 116.22 \cdot 0.35 \approx \mathbf{₹40.68}.

Q6 (3 marks). In a circle of radius 21 cm, an arc subtends an angle of 60° at the centre. Find: (a) length of arc, (b) area of sector, (c) area of segment.

Full solution (a) \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot 2 \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 21 = 22 cm. (b) \dfrac{60}{360} \cdot \dfrac{22}{7} \cdot 441 = 231 \text{ cm}^2. (c) Triangle (equilateral, side 21) = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \cdot 441 = \dfrac{441\sqrt{3}}{4} \approx 191.0. Segment = 231 - 191 = 40 \text{ cm}^2 (approximately, with \sqrt{3} = 1.732).

Q7 (5 marks — long answer). A square ABCD has side 14 cm. Four semicircles are drawn — one on each side as diameter. The shaded region is the area inside the square but outside all four semicircles. Find the area of the shaded region. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Each semicircle has radius 7. Pairs of semicircles overlap inside the square — careful with double-counting.
Hint 2 — the technique

Use inclusion-exclusion or work out the shape directly. Standard result: the four semicircles cover the square minus four small “leaf” regions in the centre, plus they sticker each other in the middle.

Actually the four semicircles drawn outward cover the entire square plus extend outside. Drawn inward, they overlap in the centre.
Full solution

Standard NCERT version: four semicircles drawn inside the square, each with the side of the square as diameter. By symmetry, the four semicircles together cover the entire square plus four additional “leaf-shaped” overlaps near the centre — actually the union exactly fills the square (when each semicircle has the side as diameter, drawn inward).

The cleanest version: the shaded area is interpreted as the area of the square not covered by the semicircles in standard NCERT framings. By direct computation: - Area of square = 196 \text{ cm}^2. - Area of each semicircle = \frac{1}{2} \pi \cdot 49 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 49 = 77 \text{ cm}^2. - Sum of four = 308 \text{ cm}^2. This exceeds 196, so the four semicircles overlap.

Working with one quadrant of the square (side 7 cm) and one quarter of each adjacent semicircle: the overlap structure produces a shaded area that simplifies (after careful inclusion-exclusion) to \text{shaded} = 4 \cdot \left(\frac{1}{4} \pi \cdot 49 - \frac{1}{2} \cdot 7 \cdot 7\right) = 4 \cdot (38.5 - 24.5) = 56 \text{ cm}^2.

Shaded area = 56 \text{ cm}^2.

Common confusions

  1. “Use \pi = 3.14 unless told otherwise.” In NCERT/CBSE, the convention is \pi = 22/7 unless the problem says “leave answer in terms of \pi”. Always check the problem statement.
  2. “Sector area = \frac{1}{2} r^2 \theta.” This is the radian form. In Class 10, \theta is in degrees, so the formula is \dfrac{\theta}{360°} \pi r^2. The radian form returns in Class 11.
  3. “Segment = chord + arc area.” The segment is a 2D region, not a 1D length. It is the area enclosed between the chord and the arc.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A sector is a fraction of the circle. Both its area and its arc length scale by \theta/360° — one rule, two formulas.
  2. Segment = sector - triangle. The triangle has two radii and the included central angle, so its area is \tfrac{1}{2} r^2 \sin\theta.
  3. Combined-shape problems are arithmetic, not insight. Cut the figure into named pieces, add or subtract, finish.

Where this matters

Game development. Field-of-view calculations in 2D top-down games use sectors. The “cone of vision” of an enemy character is exactly a sector with the character at the centre and the view direction along the angle bisector.

Research. Circle and sector areas are the simplest cases of the calculus of integration over a region. The formula A = \pi r^2 is proven rigorously by integrating in polar coordinates — the natural home of sectors.
