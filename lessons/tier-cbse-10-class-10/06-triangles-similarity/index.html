Ch 6 — Triangles (Similarity)

Same shape, different size — and the algebra that proves it

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state when two triangles are congruent? See cbse9.triangles.
  2. Can you simplify \dfrac{12}{18} to \dfrac{2}{3}? See t1.ratio-proportion.
  3. Do you know the angle-sum property of a triangle (180°)?
  4. Can you read off a ratio from a diagram (e.g. “AB : BC = 3 : 2”)?

Three yeses and you are ready.

Touch

Take a printed photograph and a postage-stamp-sized version of the same photograph. Lay them side by side. Pick any two corresponding points on each — say, two corners of the same building. Measure the distance between those points on each photograph.

Repeat for another pair of corresponding points. Compute the ratios.

viewof scale = Inputs.range([0.4, 2.5], {value: 1.6, step: 0.1, label: "Scale factor"})
{
  const w = 460, h = 280;
  const pts = [[40, 200], [120, 80], [180, 220]]; // small triangle
  const ptsBig = pts.map(([x, y]) => [w / 2 + (x - 110) * scale, h / 2 + (y - 150) * scale]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");
  function drawTri(P, color) {
    const path = `M${P[0][0]},${P[0][1]} L${P[1][0]},${P[1][1]} L${P[2][0]},${P[2][1]} Z`;
    svg.append("path").attr("d", path).attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", color)
      .attr("stroke-width", 2);
    P.forEach((pt, i) => {
      svg.append("circle").attr("cx", pt[0]).attr("cy", pt[1]).attr("r", 3).attr("fill", color);
    });
  }
  drawTri(pts, "#26a");
  drawTri(ptsBig, "#c33");
  // measure side AB on each
  const dist = (P, Q) => Math.hypot(P[0] - Q[0], P[1] - Q[1]);
  const ab1 = dist(pts[0], pts[1]);
  const ab2 = dist(ptsBig[0], ptsBig[1]);
  const bc1 = dist(pts[1], pts[2]);
  const bc2 = dist(ptsBig[1], ptsBig[2]);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", 10).attr("y", 14).attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`AB ratio: ${(ab2 / ab1).toFixed(2)},   BC ratio: ${(bc2 / bc1).toFixed(2)}`);
  return svg.node();
}

The two ratios should be the same number — the scale factor. Drag and confirm.

Wonder

  1. The two triangles in the widget have the same shape but different size. What about the angles — are they equal? Why?
  2. If you saw a triangle from far away (small) and the same triangle close up (big), how could you tell if they are “the same” triangle?
  3. Suppose you cut a triangle into a tiny one inside (with a line parallel to one side). Does the small triangle have the same angles as the big one?
  4. If two triangles have the same angles, must their sides be in the same ratio?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Yes, all three angles match. (2) Equal angles + ratio of sides constant. (3) Yes — that is the Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT). (4) Yes — the AA criterion.

Discover

Problem 1 — A line parallel to a side

In \triangle ABC, a line through a point D on AB is drawn parallel to BC, meeting AC at E. If AD = 2, DB = 3, and AE = 4, find EC.

Hint 1 — a nudge The Basic Proportionality Theorem says \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}.
Full solution \dfrac{2}{3} = \dfrac{4}{EC} \Rightarrow EC = 6.

Problem 2 — Why does BPT work?

Sketch \triangle ABC with DE \parallel BC. Why are \triangle ADE and \triangle ABC “the same shape”?

Hint 1 — a nudge List the angles of each. Use the fact that DE \parallel BC and AB is a transversal.
Full solution \angle A is common. \angle ADE = \angle ABC (corresponding angles, DE \parallel BC and AB transversal). So all three angles match. The triangles have the same angles — they are the same shape, just at different scale.

Problem 3 — AA criterion

\triangle ABC has \angle A = 50°, \angle B = 70°. \triangle DEF has \angle D = 50°, \angle E = 70°. Without measuring, must \triangle ABC and \triangle DEF be the same shape?

Hint 1 — a nudge What is the third angle of each?
Full solution Both have \angle C = \angle F = 60° (angle sum). All three angles match, so the two triangles are the same shape — they are similar. Just two matching angles is enough; the third comes free.

Problem 4 — Ratio of sides

In two similar triangles, \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF, with AB = 4, DE = 6. If BC = 5, what is EF?

Hint 1 — a nudge The scale factor is the same on every pair of corresponding sides.
Full solution Scale factor = 6/4 = 3/2. So EF = 5 \cdot 3/2 = 7.5.

Problem 5 — Ratio of areas

For the same triangles in Problem 4, what is the ratio of their areas?

Hint 1 — a nudge If sides scale by k, what does that do to the area?
Hint 2 — the technique Area is a 2D quantity. Each side scales by k → area scales by k^2.
Full solution \dfrac{\text{area of } \triangle DEF}{\text{area of } \triangle ABC} = \left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 = \dfrac{9}{4}.

Problem 6 — Pythagoras as a similarity argument

A right triangle has legs a, b and hypotenuse c. Drop a perpendicular from the right-angle vertex to the hypotenuse. You get three right triangles. Argue why each smaller triangle is similar to the big one.

Hint 1 — a nudge Each small triangle shares one acute angle with the big triangle. Plus the right angle. AA criterion.
Full solution Let the perpendicular hit the hypotenuse at D, splitting it into segments p (next to a) and q (next to b). The two small triangles are similar to the big one (each shares one acute angle and a right angle). By similarity: \dfrac{a}{c} = \dfrac{p}{a} and \dfrac{b}{c} = \dfrac{q} {b}. So a^2 = pc and b^2 = qc. Add: a^2 + b^2 = c(p + q) = c \cdot c = c^2. That is the Pythagorean Theorem, proved by similarity. (Euclid VI.31.)

The oldest recorded use of similar triangles is Thales of Miletus (c. 600 BCE) measuring the height of the Great Pyramid of Khufu by waiting until his own shadow equalled his height — then measuring the pyramid’s shadow. Same Sun, same instant: two similar right triangles, ratio 1{:}1, no climbing. Two centuries later Eratosthenes used the same trick on a much larger scale — a stick in Alexandria, a well in Syene, two similar sectors of a circle — to compute the circumference of the whole Earth to within ~2% of the modern value (c. 240 BCE), all without leaving Egypt. The chapter you’re working through is what made surveying, navigation, and eventually GPS possible.

“Similar” is “same shape, free scale.” Once you accept that two triangles share angles, every length-ratio in one is the same length-ratio in the other — and every area-ratio is the square of that. Most board proofs in this chapter are just: spot two triangles that share two angles, write down one ratio of sides, rearrange.

Name

Two triangles are similar (written \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF) if their corresponding angles are equal and their corresponding sides are in the same ratio. Either condition implies the other.

Criteria for similarity:

  • AA (Angle-Angle): Two pairs of equal angles ⟹ similar (third comes free).
  • SAS (Side-Angle-Side): Two pairs of sides proportional, included angles equal ⟹ similar.
  • SSS (Side-Side-Side): Three pairs of sides proportional ⟹ similar.

Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT, also Thales’ theorem). A line parallel to one side of a triangle, intersecting the other two sides in distinct points, divides those sides in the same ratio.

Areas of similar triangles. If \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF with ratio of sides k, then \dfrac{\text{area}(\triangle ABC)}{\text{area}(\triangle DEF)} = k^2.

A verse:

Equal angles, sides in step — that’s similar in a flash. Two angles match? The third’s the same — it’s free, no cash. Lengths grow by k, the area by k-squared makes its mark. Parallel lines on a triangle? Equal cuts, that’s the trademark.

Apply

Worked example

In \triangle ABC, D and E are points on AB and AC respectively such that DE \parallel BC. If AD = 6 \text{ cm}, DB = 9 \text{ cm}, and AE = 8 \text{ cm}, find AC.

By BPT (since DE \parallel BC): \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}, so \dfrac{6}{9} = \dfrac{8}{EC}, giving EC = 12 \text{ cm}.

Therefore AC = AE + EC = 8 + 12 = \mathbf{20 \text{ cm}}.

Practice — Warm

W1. State the AA similarity criterion.

Full solution If two angles of one triangle equal two angles of another, the triangles are similar.

W2. \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF with AB = 5, DE = 7. Ratio of their areas?

Full solution (5/7)^2 = 25/49.

W3. In \triangle ABC, DE \parallel BC with D on AB, E on AC, AD = 4, DB = 6, EC = 9. Find AE.

Full solution \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC} \Rightarrow \dfrac{4}{6} = \dfrac{AE}{9} \Rightarrow AE = 6.

Practice — Working

P1. Two poles of heights 6 \text{ m} and 11 \text{ m} stand on level ground. The distance between their feet is 12 \text{ m}. Find the distance between their tops.

Hint 1 — a nudge Right triangle: horizontal leg 12, vertical leg 11 - 6 = 5.
Full solution By Pythagoras: \sqrt{12^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{169} = 13 \text{ m}.

P2. A vertical pole of length 6 \text{ m} casts a shadow of 4 \text{ m} on the ground. At the same time, a tower casts a shadow of 28 \text{ m}. Find the height of the tower.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pole/shadow and tower/shadow form similar right triangles (sun’s rays are parallel).
Full solution \dfrac{6}{4} = \dfrac{H}{28} \Rightarrow H = 42 \text{ m}.

P3. In \triangle ABC, \angle B = 90°. D is on AC such that BD \perp AC. If AD = 4, DC = 9, find BD.

Hint 1 — a nudge The geometric-mean / altitude-on-hypotenuse fact: BD^2 = AD \cdot DC.
Full solution BD^2 = 4 \cdot 9 = 36 \Rightarrow BD = 6.

Practice — Stretch

S1. In a trapezium ABCD with AB \parallel DC, the diagonals AC and BD intersect at O. Prove that \dfrac{OA}{OC} = \dfrac{OB}{OD}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Look for similar triangles formed by the diagonals.
Full solution \triangle AOB \sim \triangle COD by AA: \angle AOB = \angle COD (vertically opposite); \angle OAB = \angle OCD (alternate angles, AB \parallel DC). By similarity, \dfrac{OA}{OC} = \dfrac{OB}{OD} (corresponding sides).

S2. In \triangle ABC, D is the midpoint of BC. From D a line is drawn parallel to AB, meeting AC at E. Show that E is the midpoint of AC, and that DE = \frac{1}{2}AB.

Hint 1 — a nudge Apply BPT in \triangle ABC with the line DE.
Full solution By BPT, \dfrac{CD}{DB} = \dfrac{CE}{EA}. But CD = DB (D is midpoint), so CE = EA — E is the midpoint of AC. For the length: \triangle CDE \sim \triangle CBA with ratio 1/2, so DE = \frac{1}{2}AB. (This is the Midpoint Theorem.)

Board-Pattern Practice

Q1 (1 mark — MCQ). \triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR with AB/PQ = 3/4. Then area (\triangle ABC) / area (\triangle PQR) is: (a) 3/4 (b) 9/16 (c) 4/3 (d) 16/9.

Full solution (3/4)^2 = 9/16. (b).

Q2 (1 mark — assertion-reason). Assertion (A): If two triangles are similar, their perimeters are in the same ratio as their corresponding sides. Reason (R): The ratio of areas of two similar triangles is the square of the ratio of corresponding sides.

Full solution A is true (perimeters scale linearly). R is true. R does not explain A — area is a different fact. Correct mark: A and R both true, but R is not the reason for A.

Q3 (2 marks). In \triangle ABC, DE \parallel BC. If AD = x, DB = x - 2, AE = x + 2, EC = x - 1, find x.

Hint 1 — a nudge BPT: \dfrac{x}{x - 2} = \dfrac{x + 2}{x - 1}.
Full solution Cross-multiply: x(x - 1) = (x + 2)(x - 2), i.e. x^2 - x = x^2 - 4 \Rightarrow x = 4.

Q4 (2 marks). State and prove that the ratio of the areas of two similar triangles is equal to the square of the ratio of their corresponding sides. (For two specific triangles you may use a sketch.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Standard NCERT theorem. Use the formula \frac{1}{2} base × height on each.
Full solution Let \triangle ABC \sim \triangle PQR with corresponding sides in ratio k. Drop altitudes AM (from A on BC) and PN (from P on QR). Then \triangle ABM \sim \triangle PQN (AA — both have right angle at M, N and \angle B = \angle Q), so AM / PN = AB/PQ = k. Also BC/QR = k. Therefore \dfrac{\text{ar}(\triangle ABC)}{\text{ar}(\triangle PQR)} = \dfrac{\frac{1}{2}BC \cdot AM}{\frac{1}{2}QR \cdot PN} = k \cdot k = k^2. \square

Q5 (3 marks). In a \triangle ABC, right-angled at B, BD \perp AC with AB = 12, BC = 5. Find BD.

Hint 1 — a nudge AC = ? first, by Pythagoras. Then use area = \frac{1}{2}AB \cdot BC = \frac{1}{2}AC \cdot BD.
Full solution AC = \sqrt{144 + 25} = 13. Area = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 12 \cdot 5 = 30. Also area = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 13 \cdot BD, so BD = \dfrac{60}{13}.

Q6 (3 marks). In \triangle ABC, D and E are on AB and AC respectively, such that DE \parallel BC and \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{2} {3}. If \text{ar}(\triangle ABC) = 50 \text{ cm}^2, find ar(\triangle ADE).

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle ADE \sim \triangle ABC. Find the ratio of sides first.
Full solution \dfrac{AD}{AB} = \dfrac{2}{2 + 3} = \dfrac{2}{5}. Area ratio = (2/5)^2 = 4/25. So ar(\triangle ADE) = \dfrac{4}{25} \cdot 50 = 8 \text{ cm}^2.

Q7 (5 marks — long answer). State and prove the Basic Proportionality Theorem (BPT). Use it to prove that if a line divides any two sides of a triangle in the same ratio, then the line must be parallel to the third side (the converse).

Hint 1 — a nudge Forward: drop perpendiculars from D, E to BC for the area-ratio proof. Converse: assume the ratio-equality; show the line is parallel by contradiction (or by uniqueness).
Full solution

Forward (BPT). In \triangle ABC, let DE \parallel BC with D on AB, E on AC. Drop perpendiculars from D to BC at M and from E to BC at N. Then ar(\triangle ADE) shares an altitude with \triangle DBE (from E, common vertex). So \dfrac{\text{ar}(\triangle ADE)}{\text{ar}(\triangle DBE)} = \dfrac{AD} {DB}. Similarly with vertex D: \dfrac{\text{ar}(\triangle ADE)}{\text{ar}(\triangle DEC)} = \dfrac{AE} {EC}. But \triangle DBE and \triangle DEC have the same base DE and equal heights (because DE \parallel BC), so they have equal area. Hence \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}. \square

Converse. Suppose a line through D on AB meets AC at E with \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE}{EC}. Draw DE' parallel to BC from D, meeting AC at E'. By BPT, \dfrac{AD}{DB} = \dfrac{AE'}{E'C}. So \dfrac{AE}{EC} = \dfrac{AE'}{E'C}, which forces E = E'. Hence DE \parallel BC. \square

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Similar figures share angles, scale sides. Equal corresponding angles ⟺ proportional corresponding sides — either implies the other for triangles.
  2. AA is enough for triangles. Two pairs of equal angles force the third (since angles sum to 180°), and similarity follows. SSS and SAS are the other two criteria.
  3. Areas scale by the square of the side-ratio — the same square-cube law that governs why elephants can’t have the body-plan of an ant. A common 2- or 3-mark trap on the board paper.

Common confusions

  1. “Similar means same size.” No — similar means same shape. Same size with same shape is congruent. Congruent ⟹ similar; similar ⟹ congruent only if the scale factor is 1.
  2. “BPT needs the line to bisect.” No — BPT works for any line parallel to a side. It divides the other two sides in the same ratio (whatever that ratio is).
  3. “Area ratio equals side ratio.” No — area ratio equals square of side ratio. A common board mistake worth ₹2-3.
