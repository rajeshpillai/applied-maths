Ch 12 — Surface Areas and Volumes (Combined Solids)

Glue a cone on a hemisphere, slice a frustum off a cone — and add the parts

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state the volume and surface area of a cube of side a? See cbse9.surface-areas-and-volumes.
  2. Can you state the volume and CSA (curved surface area) of a cylinder of radius r and height h?
  3. Do you know the volume and CSA of a cone of radius r, height h, slant height \ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}?
  4. Do you know the volume and surface area of a sphere of radius r?

Three yeses, ready.

Touch

A pencil. The body is a cylinder; the sharpened tip is a cone. Pencil = cylinder + cone (glued along a flat face).

A capsule (the medicine kind). The middle is a cylinder; the two end caps are hemispheres of a sphere. Capsule = cylinder + 2 hemispheres.

viewof shape = Inputs.select(["pencil (cylinder + cone)", "capsule (cylinder + 2 hemispheres)", "ice-cream (cone + hemisphere)"], {label: "Composite shape"})
viewof rad12 = Inputs.range([1, 5], {value: 2, step: 0.5, label: "Radius r (cm)"})
viewof len12 = Inputs.range([2, 12], {value: 6, step: 1, label: "Cylinder length / cone height (cm)"})
{
  const r = rad12, L = len12;
  let report;
  if (shape === "pencil (cylinder + cone)") {
    const Vcyl = Math.PI * r * r * L;
    const Vcone = Math.PI * r * r * L / 3;
    const Vtot = Vcyl + Vcone;
    report = `Pencil volume = πr²L + (1/3)πr²L = ${Vtot.toFixed(2)} cm³`;
  } else if (shape === "capsule (cylinder + 2 hemispheres)") {
    const Vcyl = Math.PI * r * r * L;
    const Vsph = (4 / 3) * Math.PI * r * r * r;
    const Vtot = Vcyl + Vsph;
    report = `Capsule volume = πr²L + (4/3)πr³ = ${Vtot.toFixed(2)} cm³`;
  } else {
    const Vcone = Math.PI * r * r * L / 3;
    const Vhemi = (2 / 3) * Math.PI * r * r * r;
    const Vtot = Vcone + Vhemi;
    report = `Ice-cream volume = (1/3)πr²L + (2/3)πr³ = ${Vtot.toFixed(2)} cm³`;
  }
  return html`<pre style="font-family: ui-monospace, monospace; font-size: 13px;">${report}
r = ${r} cm, L = ${L} cm</pre>`;
}

The recipe: identify the parts, compute each volume separately, then add. The same applies to surface area — but be careful: glued faces disappear from the outside.

Wonder

  1. The pencil has a cylinder volume + a cone volume. Why do we add them? When does an and of two shapes become a plus?
  2. The capsule’s surface is the lateral surface of the cylinder + the two hemispheres’ surfaces. The two flat circles where they meet — are they visible from outside?
  3. A frustum (a bucket) is a cone with the tip chopped off. How would you compute its volume?
  4. If you melt a sphere and re-cast it as a cube, what stays the same and what changes?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Volumes add when shapes don’t overlap. (2) The flat join-faces are hidden — don’t include them in surface area. (3) Frustum volume = full cone volume - tip cone volume. (4) Volume is preserved; surface area generally is not.

Discover

Problem 1 — Volume of a cone + hemisphere

A solid is in the shape of a cone of radius 7 cm and height 24 cm mounted on a hemisphere of the same radius. Find the total volume. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Total = cone volume + hemisphere volume.
Full solution Cone: \frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 49 \cdot 24 = 22 \cdot 56 = 1232 \text{ cm}^3. Hemisphere: \frac{2}{3} \pi r^3 = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 343 = \frac{2156}{3} \approx 718.67 \text{ cm}^3. Total \approx \mathbf{1950.67 \text{ cm}^3}.

Problem 2 — Surface area of a capsule

A capsule has a cylindrical part of length 10 cm and hemispherical ends of radius 3 cm. Find the total surface area. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Cylinder lateral + 2 hemispheres’ curved surfaces — no flat faces.
Full solution Cylinder lateral = 2\pi r h = 2 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 3 \cdot 10 = \frac{1320}{7} \approx 188.57. Two hemispheres’ curved = full sphere = 4\pi r^2 = 4 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 9 = \frac{792}{7} \approx 113.14. Total \approx \mathbf{301.71 \text{ cm}^2}.

Problem 3 — Frustum volume by subtraction

A cone has height H and base radius R. The top portion is sliced off by a plane parallel to the base, forming a small cone of height h and top radius r (and leaving a frustum). Argue why the frustum’s volume is \frac{1}{3} \pi (R^2 H - r^2 h), and check the standard formula V_{\text{frustum}} = \frac{1}{3} \pi h_f (R^2 + r^2 + Rr), where h_f = H - h is the frustum’s height.

Hint 1 — a nudge Big cone - small cone gives frustum. Use r/R = h/H (similar triangles) to relate.
Full solution By similar triangles, r/R = h/H, so h = Hr/R. Big cone volume: \frac{1}{3}\pi R^2 H. Small cone: \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 \cdot Hr/R = \frac{1}{3}\pi H r^3/R. Frustum volume = \frac{1}{3}\pi H \cdot \dfrac{R^3 - r^3}{R} = \frac{1}{3}\pi H \cdot \dfrac{(R - r)(R^2 + Rr + r^2)}{R}. With h_f = H - h = H(R - r)/R: V = \frac{1}{3}\pi h_f (R^2 + Rr + r^2). \square

Problem 4 — Melting and recasting

A solid sphere of radius 6 cm is melted and recast into a cylinder of radius 4 cm. Find the height of the cylinder.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume is preserved.
Full solution \frac{4}{3} \pi (6)^3 = \pi (4)^2 h \Rightarrow \frac{4 \cdot 216}{3} = 16 h \Rightarrow 288 = 16 h \Rightarrow h = 18 cm.

Problem 5 — Hollow shape

A hollow sphere has external radius 4 cm and internal radius 2 cm. It is melted and recast into a cone of base radius 4 cm. Find the height.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume of the hollow shell = \frac{4}{3}\pi (R^3 - r^3).
Full solution Shell volume = \frac{4}{3}\pi(64 - 8) = \frac{4}{3}\pi \cdot 56 = \frac{224 \pi}{3}. Cone volume = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h = \frac{1}{3}\pi \cdot 16 \cdot h. Equate: \frac{224\pi}{3} = \frac{16\pi h}{3} \Rightarrow h = 14 cm.

Problem 6 — Surface area of a frustum

A bucket has top radius 20 cm, bottom radius 8 cm, height 16 cm. Find its slant height and curved surface area.

Hint 1 — a nudge Slant height \ell = \sqrt{h^2 + (R - r)^2}. CSA = \pi (R + r) \ell.
Full solution \ell = \sqrt{16^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{256 + 144} = \sqrt{400} = 20 cm. CSA = \pi (20 + 8) \cdot 20 = 560 \pi. With \pi = 22/7: 560 \cdot \frac{22}{7} = 1760 \text{ cm}^2.

Archimedes’ epitaph (~212 BCE). Archimedes considered his discovery that a sphere inscribed in a cylinder of equal height fills exactly two thirds of the cylinder’s volume — so beautiful that he asked for the figure to be carved on his tombstone. His proof, in On the Sphere and Cylinder, is the earliest known volume calculation that uses what we would now call integration. Centuries later the Roman orator Cicero, visiting Syracuse, found the overgrown grave by recognising Archimedes’ sphere-and-cylinder carving — and reported the discovery in his letters. The sphere/cylinder ratio you’ll prove in problem P1 is that same 2,200-year-old fact.

Volumes add when shapes don’t overlap; surface areas omit hidden joining faces. That single principle — together with the standard formulas in Name — handles every “combined solid” problem in this chapter. Melting and recasting is the same idea backwards: total volume is conserved, so equate before and after.

Name

Standard solid formulas (r = radius, h = height, \ell = slant height, a = side):

Solid Volume Curved/Lateral SA Total SA
Cube a^3 4a^2 6a^2
Cuboid l \times b \times h lbh 2h(l + b) 2(lb + bh + hl)
Cylinder \pi r^2 h 2\pi r h 2\pi r(r + h)
Cone (\ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}) \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \pi r \ell \pi r(r + \ell)
Sphere \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 4\pi r^2 4\pi r^2
Hemisphere \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 2\pi r^2 3\pi r^2

Frustum of a cone (radii R, r; height h_f; slant \ell_f = \sqrt{ h_f^2 + (R - r)^2}):

  • Volume: \frac{1}{3} \pi h_f (R^2 + r^2 + Rr).
  • Curved SA: \pi (R + r) \ell_f.
  • Total SA: \pi(R + r)\ell_f + \pi R^2 + \pi r^2.

Combined solids: add volumes for non-overlapping pieces; for surface areas, exclude internal joining faces. Melting and recasting = volume conserved, surface area generally changes.

A verse:

Cone, sphere, cylinder, cube — each one its formula true. Glue them — add their volumes; the joining face does not show through. Melt and recast? Volume holds — every drop accounted for. Frustum’s a chopped cone: subtract the tip, that’s all you need to score.

Apply

Worked example

A toy is in the form of a cone of radius 3.5 cm mounted on a hemisphere of the same radius. The total height of the toy is 15.5 cm. Find the total surface area of the toy. (Use \pi = 22/7.)

The hemisphere has radius 3.5 cm. The cone sits on it. So cone height = 15.5 - 3.5 = 12 cm.

Slant height of cone: \ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2} = \sqrt{12.25 + 144} = \sqrt{156.25} = 12.5 cm.

Total surface area = (cone curved SA) + (hemisphere curved SA). The flat circle where they meet is hidden.

= \pi r \ell + 2 \pi r^2 = \pi r (\ell + 2r) = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 3.5 \cdot (12.5 + 7) = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 3.5 \cdot 19.5 = 11 \cdot 19.5 = \mathbf{214.5 \text{ cm}^2}.

Practice — Warm

W1. Volume of a cylinder, r = 7 cm, h = 10 cm.

Full solution \pi r^2 h = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 49 \cdot 10 = 1540 \text{ cm}^3.

W2. A solid hemisphere has radius 7 cm. Find its total surface area.

Full solution 3\pi r^2 = 3 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 49 = 462 \text{ cm}^2.

W3. A cone has radius 5, slant height 13. Find height and CSA.

Full solution h = \sqrt{169 - 25} = 12. CSA = \pi r \ell = 65\pi \approx 204.20 \text{ cm}^2.

Practice — Working

P1. A solid in the form of a right circular cylinder, with one end a hemisphere and the other a cone. The common radius is 4 cm, the height of the cylinder is 40 cm, the height of the cone is 3 cm. Find the volume.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume = cylinder + hemisphere + cone.
Full solution Cylinder: \pi \cdot 16 \cdot 40 = 640\pi. Hemisphere: \frac{2}{3}\pi \cdot 64 = \frac{128\pi}{3}. Cone: \frac{1}{3}\pi \cdot 16 \cdot 3 = 16\pi. Total = 640\pi + \frac{128\pi}{3} + 16\pi = \frac{1920\pi + 128\pi + 48\pi}{3} = \frac{2096\pi}{3} \approx \frac{2096 \cdot 22}{21} = \frac{46112}{21} \approx 2195.81 \text{ cm}^3.

P2. A cylindrical container of radius 6 cm and height 15 cm is full of ice cream which is divided among 10 children in equal cones with hemispherical tops on top. If the height of the conical portion is 4 times the radius of its base, find the radius of the ice-cream cone.

Hint 1 — a nudge Total ice cream volume / 10 = volume per child = cone + hemisphere.
Full solution Total ice cream = \pi \cdot 36 \cdot 15 = 540\pi. Per child: 54\pi. Per child = cone + hemisphere = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 (4r) + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 + \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = 2\pi r^3. 2\pi r^3 = 54\pi \Rightarrow r^3 = 27 \Rightarrow r = 3 cm.

P3. A drinking glass is in the shape of a frustum of a cone of height 14 cm. The diameters of its two circular ends are 4 cm and 2 cm. Find the capacity (volume) of the glass.

Hint 1 — a nudge R = 2, r = 1, h_f = 14.
Full solution V = \frac{1}{3}\pi h_f(R^2 + r^2 + Rr) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 14 \cdot (4 + 1 + 2) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 14 \cdot 7 = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 22 \cdot 14 = \frac{308}{3} \approx 102.67 \text{ cm}^3.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A vessel is in the form of an inverted cone, height 8 cm and radius at top 5 cm. It is filled with water up to the brim. When lead shots, each spherical with radius 0.5 cm, are dropped into the vessel, one-fourth of the water flows out. Find the number of lead shots.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume displaced = \frac{1}{4} vessel volume.
Full solution Vessel volume = \frac{1}{3}\pi \cdot 25 \cdot 8 = \frac{200\pi}{3}. \frac{1}{4} of this = \frac{50\pi}{3}. Each lead shot = \frac{4}{3}\pi (0.5)^3 = \frac{4\pi}{3 \cdot 8} = \frac{\pi}{6}. n = \dfrac{50\pi/3}{\pi/6} = \dfrac{50 \cdot 6}{3} = 100 shots.

S2. Water is being pumped out through a circular pipe of internal diameter 7 cm at the rate of 5 m/s. Calculate the volume of water (in litres) discharged in 30 minutes.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume per second = cross-section area × speed. Convert units.
Full solution Cross-section area = \pi (3.5)^2 = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 12.25 = 38.5 \text{ cm}^2 = 0.00385 \text{ m}^2. Volume per second = 0.00385 \cdot 5 = 0.01925 \text{ m}^3 = 19.25 litres. Per 30 min (1800 s): 19.25 \cdot 1800 = \mathbf{34650} litres.

Board-Pattern Practice

Q1 (1 mark — MCQ). The volume of a sphere is \dfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3. The volume of a hemisphere of the same radius is: (a) \dfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3 (b) \dfrac{1}{2}\pi r^3 (c) \dfrac{4}{6}\pi r^3 (d) Both (a) and (c).

Full solution \dfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = \dfrac{4}{6}\pi r^3. (d).

Q2 (1 mark — MCQ). A solid is hemispherical at one end and conical at the other. The radii of both are equal. The slant height of the cone is 5 cm and base radius 4 cm. The total surface area is: (a) 20\pi (b) 52\pi (c) 32\pi (d) 36\pi.

Full solution Cone CSA = \pi r \ell = 20\pi. Hemisphere curved = 2\pi r^2 = 32\pi. Total = 52\pi. (b).

Q3 (2 marks). A wooden article was made by scooping out a hemisphere from each end of a solid cylinder. If the height of the cylinder is 20 cm and its base radius is 3.5 cm, find the volume of the wood remaining.

Hint 1 — a nudge Cylinder volume - 2 hemispheres = cylinder - 1 sphere.
Full solution

V = \pi r^2 h - \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{22}{7}(12.25 \cdot 20 - \frac{4}{3} \cdot 42.875) = \frac{22}{7}(245 - 57.17) = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 187.83 \approx 590.33 \text{ cm}^3.

Cleaner: \frac{22}{7}(245 - \frac{171.5}{3}) = \frac{22}{7} \cdot \frac{735 - 171.5}{3} = \frac{22}{7} \cdot \frac{563.5}{3} = \frac{12397} {21} \approx \mathbf{590.33 \text{ cm}^3}.

Q4 (2 marks). A solid metallic sphere of radius 6 cm is melted and drawn into a wire of diameter 0.4 cm. Find the length of the wire.

Hint 1 — a nudge Sphere volume = wire (cylinder) volume.
Full solution \frac{4}{3}\pi (6)^3 = \pi (0.2)^2 \cdot L \Rightarrow 288\pi = 0.04\pi L \Rightarrow L = 7200 cm = 72 m.

Q5 (3 marks). A solid is composed of a cylinder with hemispherical ends. If the whole length of the solid is 108 cm and the diameter of the hemispherical ends is 36 cm, find the cost of polishing its surface at the rate of \text{₹}7 per 1000 \text{ cm}^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge r = 18, cylinder height L = 108 - 2 \cdot 18 = 72.
Full solution Cylinder lateral = 2\pi r L = 2 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 18 \cdot 72 = \frac{56\pi \cdot 162}{?} — recompute cleanly: 2\pi r L = 2 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 18 \cdot 72 = \frac{2 \cdot 22 \cdot 18 \cdot 72}{7} = \frac{57024}{7} \approx 8146.29. Two hemispheres = sphere = 4\pi r^2 = 4 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 324 = \frac{28512}{7} \approx 4073.14. Total surface = \frac{57024 + 28512}{7} = \frac{85536}{7} \approx 12219.43 \text{ cm}^2. Cost = \frac{12219.43}{1000} \cdot 7 \approx \mathbf{₹85.54}.

Q6 (3 marks). A bucket open at the top is in the shape of a frustum of a cone with capacity 12308.8 \text{ cm}^3. The radii of its top and bottom are 20 cm and 12 cm. Find the height of the bucket.

Hint 1 — a nudge V = \frac{1}{3}\pi h(R^2 + r^2 + Rr) = \frac{1}{3}\pi h(400 + 144 + 240).
Full solution 12308.8 = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 3.14 \cdot h \cdot 784 \Rightarrow 12308.8 = 820.69 \cdot h \Rightarrow h \approx 15 cm.

Q7 (5 marks — long answer). A tent is in the shape of a cylinder surmounted by a conical top. If the height and diameter of the cylindrical part are 2.1 m and 4 m respectively, and the slant height of the top is 2.8 m, find the area of the canvas used for making the tent. Also find the cost of the canvas at the rate of \text{₹}500 per \text{m}^2. (Note: the floor of the tent is not canvas.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Canvas = cylinder lateral + cone curved.
Full solution r = 2 m, h_{\text{cyl}} = 2.1 m, \ell = 2.8 m. Cylinder lateral = 2\pi r h = 2 \cdot \frac{22}{7} \cdot 2 \cdot 2.1 = \frac{2 \cdot 22 \cdot 4.2}{7} = \frac{184.8}{7} = 26.4 \text{ m}^2. Cone curved = \pi r \ell = \frac{22}{7} \cdot 2 \cdot 2.8 = \frac{123.2}{7} = 17.6 \text{ m}^2. Total canvas = 44 \text{ m}^2. Cost = 44 \cdot 500 = \mathbf{₹22000}.

Common confusions

  1. “Surface area of glued solid = sum of all surface areas.” No — the internal faces (where the parts meet) are hidden. Subtract those, or compute only the externally visible parts.
  2. “Melting changes the volume.” It doesn’t (assuming no spillage). Volume is conserved; that’s the whole point of recasting problems.
  3. “Slant height of a cone equals the radius.” No — slant height \ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}. It’s the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by the height and the base radius.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Add the parts; hide the joints. Combined-solid volume sums; the internal faces where parts meet are not on the surface.
  2. Melting conserves volume, not surface area. Recasting problems are one-line equations: V_{\text{before}} = V_{\text{after}}.
  3. A frustum is the difference of two cones. Or apply V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi h_f(R^2 + Rr + r^2) directly — same answer, less arithmetic.

Where this matters

Game development. Every 3D model in a game is internally a list of primitive solids (boxes, spheres, cylinders, cones, frusta) with known volumes — used for physics, collision, mass, and inertia calculations.

Research. The volume formulas you’ve been using all come from the calculus method of Pappus and Cavalieri — slicing a solid into infinitesimally thin layers. The Class 12 integration chapter re-derives every one of them via \int_a^b \pi r(x)^2 \, dx.
