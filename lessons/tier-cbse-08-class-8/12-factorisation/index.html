Ch 12 — Factorisation

Run the multiplication backwards — turn a sum into a product

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you expand (x + 3)(x + 5)? If not, revisit Algebraic Expressions and Identities.
  2. Do you remember the three identities (a+b)^2, (a-b)^2, and (a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2?
  3. Can you write 24 as a product of primes? If not, revisit Divisibility and Primes.
  4. Do you know what the greatest common factor (GCF) of 12 and 18 is?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Take 12 identical buttons (or coins). Arrange them in rectangles — all the rectangles you can make.

You will find: 1 \times 12, 2 \times 6, 3 \times 4. (And the mirror images.) Each rectangle is a “factoring” of 12 into a product of two whole numbers.

The widget below does the same for variable expressions.

viewof expr = Inputs.select(
  ["6x + 9", "x^2 + 5x", "x^2 + 7x + 12", "x^2 - 16"],
  {label: "Expression"}
)
{
  const e = expr;
  const map = {
    "6x + 9":         "= 3 &middot; (2x + 3)",
    "x^2 + 5x":       "= x &middot; (x + 5)",
    "x^2 + 7x + 12":  "= (x + 3) &middot; (x + 4)",
    "x^2 - 16":       "= (x - 4) &middot; (x + 4)",
  };
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;line-height:1.6;">
    <div><b>${e}</b> &nbsp; ${map[e]}</div>
    <div style="color:#666;margin-top:0.4rem;">
      Each expression is being shown as a product of simpler pieces.
      How would you find that product *yourself*?
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

Each example is a product, written as a sum. The job of this chapter is to run the rectangle-game in reverse: given the sum, recover the product.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. In 6x + 9, what number divides both terms? What about x^2 + 5x — is there a variable that divides both?
  2. We discovered (a+b)(a-b) = a^2 - b^2 in the last chapter. So if you see x^2 - 25, what should you reach for?
  3. The expression x^2 + 7x + 12 has constant 12 and middle coefficient 7. Can you guess the two numbers we’ll need?
  4. Could every algebraic sum be factorised into two simpler pieces? Or are some sums “prime”?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

Factorisation has four main techniques: pull out the common factor, group, recognise an identity (a^2 - b^2, a^2 + 2ab + b^2, a^2 - 2ab + b^2), and split the middle term. Some expressions are irreducible — like prime numbers in algebra-land — and stay sums.

Discover

Problem 1 — Common factor

Factorise 6x + 9.

Hint 1 — a nudge What is the GCF of 6 and 9?
Full solution

GCF = 3. So 6x + 9 = 3(2x + 3).

Check by expanding: 3 \cdot 2x + 3 \cdot 3 = 6x + 9. ✓

Problem 2 — Variable common factor

Factorise x^2 + 5x.

Hint 1 — a nudge What divides both x^2 and 5x?
Full solution Both contain x. So x^2 + 5x = x(x + 5).

Problem 3 — Group and factor

Factorise ax + ay + bx + by.

Hint 1 — a nudge The first two terms share an a; the last two share a b. Group them.
Hint 2 — the technique a(x + y) + b(x + y) — both pieces share (x+y).
Full solution ax + ay + bx + by = a(x + y) + b(x + y) = (x + y)(a + b).

Problem 4 — Difference of squares

Factorise x^2 - 49.

Hint 1 — a nudge 49 = 7^2. Identity: a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b).
Full solution x^2 - 49 = (x + 7)(x - 7).

Problem 5 — Perfect square

Factorise x^2 + 10x + 25.

Hint 1 — a nudge 25 = 5^2 and 10 = 2 \cdot 5. Identity: (a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2.
Full solution x^2 + 10x + 25 = (x + 5)^2.

Problem 6 — Split the middle term

Factorise x^2 + 7x + 12.

Hint 1 — a nudge You need two numbers whose sum is 7 and product is 12.
Hint 2 — the technique Factors of 12: 1 \cdot 12, 2 \cdot 6, 3 \cdot 4. Of these, 3 + 4 = 7. So split: 7x = 3x + 4x.
Full solution x^2 + 7x + 12 = x^2 + 3x + 4x + 12 = x(x + 3) + 4(x + 3) = (x + 3)(x + 4).

Problem 7 — Polynomial division

Divide 6x^3 - 8x^2 + 4x by 2x.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pull the common factor 2x from each term.
Full solution

6x^3 - 8x^2 + 4x = 2x(3x^2 - 4x + 2). Dividing by 2x: 3x^2 - 4x + 2.

Polynomial division is just factorisation followed by cancellation when the divisor is a factor.

You have just used four techniques: common factor, grouping, identity, and middle-term split. The chapter is the choreography of switching between them.

The Greek mathematician Diophantus of Alexandria (c. 200 – c. 284 CE), in his Arithmetica, was the first to write equations in something resembling modern symbolic form — and the first to factor expressions to solve them. A millennium and a half later, his book sat on Pierre de Fermat’s desk; in its margin, next to a problem on writing a square as a sum of two squares, Fermat scribbled the conjecture that became Fermat’s Last Theorem — finally proved by Andrew Wiles in 1995. Every chain of factoring you do in this chapter is in Diophantus’ direct line.

Factoring is running multiplication backwards. The identities you proved in Chapter 8 — (a+b)^2, (a-b)^2, difference of squares — are the same equalities, read right-to-left. The chapter’s only new skill is pattern-recognition: looking at a sum and seeing the product hiding inside.

Name

To factorise an expression is to write it as a product of simpler expressions whose product equals the original.

The four techniques:

  1. Common factor. Pull out the largest expression dividing every term.
  2. Grouping. Pair terms so each pair has a common factor; pull out the shared binomial.
  3. Identity. Recognise a^2 - b^2, (a \pm b)^2, etc., and apply.
  4. Splitting the middle term. For x^2 + bx + c, find numbers p, q with p + q = b and pq = c, then split bx = px + qx and group.

Polynomial division is just factorisation: if g(x) divides f(x), then f(x) = g(x) \cdot q(x), and the quotient is what is left after extracting the factor.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Pull the common, group the four, spot the identity-shape; Or split the middle into two whose sum is right, whose product is too. Then the sum has become a product — And the product reveals what the sum could not.

Apply

Worked example

Factorise 4x^2 - 25y^2.

Competent solver’s path:

  1. Spot the shape: difference of two squares.
  2. 4x^2 = (2x)^2 and 25y^2 = (5y)^2.
  3. Apply a^2 - b^2 = (a+b)(a-b) with a = 2x, b = 5y.

Answer: 4x^2 - 25y^2 = (2x + 5y)(2x - 5y).

Practice — Warm

W1. Factorise 12x + 8.

Full solution 4(3x + 2).

W2. Factorise a^2 - 81.

Full solution (a + 9)(a - 9).

W3. Factorise y^2 + 6y + 9.

Full solution (y + 3)^2.

Practice — Working

P1. Factorise a^2 - 14a + 49.

Hint 1 — a nudge 49 = 7^2 and 14 = 2 \cdot 7. Perfect square of a difference.
Full solution (a - 7)^2.

P2. Factorise x^2 + 9x + 18.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two numbers with sum 9 and product 18.
Full solution 3 + 6 = 9, 3 \cdot 6 = 18. Split: 9x = 3x + 6x. x^2 + 3x + 6x + 18 = x(x + 3) + 6(x + 3) = (x + 3)(x + 6).

P3. Factorise 6x^2 + 17x + 5.

Hint 1 — a nudge For ax^2 + bx + c, find two numbers whose product is ac and whose sum is b. Here ac = 30, b = 17.
Hint 2 — the technique 15 \cdot 2 = 30, 15 + 2 = 17. Split 17x = 15x + 2x.
Full solution 6x^2 + 17x + 5 = 6x^2 + 15x + 2x + 5 = 3x(2x + 5) + 1(2x + 5) = (2x + 5)(3x + 1).

Practice — Stretch

S1. Factorise a^4 - b^4 completely.

Hint 1 — a nudge Difference of squares applied twice.
Hint 2 — the technique a^4 - b^4 = (a^2)^2 - (b^2)^2. Now factor again.
Full solution

a^4 - b^4 = (a^2 + b^2)(a^2 - b^2) = (a^2 + b^2)(a + b)(a - b).

(Note: a^2 + b^2 is not further factorisable using real-number techniques.)

S2. Factorise x^3 + 2x^2 - x - 2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Group the four terms in pairs.
Hint 2 — the technique Group: (x^3 + 2x^2) - (x + 2) = x^2(x + 2) - 1(x + 2).
Full solution x^3 + 2x^2 - x - 2 = x^2(x+2) - 1(x+2) = (x+2)(x^2 - 1) = (x+2)(x+1)(x-1).

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). x^2 - 9 factorises as (a) (x-3)(x+3) (b) (x-9)(x+9) (c) (x-3)^2 (d) (x+3)^2.

Full solution Difference of squares. Answer: (a).

2-mark (short answer). Factorise 25x^2 - 70xy + 49y^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge 25x^2 = (5x)^2 and 49y^2 = (7y)^2 and 70xy = 2 \cdot 5x \cdot 7y. (a-b)^2 shape.
Full solution (5x - 7y)^2.

3-mark (short answer II). Factorise x^2 + x - 132.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two numbers with product -132 and sum 1.
Hint 2 — the technique Try 12 and -11: product -132, sum 1. ✓
Full solution

x^2 + x - 132 = x^2 + 12x - 11x - 132 = x(x + 12) - 11(x + 12) = (x + 12)(x - 11).

Check: (x+12)(x-11) = x^2 - 11x + 12x - 132 = x^2 + x - 132. ✓

4-mark (long answer). (i) Factorise a^2 - 2ab + b^2 - c^2. (ii) Hence find the value when a = 7, b = 4, c = 5.

Hint 1 — a nudge The first three terms form a perfect square. Then a difference of squares appears.
Full solution

(i) a^2 - 2ab + b^2 = (a-b)^2. So a^2 - 2ab + b^2 - c^2 = (a-b)^2 - c^2 = (a - b + c)(a - b - c).

(ii) a - b = 3. So (a-b+c) = 8 and (a-b-c) = -2. Product: 8 \times (-2) = -16.

(Verify by direct substitution: 49 - 56 + 16 - 25 = -16. ✓)

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 12.6 (“factorise a^2 - b^2 + 2bc - c^2”) is a near-twin of the 4-mark item — both use group three terms as a square, then difference-of-squares. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2019 Set 1, Q11 (“factorise x^2 + 6x + 8”) matches Working P2 exactly.

Common confusions

  1. “a^2 + b^2 = (a + b)(a - b).” No — that’s a^2 - b^2. The sum of two squares does not factor over the real numbers (in this chapter’s toolkit).
  2. “Splitting the middle term works for any trinomial.” Only for trinomials of the form ax^2 + bx + c where the discriminant b^2 - 4ac is a perfect square. Otherwise the roots are irrational and you need the formula from Class 10.
  3. “x^2 + 1 can be factorised.” Not over the real numbers. You meet its complex factorisation (x + i)(x - i) later, in Tier 4.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. To factor is to write a sum as a product — the inverse of “expand”. The product reveals the structure (roots, common pieces) that the sum hid.
  2. Four techniques cover almost everything in Class 8: common factor, grouping, identity-spot, and middle-term split. Try them in that order.
  3. Some sums are irreducible — like primes among the integers. x^2 + 1, x^2 + x + 1, and a^2 + b^2 stay sums under real-number factoring; meeting them is normal, not failure.

Self-Assessment

Where this matters

Research. Factoring polynomials is the discrete cousin of factoring integers. The same algorithms — Gauss’s method, Berlekamp’s algorithm — show up in symbolic computation systems like Mathematica and SymPy.

Encryption. RSA security depends on a very specific factorisation problem being hard: factoring a 2048-bit integer that is the product of two large primes. The fact that integer factorisation is hard while polynomial factorisation (today’s lesson) is easy is the unsung asymmetry of modern cryptography.
