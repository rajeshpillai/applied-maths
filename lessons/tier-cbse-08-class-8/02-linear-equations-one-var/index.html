Ch 2 — Linear Equations in One Variable

Balance the scales — what you do to one side, do to the other

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you read 3x + 5 as “three times some unknown number, plus five”? If not, revisit Intro to Variables.
  2. Are you comfortable adding \tfrac{2}{3} and \tfrac{1}{4}? If not, revisit Rational Numbers.
  3. If both sides of 2 = 2 are multiplied by 5, do you trust that the equation still holds?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Imagine an old-fashioned two-pan balance. On the left pan: a known weight of 7 grams plus an unknown stone of weight x. On the right pan: a known weight of 19 grams. The pans hang level — they are balanced.

Move the slider. Watch the pans. Find the value of x that makes them hang level.

viewof guess = Inputs.range([0, 25], {value: 5, step: 1, label: "Your guess for x (grams)"})
{
  const left = guess + 7;
  const right = 19;
  const tilt = left === right ? 0 : (left > right ? -10 : 10);
  const status = left === right ? "BALANCED" : (left > right ? "left pan heavier" : "right pan heavier");
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;text-align:center;line-height:1.5;">
    <div style="margin-bottom:0.5rem;">
      <b>Left pan:</b> x + 7 = ${left} g &nbsp;&nbsp;|&nbsp;&nbsp;
      <b>Right pan:</b> 19 g
    </div>
    <div style="font-size:2rem;color:${left===right?"green":"#999"};transform:rotate(${tilt}deg);transition:0.3s;">
      &#x2696;
    </div>
    <div style="color:${left===right?"green":"#666"};margin-top:0.4rem;">
      ${status}
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

When you find the balance you have solved the puzzle. The arithmetic behind the answer is the topic of this lesson.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. If both pans gain the same extra weight, do they remain balanced?
  2. If both pans are halved (you remove half the weight from each), do they still hang level?
  3. The puzzle on the slider had only one balance point. Could a similar puzzle have two answers — two different stone-weights that work?
  4. Could a balanced equation have no answer at all?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

The balance is preserved if you do the same thing to both sides. That is the only rule the chapter really uses. Whether the equation has one answer, no answer, or many answers depends on its shape — we’ll see all three cases.

Discover

Problem 1 — Undo addition

A box has x apples. You put in 5 more. Now there are 12. How many were there?

Hint 1 — a nudge Write the situation as an equation, then undo the +5.
Hint 2 — the technique x + 5 = 12. Subtract 5 from both sides.
Full solution x + 5 = 12 \;\Rightarrow\; x = 12 - 5 = 7. Seven apples.

Problem 2 — Undo multiplication

You have 3x rupees. You divide them equally among three friends; each gets 24 rupees. What is x?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each friend got \tfrac{3x}{3} = x, but actually rethink: total = 3x, three friends, so each gets x. We’re told each gets 24.
Full solution Each friend gets x = 24. Or read it differently: 3x = 72, so x = 24. (Multiplication is undone by division.)

Problem 3 — Two operations, one variable

Solve 2x + 3 = 11.

Hint 1 — a nudge x is wrapped first by multiplying by 2, then by adding 3. Unwrap in reverse order.
Hint 2 — the technique First subtract 3 from both sides; then divide by 2.
Full solution 2x + 3 = 11 \Rightarrow 2x = 8 \Rightarrow x = 4.

Problem 4 — Variable on both sides

Solve 5x - 7 = 3x + 9.

Hint 1 — a nudge Get all x-terms on one side and all numbers on the other. Subtract 3x from both sides first.
Hint 2 — the technique 5x - 3x - 7 = 9 \Rightarrow 2x - 7 = 9.
Full solution 5x - 7 = 3x + 9 \Rightarrow 2x - 7 = 9 \Rightarrow 2x = 16 \Rightarrow x = 8. Check: 5(8)-7 = 33 and 3(8)+9 = 33. Balanced.

Problem 5 — Brackets and fractions

Solve \dfrac{x+1}{2} = \dfrac{x-3}{3}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Clear the denominators by multiplying both sides by 6.
Hint 2 — the technique 3(x+1) = 2(x-3), then expand.
Full solution Multiply both sides by 6: 3(x+1) = 2(x-3) \Rightarrow 3x + 3 = 2x - 6 \Rightarrow x = -9. Check: LHS = \tfrac{-9+1}{2} = -4; RHS = \tfrac{-9-3}{3} = -4. ✓

Problem 6 — Word puzzle

A father is currently four times as old as his son. Twenty years from now, he will be only twice as old. Find their present ages.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let the son’s age now be x. Then the father’s age is 4x.
Hint 2 — the technique In 20 years: son is x+20, father is 4x+20. The condition 4x+20 = 2(x+20) gives the equation.
Full solution 4x + 20 = 2(x+20) = 2x + 40 \Rightarrow 2x = 20 \Rightarrow x = 10. Son is 10, father is 40. Check 20 years later: father 60, son 30 — yes, exactly twice.

You have just solved equations by always doing the same operation on both sides until x stood alone. That recipe is the whole chapter.

The word algebra comes from the title of the 9th-century textbook al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wal-muqābala by the Persian scholar al-Khwārizmī (c. 825 CE). The phrase al-jabr literally means “the restoring” — the act of moving a term from one side of an equation to the other and reversing its sign. So when you “transpose -3 across = and write +3”, you are performing al-Khwārizmī’s al-jabr — twelve hundred years on, and still doing the same dance.

A linear equation is a balance, not a sentence to be parsed. The equals sign means the two sides weigh the same. Every step you take is allowed for one reason only: it preserves the balance. Once you see this, you stop memorising “transposition rules” and just do the same thing to both sides — and the unknown drifts towards solitude on its own.

Name

A linear equation in one variable is an equation of the form ax + b = cx + d where a, b, c, d are rational numbers and a \neq c. It has exactly one solution, found by isolating x.

The rule of balance: if A = B, then for any rational k, A + k = B + k, A - k = B - k, kA = kB, and (if k \neq 0) A/k = B/k.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Move terms across with the sign reversed; Same act on both sides keeps the truth. When at last the unknown stands alone — Read it off; that is the proof.

The standard recipe:

  1. Clear fractions (multiply both sides by the LCM of denominators).
  2. Open brackets (distributive law).
  3. Move all x-terms to one side, all constants to the other.
  4. Divide by the coefficient of x.
  5. Always verify by substituting back.

Apply

Worked example

The sum of three consecutive integers is 72. Find them.

Competent solver’s path:

  1. Let the smallest integer be x. The next two are x+1 and x+2.
  2. Sum: x + (x+1) + (x+2) = 72.
  3. Combine: 3x + 3 = 72.
  4. Subtract 3: 3x = 69.
  5. Divide by 3: x = 23.

The integers are 23, 24, 25. Check: 23 + 24 + 25 = 72. ✓

Practice — Warm

W1. Solve 7x = 42.

Full solution x = 42 / 7 = 6.

W2. Solve x - 9 = -3.

Full solution Add 9 to both sides: x = 6.

W3. Solve \tfrac{x}{4} = -2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Multiply both sides by 4.
Full solution x = -8.

Practice — Working

P1. Solve 4x + 9 = 25 - 2x.

Hint 1 — a nudge Get all x on the left. Add 2x to both sides.
Full solution 6x + 9 = 25 \Rightarrow 6x = 16 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{8}{3}.

P2. Solve 5(x-2) = 3(x+4).

Hint 1 — a nudge Open the brackets first.
Full solution 5x - 10 = 3x + 12 \Rightarrow 2x = 22 \Rightarrow x = 11.

P3. Solve \dfrac{2x-3}{4} - \dfrac{x+1}{6} = \dfrac{1}{2}.

Hint 1 — a nudge LCM of denominators is 12. Multiply through.
Full solution Multiply by 12: 3(2x-3) - 2(x+1) = 6 \Rightarrow 6x - 9 - 2x - 2 = 6 \Rightarrow 4x - 11 = 6 \Rightarrow 4x = 17 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac{17}{4}.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A two-digit number has digit-sum 9. When the digits are swapped, the new number is 27 greater than the original. Find the number.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let the tens digit be t and the units digit be u. The number is 10t + u.
Hint 2 — the technique t + u = 9 and 10u + t = 10t + u + 27.
Full solution From the second equation: 9u - 9t = 27 \Rightarrow u - t = 3. Combined with t + u = 9: add to get 2u = 12 \Rightarrow u = 6 and t = 3. Original number: 36. Check: swap gives 63, which is 36 + 27. ✓

S2. Distance from town A to town B is travelled by a car at 60 km/h, and back at 40 km/h. The total trip takes 5 hours. Find the one-way distance.

Hint 1 — a nudge Time = distance ÷ speed. Let one-way distance be d km.
Hint 2 — the technique \tfrac{d}{60} + \tfrac{d}{40} = 5.
Full solution LCM(60, 40) = 120. Multiply through: 2d + 3d = 600 \Rightarrow 5d = 600 \Rightarrow d = 120 km. One-way distance is 120 km.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). The solution of 3(x-1) = 2x + 5 is (a) 5 (b) -2 (c) 8 (d) 2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Open the bracket, collect terms.
Full solution 3x - 3 = 2x + 5 \Rightarrow x = 8. Answer: (c).

2-mark (short answer). Solve and verify: 7y + 4 = -4y - 18.

Hint 1 — a nudge Add 4y to both sides; subtract 4.
Full solution 7y + 4y = -18 - 4 \Rightarrow 11y = -22 \Rightarrow y = -2. Check: LHS = 7(-2)+4 = -10; RHS = -4(-2) - 18 = 8 - 18 = -10. ✓

3-mark (short answer II). Solve \dfrac{3x+1}{16} + \dfrac{2x-3}{7} = \dfrac{x+3}{8} + \dfrac{3x-1}{14}.

Hint 1 — a nudge LCM of 16, 7, 8, 14 is 112. Multiply through.
Hint 2 — the technique After multiplying: 7(3x+1) + 16(2x-3) = 14(x+3) + 8(3x-1).
Full solution

7(3x+1) + 16(2x-3) = 14(x+3) + 8(3x-1) 21x + 7 + 32x - 48 = 14x + 42 + 24x - 8 53x - 41 = 38x + 34 15x = 75 \boxed{x = 5}.

Check: LHS = \tfrac{16}{16} + \tfrac{7}{7} = 1 + 1 = 2; RHS = \tfrac{8}{8} + \tfrac{14}{14} = 2. ✓

5-mark (long answer). Two pipes can together fill a tank in 12 minutes. Pipe A alone takes 20 minutes. (i) How long would pipe B alone take? (ii) If pipe A is shut after 5 minutes, how much longer will pipe B alone take to finish the tank?

Hint 1 — a nudge Think in rates. A fills at \tfrac{1}{20} tank/min. Together they fill at \tfrac{1}{12} tank/min.
Hint 2 — the technique Let B’s rate be \tfrac{1}{x}. Equation: \tfrac{1}{20} + \tfrac{1}{x} = \tfrac{1}{12}. Solve for x.
Full solution

(i) \tfrac{1}{20} + \tfrac{1}{x} = \tfrac{1}{12} \Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{x} = \tfrac{1}{12} - \tfrac{1}{20} = \tfrac{5 - 3}{60} = \tfrac{2}{60} = \tfrac{1}{30}.

So pipe B alone takes 30 minutes.

(ii) In 5 minutes both pipes together fill 5 \times \tfrac{1}{12} = \tfrac{5}{12} of the tank.

Remaining: 1 - \tfrac{5}{12} = \tfrac{7}{12}.

Time for B alone to finish \tfrac{7}{12} of the tank at rate \tfrac{1}{30} per min: \tfrac{7}{12} \div \tfrac{1}{30} = \tfrac{7}{12} \times 30 = \tfrac{210}{12} = 17.5 minutes.

Pipe B needs another 17.5 minutes (= 17 min 30 sec).

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 2.2 (work-rate puzzles) is a near-twin of the 5-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2020 Set 2, Q9 (“solve the equation with fractions on both sides”) matches the 3-mark item closely.

Common confusions

  1. “Transposing means flipping the sign.” It means moving and reversing. When you move +3 across =, it becomes -3. The reason is that you are subtracting 3 from both sides — the sign reversal is a consequence of the balance rule, not a magic move.
  2. “You can multiply both sides by x to clear it.” Sometimes — but if x might be 0 you can lose solutions or invent fake ones. Always pause when you multiply by something containing the unknown.
  3. “The check is optional.” It is not. A wrong sign somewhere will pass undetected unless you substitute back and read both sides.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A linear equation in one variable has the form ax + b = cx + d — and exactly one solution, found by isolating x.
  2. Whatever you do to one side of an equation, do to the other. Add, subtract, multiply (by non-zero), divide (by non-zero) — the balance rule is the only rule the chapter uses.
  3. Always substitute the answer back. The check is not optional — it is the proof that your moves preserved the balance.

Self-Assessment

Be honest with yourself.

  1. Could you explain to a friend, without notes, why moving a term across the = sign reverses its sign?
  2. Did you solve at least 5 of the 6 Discover problems and 5 of the 7 Practice problems without peeking past Hint 1?
  3. Can you turn a one-paragraph word problem into a linear equation?
  • 3 yes: move on to Understanding Quadrilaterals.
  • 2 yes: redo Stretch S2 and the 5-mark board item, then move on.
  • 0–1 yes: rebuild from Discover. Most stumbles here are about fraction handling — revisit Rational Numbers.

Where this matters

Research. “Solve for x” is the smallest possible mathematical problem, but the technique — apply inverse operations until the unknown is isolated — is the seed of the entire field of solving equations, from quadratics in Class 10 to differential equations in graduate analysis.

AI / ML. Training a neural network is, at its heart, solving millions of equations of the form gradient = 0 simultaneously. Gradient descent is the iterative cousin of “do the same thing to both sides”.
