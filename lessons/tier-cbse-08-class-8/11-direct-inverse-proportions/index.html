Ch 11 — Direct and Inverse Proportions

When two quantities march in step, and when they trade places

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you compute “3 for \text{₹}60, so 5 for ?”. If not, revisit Ratio and Proportion.
  2. Are you comfortable with \tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{c}{d} as the definition of two ratios being equal?
  3. If 4 workers take 3 days, do you have a guess (without computing) whether 8 workers take more or fewer days?
  4. Can you compute \tfrac{60 \times 5}{3} in your head?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Open your kitchen and find a recipe. “Tea for 4 people: 800 ml water.” You’re hosting 6 people instead.

The widget below scales the recipe.

viewof people = Inputs.range([1, 12], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "People"})
{
  const baseP = 4;
  const baseW = 800; // ml water
  const baseT = 8;   // g tea leaves
  const baseM = 200; // ml milk
  const factor = people / baseP;
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;line-height:1.6;">
    <table style="border-collapse:collapse;">
      <tr><th style="text-align:left;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">Ingredient</th><th style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">For 4</th><th style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">For ${people}</th></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">Water</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${baseW} ml</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${(baseW*factor).toFixed(0)} ml</td></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">Tea leaves</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${baseT} g</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${(baseT*factor).toFixed(1)} g</td></tr>
      <tr><td style="padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">Milk</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${baseM} ml</td><td style="text-align:right;padding:0.2rem 0.6rem;">${(baseM*factor).toFixed(0)} ml</td></tr>
    </table>
  </div>`;
}

Slide the people count from 4 to 12. Every ingredient triples together. They march in step. Now imagine a different problem: you have 24 litres of paint, enough to paint one wall in 3 days. With twice the painters, half the time. Two quantities, but moving in opposite directions.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. In the tea recipe, doubling the people doubles every ingredient. What stays the same? Look at “ml of water per person”, not just “ml of water”.
  2. With more painters, fewer days. Is “people × days” constant?
  3. If you draw “ingredient amount” on the vertical axis against “people” on the horizontal, what shape do you get? What if you plotted “days” against “painters”?
  4. Can you imagine a pair of quantities that go neither both up nor one up, one down?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

The recipe quantities have a constant ratio per person — that is direct proportion. The painters and the days have a constant product — that is inverse proportion. The chapter is built on spotting which of the two regimes a problem belongs to.

Discover

Problem 1 — Recipe scaling

If 5 kg of rice costs \text{₹}350, how much do 8 kg cost?

Hint 1 — a nudge Set up the proportion: \dfrac{\text{kg}_1}{\text{cost}_1} = \dfrac{\text{kg}_2}{\text{cost}_2}.
Hint 2 — the technique \tfrac{5}{350} = \tfrac{8}{x}. Cross-multiply.
Full solution 5x = 350 \times 8 = 2800 \Rightarrow x = 560. \text{₹}560.

Problem 2 — Painters and days

If 4 painters paint a wall in 9 days, how long will 6 painters take (working at the same pace)?

Hint 1 — a nudge More painters → fewer days. Inverse proportion.
Hint 2 — the technique Painters \times days $= $ constant. 4 \times 9 = 6 \times x.
Full solution x = \tfrac{4 \times 9}{6} = 6 days.

Problem 3 — Spot the regime

For each pair, decide whether the two quantities are in direct or inverse proportion (or neither):

  1. Number of pencils bought and total cost (price per pencil fixed).
  2. Speed of a car and time to cover a fixed distance.
  3. Age of a child and their height.
  4. Number of taps filling a tank and time to fill it.
Hint 1 — a nudge “Both go up together?” → direct. “One up, one down, with a constant product?” → inverse.
Full solution
  1. Direct. (b) Inverse. (c) Neither (correlated, but not in a fixed ratio or product). (d) Inverse.

Problem 4 — Constant of proportionality

If y is directly proportional to x, and y = 12 when x = 4, find y when x = 11.

Hint 1 — a nudge Direct: \tfrac{y}{x} is constant.
Hint 2 — the technique \tfrac{12}{4} = 3. So y = 3x for any x.
Full solution y = 3 \times 11 = 33.

Problem 5 — Constant product

If y is inversely proportional to x, and y = 8 when x = 5, find y when x = 20.

Hint 1 — a nudge Inverse: xy is constant.
Full solution xy = 5 \cdot 8 = 40. When x = 20, y = \tfrac{40}{20} = 2.

Problem 6 — Mixed problem

A car travels 480 km in 8 hours. (i) At the same speed, how far does it go in 5 hours? (ii) If the speed is doubled, how long does it take to go 480 km?

Hint 1 — a nudge
  1. is direct (more time → more distance, same speed). (ii) is inverse (more speed → less time, same distance).
Full solution

(i) Speed = 480 / 8 = 60 km/h. In 5 hours: 300 km.

(ii) New speed = 120 km/h. Time = 480 / 120 = 4 hours.

You have just seen — by looking at recipes, painters, taps, cars — two ways two quantities can be linked.

The Greek mathematician Eudoxus of Cnidus (c. 408 – c. 355 BCE) gave the world its first rigorous theory of proportion. Earlier Pythagoreans had assumed every two lengths share a common unit; Eudoxus’ definition (preserved in Book V of Euclid’s Elements) handled incommensurable magnitudes — like the diagonal and side of a square — by comparing ratios through all pairs of multiples. Two thousand years later, his definition still underlies the modern theory of real numbers. The everyday “y = kx” you write today rests on Eudoxus’ shoulders.

Direct and inverse proportion are the same shape seen from two angles: something is constant. In direct, the ratio y/x = k is constant; in inverse, the product xy = k is constant. Spotting the regime is just asking “what stays the same as the two quantities change?”.

Name

Two quantities x and y are in direct proportion if their ratio \tfrac{y}{x} is the same for every paired value. Equivalently: y = kx \qquad \text{for some constant } k > 0.

They are in inverse proportion if their product xy is the same for every pair. Equivalently: xy = k \quad\text{or}\quad y = \frac{k}{x} \qquad k > 0.

The constant k is called the constant of proportionality.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Direct: as one rises, the other rises in step; Their ratio is fixed — multiply or divide together. Inverse: as one rises, the other must fall; Their product is fixed — when in doubt, make them swap.

Apply

Worked example

20 workers can build a wall in 15 days. How many days will 25 workers take?

Competent solver’s path:

  1. More workers → fewer days. Inverse proportion.
  2. Constant product: workers \times days = 20 \times 15 = 300.
  3. With 25 workers: days = \tfrac{300}{25} = 12.

Answer: 12 days.

Practice — Warm

W1. If 7 pens cost \text{₹}140, find the cost of 11 pens.

Full solution Direct. Cost per pen = 140/7 = \text{₹}20. Eleven pens: \text{₹}220.

W2. If 12 cows finish a field in 5 days, how many days will 15 cows take?

Full solution Inverse. 12 \times 5 = 60 = 15 \times d \Rightarrow d = 4 days.

W3. y is directly proportional to x, and y = 21 when x = 7. What is y when x = 13?

Full solution k = 21/7 = 3. y = 3 \cdot 13 = 39.

Practice — Working

P1. A car uses 9 litres of petrol for 108 km. (i) How far on 15 litres? (ii) How many litres for 360 km?

Hint 1 — a nudge Direct. \tfrac{\text{km}}{\text{litres}} is constant.
Full solution 108 / 9 = 12 km/litre. (i) 15 \times 12 = 180 km. (ii) 360 / 12 = 30 litres.

P2. A school feeds 250 students with rations sufficient for 40 days. If 50 extra students join, how long will the same rations last?

Hint 1 — a nudge More mouths → fewer days. Inverse.
Full solution 250 \times 40 = 300 \times d \Rightarrow d = 10000/300 = \tfrac{100}{3} \approx 33.33 days.

P3. x and y are in inverse proportion. Some paired values:

x 4 5 \;?\; 20
y 30 \;?\; 10 \;?\;

Fill in the blanks.

Hint 1 — a nudge xy is constant. From 4 \times 30 = 120.
Full solution xy = 120. - x = 5 \Rightarrow y = 24. - y = 10 \Rightarrow x = 12. - x = 20 \Rightarrow y = 6.

Practice — Stretch

S1. 6 taps fill a tank in 12 minutes. How long would 9 taps of the same flow rate take?

Full solution Inverse. 6 \times 12 = 72 = 9 \times t \Rightarrow t = 8 minutes.

S2. Tea-bag manufacture: 25 workers produce 1500 bags in 6 days. How many bags will 30 workers produce in 8 days at the same rate?

Hint 1 — a nudge This is a combined proportion: bags \propto workers \times days.
Hint 2 — the technique Per-worker-per-day production: \tfrac{1500}{25 \times 6} = 10 bags. Then scale.
Full solution Rate per worker per day: \tfrac{1500}{25 \times 6} = 10 bags. With 30 workers in 8 days: 30 \times 8 \times 10 = 2400 bags.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). If y is directly proportional to x and y = 18 when x = 6, then y when x = 10 is (a) 30 (b) 24 (c) 20 (d) 36.

Full solution k = 3, so y = 30. Answer: (a).

2-mark (short answer). A truck covers a distance of 360 km in 5 hours. How long will it take to cover 450 km at the same speed?

Hint 1 — a nudge Direct (same speed). Time \propto distance.
Full solution \tfrac{360}{5} = \tfrac{450}{t} \Rightarrow t = \tfrac{450 \times 5}{360} = \tfrac{2250}{360} = 6.25 hours = 6 h 15 min.

3-mark (short answer II). 14 pumps working 8 hours a day empty a tank in 6 days. In how many days will 9 pumps working 12 hours a day empty the same tank? (Assume same pump capacity.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Total pump-hours is constant: pumps × hours-per-day × days = constant.
Hint 2 — the technique 14 \times 8 \times 6 = 9 \times 12 \times d.
Full solution

LHS = 672. d = \tfrac{672}{9 \times 12} = \tfrac{672}{108} = \tfrac{56}{9} \approx 6.22 days.

(Often expressed as 6\tfrac{2}{9} days.)

4-mark (long answer). A contractor planned to complete a road in 30 days with 40 men. After 10 days, 40\% of the work was done. (i) Find the original total man-days planned. (ii) Find the man-days already used. (iii) Find the man-days remaining and decide how many extra men he must hire to finish the job in the remaining 20 days.

Hint 1 — a nudge Plan first, then track what’s done, then size the rest.
Full solution

(i) Planned man-days = 40 \times 30 = 1200.

(ii) Used so far = 40 \times 10 = 400 man-days.

(iii) Work-completed should match man-days-spent in proportion if the plan is on track. But 400 man-days = \tfrac{400}{1200} = \tfrac{1}{3} of the plan, while only 40\% = \tfrac{2}{5} should have been done — wait, \tfrac{1}{3} = 33.3\%, but 40\% has been done. So progress is ahead of schedule by man-days spent.

Let’s redo with the 40\% figure as ground truth: - 400 man-days produced 40\% of the work. So 100\% of the work needs \tfrac{400}{0.4} = 1000 man-days total. - Already used: 400. Remaining: 600 man-days. - Available time: 20 days. Required workforce: \tfrac{600}{20} = 30 men. - Currently 40 men are on site. He can actually release 10 men — no extras needed.

(The contractor’s pace was faster than budgeted; the extra-men question turns into a release-men answer. This is the kind of trap CBSE long answers like to set.)

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 11.5 (combined work-rate problem with men, hours, and days) is a near-twin of the 3-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2017 Set 2, Q22 (“contractor with revised manpower”) matches the 4-mark item closely.

Common confusions

  1. “More always means direct proportion.” Not always. More workers, fewer days is also a “more of one” situation — but inverse. Look at whether the product is constant or the ratio is constant.
  2. “x and y correlated → x and y proportional.” No. “Older children tend to be taller” is a correlation, but it is neither direct nor inverse proportion.
  3. “In inverse, \tfrac{y}{x} is constant.” No — xy is constant. The two are different.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. Direct proportion: y = kx. The ratio is fixed; doubling one doubles the other.
  2. Inverse proportion: xy = k. The product is fixed; doubling one halves the other.
  3. The first move on every word problem is not to compute — it is to ask “are these two going up together, or trading places?” — and write down which constant rules.

Where this matters

Databases. Index lookup time is inversely proportional to memory spent on the index — the classic memory-vs-speed trade-off. Database designers tune $k = $ memory \times time to fit the workload.

AI / ML. Training time is directly proportional to the number of training examples (with the network fixed) — a fact every ML engineer rediscovers when their dataset doubles.

Research. The first rule of physics — Newton’s third law, Boyle’s law ($PV = $ constant), Coulomb’s law (\propto 1/r^2) — is always a proportionality. Recognising the regime is the first step in every modelling exercise.
