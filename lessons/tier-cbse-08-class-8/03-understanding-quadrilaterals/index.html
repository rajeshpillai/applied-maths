Ch 3 — Understanding Quadrilaterals

Four sides, one closed shape — and a whole family of names

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you tell a triangle from a quadrilateral on sight?
  2. Do you remember that the angles of a triangle add up to 180^\circ? If not, revisit Shapes and Angles.
  3. Can you measure an angle with a protractor?
  4. Do you know what parallel lines means — lines that never meet?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Cut four sticks from a sheet of stiff paper or use four pencils. Lay them on a table, end to end, so they form a closed loop with four corners. (Tape the corners loosely if you can — pinholes work.)

Push gently on one corner. Watch what happens. Push on the opposite corner. Watch again.

viewof angle = Inputs.range([20, 160], {value: 90, step: 1, label: "Bottom-left angle (degrees)"})
viewof side = Inputs.range([60, 180], {value: 120, step: 5, label: "Slant length"})
{
  const a = (angle * Math.PI) / 180;
  const b = side;
  const w = 200;
  const x1 = 50, y1 = 200;
  const x2 = x1 + w, y2 = y1;
  const x3 = x2 + b * Math.cos(Math.PI - a), y3 = y2 - b * Math.sin(a);
  const x4 = x1 + b * Math.cos(a), y4 = y1 - b * Math.sin(a);
  const angles = [a, Math.PI - a, a, Math.PI - a].map(r => Math.round((r * 180 / Math.PI) * 10) / 10);
  const sum = angles.reduce((s,x)=>s+x,0);
  return html`<svg width="380" height="240" style="background:#fafafa;border:1px solid #ddd;">
    <polygon points="${x1},${y1} ${x2},${y2} ${x3},${y3} ${x4},${y4}"
      fill="#bde0fe" stroke="#1d4ed8" stroke-width="2"/>
    <text x="190" y="225" text-anchor="middle" font-family="system-ui" font-size="13" fill="#444">
      angle sum &asymp; ${sum.toFixed(1)}&deg;
    </text>
  </svg>`;
}

Move both sliders. The shape changes — it becomes a tall slanted box, then a squashed one — but watch the angle sum at the bottom. Does anything about it stay constant?

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. As you slid the corner, the four angles each changed. Did their sum change?
  2. Two opposite sides of your loop look parallel. Does the loop have to have parallel sides for the corners to form?
  3. If two adjacent sides are equal in length, what does the shape start to look like?
  4. Could a four-sided closed shape have a reflex angle (more than 180^\circ) at one corner?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

The angle sum stays at 360^\circ no matter how you push. The chapter is built around that fact, plus a tree of special quadrilaterals — each defined by which sides or angles are made equal or parallel.

Discover

Problem 1 — Cut a square along a diagonal

Take a paper square and cut it along one diagonal. You now have two triangles. What can you say about each of them?

Hint 1 — a nudge Are the triangles right-angled? Are they identical?
Full solution

The cut goes corner to corner, so each triangle has the right-angle corner of the square. The two legs are equal (sides of the square). The two triangles are congruent right isoceles triangles. Each has angles 90^\circ, 45^\circ, 45^\circ.

The angles of the square split as: at the cut corners, 90^\circ = 45^\circ + 45^\circ. At the uncut corners, the 90^\circ stays intact. Sum: 90 + 45 + 45 + 90 + 45 + 45 = 360^\circ.

Problem 2 — Cut any quadrilateral

Now do it again with a random (non-square) four-sided paper shape. Cut along one diagonal. You get two triangles. What is the sum of all the angles in those two triangles?

Hint 1 — a nudge Each triangle’s angles add to 180^\circ. There are two of them.
Hint 2 — the technique 2 \times 180^\circ = 360^\circ. Now: those six triangle-angles together account for the four corners of the original quadrilateral — each quadrilateral corner is split into two pieces (or stays whole) by the diagonal.
Full solution Two triangles together have 2 \times 180 = 360^\circ of angle. When you reassemble into the quadrilateral, those angles regroup into the four corner angles. So the angle sum of any quadrilateral is 360^\circ.

Problem 3 — A missing angle

A quadrilateral has angles 80^\circ, 90^\circ, 110^\circ, and a fourth that you don’t know. Find the fourth angle.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the fact you just proved.
Full solution 80 + 90 + 110 + x = 360 \Rightarrow x = 80^\circ.

Problem 4 — Spot the parallelogram property

Draw a parallelogram (a four-sided shape with both pairs of opposite sides parallel). Measure both pairs of opposite sides.

Hint 1 — a nudge You don’t need to measure to predict.
Full solution Opposite sides of a parallelogram are equal in length as well as parallel. (Reason: drawing a diagonal makes two congruent triangles by ASA — alternate interior angles plus the shared diagonal.)

Problem 5 — Diagonals of a rhombus

A rhombus has all four sides equal. Draw both diagonals. What do you notice about them where they cross?

Hint 1 — a nudge Look at the angle they make. And the lengths from the crossing point to the corners.
Full solution The diagonals of a rhombus cross at right angles and bisect each other. (Each diagonal divides the rhombus into two congruent triangles which are mirror images.)

Problem 6 — Exterior angles

You walk around a closed quadrilateral path, turning at each corner. You return to your start. Through what total angle have you turned?

Hint 1 — a nudge Walking around any closed shape and ending up where you started, facing the same way, your total turn is one full circle.
Full solution 360^\circ. The sum of the exterior angles of any convex polygon — not just a quadrilateral — is 360^\circ.

You have just discovered the angle sum, the parallelogram side property, the rhombus diagonal property, and the exterior-angle fact — the four load-bearing facts of the chapter.

Euclid’s Elements (c. 300 BCE), the most-printed mathematics textbook in history, classified quadrilaterals in Book I, Definitions 22 — square, oblong (rectangle), rhombus, rhomboid (parallelogram), and trapezia — almost the same family tree you just met. Two thousand three hundred years later, the names live on; the only major change is that “trapezium” in modern Indian and British usage means “at least one pair of parallel sides”, whereas Euclid’s trapezia meant “no pairs parallel”. The geometry, though, is unchanged.

A quadrilateral is two triangles glued along a diagonal. That single move explains everything: angle sum is 2 \times 180^\circ = 360^\circ, parallelograms split into a pair of congruent triangles, rhombi into four right triangles, and rectangles into two equal right triangles. Whenever you are stuck on a quadrilateral problem, draw the diagonal — the triangles you already know will do the work.

Name

A quadrilateral is a closed plane figure bounded by four line segments.

Angle-sum property: the four interior angles of any quadrilateral add to 360^\circ.

Family tree (each name adds one more constraint):

  • Trapezium — at least one pair of parallel sides.
  • Parallelogram — both pairs of opposite sides parallel; opposite sides equal; opposite angles equal; diagonals bisect each other.
  • Rectangle — parallelogram + all angles 90^\circ; diagonals equal.
  • Rhombus — parallelogram + all sides equal; diagonals perpendicular and bisect each other.
  • Square — rectangle + rhombus; all of the above at once.
  • Kite — two pairs of adjacent sides equal; one diagonal bisects the other at 90^\circ.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Four-sided closed — three-sixty in all; Both pairs parallel, sides match, angles match, diagonals halve. Square the prince of the family — every property at once; Rhombus the dancer — perpendicular cross at the heart.

Apply

Worked example

In a parallelogram ABCD, \angle A = 70^\circ. Find the other three angles.

Competent solver’s path:

  1. Opposite angles of a parallelogram are equal: \angle C = \angle A = 70^\circ.
  2. Adjacent angles are supplementary (they sit on the same side of a transversal cutting two parallel lines): \angle B = 180 - 70 = 110^\circ.
  3. By the same rule, \angle D = 110^\circ.

Check: 70 + 110 + 70 + 110 = 360^\circ. ✓

Practice — Warm

W1. Three angles of a quadrilateral are 60^\circ, 70^\circ, 130^\circ. Find the fourth.

Full solution 60 + 70 + 130 + x = 360 \Rightarrow x = 100^\circ.

W2. True or false: every square is a rectangle.

Full solution True. A rectangle requires four right angles; a square has them plus equal sides. Every square satisfies the rectangle definition.

W3. True or false: every rhombus is a square.

Full solution False. A rhombus needs only equal sides; its angles need not be 90^\circ. A square is a rhombus, but not every rhombus is a square.

Practice — Working

P1. In a parallelogram PQRS, \angle P = 3\angle Q. Find all four angles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Adjacent angles of a parallelogram sum to 180^\circ.
Full solution \angle P + \angle Q = 180 and \angle P = 3\angle Q. 3\angle Q + \angle Q = 180 \Rightarrow \angle Q = 45^\circ, \angle P = 135^\circ. Opposite angles equal: \angle R = 45^\circ, \angle S = 135^\circ.

P2. A rhombus has a diagonal of length 24 cm and the other of length 10 cm. Find the side length.

Hint 1 — a nudge The diagonals bisect at right angles. Each side is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with half-diagonals as legs.
Full solution Half-diagonals: 12 cm and 5 cm. Side = \sqrt{12^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{144 + 25} = \sqrt{169} = 13 cm.

P3. The exterior angle at one vertex of a regular hexagon is what?

Hint 1 — a nudge Sum of exterior angles is 360^\circ, divided equally among 6 corners.
Full solution 360 / 6 = 60^\circ.

Practice — Stretch

S1. In quadrilateral ABCD, \angle A : \angle B : \angle C : \angle D = 1 : 2 : 3 : 4. Find each angle and identify any pair of parallel sides if you can.

Hint 1 — a nudge Total = 1+2+3+4 = 10 parts. Total angle = 360^\circ.
Full solution

Each part = 36^\circ. Angles: 36^\circ, 72^\circ, 108^\circ, 144^\circ.

Adjacent pairs: \angle A + \angle B = 108, \angle B + \angle C = 180^\circ, \angle C + \angle D = 252, \angle D + \angle A = 180^\circ.

Two adjacent sums equal 180^\circ: that means BC \parallel AD (co-interior angles supplementary). It is a trapezium.

S2. A kite has two pairs of consecutive equal sides AB = AD = 5 cm and CB = CD = 12 cm, with \angle B = \angle D = 90^\circ. Find the length of diagonal AC.

Hint 1 — a nudge Triangle ABC is right-angled at B.
Full solution AC = \sqrt{AB^2 + BC^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13 cm.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). Sum of the interior angles of a polygon with 7 sides is (a) 720^\circ (b) 900^\circ (c) 1080^\circ (d) 540^\circ.

Hint 1 — a nudge Formula: (n-2) \times 180^\circ.
Full solution (7-2) \times 180 = 5 \times 180 = 900^\circ. Answer: (b).

2-mark (short answer). Two adjacent angles of a parallelogram are in the ratio 2:3. Find each angle of the parallelogram.

Full solution Adjacent angles sum to 180^\circ. Parts: 2+3 = 5. Each part = 36^\circ. Angles: 72^\circ, 108^\circ, 72^\circ, 108^\circ.

3-mark (short answer II). ABCD is a parallelogram. The bisectors of \angle A and \angle B meet at P. Show that \angle APB = 90^\circ.

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle A + \angle B = 180^\circ.
Hint 2 — the technique In triangle APB: \angle PAB = \tfrac{1}{2}\angle A and \angle PBA = \tfrac{1}{2}\angle B.
Full solution

\angle PAB + \angle PBA = \tfrac{1}{2}(\angle A + \angle B) = \tfrac{1}{2}(180^\circ) = 90^\circ.

In triangle APB, angles sum to 180^\circ: \angle APB = 180^\circ - 90^\circ = 90^\circ. ✓

4-mark (long answer). ABCD is a quadrilateral with \angle A = 100^\circ, \angle B = 80^\circ, \angle C = 100^\circ. (i) Find \angle D. (ii) Show that AB \parallel DC. (iii) What special quadrilateral is ABCD at minimum?

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the angle sum, then look for supplementary co-interior pairs.
Full solution

(i) \angle D = 360 - (100 + 80 + 100) = 80^\circ.

(ii) Consider sides AB and DC with transversal BC. The co-interior angles are \angle B = 80^\circ and \angle C = 100^\circ, summing to 180^\circ. So AB \parallel DC.

(iii) Consider sides AD and BC with transversal AB. Co-interior angles \angle A + \angle B = 180^\circ as well. So AD \parallel BC.

Both pairs of opposite sides are parallel — ABCD is at minimum a parallelogram.

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 3.4 (angle bisectors in a parallelogram) is a near-twin of the 3-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2018 Set 1, Q11 (“if three angles of a quadrilateral are given…”) matches the 4-mark item closely.

Common confusions

  1. “A square is not a rectangle.” It is. The hierarchy is inclusive — every square satisfies the rectangle definition (four right angles).
  2. “All quadrilaterals with equal diagonals are rectangles.” No. An isoceles trapezium also has equal diagonals. The full criterion for a rectangle is parallelogram with one right angle (or parallelogram with equal diagonals).
  3. “Diagonals of a kite bisect each other.” Only one of them gets bisected (by the axis of symmetry). The other does the bisecting.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A quadrilateral is a closed plane figure with four sides — and the four interior angles always sum to 360^\circ, no matter the shape.
  2. The family is a hierarchy: trapezium \subset parallelogram \subset rectangle \cap rhombus = square. Each name adds one more constraint on sides or angles.
  3. When stuck, draw a diagonal. It splits any quadrilateral into two triangles — and the triangle properties you already know will solve the problem.

Where this matters

Game development. Every flat surface in a 2D game — platforms, tile maps, UI panels — is a rectangle or quadrilateral mesh. Collision detection between rotated rectangles uses the diagonal and side properties of this chapter.

Research. Tessellations of the plane by quadrilaterals are at the heart of finite-element methods, the numerical workhorse for solving partial differential equations. The mesh quality is judged by how close each cell is to a square — the prince of the family.
