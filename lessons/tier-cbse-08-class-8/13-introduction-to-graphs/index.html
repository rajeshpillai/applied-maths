Ch 13 — Introduction to Graphs

Two number lines crossed — a place for every pair of numbers

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you read a thermometer or a ruler? If not, revisit The Number Line.
  2. Have you ever made a bar chart by hand? If not, revisit Data and Average.
  3. Do you know what positive and negative mean on a number line?
  4. Can you describe a place in your school using “two streets across, then one street up”?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Find a sheet of squared graph paper, or imagine one. Draw a horizontal line across the middle and a vertical line down the middle. Where they cross, write 0. To the right of the cross, mark 1, 2, 3, \ldots. Going up, mark 1, 2, 3, \ldots.

Pick a place on the paper. How would you tell a friend where it is, just using two numbers?

The slider below shows the same idea.

viewof px = Inputs.range([0, 10], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "Across (x)"})
viewof py = Inputs.range([0, 10], {value: 3, step: 1, label: "Up (y)"})
{
  const k = 30;
  const margin = 25;
  const cells = [];
  for (let i = 0; i <= 10; i++) {
    cells.push(`<line x1="${margin+i*k}" y1="${margin}" x2="${margin+i*k}" y2="${margin+10*k}" stroke="#eee"/>`);
    cells.push(`<line x1="${margin}" y1="${margin+i*k}" x2="${margin+10*k}" y2="${margin+i*k}" stroke="#eee"/>`);
  }
  cells.push(`<line x1="${margin}" y1="${margin}" x2="${margin}" y2="${margin+10*k}" stroke="#333" stroke-width="1.5"/>`);
  cells.push(`<line x1="${margin}" y1="${margin+10*k}" x2="${margin+10*k}" y2="${margin+10*k}" stroke="#333" stroke-width="1.5"/>`);
  const cx = margin + px*k;
  const cy = margin + (10-py)*k;
  cells.push(`<line x1="${margin}" y1="${cy}" x2="${cx}" y2="${cy}" stroke="#1d4ed8" stroke-dasharray="3,3"/>`);
  cells.push(`<line x1="${cx}" y1="${margin+10*k}" x2="${cx}" y2="${cy}" stroke="#1d4ed8" stroke-dasharray="3,3"/>`);
  cells.push(`<circle cx="${cx}" cy="${cy}" r="6" fill="#dc2626"/>`);
  cells.push(`<text x="${cx+10}" y="${cy-6}" font-size="13" font-family="system-ui">(${px}, ${py})</text>`);
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;">
    <svg width="${margin*2 + 10*k}" height="${margin*2 + 10*k}" style="background:#fafafa;">
      ${cells.join("")}
    </svg>
  </div>`;
}

Slide the two numbers. The red dot moves. Two numbers determine a position. The first says across; the second says up. They are not interchangeable.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. If you swap the two numbers — point (4, 3) becomes (3, 4) — do you arrive at the same place?
  2. The point (0, 5) sits on the up-line. The point (5, 0) sits on the across-line. The point (0, 0) sits where?
  3. What do you call the point a temperature graph touches when the temperature is 0\,°C?
  4. Can you imagine a chart where every dot is the same height? Could you describe that chart in just one sentence?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

A horizontal line is “all dots have the same up-value”. A vertical line is “all dots have the same across-value”. Lines that climb left-to-right are the next step — and they encode the relationship y = mx + b. The chapter starts with bar charts and ends with straight-line graphs of linear equations.

Discover

Problem 1 — Plot a point

On graph paper (or in your head), plot the point (3, 4). Then plot (4, 3). Are they the same point?

Hint 1 — a nudge First number = how far right; second number = how far up.
Full solution (3, 4) is 3 right and 4 up. (4, 3) is 4 right and 3 up. They are different points. Order matters.

Problem 2 — Read a bar chart

A bar chart of fruit sales (in kg) shows: apple 30, banana 50, orange 20, mango 40. (i) Which fruit sold most? (ii) Which sold least? (iii) Total kg sold?

Hint 1 — a nudge Find the tallest and shortest bars; sum the heights.
Full solution (i) Banana (50 kg). (ii) Orange (20 kg). (iii) Total = 30 + 50 + 20 + 40 = 140 kg.

Problem 3 — A linear graph

Plot the points (0, 1), (1, 3), (2, 5), (3, 7) on graph paper. What shape do they form? Can you spot a rule connecting x and y?

Hint 1 — a nudge Check: when x goes up by 1, what happens to y?
Hint 2 — the technique x goes up by 1, y goes up by 2. So y = 2x + ?. Plug in x = 0.
Full solution

The four points lie on a straight line. Each unit right takes you 2 units up. At x = 0, y = 1. So y = 2x + 1.

(All four points satisfy y = 2x + 1. Verify: 2 \cdot 3 + 1 = 7. ✓)

Problem 4 — Read a linear graph

A graph shows distance against time for a car: at t = 0 hour the distance is 0 km; at t = 2 the distance is 120 km; at t = 4 it is 240 km. (i) What is the speed? (ii) How far at t = 7?

Hint 1 — a nudge Speed = distance change / time change. (Constant speed → straight line through origin.)
Full solution

(i) Speed = 120 / 2 = 60 km/h.

(ii) Distance = 60 \times 7 = 420 km.

Problem 5 — Fill in the table

If y = 3x - 1, complete the table and plot the points.

x 0 1 2 3
y ? ? ? ?
Full solution
x 0 1 2 3
y -1 2 5 8
When plotted, all four points lie on a straight line. They all satisfy y = 3x - 1.

Problem 6 — Pie vs bar vs line

You want to display: (i) percentage marks of one student in different subjects, (ii) population of a town from year 1980 to 2020, (iii) the share of expenses by category in a household budget. For each, which of bar, line, pie is the most natural choice?

Hint 1 — a nudge Categories vs over-time vs share-of-whole.
Full solution
  1. bar chart (categories: subjects). (ii) line graph (continuous time trend). (iii) pie chart (parts of a whole).

You have just plotted points, read straight-line graphs, computed slope from two readings, and matched chart-type to data type. That is the chapter.

The idea of pinning a point in the plane with two numbers was published in 1637 by René Descartes as an appendix — La Géométrie — to his philosophical Discours de la méthode. Legend (probably embellished) says Descartes was lying ill in bed watching a fly crawl across the ceiling, and realised its position was perfectly fixed by its distance from two adjacent walls. Whatever the truth, his appendix married algebra to geometry: every equation became a curve, every curve an equation. The Cartesian plane on your graph paper is named for him.

The coordinate plane is the place where algebra and geometry meet. An equation like y = 2x + 1 is a rule; its graph is a picture; the picture is a straight line precisely because the rule is linear. The slope is the rule’s “pace of change”, and the y-intercept is its “starting point”. Two numbers, one line.

Name

The Cartesian plane is two number lines crossed at right angles at the origin (0, 0). The horizontal line is the x-axis; the vertical line is the y-axis. Every point is named by an ordered pair (x, y) — first x across, then y up.

A linear graph is a straight line. The graph of y = mx + c is a straight line; m is the slope (how much y changes per unit of x) and c is the y-intercept (the y-value at x = 0).

Common graph types: - Bar chart — separate categories. - Histogram — continuous numerical bins (no gaps). - Pie chart — share of a whole. - Line graph — change over a continuous variable, especially time.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Across is x, up is y; the cross is the origin’s home. Two numbers, in order — one place; reverse them, you’ve roamed. Steps right by one, jumps up by m — slope, and the line is born: y = m x + c — c is where it cuts the upright at noon.

Apply

Worked example

The cost of 1 kg of a vegetable is \text{₹}40. (i) Make a table showing cost for 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 kg. (ii) Draw a graph of cost (on y-axis) against weight (on x-axis). (iii) Use the graph to find the cost of 3.5 kg.

Competent solver’s path:

(i)

Weight (kg) 1 2 3 4 5
Cost (₹) 40 80 120 160 200

(ii) All five points lie on the line y = 40 x, passing through the origin.

(iii) At x = 3.5, y = 40 \cdot 3.5 = \text{₹}140. Reading the graph at 3.5 kg gives the same answer.

Practice — Warm

W1. Plot the points A(2, 5), B(0, 4), C(-1, 3), D(3, 0) and name the axes each lies on, if any.

Hint 1 — a nudge y-axis: x = 0. x-axis: y = 0.
Full solution A is in the interior. B lies on the y-axis. C is in the interior (this lesson concentrates on the first quadrant — negative coordinates appear in Class 9). D lies on the x-axis.

W2. From the table y = 2x for x \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4\}, plot the points. Are they collinear?

Full solution (0,0), (1,2), (2,4), (3,6), (4,8) — all on the line y = 2x. Yes, collinear.

W3. A line passes through (0, 5) and (2, 9). Find its slope.

Hint 1 — a nudge Slope = (change in y) / (change in x).
Full solution \dfrac{9 - 5}{2 - 0} = \dfrac{4}{2} = 2.

Practice — Working

P1. A car uses petrol at \text{₹}100 per litre. Make a table showing cost for 1 to 5 litres, and draw the graph.

Hint 1 — a nudge Cost $= 100 $ litres.
Full solution
Litres 1 2 3 4 5
Cost (₹) 100 200 300 400 500
The graph is a straight line through the origin with slope 100.

P2. A bar chart of monthly savings (in \text{₹}) of a family for 5 months: Jan 2000, Feb 1500, Mar 2500, Apr 1800, May 3000. Find the (i) total saved, (ii) average saving per month.

Full solution

(i) Total = 2000 + 1500 + 2500 + 1800 + 3000 = \text{₹}10800.

(ii) Average = 10800 / 5 = \text{₹}2160.

P3. Plot A(1, 1), B(1, 4), C(5, 4), D(5, 1). What shape is ABCD? Find its area.

Hint 1 — a nudge The two pairs of opposite sides are vertical and horizontal.
Full solution ABCD is a rectangle (sides 4 and 3 at right angles). Width |AD| = 5 - 1 = 4. Height |AB| = 4 - 1 = 3. Area = 4 \times 3 = 12 square units.

Practice — Stretch

S1. The graph of y = mx passes through (4, 12). Find m, and the value of y when x = 9.

Hint 1 — a nudge Substitute the point. 12 = 4m.
Full solution m = 3. So y = 3x. At x = 9: y = 27.

S2. A water tank holds 200 litres. A tap fills it at 5 litres per minute. (i) Make a table showing water in the tank at t = 0, 5, 10, 15, 20 minutes. (ii) Plot the line graph. (iii) After how many minutes will the tank be full?

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume V = 5t until full.
Full solution
t (min) 0 5 10 15 20
V (L) 0 25 50 75 100

Line through origin, slope 5.

Tank full when V = 200 \Rightarrow t = 200 / 5 = 40 minutes.

Full solution Answer: (a) origin.

Hint 1 — a nudge The two points share an x-coordinate. The line is vertical.
Full solution Distance = |7 - 3| = 4 units.

3-mark (short answer II). Draw the graph of y = 2x + 3 for x = -1, 0, 1, 2. Use the graph to find y when x = 1.5.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute the four y-values, plot, then read between the third and fourth points.
Full solution
x -1 0 1 2

All four points lie on the same straight line.

4-mark (long answer). A factory’s monthly production for the first 6 months of 2024 (in thousand units) was: Jan 20, Feb 24, Mar 32, Apr 30, May 40, Jun 44. (i) Draw a line graph. (ii) Find the total production for the half-year. (iii) In which month was the increase from the previous month largest?

Hint 1 — a nudge Plot months on x-axis and production on y-axis. Compute differences between consecutive months.
Full solution

(i) Plot the six points (1, 20), (2, 24), (3, 32), (4, 30), (5, 40), (6, 44) and join with line segments.

(ii) Total = 20 + 24 + 32 + 30 + 40 + 44 = 190 thousand units = 1{,}90{,}000 units.

(iii) Differences: Feb +4, Mar +8, Apr -2, May +10, Jun +4. Largest jump: May (+10).

Common confusions

  1. “(3, 4) is the same as (4, 3).” No. The first number is across (x); the second is up (y). Order matters absolutely.
  2. “A bar chart and a histogram are the same.” Bar charts have gaps between bars (separate categories). Histograms have no gaps (continuous bins). The kind of data on the x-axis decides which.
  3. “A line graph means values are continuous between data points.” Joining data points with line segments is a visual aid, not always a claim that the data really took those in-between values. (E.g., monthly sales — there is no “1.5 March”.)

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. The coordinate plane names every point with an ordered pair (x, y) — across first, up second. Swap the order and you land somewhere else.
  2. A linear rule y = mx + c has a straight-line graph. The slope m tells you the rise per step right; the y-intercept c tells you where the line crosses the vertical axis.
  3. Different data want different charts: bar for categories, histogram for continuous bins, pie for shares of a whole, line for change over time.
