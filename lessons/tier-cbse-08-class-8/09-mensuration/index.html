Ch 9 — Mensuration

Measure the surface, fill the inside — areas and volumes for the shapes you actually meet

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Do you remember the area of a rectangle (l \times b) and a triangle (\tfrac{1}{2} \times b \times h)? If not, revisit Area, Perimeter, Volume.
  2. Can you tell a rhombus from a trapezium? If not, revisit Understanding Quadrilaterals.
  3. Do you know that \pi \approx 3.14 and that the circumference of a circle is 2 \pi r?
  4. Can you compute 7^2 in your head?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Take a sheet of paper and cut a trapezium — a four-sided shape with exactly one pair of parallel sides. Cut a second copy and rotate it 180^\circ. Slide them together so the parallel sides line up. What shape do you get?

The widget below shows it. Slide a and b (the two parallel side lengths) and h (the height).

viewof aTrap = Inputs.range([2, 8], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "Top side a"})
viewof bTrap = Inputs.range([2, 10], {value: 7, step: 1, label: "Bottom side b"})
viewof hTrap = Inputs.range([2, 6], {value: 3, step: 1, label: "Height h"})
{
  const a=aTrap, b=bTrap, h=hTrap;
  const k=24;
  const offX = 30;
  const x1 = offX + (b-a)*k/2;
  const trap1 = `M ${offX},${h*k} L ${offX+b*k},${h*k} L ${x1+a*k},0 L ${x1},0 Z`;
  const offX2 = offX + b*k + 30;
  const x2 = offX2 + (b-a)*k/2;
  const trap2 = `M ${offX2+b*k},0 L ${offX2},0 L ${x2},${h*k} L ${x2+a*k},${h*k} Z`;
  const para = `M 0,${h*k+50} L ${(a+b)*k},${h*k+50} L ${(a+b)*k - (b-a)*k/2 + a*k - a*k},${50} L ${(b-a)*k/2 + 0 + a*k - a*k + 0},${50} Z`;
  const px=20, py=h*k+30;
  const paraPath = `M ${px},${py+h*k} L ${px+(a+b)*k},${py+h*k} L ${px+(a+b)*k - (b-a)*k/2},${py} L ${px + (b-a)*k/2},${py} Z`;
  const area = (a+b)*h/2;
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;">
    <svg width="${(a+b)*k+100}" height="${2*h*k+90}">
      <path d="${trap1}" fill="#bde0fe" stroke="#1d4ed8"/>
      <path d="${trap2}" fill="#fed7aa" stroke="#c2410c"/>
      <path d="${paraPath}" fill="#bbf7d0" stroke="#15803d"/>
    </svg>
    <div style="color:#444;margin-top:0.4rem;">
      Two trapezia joined make a parallelogram of base (a+b) = ${a+b},
      height ${h}. Area = ${(a+b)*h}. So one trapezium has area
      <b>(a+b)h/2 = ${area}</b>.
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

Two trapezia, rotated and pasted, become a parallelogram with base a+b and height h. So one trapezium has area \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h. That is the chapter’s first formula — proved by your scissors.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. Why does the trapezium-pair give a parallelogram, not a rectangle?
  2. The same trick with two triangles also gives a parallelogram. What does that say about a triangle’s area?
  3. A circle isn’t made of straight lines. How would you go about measuring its area?
  4. A box (a cuboid) has six rectangular faces. What is the relation between the surface area and the volume? Is there a simple one?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

The chapter has three families of formulas: areas of plane figures (trapezium, rhombus, polygon), surface areas of solids (cuboid, cube, cylinder), and volumes of those solids. You will derive several from the rectangle / triangle areas you already trust.

Discover

Problem 1 — Area of a trapezium

A trapezium has parallel sides of 7 cm and 5 cm and the perpendicular distance between them is 4 cm. Find its area.

Hint 1 — a nudge You just derived the formula in Touch.
Full solution A = \tfrac{1}{2}(7 + 5) \times 4 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 12 \cdot 4 = 24 cm^2.

Problem 2 — Area of a rhombus

A rhombus has diagonals d_1 = 10 cm and d_2 = 24 cm. Find its area.

Hint 1 — a nudge The diagonals split the rhombus into four congruent right triangles. Add their areas.
Hint 2 — the technique Each small triangle has legs \tfrac{d_1}{2} and \tfrac{d_2}{2}. Four of them.
Full solution

Area = 4 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{d_1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{d_2}{2} = \tfrac{d_1 d_2}{2} = \tfrac{10 \cdot 24}{2} = 120 cm^2.

(General formula: area of rhombus = \tfrac{1}{2} d_1 d_2.)

Problem 3 — Surface area of a cuboid

Find the total surface area of a cuboid with l = 8, b = 5, h = 3 cm.

Hint 1 — a nudge Six faces, three different pairs.
Full solution Two faces of l \times b, two of b \times h, two of l \times h. \text{TSA} = 2(lb + bh + lh) = 2(40 + 15 + 24) = 2 \cdot 79 = 158 cm^2.

Problem 4 — Surface area of a cube

A cube has edge 7 cm. Find its surface area.

Hint 1 — a nudge Six congruent square faces.
Full solution \text{TSA} = 6 \cdot 7^2 = 6 \cdot 49 = 294 cm^2.

Problem 5 — Volume of a cuboid and a cylinder

  1. Find the volume of a cuboid with sides 10 cm, 4 cm, 3 cm.
  2. Find the volume of a cylinder of radius 7 cm and height 10
  1. Take \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.
Hint 1 — a nudge Cuboid: V = lbh. Cylinder: V = \pi r^2 h.
Full solution

(i) V = 10 \cdot 4 \cdot 3 = 120 cm^3.

(ii) V = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 7^2 \cdot 10 = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 49 \cdot 10 = 22 \cdot 70 = 1540 cm^3.

Problem 6 — Curved surface of a cylinder

A cylinder has radius r = 3.5 cm and height h = 10 cm. Find its curved surface area (just the side, not the top and bottom). Take \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Imagine slitting the cylinder along its height and unrolling it. The unrolled side is a rectangle of width 2 \pi r (the circumference) and height h.
Full solution

CSA = 2 \pi r h = 2 \cdot \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 3.5 \cdot 10 = 2 \cdot \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot \tfrac{35}{10} \cdot 10 = 220 cm^2.

(Total surface area of a cylinder includes the two circular ends: \text{TSA} = 2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^2 = 2 \pi r (h + r).)

You have just measured plane areas and the surface and volume of two solids — using rectangles, circles, and the trapezium trick.

The word mensuration (“measuring”) and most of its formulas trace back to Heron of Alexandria (c. 60 CE), whose Metrica compiled the area and volume rules of antiquity into one book — including a triangle’s area from just its three sides (you meet Heron’s formula in Class 9). Two centuries earlier, Archimedes had already worked out the volume of a sphere is exactly two-thirds the volume of the cylinder it just fits inside — a result he was so proud of, he asked for the picture to be carved on his tomb.

Every solid in this chapter is a cylinder in disguise: pile up identical cross-sections of area A to height h and the volume is V = A \times h. A cuboid is the special case where the cross-section is a rectangle; a cylinder is the case where it is a circle. The same one idea covers them all.

Name

Plane figures.

Shape Area
Trapezium with parallel sides a, b, height h \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h
Rhombus with diagonals d_1, d_2 \tfrac{1}{2} d_1 d_2
General quadrilateral (split by diagonal of length d with offsets h_1, h_2) \tfrac{1}{2} d (h_1 + h_2)

Solids.

Solid Lateral / curved surface Total surface area Volume
Cuboid (l, b, h) 2(l + b)h 2(lb + bh + lh) lbh
Cube (edge a) 4a^2 6a^2 a^3
Cylinder (r, h) 2 \pi r h 2 \pi r (h + r) \pi r^2 h

One litre = 1000 cm^3 = 1000 ml.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Trapezium: parallels added, halved, times height. Rhombus: diagonals’ product, halved, comes through. Cuboid: pair-product summed, doubled, the surface gleams; Cylinder: roll it flat — a rectangle of 2 \pi r by height.

Apply

Worked example

A field is in the shape of a trapezium with parallel sides 25 m and 35 m, separated by 20 m. (i) What is the area of the field? (ii) If the field is to be levelled at a cost of \text{₹}12 per m^2, what is the total cost?

Competent solver’s path:

  1. A = \tfrac{1}{2}(25 + 35) \times 20 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 60 \cdot 20 = 600 m^2.
  2. Cost = 600 \times 12 = \text{₹}7200.

Practice — Warm

W1. Area of a trapezium with parallel sides 9 cm and 11 cm and height 6 cm.

Full solution \tfrac{1}{2}(9+11) \cdot 6 = 60 cm^2.

W2. Surface area of a cube of edge 5 cm.

Full solution 6 \cdot 25 = 150 cm^2.

W3. Volume of a cylinder, r = 7 cm, h = 5 cm. Use \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.

Full solution V = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 49 \cdot 5 = 22 \cdot 35 = 770 cm^3.

Practice — Working

P1. A rhombus has area 96 cm^2 and one diagonal 12 cm. Find the other diagonal.

Full solution \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 12 \cdot d_2 = 96 \Rightarrow d_2 = 16 cm.

P2. A closed box has dimensions 40 \times 25 \times 15 cm. Find the area of metal sheet needed to make it.

Hint 1 — a nudge “Closed” → all six faces. Use TSA of cuboid.
Full solution 2(lb + bh + lh) = 2(40 \cdot 25 + 25 \cdot 15 + 40 \cdot 15) = 2(1000 + 375 + 600) = 2 \cdot 1975 = 3950 cm^2.

P3. An open cylindrical tank (\pi = \tfrac{22}{7}) has radius 1.4 m and height 3 m. (i) Find its capacity in litres. (ii) Find the area of metal needed to make it (open — only side and bottom).

Hint 1 — a nudge Capacity = volume. 1 m^3 = 1000 litres.
Full solution

(i) V = \pi r^2 h = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 1.96 \cdot 3 = \tfrac{22 \cdot 5.88}{7} = \tfrac{129.36}{7} \approx 18.48 m^3. 1 m^3 = 1000 litres, so capacity \approx 18{,}480 litres.

(ii) Metal area = CSA + bottom area = 2 \pi r h + \pi r^2 = \pi r (2h + r). = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 1.4 \cdot (6 + 1.4) = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 1.4 \cdot 7.4 = \tfrac{22 \cdot 1.4 \cdot 7.4}{7} = \tfrac{227.92}{7} \approx 32.56 m^2.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A cuboidal water tank measures 6 m \times 5 m \times 4.5 m. How many cubical buckets of edge 30 cm can be filled completely from this tank?

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume of tank in cm^3, divided by volume of one bucket in cm^3. Mind the units.
Full solution Tank volume = 600 \times 500 \times 450 = 1{,}35{,}000{,}000 cm^3. Bucket volume = 30^3 = 27{,}000 cm^3. Number of buckets = \tfrac{1{,}35{,}000{,}000}{27{,}000} = 5000.

S2. A solid metallic cylinder of diameter 14 cm and height 10 cm is melted and recast into smaller cubes of edge 1 cm. How many cubes are formed? (Take \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Cylinder volume / cube volume.
Full solution V_{\text{cyl}} = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 7^2 \cdot 10 = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 49 \cdot 10 = 1540 cm^3. V_{\text{cube}} = 1 cm^3. Number of cubes = 1540.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). Surface area of a cube of edge a is (a) a^2 (b) 3a^2 (c) 6a^2 (d) a^3.

Full solution Six congruent square faces. Answer: (c).

2-mark (short answer). Find the area of a quadrilateral whose diagonal is 26 cm and the perpendicular offsets to the two opposite vertices are 6 cm and 9 cm.

Hint 1 — a nudge Area of a general quadrilateral split by a diagonal: \tfrac{1}{2} d (h_1 + h_2).
Full solution A = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 26 \cdot (6 + 9) = 13 \cdot 15 = 195 cm^2.

3-mark (short answer II). A cylindrical pipe has inner radius 3.5 cm, outer radius 4 cm, and length 20 cm. Find the volume of the pipe material. (Take \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume of material = volume of outer cylinder − volume of inner cylinder.
Full solution V = \pi (R^2 - r^2) h = \tfrac{22}{7}(16 - 12.25) \cdot 20 = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 3.75 \cdot 20 = \tfrac{22 \cdot 75}{7} = \tfrac{1650}{7} \approx 235.71 cm^3.

5-mark (long answer). The water in a rectangular pool measures 80 m \times 30 m and is 5 m deep. (i) Find the volume of water in cubic metres and in litres. (ii) The pool is to be drained through a cylindrical pipe of radius 35 cm at 2 m/s. How long does it take? (Take \pi = \tfrac{22}{7}.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume per second through pipe = pipe cross-section × speed. Then time = total volume / volume per second.
Full solution

(i) Volume = 80 \times 30 \times 5 = 12000 m^3 = 12000 \times 1000 = 1{,}20{,}00{,}000 litres.

(ii) Convert pipe radius: r = 35 cm = 0.35 m. Cross-section = \pi r^2 = \tfrac{22}{7} \cdot 0.1225 = \tfrac{22 \cdot 0.1225}{7} = \tfrac{2.695}{7} = 0.385 m^2.

Volume drained per second = 0.385 \times 2 = 0.77 m^3/s.

Total time = \tfrac{12000}{0.77} \approx 15{,}584 seconds \approx 4 hours 20 minutes.

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 9.5 (drain a tank through a cylindrical pipe) is a near-twin of the 5-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2018 Set 3, Q19 (“hollow cylindrical pipe — volume of material”) matches the 3-mark item closely.

Common confusions

  1. “1 m^2 = 100 cm^2.” No — 1 m = 100 cm, so 1 m^2 = 100 \times 100 = 10{,}000 cm^2. Watch the units.
  2. “1 m^3 = 1000 cm^3.” No — 1 m^3 = 100^3 = 10^6 cm^3. But 1 litre = 1000 cm^3, hence 1 m^3 = 1000 litres.
  3. “Surface area and volume scale the same way.” They don’t. Doubling the edge multiplies area by 4 and volume by 8. (The square-cube law.)

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. The trapezium area, \tfrac{1}{2}(a+b)h, falls out of pasting two trapezia into a parallelogram — a scissors proof, not a memorised formula.
  2. Every solid in the chapter splits into a base (area A) and a height h: volume is V = Ah, total surface area is the sum of all face areas.
  3. Watch units. 1 m \neq 100 cm^2, and 1 m^3 = 1000 litres — the square-cube law bites here, and on every engineering exam.
