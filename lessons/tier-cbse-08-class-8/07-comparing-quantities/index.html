Ch 7 — Comparing Quantities

Percentages, profit and loss, simple and compound interest — the everyday algebra of money

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Do you know that \tfrac{1}{2} means the same as 50\%? If not, revisit Fractions as Parts.
  2. Are you comfortable with ratios like 3 : 4? If not, revisit Ratio and Proportion.
  3. Can you compute 25\% of 200 in your head?
  4. If something costs \text{₹}80 and you sell it for \text{₹}100, would you call that a profit or a loss?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Open your wallet or imagine one. You have \text{₹}500. A shop offers “20\% off” on an item priced at \text{₹}250.

How much do you actually pay? How much do you save? Slide below to explore.

viewof price = Inputs.range([100, 1000], {value: 250, step: 10, label: "Marked price (rupees)"})
viewof discount = Inputs.range([0, 60], {value: 20, step: 1, label: "Discount %"})
{
  const saving = price * discount / 100;
  const pay = price - saving;
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;line-height:1.7;">
    <div><b>Marked price:</b> &#8377; ${price}</div>
    <div><b>Discount of ${discount}%:</b> &#8377; ${saving.toFixed(2)} off</div>
    <div><b>You pay:</b> &#8377; ${pay.toFixed(2)}</div>
  </div>`;
}

Push the discount up and down. The amount-paid and amount-saved are proportional to the discount percentage. The whole chapter sits on that simple fact.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. A discount of 20\% on \text{₹}500 — is that the same number of rupees as 50\% on \text{₹}200?
  2. If a price increases by 10\% and then decreases by 10\%, do you end up at the original price?
  3. You earn \text{₹}100 interest in one year on \text{₹}1000. Next year, would you expect the same \text{₹}100 — or more, or less?
  4. If a bag’s marked price is \text{₹}800 but you bought it for \text{₹}600, what fraction of the marked price was the discount?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

Question 2 has a counter-intuitive answer: +10\% then -10\% does not return you to the start. (100 \to 110 \to 99.) Percentages compose in a multiplicative way, not an additive way. That is the seed of compound interest, the headline idea of this chapter.

Discover

Problem 1 — Percent of a quantity

What is 35\% of 80?

Hint 1 — a nudge “35\%” means “\tfrac{35}{100}”.
Full solution \tfrac{35}{100} \times 80 = \tfrac{35 \times 80}{100} = \tfrac{2800}{100} = 28.

Problem 2 — Express as a percent

A class has 25 students; 9 are absent. What percentage are absent?

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute \tfrac{9}{25}, then multiply by 100.
Full solution \tfrac{9}{25} \times 100 = \tfrac{900}{25} = 36\%.

Problem 3 — Profit or loss

A trader buys a chair for \text{₹}1{,}200 and sells it for \text{₹}1{,}500. What is the profit, and what is the profit percent?

Hint 1 — a nudge Profit = \text{SP} - \text{CP}. Profit percent uses CP as the base.
Hint 2 — the technique Profit% = \tfrac{\text{Profit}}{\text{CP}} \times 100.
Full solution Profit = 1500 - 1200 = \text{₹}300. Profit% = \tfrac{300}{1200} \times 100 = 25\%.

Problem 4 — Discount

The marked price (MP) of a shirt is \text{₹}800. After a discount of 15\%, what is the selling price (SP)?

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute the discount amount, then subtract.
Full solution

Discount = 15\% of \text{₹}800 = \text{₹}120. SP = 800 - 120 = \text{₹}680.

(Equivalently: SP = 85\% of MP = 0.85 \times 800 = 680.)

Problem 5 — Simple interest

Find the simple interest (SI) on \text{₹}5{,}000 for 3 years at 8\% per annum.

Hint 1 — a nudge \text{SI} = \tfrac{P \times R \times T}{100}.
Full solution

\text{SI} = \tfrac{5000 \times 8 \times 3}{100} = \tfrac{120000}{100} = \text{₹}1200.

Total amount after 3 years: 5000 + 1200 = \text{₹}6200.

Problem 6 — Compound interest, year by year

Find the compound interest (CI) on \text{₹}1{,}000 for 2 years at 10\% per annum, compounded annually.

Hint 1 — a nudge Year 1: simple interest. Year 2: interest on the new principal.
Hint 2 — the technique After year 1: amount = 1000 + 100 = 1100. Year 2 interest is on 1100, not on 1000.
Full solution
  • After year 1: 1000 \times 1.10 = 1100.
  • After year 2: 1100 \times 1.10 = 1210.

CI = 1210 - 1000 = \text{₹}210.

(Compare with SI for the same case: \tfrac{1000 \cdot 10 \cdot 2}{100} = \text{₹}200. Compound interest beats simple interest by \text{₹}10 — the “interest on interest”.)

You have just used the same engine — percent of a quantity — to compute discount, profit, simple interest, and compound interest. Four shop-floor problems, one idea.

In 1202 CE, the Pisan merchant–mathematician Leonardo of Pisa (now better known as Fibonacci) published Liber Abaci — “The Book of Calculation” — and introduced European traders to Hindu-Arabic numerals, percentage calculations, and currency conversion. One full chapter taught the method of computing per cento — “per hundred” — for problems of profit, loss, and interest, almost exactly the formulas you just used. Without that book, the modern banking and accounting system would have arrived several centuries later than it did.

Every formula in this chapter — discount, profit %, loss %, simple interest, compound interest, depreciation — is the same one move: multiply by (1 + \tfrac{r}{100}) to grow, by (1 - \tfrac{r}{100}) to shrink, and repeat if the change happens more than once. The difference between simple and compound interest is just whether you do that multiplication once on the original sum (simple) or once each period on the running total (compound).

Name

Percent. r\% means \tfrac{r}{100}. To find r\% of a quantity x: compute \tfrac{r}{100} \cdot x.

Profit / loss. \text{Profit\%} = \frac{\text{SP} - \text{CP}}{\text{CP}} \times 100, \qquad \text{Loss\%} = \frac{\text{CP} - \text{SP}}{\text{CP}} \times 100.

Simple interest. \text{SI} = \dfrac{P \cdot R \cdot T}{100}.

Compound interest, annual compounding. Amount after n years: A = P \left(1 + \tfrac{R}{100}\right)^n, and \text{CI} = A - P.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Percent is per-hundred — divide and you read it right. Profit on cost; discount on price displayed. Simple grows by addition; compound grows by multiplication — Each year a new pile, each pile breeds the next.

Apply

Worked example

An article was bought for \text{₹}4{,}000 and sold so as to gain 15\%. Find the selling price.

Competent solver’s path:

  1. Profit = 15\% of \text{₹}4000 = \text{₹}600.
  2. SP = \text{CP} + \text{Profit} = 4000 + 600 = \text{₹}4600.

(Or directly: SP = 1.15 \times \text{CP} = 1.15 \times 4000 = \text{₹} 4600.)

Answer: \text{₹}4600.

Practice — Warm

W1. Convert \tfrac{3}{8} to a percentage.

Full solution \tfrac{3}{8} = \tfrac{3 \times 100}{8}\% = 37.5\%.

W2. A pen costs \text{₹}50. After a 20\% discount, what is the selling price?

Full solution Discount = \text{₹}10. SP = \text{₹}40.

W3. SI on \text{₹}8000 at 12\% for 2.5 years?

Full solution \tfrac{8000 \times 12 \times 2.5}{100} = \tfrac{240000}{100} = \text{₹} 2400.

Practice — Working

P1. A shopkeeper marks his goods at 40\% above cost price and gives a discount of 10\%. Find his profit percent.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pick a convenient CP — say \text{₹}100.
Full solution Take CP = \text{₹}100. MP = 100 + 40 = \text{₹}140. SP = 140 - 10\% of 140 = 140 - 14 = \text{₹}126. Profit = 126 - 100 = \text{₹}26. Profit% = 26\%.

P2. Find CI on \text{₹}8000 for 2 years at 5\% per annum, compounded annually.

Hint 1 — a nudge A = P(1 + R/100)^n.
Full solution A = 8000 \times (1.05)^2 = 8000 \times 1.1025 = \text{₹}8820. CI = 8820 - 8000 = \text{₹}820.

P3. A scooter is bought for \text{₹}60{,}000 and depreciates by 10\% each year. Find its value after 2 years.

Hint 1 — a nudge Depreciation works just like compound interest, but with a minus sign.
Full solution V = 60000 \times (0.9)^2 = 60000 \times 0.81 = \text{₹}48{,}600.

Practice — Stretch

S1. The population of a town is 1{,}21{,}000 and grows at 10\% per year. What was the population two years ago?

Hint 1 — a nudge The current population is after two years of growth: P_0 \times (1.1)^2 = 121000.
Full solution

P_0 = \tfrac{121000}{(1.1)^2} = \tfrac{121000}{1.21} = 1{,}00{,}000.

Two years ago: 1,00,000 people.

S2. Sameer borrowed \text{₹}25{,}000 at 12\% p.a. compounded half-yearly for 1 year. Find the amount he repays.

Hint 1 — a nudge Half-yearly compounding: rate per period is \tfrac{12}{2} = 6\%, and there are 2 periods.
Full solution A = 25000 \times (1.06)^2 = 25000 \times 1.1236 = \text{₹}28{,}090.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). 25\% of 80 is (a) 15 (b) 20 (c) 25 (d) 30.

Full solution \tfrac{25}{100} \times 80 = 20. Answer: (b).

2-mark (short answer). A man bought a watch for \text{₹}1{,}500 and sold it at a loss of 8\%. Find the selling price.

Full solution Loss = 8\% of 1500 = \text{₹}120. SP = 1500 - 120 = \text{₹}1380.

3-mark (short answer II). A sum of money becomes \text{₹}6655 in 3 years at 10\% per annum, compounded annually. Find the principal.

Hint 1 — a nudge A = P(1 + R/100)^n. Solve for P.
Hint 2 — the technique P = \dfrac{A}{(1.1)^3}. (1.1)^3 = 1.331.
Full solution

P = \tfrac{6655}{1.331} = \text{₹}5000.

Check: 5000 \to 5500 \to 6050 \to 6655. ✓

4-mark (long answer). A trader allows a discount of 10\% on his marked price and still gains 20\%. (i) If the cost price of an article is \text{₹}540, find the marked price. (ii) Find the discount amount and the selling price.

Hint 1 — a nudge SP = 1.20 \times \text{CP} (since gain is 20\%). SP = 0.90 \times \text{MP} (since discount is 10\%).
Full solution

(i) SP = 1.20 \times 540 = \text{₹}648. SP = 0.90 \times \text{MP} \Rightarrow \text{MP} = \tfrac{648}{0.9} = \text{₹}720.

(ii) Discount = 10\% of 720 = \text{₹}72. SP = 720 - 72 = \text{₹}648. ✓

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 7.5 (interlocking discount and gain percent) is a near-twin of the 4-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2018 Set 2, Q21 (“compound interest half-yearly”) matches Stretch S2 exactly.

Common confusions

  1. “Profit% is computed on the SP.” It is on the CP. Always. Discount% is on the MP. Mixing the bases is the most common error in this chapter.
  2. “+10\% then -10\% returns to the start.” It does not. (100 \to 110 \to 99.) Percentage changes compose multiplicatively.
  3. “Compound interest is just n times simple interest.” No. Compound is bigger than that, because each year’s interest itself earns interest the next year.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A percent is a ratio out of 100 — and percent of a quantity is just \tfrac{r}{100} \times x. Every other formula in the chapter is built on this single move.
  2. Profit % uses the cost price as base; discount % uses the marked price. Pick the wrong base and the answer is wrong.
  3. Simple interest grows by addition; compound interest grows by multiplication. After n years at rate r: A = P\bigl(1 + \tfrac{r}{100}\bigr)^n — the same formula governs population growth, depreciation (with a minus), and bank deposits.
