Ch 10 — Exponents and Powers

How to write very big and very small numbers without running out of paper

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you compute 2^3, 3^2, 5^2 in your head? If not, revisit Multiplication as Area.
  2. Are you comfortable with -3 and -5 as numbers you can do arithmetic on? If not, revisit Negative Numbers.
  3. Do you know that a \cdot a \cdot a is written a^3?
  4. Can you tell which is bigger, \tfrac{1}{100} or \tfrac{1}{10}?

If three were a confident yes, read on.

Touch

Take a strip of paper. Fold it in half. Count the layers — 2. Fold again — 4 layers. Again — 8. Once more — 16. Each fold doubles the count.

The slider below shows the doubling.

viewof folds = Inputs.range([0, 12], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "Number of folds"})
{
  const f = folds;
  const layers = 2 ** f;
  const thicknessMm = 0.1 * layers;
  const human = thicknessMm < 10 ? `${thicknessMm.toFixed(1)} mm` :
                thicknessMm < 1000 ? `${(thicknessMm/10).toFixed(1)} cm` :
                thicknessMm < 1e6 ? `${(thicknessMm/1000).toFixed(1)} m` :
                `${(thicknessMm/1e6).toFixed(2)} km`;
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;line-height:1.7;">
    <div><b>Folds:</b> ${f}</div>
    <div><b>Layers:</b> ${layers} = 2<sup>${f}</sup></div>
    <div><b>Total thickness</b> (paper 0.1 mm): ${human}</div>
  </div>`;
}

Slide up to 12 folds. The layer count grows from 1 to 4096. Push to 42 folds (in your head): the count exceeds 4 \times 10^{12} — the paper would reach the moon. Repeated multiplication explodes.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. After 10 folds you have 1024 layers. After 20 folds, you have one million. By how many factors does each fold multiply your stack?
  2. If 2^{10} \approx 1000, can you guess what 2^{20} is, without computing?
  3. We have a name for a \cdot a (“a squared”) and a \cdot a \cdot a (“a cubed”). What might a^0 mean? Or a^{-1}?
  4. Could you write the number 0.000\,000\,1 using a power, without spelling out the seven zeros?
TipWhat you might notice (don’t peek until you’ve thought)

The pattern 2^1 = 2, 2^2 = 4, 2^3 = 8, 2^4 = 16, \ldots has a built-in rule: going up one step multiplies by 2. Reverse the rule: going down one step divides by 2. So 2^0 should equal 1 (one step below 2^1 = 2, divide by 2). 2^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{2}. 2^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{4}. The negative exponents fall out of the pattern.

Discover

Problem 1 — Multiply same base

What is 2^3 \times 2^4? Can you write the answer as a single power of 2?

Hint 1 — a nudge 2^3 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 (three twos). 2^4 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 (four twos). Together?
Full solution

2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128.

Rule: a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}.

Problem 2 — Divide same base

What is \tfrac{2^7}{2^3} as a single power of 2?

Hint 1 — a nudge Three of the seven 2s in the top cancel against the three 2s in the bottom.
Full solution

\tfrac{2^7}{2^3} = 2^{7-3} = 2^4 = 16.

Rule: \dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}.

Problem 3 — Power of a power

What is (2^3)^4?

Hint 1 — a nudge (2^3)^4 means four copies of 2^3 multiplied together.
Full solution

(2^3)^4 = 2^3 \cdot 2^3 \cdot 2^3 \cdot 2^3 = 2^{3 \cdot 4} = 2^{12} = 4096.

Rule: (a^m)^n = a^{mn}.

Problem 4 — Zero exponent

Use the division rule on \tfrac{2^5}{2^5}.

Hint 1 — a nudge \tfrac{2^5}{2^5} = 2^{5-5} = 2^0. But also \tfrac{2^5}{2^5} = 1.
Full solution 2^0 = 1. And in general, a^0 = 1 for any nonzero a.

Problem 5 — Negative exponent

Use the division rule on \tfrac{2^3}{2^5}.

Hint 1 — a nudge By the rule: 2^{3-5} = 2^{-2}. By cancellation: \tfrac{1}{2^2} = \tfrac{1}{4}.
Full solution

2^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{2^2} = \tfrac{1}{4}.

Rule: a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}.

Problem 6 — A very small number

Express 0.000\,07 in standard form: as a \times 10^n with 1 \leq a < 10 and n an integer.

Hint 1 — a nudge Move the decimal point so that exactly one nonzero digit is to the left of it. Count moves; each move to the right of the original is a negative power of 10.
Full solution 0.000\,07 = 7 \times 10^{-5}.

Problem 7 — A very big number

Express 24{,}50{,}00{,}00{,}000 in standard form.

Hint 1 — a nudge The decimal sits at the end. Move it so only 2.45 is to the left.
Full solution 24{,}50{,}00{,}00{,}000 = 2.45 \times 10^{10}.

You have just discovered every law you need: multiplication adds exponents, division subtracts, powers of powers multiply, a^0 = 1, a^{-n} = 1/a^n. Standard form rides on the same rules with base 10.

Decimal exponents — and the very idea of writing fractions as a power of ten — were popularised by the Flemish engineer Simon Stevin in his 36-page pamphlet De Thiende (“The Tenth”, 1585). Stevin argued that once arithmetic was done in base 10, it could finally be performed by “every astrologer, surveyor, tapestry-maker, gauger, mintmaster, and the like”. The modern decimal point arrived a generation later; the negative exponent had to wait until John Wallis (1685) gave it the meaning a^{-n} = 1/a^n that you discovered in Discover Problem 5.

Every law of exponents falls out of one observation: a^n counts how many copies of a are stacked. Multiplying joins two stacks (add counts); dividing cancels (subtract counts); a power of a power is a stack of stacks (multiply counts); the exponent 0 is “no copies” and equals 1, the multiplicative identity.

Name

For any nonzero base a and integers m, n:

Law Formula
Product, same base a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}
Quotient, same base \dfrac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}
Power of a power (a^m)^n = a^{mn}
Power of a product (ab)^n = a^n b^n
Zero exponent a^0 = 1
Negative exponent a^{-n} = \dfrac{1}{a^n}

Standard form (scientific notation): any positive number can be written as k \times 10^n, \qquad 1 \leq k < 10, \quad n \in \mathbb{Z}.

A short verse, in the spirit of Bhāskara’s Līlāvatī:

Same base meeting: add the powers; same base parting: subtract. A power of a power: multiply through. Zero on top: the answer is one; Negative exponent: a fraction is born.

Apply

Worked example

Simplify \dfrac{(3^4)^2 \cdot 3^{-3}}{3^5}.

Competent solver’s path:

  1. (3^4)^2 = 3^{8}.
  2. Numerator: 3^8 \cdot 3^{-3} = 3^{8 + (-3)} = 3^5.
  3. Whole expression: \dfrac{3^5}{3^5} = 3^{0} = 1.

Answer: 1.

Practice — Warm

W1. Simplify 5^{-2}.

Full solution 5^{-2} = \tfrac{1}{25}.

W2. Simplify (2^3)^4 \cdot 2^{-10}.

Full solution (2^3)^4 = 2^{12}. 2^{12} \cdot 2^{-10} = 2^{2} = 4.

W3. Express 0.000\,000\,002 in standard form.

Full solution 2 \times 10^{-9}.

Practice — Working

P1. Find the value of \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^{-3}.

Hint 1 — a nudge \left(\tfrac{a}{b}\right)^{-n} = \left(\tfrac{b}{a}\right)^{n}.
Full solution \left(\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^{-3} = \left(\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^{3} = \tfrac{27}{8}.

P2. Simplify \dfrac{(-3)^4 \times (5)^2}{(-3)^2 \times (5)^3}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the same-base rules separately for (-3) and for 5.
Full solution \dfrac{(-3)^4}{(-3)^2} = (-3)^{2} = 9. \dfrac{5^2}{5^3} = 5^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{5}. Product: 9 \cdot \tfrac{1}{5} = \tfrac{9}{5}.

P3. Express in standard form: 1{,}50{,}00{,}00{,}000 + 0.000\,000\,15. Then add them as standard-form numbers (you do not need a single common form — write each separately).

Hint 1 — a nudge Each in standard form independently.
Full solution

1{,}50{,}00{,}00{,}000 = 1.5 \times 10^{9}. 0.000\,000\,15 = 1.5 \times 10^{-7}.

(Sum is dominated by the first: \approx 1.5 \times 10^9.)

Practice — Stretch

S1. Compare without computing each: 2^{30} versus 3^{20}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Rewrite both in a common form. 2^{30} = (2^3)^{10} = 8^{10}. 3^{20} = (3^2)^{10} = 9^{10}.
Full solution 2^{30} = 8^{10} and 3^{20} = 9^{10}. Since 9 > 8, 9^{10} > 8^{10}. So \boxed{3^{20} > 2^{30}}.

S2. A bacterium divides every 20 minutes. Starting with 1 bacterium, how many will there be after 4 hours? Express in standard form.

Hint 1 — a nudge 4 hours = 12 doubling periods.
Full solution 2^{12} = 4096. In standard form: 4.096 \times 10^3.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{-3} equals (a) \tfrac{1}{8} (b) -8 (c) 8 (d) \tfrac{3}{2}.

Full solution \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{-3} = 2^3 = 8. Answer: (c).

2-mark (short answer). Simplify (2^{-3} + 3^{-1})^{-1}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute the sum inside, then take its reciprocal.
Full solution

2^{-3} + 3^{-1} = \tfrac{1}{8} + \tfrac{1}{3} = \tfrac{3 + 8}{24} = \tfrac{11}{24}.

Reciprocal: \tfrac{24}{11}.

3-mark (short answer II). The mass of an electron is approximately 9.11 \times 10^{-31} kg. The mass of a proton is approximately 1.67 \times 10^{-27} kg. About how many times heavier than the electron is the proton?

Hint 1 — a nudge Divide the two standard-form numbers; subtract exponents.
Full solution

Ratio = \dfrac{1.67 \times 10^{-27}}{9.11 \times 10^{-31}} = \dfrac{1.67}{9.11} \times 10^{-27 - (-31)} = 0.1833 \times 10^4 \approx 1833.

The proton is about 1{,}833 times heavier.

4-mark (long answer). (i) Express in standard form: the diameter of a hydrogen atom, 0.000\,000\,000\,1 m. (ii) About 6.022 \times 10^{23} atoms make 1 gram of hydrogen. If each atom has the diameter from (i), find the total length of all those atoms placed end-to-end. Express in metres in standard form, and convert to kilometres.

Hint 1 — a nudge Total length = number of atoms × diameter of one. Use the laws of exponents.
Full solution

(i) 0.000\,000\,000\,1 = 1 \times 10^{-10} m.

(ii) Total = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \times 1 \times 10^{-10} = 6.022 \times 10^{13} m.

In km: divide by 1000 = 10^3: 6.022 \times 10^{13} \div 10^3 = 6.022 \times 10^{10} km.

(That’s about 60 billion km — roughly 400 times the Earth–Sun distance. The point: standard form is necessary once numbers get this unwieldy.)

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Exemplar Problem 10.4 (atomic-scale ratios using standard form) is a near-twin of the 4-mark item. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2019 Set 2, Q9 (“simplify (a^{-3})^4 \cdot a^5”) matches Working P2 in style.

Common confusions

  1. “a^0 = 0.” No — a^0 = 1 (for nonzero a). The “zero” lives in the exponent, not in the result.
  2. “2^{-3} = -8.” No. Negative exponent means reciprocal, not negation: 2^{-3} = \tfrac{1}{8}.
  3. “(a + b)^n = a^n + b^n.” Almost never true (except n = 1). This is the same trap as in Algebraic Identities — distributing a power across a sum is wrong.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. The exponent counts copies. The six laws — product, quotient, power-of-power, power-of-product, zero, and negative — are six ways of saying “stack on, stack off”.
  2. a^0 = 1 and a^{-n} = 1/a^n are not arbitrary rules. They are the only values that keep the product and quotient laws working continuously across the boundary.
  3. Standard form, k \times 10^n, is how scientists, engineers, and computers store numbers from electron-masses to galactic distances on the same page.

Self-Assessment

Be honest with yourself.

  1. Could you state and justify (with a quick example) all six exponent laws?
  2. Did you solve at least 6 of the 7 Discover problems and 5 of the 7 Practice problems without peeking past Hint 1?
  3. Given a number like 0.000\,000\,000\,42, can you write it in standard form in five seconds?
  • 3 yes: move on to Direct and Inverse Proportions.
  • 2 yes: redo Working P2 and the 4-mark board item, then move on.
  • 0–1 yes: redo Discover problems 4 and 5 — until a^0 = 1 and a^{-n} = 1/a^n feel like consequences, not memorised rules.

Where this matters

Systems. Computer memory is sized in powers of 2: a kilobyte is 2^{10} = 1024 bytes, a megabyte is 2^{20}, a gigabyte is 2^{30}. Every layer of a computer’s address space is a power of two.

Encryption. RSA’s “modular exponentiation” — computing a^b \bmod n for b a 2048-bit number — is the inner loop of every TLS handshake on the internet. The laws of exponents you learnt today are literally the algorithm.

AI / ML. Floating-point numbers, the only way computers store real numbers, are standard-form numbers: \pm m \times 2^e. Every neural-net weight, every gradient, every learning rate is one such number.
