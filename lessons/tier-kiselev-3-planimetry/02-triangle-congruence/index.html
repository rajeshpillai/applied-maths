Ch 2 — The Triangle (Congruence)

SSS, SAS, ASA — and the isosceles triangle theorem in three lines

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state and use the vertical-angles-equal theorem? See kg1.lines-and-angles.
  2. Can you describe what a triangle is — three vertices, three sides, three angles?
  3. Are you comfortable measuring an angle and a length?
  4. Do you accept that two figures are the same shape and size — that is, congruent — if you can lay one on top of the other?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

Three sticks of fixed lengths. Try to build a triangle. Can you do it more than one way (allowing flips and rotations)?

viewof a = Inputs.range([1, 6], {value: 4, step: 0.1, label: "Side a"})
viewof b = Inputs.range([1, 6], {value: 3, step: 0.1, label: "Side b"})
viewof c = Inputs.range([1, 6], {value: 5, step: 0.1, label: "Side c"})
{
  const w = 380, h = 320, padX = 40, padY = 40;
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 8]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 7]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  // Place A at origin, B at (c, 0), and find C from the two lengths a (BC) and b (CA)
  const Ax = 0, Ay = 0;
  const Bx = c, By = 0;
  // C: distance b from A, a from B; intersection above x-axis
  const cosA = (b * b + c * c - a * a) / (2 * b * c);
  const valid = c > 0 && b > 0 && a > 0 && cosA >= -1 && cosA <= 1
    && a + b > c && a + c > b && b + c > a;

  if (valid) {
    const angA = Math.acos(cosA);
    const Cx = b * Math.cos(angA);
    const Cy = b * Math.sin(angA);

    svg.append("polygon")
      .attr("points", `${xS(Ax)},${yS(Ay)} ${xS(Bx)},${yS(By)} ${xS(Cx)},${yS(Cy)}`)
      .attr("fill", "#26a").attr("fill-opacity", 0.18)
      .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 2);

    const lab = (px, py, t, dx, dy) => {
      svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(px) + dx).attr("y", yS(py) + dy)
        .attr("font-size", 12).attr("font-weight", "bold").text(t);
    };
    lab(Ax, Ay, "A", -10, 14);
    lab(Bx, By, "B", 8, 14);

    svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
      .attr("font-size", 11)
      .text(`Triangle exists: a=${a.toFixed(2)}, b=${b.toFixed(2)}, c=${c.toFixed(2)}`);
  } else {
    svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", h / 2)
      .attr("font-size", 13).attr("fill", "#a26")

When the lengths satisfy the triangle inequality (a + b > c, etc.) the triangle exists — and its shape is fixed. Three lengths, one triangle (up to flipping).

Wonder

  1. Three lengths fix a triangle. Two lengths and the angle between them — does that fix a triangle too?
  2. Two angles and the side between them — does that fix a triangle?
  3. Two triangles match perfectly when laid one over the other. What are the minimum pieces of information you’d need to verify they match without lifting the paper?
  4. A triangle has two equal sides. Must it have two equal angles? Why?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Yes — Side-Angle-Side (SAS). (2) Yes — Angle-Side-Angle (ASA). (3) Three pieces — and they must be of the right kinds. SSS, SAS, ASA work; SSA does not (the ambiguous case). (4) Yes, and the proof is one of the cleanest in Kiselev — the isosceles triangle theorem.

Discover

Problem 1 — The triangle inequality

For any triangle with sides a, b, c, show that a + b > c.

Hint 1 — a nudge The straight path from one vertex to another is the shortest one (an axiom). Compare it to the path that goes via a third vertex.
Full solution Let the triangle be ABC with |BC| = a, |CA| = b, |AB| = c. The straight segment from A to B has length c. The path A \to C \to B has length b + a. By the straight-line shortest axiom, c < a + b. By symmetry, the same applies to the other two sides. (Planimetry §39.)

Problem 2 — Side-Side-Side congruence (SSS)

Two triangles ABC and A'B'C' have |AB| = |A'B'|, |BC| = |B'C'|, |CA| = |C'A'|. Show that the triangles are congruent — i.e., a rigid motion takes one onto the other.

Hint 1 — a nudge Lay \triangle A'B'C' on \triangle ABC so that A' matches A and B' matches B. Argue that C' must land on C.
Full solution Place A' \to A and B' \to B (possible because |A'B'| = |AB|). Now C' must lie at distance |A'C'| = |AC| from A and at distance |B'C'| = |BC| from B. There are at most two such points (the two intersections of two circles), one on each side of line AB. If C' lands on the same side as C, then C' = C — done. If on the opposite side, flip \triangle A'B'C' across AB and now C' lands on C. Either way, a rigid motion (possibly including a flip) takes one triangle onto the other. Triangles are congruent. QED. (Planimetry §44.)

Problem 3 — Side-Angle-Side congruence (SAS)

Two triangles have two pairs of equal sides and the included angle equal. Show they are congruent.

Hint 1 — a nudge Lay them so the equal angle and one of its sides match. Where must the third vertex go?
Full solution Suppose |AB| = |A'B'|, \angle B = \angle B', |BC| = |B'C'|. Lay B' \to B, A' \to A, and aligned-side onto aligned-side. The angle at B matches the angle at B', so the direction from B to C matches the direction from B' to C'. Since the lengths |BC| and |B'C'| are equal, C' \to C. The third vertices coincide. Triangles congruent. QED. (Planimetry §45.)

Problem 4 — Angle-Side-Angle congruence (ASA)

Two triangles have two pairs of equal angles and the included side equal. Show they are congruent.

Hint 1 — a nudge Lay the equal sides on top. The two rays from each end go off at the matching angles — where must the third vertex be?
Full solution Suppose |BC| = |B'C'|, \angle B = \angle B', \angle C = \angle C'. Lay B \to B', C \to C' aligned. The ray from B at angle \angle B matches the ray from B' at angle \angle B' — they are the same ray. Similarly for the rays from C and C'. The two rays meet at one point — the apex A, which equals A'. Congruent. (Planimetry §47.)

Problem 5 — Isosceles triangle theorem

Let \triangle ABC have |AB| = |AC|. Show that \angle B = \angle C.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare \triangle ABC with itself, but with vertices renamed A \to A, B \to C, C \to B — i.e., flipped. Use SSS or SAS.
Full solution

Compare \triangle ABC with \triangle ACB (same triangle, vertices renamed). Sides: |AB| = |AC| (given), |AC| = |AB| (given), |BC| = |CB| (same segment). By SSS the two triangles are congruent. Hence the corresponding angles are equal — in particular, \angle B = \angle C. QED. (Planimetry §51 — Pappus’ proof, recovered by Kiselev.)

Three lines, no auxiliary construction. The isosceles triangle is its own mirror.

Problem 6 — Converse: equal angles ⇒ equal sides

Let \triangle ABC have \angle B = \angle C. Show that |AB| = |AC|.

Hint 1 — a nudge Same trick — compare \triangle ABC with \triangle ACB, but use ASA this time.
Full solution Compare \triangle ABC with \triangle ACB. Angles: \angle B = \angle C (given), \angle C = \angle B (given), and |BC| = |CB| (same segment). By ASA, the two triangles are congruent. Hence |AB| = |AC| (corresponding sides). QED.

Problem 7 — Exterior-angle theorem (weak form)

In \triangle ABC, extend side BC past C to a ray. The angle formed outside the triangle at C is the exterior angle at C. Show that this exterior angle is strictly greater than either remote interior angle (\angle A or \angle B).

Hint 1 — a nudge Let M be the midpoint of AC. Extend BM past M to a point D with |BM| = |MD|. Compare \triangle ABM and \triangle CDM using SAS.
Full solution Let M be the midpoint of AC. Extend BM to D with |MD| = |BM|. Then \triangle ABM \cong \triangle CDM by SAS (|AM| = |MC| given by midpoint; \angle AMB = \angle CMD vertical angles; |MB| = |MD| by construction). Hence \angle BAM = \angle DCM, i.e. \angle A = \angle DCM. But \angle DCM is strictly inside the exterior angle at C (since D is inside the angle), so the exterior angle at C is strictly larger than \angle A. The same argument with the midpoint of AB (rather than AC) handles \angle B. QED. (Planimetry §50.)

Euclid Book I, propositions 4, 8, 26. The three congruence criteria you just proved have been the spine of Western geometry for 2,300 years. Euclid stated SAS as Proposition 4 of Book I of the Elements (~300 BCE), SSS as Proposition 8, and ASA as Proposition 26 — the three pillars on which every later theorem in his thirteen books rests. Euclid’s proof of SAS used a method of superposition (lay one triangle on top of the other) that mathematicians have argued about ever since: is “lay one figure on another” really an axiom, a theorem, or hand-waving? Hilbert in 1899 simply elevated SAS to an axiom, dispensing with the debate. The flip step in our proof of SSS (Problem 2) is borrowed straight from Euclid.

Three pieces are enough to fix a triangle. Three sides (SSS), two sides + included angle (SAS), or two angles + included side (ASA) — any of these three trios determines the triangle completely. The whole notion of congruence in plane geometry boils down to: do you have one of the three good trios?

Name

Triangle. A figure formed by three line segments connecting three non-collinear points (the vertices). Three sides, three angles; three angles sum to 180° (proved next chapter — Parallels — but already a familiar fact).

Congruence. Two triangles are congruent if a rigid motion of the plane (translation + rotation, possibly with a flip) takes one onto the other. Equivalently: corresponding sides and corresponding angles are equal.

The three congruence theorems.

  • SSS: three pairs of equal sides ⇒ congruent.
  • SAS: two pairs of equal sides + included angle ⇒ congruent.
  • ASA: two pairs of equal angles + included side ⇒ congruent.

Isosceles triangle theorem (Planimetry §51). A triangle with two equal sides has the angles opposite them equal. Conversely: a triangle with two equal angles has the sides opposite them equal.

Exterior-angle theorem, weak form (§50). An exterior angle of a triangle is strictly greater than either remote interior angle.

A verse:

Three sides, three angles — but only three pieces are needed. Side-side-side, side-angle-side, angle-side-angle have all succeeded. Equal sides force equal angles, equal angles force equal sides — And no exterior angle ever shrinks below either remote that hides.

Apply

Worked example

In \triangle ABC, |AB| = |AC| and \angle A = 40°. Find \angle B and \angle C.

By isosceles (|AB| = |AC|), \angle B = \angle C. Triangle angle sum gives \angle B + \angle C = 180° - 40° = 140°. Each is 70°.

\angle B = \angle C = 70°.

Practice — Warm

W1. Two triangles have all three sides 5, 6, 8. Are they congruent?

Full solution Yes — by SSS.

W2. \triangle ABC has |AB| = |AC|. If \angle B = 65°, find \angle A and \angle C.

Full solution \angle C = \angle B = 65° (isosceles). \angle A = 180° - 130° = 50°.

W3. Two triangles have two equal sides 5 and 7 and the included angle of 50° in each. Are they congruent?

Full solution Yes — by SAS.

Practice — Working

P1. \triangle ABC is equilateral — all three sides equal. Find each angle.

Hint 1 — a nudge Apply isosceles twice.
Full solution |AB| = |AC| \Rightarrow \angle B = \angle C. |BA| = |BC| \Rightarrow \angle A = \angle C. So \angle A = \angle B = \angle C. Sum = 180° \Rightarrow each is 60°.

P2. Two triangles have |AB| = 5, |BC| = 7, \angle A = 40° in both. Are they necessarily congruent?

Hint 1 — a nudge This is “Side-Side-Angle” — the ambiguous case. Try drawing it.
Full solution No. SSA does not guarantee congruence; sometimes two distinct triangles fit (the famous ambiguous case in trigonometry). Three of the four canonical “congruence shortcuts” (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS) work; SSA does not. (Kiselev addresses this explicitly in §49.)

P3. \triangle ABC has |AB| = |AC|. Let D be the foot of the perpendicular from A to BC. Show that |BD| = |DC|.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD. AD is shared, |AB| = |AC| given, \angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90°.
Full solution \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD share AD. |AB| = |AC| (given). \angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90°. This is Side-Angle-Side iff the angle is included between the two equal sides — but here the angle is at D, not between |AD| and the equal sides. Use right-triangle Pythagoras: |BD|^2 = |AB|^2 - |AD|^2 = |AC|^2 - |AD|^2 = |DC|^2, so |BD| = |DC|. (Or: by the isosceles theorem, \angle B = \angle C; then ASA gives \triangle ABD \cong \triangle ACD, so |BD| = |DC|.) The altitude from the apex of an isosceles triangle bisects the base.

Practice — Stretch

S1. In \triangle ABC, the bisector of \angle A meets BC at D. Show that if |AB| = |AC|, then D is the midpoint of BC.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD using SAS at vertex A.
Full solution \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD: |AB| = |AC| given, \angle BAD = \angle CAD (bisector), AD shared. By SAS, congruent. So |BD| = |DC|. The angle bisector from the apex of an isosceles triangle bisects the base. (Companion to P3.)

S2. Show that if two altitudes of a triangle are equal in length, then the triangle is isosceles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Suppose the altitude from B and the altitude from C are equal. Use right triangles formed by these altitudes and a third side.
Full solution Drop altitudes BE \perp AC and CF \perp AB with |BE| = |CF|. The right triangles \triangle BEC and \triangle CFB share side BC, and have equal legs |BE| = |CF|. By the right-triangle hypotenuse-leg criterion (a corollary of SSS), they are congruent. So \angle BCE = \angle CBF, i.e. \angle C = \angle B. By the isosceles converse, |AB| = |AC|. Triangle isosceles. (A nice Kiselev exercise from §53.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Planimetry, §§44–55.

K1. In a triangle, the median from a vertex is also the perpendicular from that vertex. Show the triangle is isosceles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let AD be the median (so |BD| = |DC|) and also the perpendicular (AD \perp BC). Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD.
Full solution \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD: |BD| = |DC| (median), AD shared, \angle ADB = \angle ADC = 90°. By SAS (the angle is included between the two equal sides), congruent. So |AB| = |AC| — triangle isosceles. (Planimetry §53.)

K2. Two segments AB and CD bisect each other at a point O (so |AO| = |OB| and |CO| = |OD|). Show that |AC| = |BD| and AC \parallel BD.

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle AOC and \triangle BOD — find the SAS pair.
Full solution \triangle AOC and \triangle BOD: |AO| = |OB|, |CO| = |OD|, \angle AOC = \angle BOD (vertical angles). By SAS, congruent. Hence |AC| = |BD|, and corresponding angles \angle OAC = \angle OBD, which are alternate-interior angles for the transversal AB cutting lines AC and BD. By the converse alternate-interior- angles theorem (we’ll prove it formally in Ch 4), AC \parallel BD. QED. (Planimetry §47, exercise.)

K3. A triangle ABC has a point D on side BC with |AD| = |AB|. Compare \angle ADC to \angle ABC.

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle ABD is isosceles with |AB| = |AD| — what does that say about its angles?
Full solution \triangle ABD isosceles, so \angle ABD = \angle ADB. Now \angle ADC is the exterior angle of \triangle ABD at D, so \angle ADC = 180° - \angle ADB = 180° - \angle ABD. Hence \angle ADC is the supplement of \angle ABC. (Planimetry §52, typical Kiselev exterior-angle exercise.)

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. SSS, SAS, ASA — three trios that fix a triangle. Memorise the three included pieces and you can decide congruence at sight; SSA does not make the list.
  2. Equal sides ⇔ equal opposite angles. The isosceles theorem and its converse are one idea seen from two sides — and Pappus’s three-line proof is the cleanest in school geometry.
  3. Congruence is rigid motion. Two triangles are congruent exactly when one can be slid, rotated, and (if needed) flipped onto the other. The three criteria are checks that this motion exists.

Common confusions

  1. “SSA is a congruence theorem.” It is not. The ambiguous case — two non-included sides and a non-included angle — admits two non-congruent triangles in general. (See P2.) Only SSS, SAS, ASA, and the right-triangle hypotenuse-leg case are reliable.
  2. “Isosceles requires an axis of symmetry.” The theorem says equal sides ⇒ equal angles, but does not require you to find the axis. Pappus’ proof (Problem 5) doesn’t construct an axis at all.
  3. “Congruence is the same as similarity.” It is not. Congruent = same shape and size. Similar = same shape, possibly different size — which we’ll cover in Ch 7. Congruence is similarity with scale factor 1.

Where this matters

Game development. Triangle congruence is the foundation of bone rigging in 3D animation — when a limb deforms, the polygons on its surface must remain congruent up to rigid motion to avoid visible distortion. The SSS / SAS / ASA invariants are the correctness conditions for any deformation model.

Research. The isosceles-triangle theorem (and Pappus’ proof) generalises to any symmetric configuration: if a structure is invariant under a transformation, the orbits of points under that transformation force equality between corresponding parts. This is the seed of Klein’s Erlangen Programme — geometry as the study of group actions.
