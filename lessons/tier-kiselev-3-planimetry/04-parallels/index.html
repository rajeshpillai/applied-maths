Ch 4 — Parallels

When two lines never meet — and why every triangle has 180°

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you name the angles formed when two lines cross — adjacent, vertical, supplementary? See cbse9.lines-and-angles.
  2. Do you accept that two lines crossing make vertical angles equal?
  3. Can you describe what parallel means in everyday language — railway tracks, ruled lines on a page?
  4. Are you comfortable with the word theorem meaning “a statement we prove from the axioms and earlier theorems”?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

Two horizontal lines, drawn close together. A third line — the transversal — cuts across both. Drag the transversal: watch the eight angles update.

viewof slope = Inputs.range([-3, 3], {value: 1.2, step: 0.1, label: "Transversal slope"})
viewof xshift = Inputs.range([-2, 2], {value: 0, step: 0.1, label: "Transversal offset"})
{
  const w = 460, h = 320, padX = 40, padY = 40;
  const xMin = -5, xMax = 5;
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([xMin, xMax]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-3, 3]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  // two parallel horizontal lines at y = -1 and y = 1
  for (const y of [-1, 1]) {
    svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(xMin)).attr("x2", xS(xMax))
      .attr("y1", yS(y)).attr("y2", yS(y))
      .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 2);
  }
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX + 5).attr("y", yS(1) - 5)
    .attr("font-size", 11).text("line a");
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX + 5).attr("y", yS(-1) - 5)
    .attr("font-size", 11).text("line b");

  // transversal: y = slope * (x - xshift), drawn long
  const xL = xMin, xR = xMax;
  const yL = slope * (xL - xshift), yR = slope * (xR - xshift);
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(xL)).attr("x2", xS(xR))
    .attr("y1", yS(yL)).attr("y2", yS(yR))
    .attr("stroke", "#a26").attr("stroke-width", 2);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", w - padX - 60).attr("y", yS(yR) - 5)
    .attr("font-size", 11).text("transversal");

  // mark intersection points
  const ix1 = xshift + 1 / slope;   // y = 1 cut
  const ix2 = xshift - 1 / slope;   // y = -1 cut
  const angleDeg = Math.round(Math.atan(slope) * 180 / Math.PI * 10) / 10;

  if (Math.abs(slope) > 0.05) {
    svg.append("circle").attr("cx", xS(ix1)).attr("cy", yS(1))
      .attr("r", 4).attr("fill", "#333");
    svg.append("circle").attr("cx", xS(ix2)).attr("cy", yS(-1))
      .attr("r", 4).attr("fill", "#333");
    svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(ix1) + 8).attr("y", yS(1) - 8)
      .attr("font-size", 11).text(`A`);
    svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(ix2) + 8).attr("y", yS(-1) - 8)
      .attr("font-size", 11).text(`B`);
  }

  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
    .attr("font-size", 13)
    .text(`Transversal angle (with horizontal): ${angleDeg}°`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY + 8)
    .attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`The four "upper" angles at A equal the four "lower" angles at B.`);
  return svg.node();
}

The two horizontal lines never meet — they are parallel. The transversal makes the same angle at A as at B.

Wonder

  1. Suppose I drew two lines that look parallel. How could I be sure they are parallel — without extending them to infinity?
  2. The transversal cuts angles at A and at B. Some are equal, some sum to 180°. Can you spot a pattern in which is which?
  3. If the four upper angles at A have measures \alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta, do the four at B have the same multiset of measures? Why?
  4. Consider any triangle. Drag a parallel through one vertex — what do you notice about the three angles of the triangle and the straight angle at that vertex?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Alternate-interior and corresponding angles are equal; co-interior angles sum to 180°. (3) Yes — the transversal makes the same set of four angles at each crossing of a parallel.
  2. The three triangle-angles together fill the straight angle — this is the proof of the triangle-angle-sum theorem.

Discover

We work in classical Euclidean style: each step uses only earlier results.

Problem 1 — Definition

State precisely what parallel means. Two phrasings are candidates: (a) lines that never meet; (b) lines that are everywhere the same distance apart. Are they equivalent?

Hint 1 — a nudge Both are tempting. Equivalence requires a proof — and that proof needs the angle theory we are about to develop.
Full solution Kiselev takes (a) as the definition: parallel lines are two lines in the same plane that do not meet, however far they are extended. He then proves (b) as a theorem (Planimetry §86): parallels are equidistant. The two phrasings are equivalent in Euclidean geometry, but only after a proof.

Problem 2 — A construction of parallels

Given a line \ell and a point P not on \ell, construct a line through P that does not meet \ell.

Hint 1 — a nudge Drop a perpendicular from P to \ell. Then erect a perpendicular to that perpendicular at P.
Full solution Drop perpendicular PF from P to \ell (F \in \ell). Through P, erect a line m perpendicular to PF. Then m does not meet \ell — for if it did, at some point Q, the triangle PFQ would have two right angles (\angle PFQ and \angle FPQ), giving 180° between just two angles, leaving none for \angle PQF. Existence is proved. (Planimetry §76.)

Problem 3 — The parallel postulate (Euclid V)

The construction above produces one line through P that does not meet \ell. Could there be more than one?

Full solution Euclid’s Fifth Postulate asserts: exactly one parallel through P. This is independent of the other axioms — there are non-Euclidean geometries (hyperbolic, with infinitely many parallels; spherical, with none) in which it fails. In this lesson we accept the Fifth Postulate as a working axiom; everything that follows depends on it.

Problem 4 — Alternate-interior angles

A transversal cuts parallel lines a and b at A and B. Number the four angles at A as 1, 2, 3, 4 and at B as 5, 6, 7, 8, matching the figure below. Show that alternate-interior angles are equal: \angle 3 = \angle 6.

              1 \\ 2
        ───────A────── (line a)
              4 \\ 3
                 \\
              5 \\ 6
        ───────B────── (line b)
              8 \\ 7
Hint 1 — a nudge Argue by contradiction. Suppose \angle 3 \ne \angle 6. Then one of the angles (say \angle 3) is larger; rotate the transversal at B to make \angle 6 equal \angle 3, and see what goes wrong.
Full solution Suppose \angle 3 \ne \angle 6. WLOG \angle 3 > \angle 6. Then a line b' at B making angle \angle 3 with the transversal would also not meet a (by the same argument as Problem 2 generalised), contradicting the uniqueness of the parallel through B. So \angle 3 = \angle 6. (Kiselev’s classical proof; Planimetry §80.)

Problem 5 — Corresponding angles

Show that corresponding angles are equal: \angle 2 = \angle 6 in the same figure.

Hint 1 — a nudge \angle 2 and \angle 3 are vertical (or supplementary, depending on labelling) — link via Problem 4.
Full solution \angle 2 = \angle 4 (vertical angles, from earlier chapter). \angle 4 and \angle 3 are supplementary (\angle 4 + \angle 3 = 180°). Likewise \angle 6 + \angle 7 = 180° and \angle 6 = \angle 8 (vertical). From Problem 4, \angle 3 = \angle 6, hence \angle 4 = \angle 7, etc. The corresponding pair \angle 2 = \angle 6 falls out.

Problem 6 — Triangle-angle-sum

Let \triangle ABC be any triangle. Prove that \angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180°.

Hint 1 — a nudge Through the vertex C, draw a line parallel to AB. Use Problem 4 twice.
Full solution Let m be the line through C parallel to AB (exists by Problem 2; unique by Problem 3). The angles on one side of m at C are three: an angle equal to \angle A (alternate interior with the transversal AC); the original \angle C of the triangle; an angle equal to \angle B (alternate interior with the transversal BC). The three together form a straight angle along m, so \angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180°. QED. (Kiselev’s classical proof; Planimetry §89.)

Problem 7 — A converse

Suppose two lines a and b are cut by a transversal so that alternate-interior angles are equal. Prove that a \parallel b.

Full solution If a and b met at some point Q, the transversal together with a and b would form a triangle with two given equal alternate- interior angles. By the exterior-angle theorem (earlier in Kiselev, §78), an exterior angle of a triangle is strictly greater than the remote interior angle — contradicting the equality the hypothesis gives. Hence a and b never meet — i.e., a \parallel b.

Lobachevsky and the Russian discovery of non-Euclidean geometry. For two thousand years mathematicians tried — and failed — to prove the parallel postulate from the other axioms. The Italian Jesuit Giovanni Saccheri (1733) came heartbreakingly close to a proof; in fact he had already constructed a non-Euclidean geometry but could not bring himself to believe in it. The breakthrough came from Nikolai Lobachevsky, professor at Kazan University, who in 1829 published a system in which infinitely many parallels pass through a point off a line — hyperbolic geometry. The Hungarian János Bolyai discovered the same independently in 1832. Their work was greeted with confusion and (in Lobachevsky’s case) public ridicule; Gauss privately agreed but never published. Today hyperbolic and spherical geometries are the foundation of general relativity. Kiselev’s planimetry sits inside one corner — the Euclidean corner — of a much larger geometric landscape, and that landscape is part of Russia’s mathematical heritage.

The parallel postulate is what makes triangle angles sum to exactly 180°. Change the postulate (allow many parallels, or none) and the angle sum changes too — less than 180° on a saddle, more than 180° on a sphere. The flatness of school geometry is a choice, not a necessity.

Name

Definition. Two lines a, b in a plane are parallel (a \parallel b) if they do not meet, however far extended.

Postulate (Euclid V). Through a point not on a given line, there is exactly one parallel to that line.

Theorem (alternate-interior angles). If a \parallel b and a transversal cuts them, then alternate-interior angles are equal. Conversely, if alternate-interior angles are equal then a \parallel b.

Theorem (triangle-angle-sum). The three angles of any triangle sum to a straight angle (180°).

A verse:

Two lines never meeting — that’s parallel, and one rule reigns. Through a point off a line, one such line, no more, remains. A transversal makes alternates equal, that’s how we agree — And the three angles of any triangle sum to one-eighty.

Apply

Worked example

In the figure below, AB \parallel CD. The transversal EF cuts AB at G and CD at H. If \angle AGE = 65°, find every angle at G and at H.

At G: \angle AGE = 65°, so its vertical angle \angle BGF = 65°. Its supplements are \angle AGF = \angle BGE = 115°.

At H: corresponding to \angle AGE is \angle CHG = 65° (parallels + transversal). Then the four angles at H are 65°, 115°, 65°, 115° in the same arrangement.

All eight angles are either 65° or 115°.

Practice — Warm

W1. Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal. One acute angle formed is 42°. List all eight angles.

Full solution Each crossing has angles 42°, 138°, 42°, 138°. Both crossings have the same set, by Problems 4–5 above.

W2. A triangle has angles 40° and 75°. Find the third.

Full solution 180° - 40° - 75° = 65°.

W3. Two angles of a triangle are equal, and the third is 50°. Find the equal pair.

Full solution 180° - 50° = 130°, split equally: 65° each.

Practice — Working

P1. In a triangle the second angle is twice the first, and the third is three times the first. Find all three.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let the first be x. Sum: x + 2x + 3x = 6x = 180°.
Full solution x = 30°. Angles: 30°, 60°, 90° — a 30–60–90 triangle.

P2. Two parallel lines are cut by a transversal so that the two co-interior angles are in ratio 2 : 3. Find them.

Hint 1 — a nudge Co-interior angles sum to 180° (use the alt-interior theorem + linear pair).
Full solution 2x + 3x = 180°, so x = 36°. Angles 72° and 108°.

P3. Show that the exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles.

Full solution At a vertex C, the exterior angle is 180° - \angle C. By the angle-sum theorem, \angle A + \angle B = 180° - \angle C. Equate: exterior angle at C = \angle A + \angle B. (This is the exterior-angle theorem in its sharper form; Planimetry §90.)

Practice — Stretch

S1. ABCD is a quadrilateral. Prove that the sum of its four interior angles is 360°.

Hint 1 — a nudge Draw a diagonal — say AC — splitting the quadrilateral into two triangles.
Full solution Diagonal AC produces \triangle ABC and \triangle ACD. Each has angle sum 180°. Total 360°. The four interior angles of ABCD partition into the angles of these two triangles (no overlap, no leftover). Hence \angle A + \angle B + \angle C + \angle D = 360°. QED.

S2. Prove the converse of the corresponding-angles theorem: if a transversal makes equal corresponding angles with two lines, the lines are parallel.

Full solution Equal corresponding angles + supplementary linear pair \Rightarrow equal alternate-interior angles. Then by Problem 7 the lines are parallel.

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Planimetry, §§88–94.

K1. In a triangle, the angles are in arithmetic progression. Show that the middle angle is exactly 60°.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let the three angles be a - d, a, a + d. Sum to 180°.
Full solution (a - d) + a + (a + d) = 3a = 180°, so a = 60°. The middle angle is 60° regardless of d. (For d = 0 the triangle is equilateral.)

K2. Two lines \ell_1 and \ell_2 intersect a third line m at points P and Q. The interior angle at P on one side of m is 58°; the interior angle at Q on the same side of m is 122°. Are \ell_1 and \ell_2 parallel?

Hint 1 — a nudge “Same side” interior angles are co-interior. For parallels they sum to 180°.
Full solution 58° + 122° = 180°. Yes — \ell_1 \parallel \ell_2. (This is the co-interior-angles converse, a corollary of Problem 7.)

K3. ABCD is a quadrilateral with AD \parallel BC but AB \not\parallel CD (a trapezoid). Show that the angles at A and at B — adjacent to the same parallel side AB — sum to 180°.

Hint 1 — a nudge Treat AB as a transversal of the parallels AD and BC.
Full solution Side AB is a transversal of AD and BC. The angle at A is \angle DAB (interior, on the D-side of AB); the angle at B is \angle ABC (interior, on the C-side of AB). These two are co-interior angles relative to the parallels AD \parallel BC, and so sum to 180°.

Source pointer. Adapted from Givental, Kiselev’s Geometry, Book I — Planimetry, §§88–94 (Sumizdat 2006). Free PDF on cimat.mx. K3 is the standard trapezoid co-interior problem; Kiselev returns to it when classifying quadrilaterals.

Note on transcription. Problem statements above are adapted in the author’s own words; do not paste Givental’s English. The Russian original is public domain — Givental’s translation is not.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A transversal cutting parallel lines makes alternate-interior angles equal, corresponding angles equal, and co-interior angles supplementary. One picture, three identities.
  2. The parallel postulate is the one extra ingredient. Drop it and the construction in Problem 2 still works — but uniqueness, the alt-interior theorem, and the triangle-angle-sum all break.
  3. The triangle’s three angles fill a straight angle. Drawing the parallel through the apex is the canonical move that turns that visual into a proof.

Common confusions

  1. “Two lines that look parallel are parallel.” Visually suggestive isn’t proof. To declare a \parallel b you need either a construction (Problem 2) or an angle-equality (Problem 7).
  2. “The triangle-angle-sum is obvious.” It is true in Euclidean geometry; on a sphere, the angles of a triangle sum to more than 180°. The theorem rests on the parallel postulate — change the postulate, change the theorem.
  3. “Co-interior angles are always supplementary.” Only when the two lines being cut are parallel. Otherwise the two angles sum to something else, depending on how the lines diverge.

Where this matters

Game development. Parallel projection (orthographic camera) and parallax-scrolling backgrounds depend on the property that parallel lines stay parallel under translation. The proof here justifies why those engines can pre-compute alignment once.

Research. The triangle-angle-sum theorem is the cleanest example of a result that fails in non-Euclidean geometry — and that failure is the seed of differential geometry, general relativity, and the modern theory of curved spaces. Where the angle sum lands tells you what world you are in.
