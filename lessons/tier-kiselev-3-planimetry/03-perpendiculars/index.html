Ch 3 — Perpendiculars and Obliques

The shortest path from a point to a line — and why slants grow with their feet

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state the SAS and ASA congruence theorems? See kg1.triangle-congruence.
  2. Are you comfortable proving that the bisectors of an isosceles triangle’s apex angle bisect the base?
  3. Do you know what a right angle is and how to mark one in a figure?
  4. Can you state the triangle inequality in your own words?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

A line \ell horizontal across the page; a point P above it. Drop many segments from P to \ell, hitting different feet. The shortest one is the perpendicular — drag P around to confirm.

The perpendicular (solid blue) is shorter than every slant (dashed grey). The further the foot strays from F, the longer the slant gets.

Wonder

  1. From a point P above a line \ell, how many perpendiculars to \ell pass through P? Exactly one, more, or none?
  2. If two slants from P to \ell have equal length, what does that say about their feet on \ell?
  3. The perpendicular is the shortest. Is there a longest? (Trick question.)
  4. A right triangle has a 90° angle. The two legs determine the hypotenuse. Are two right triangles with equal hypotenuses and one matching leg necessarily congruent?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Exactly one — and the proof uses ASA on a hypothetical “second perpendicular”. (2) Their feet are equidistant from the foot of the perpendicular through P. (3) No — slants can grow arbitrarily as the foot moves. (4) Yes — hypotenuse-leg (HL) is a fourth congruence theorem, special to right triangles.

Discover

Problem 1 — Existence and uniqueness of the perpendicular

Given a line \ell and a point P not on \ell, show that there is exactly one perpendicular to \ell through P.

Hint 1 — a nudge Existence: drop a segment from P that strikes \ell at a right angle. Uniqueness: suppose two perpendiculars PF and PG from P both meet \ell at right angles — argue triangle inequality fails.
Full solution

Existence (Planimetry §57). Construct the perpendicular by reflecting P in \ell to obtain P'; the segment PP' crosses \ell at the foot F at right angles, by symmetry of the reflection.

Uniqueness. Suppose two perpendiculars from P meet \ell at distinct feet F and G. Then \triangle PFG has two right angles at F and G. Adding the third gives \ge 180° before \angle P is even counted — impossible. Hence F = G, and the perpendicular is unique. QED. (Planimetry §59.)

Problem 2 — The perpendicular is the shortest segment

Let PF be the perpendicular from P to \ell (so F is the foot). Let PG be any other segment from P to a point G on \ell, G \ne F. Show that |PF| < |PG|.

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle PFG is a right triangle with right angle at F. The hypotenuse PG is the side opposite the right angle.
Full solution \triangle PFG has \angle F = 90°. So \angle P + \angle G + 90° = 180°, giving \angle G < 90°. The side opposite the larger angle is longer (a corollary of the exterior-angle theorem from Ch 2). \angle F is the largest angle, so |PG| — opposite it — is the longest side. In particular, |PG| > |PF|. QED. (Planimetry §60.)

Problem 3 — Equal slants ⇒ equal projections

From P off line \ell, drop the perpendicular PF. Let PG and PH be two slants of equal length to feet G, H \in \ell with G and H on opposite sides of F. Show that |FG| = |FH|.

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle PFG and \triangle PFH are right triangles sharing leg PF and having equal hypotenuses |PG| = |PH|.
Full solution Both right triangles have the right angle at F, share leg PF, and have equal hypotenuses |PG| = |PH|. By hypotenuse-leg congruence (proved in Problem 5 below), \triangle PFG \cong \triangle PFH. Hence |FG| = |FH| — equal slants project to equally-distant feet. QED. (Planimetry §63.)

Problem 4 — Longer slant ⇔ longer projection

From P off line \ell, F the foot of the perpendicular. Let G, H \in \ell with |FG| < |FH|, both on the same side of F on \ell. Show that |PG| < |PH|.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the Pythagorean theorem (or its inequality version): |PG|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FG|^2.
Full solution Right triangles \triangle PFG and \triangle PFH share leg |PF|. Their other legs are |FG| and |FH|, with |FG| < |FH|. Hypotenuse squared = sum of squared legs: |PG|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FG|^2 < |PF|^2 + |FH|^2 = |PH|^2. Hence |PG| < |PH|. (The Pythagorean theorem is itself proved later in Ch 6 via similarity; for now Kiselev cites the exterior-angle inequality from Ch 2.)

Problem 5 — Hypotenuse-leg (HL) congruence

Two right triangles have equal hypotenuses and one pair of equal legs. Show they are congruent.

Hint 1 — a nudge Lay one on top of the other, matching the equal legs and the right angle. Where can the third vertex go?
Full solution Let \triangle ABC and \triangle A'B'C' have right angles at C and C', with |BC| = |B'C'| (equal legs) and |AB| = |A'B'| (equal hypotenuses). Lay C \to C', B \to B' aligned along the shared leg. The right angles at C and C' force ray CA to align with ray C'A'. Now A is on ray CA at distance |CA| from C; A' is on the same ray at distance |C'A'|. By Pythagoras, |CA|^2 = |AB|^2 - |BC|^2 = |A'B'|^2 - |B'C'|^2 = |C'A'|^2, so |CA| = |C'A'| — and A = A'. Triangles congruent. QED. (Planimetry §73.)

Problem 6 — Locus of equidistant points

Show that the set of points equidistant from two given points A, B is the perpendicular bisector of AB — that is, the line perpendicular to AB through its midpoint M.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two directions: (i) every point on the perpendicular bisector is equidistant; (ii) every equidistant point lies on it. Use SAS for (i) and isosceles for (ii).
Full solution

(i) Let P be on the perpendicular bisector of AB, M the midpoint. \triangle PMA and \triangle PMB: |MA| = |MB| (midpoint), \angle PMA = \angle PMB = 90° (perpendicular), PM shared. By SAS, congruent. So |PA| = |PB| — P is equidistant.

(ii) Conversely, suppose |PA| = |PB|. Then \triangle PAB is isosceles. The median from P — the segment to the midpoint M — is also the altitude (from Ch 2 Problem P3, or the isosceles converse). So PM \perp AB, meaning P lies on the perpendicular bisector. QED. (Planimetry §70 — first locus theorem.)

Pasch’s axiom (1882). When Moritz Pasch rebuilt geometry on rigorous foundations in his Vorlesungen über neuere Geometrie (1882), he discovered that Euclid had silently assumed a fact about betweenness that no axiom captured: if a line enters a triangle through one side, it must leave through one of the other two. This Pasch axiom — together with axioms of order and incidence — is what makes the uniqueness of the perpendicular (Problem 1) fully rigorous: without it, one cannot prove that the foot F is unambiguously on \ell. Hilbert kept Pasch’s axiom in his Grundlagen (1899), where it sits in the order axiom group. Kiselev, like Euclid, uses betweenness implicitly — and it works, because school geometry never tries to fool us.

The perpendicular is the shortest segment from a point to a line — and the unique one that meets at a right angle. Those two facts are the same fact, viewed differently. Closeness and perpendicularity are inseparable: every locus problem that involves “shortest distance” routes through this single insight.

Name

Definitions.

  • Perpendicular from P to line \ell: the unique segment from P to a point F on \ell that meets \ell at a right angle. F is the foot of the perpendicular.
  • Slant (oblique) from P to \ell: any other segment from P to a point G \in \ell, G \ne F.
  • Projection of slant PG onto \ell: the segment FG.
  • Perpendicular bisector of segment AB: the line through the midpoint of AB perpendicular to AB.

Theorems (Planimetry §§57–74).

  1. Existence and uniqueness: through a point P, there is exactly one perpendicular to a given line.
  2. Shortest segment: the perpendicular is shorter than every slant.
  3. Equal slants ⇔ equal projections.
  4. Longer slant ⇔ longer projection.
  5. HL congruence: two right triangles with equal hypotenuse and one equal leg are congruent.
  6. Locus theorem: the set of points equidistant from A and B is the perpendicular bisector of AB.

A verse:

From a point off a line, one perpendicular alone — the shortest path. Slants grow with their feet — equal feet give equal slants, and so on backward. Right triangles match by hypotenuse and a leg — a fourth way to align. And every point that’s fair to two ends sits on their perpendicular spine.

Apply

Worked example

P is a point and \ell a line; the perpendicular from P has foot F with |PF| = 5. A slant PG has |FG| = 12. Find |PG|.

Right triangle \triangle PFG, right-angled at F. By Pythagoras: |PG|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FG|^2 = 25 + 144 = 169. So |PG| = 13.

|PG| = 13.

Practice — Warm

W1. From P, |PF| = 4 (perpendicular). A slant has foot |FG| = 3. Find the slant length.

Full solution \sqrt{16 + 9} = 5.

W2. Two slants from P to \ell have equal length and feet on opposite sides of the perpendicular foot. Their two feet are 7 cm apart. How far is each foot from the perpendicular foot?

Full solution 3.5 cm each. (Equal projections, opposite sides.)

W3. Two right triangles have hypotenuse 13 and one leg 5. Are they congruent?

Full solution Yes — by hypotenuse-leg.

Practice — Working

P1. Three slants from P to \ell have feet at 1, 4, 9 units from the foot F of the perpendicular (|PF| = 6). List the slant lengths in increasing order.

Full solution \sqrt{36 + 1} = \sqrt{37}, \sqrt{36 + 16} = \sqrt{52}, \sqrt{36 + 81} = \sqrt{117}. Increasing as feet move away — which is the longer-slant-with-longer-projection theorem.

P2. Show that the foot of the perpendicular from P to \ell is the point on \ell closest to P.

Full solution Direct application of the shortest segment theorem (Problem 2).

P3. A and B are two points; \ell is a line not passing through either. Find the point X \in \ell minimising |AX| + |XB|.

Hint 1 — a nudge Reflect A across \ell to get A'. The path A \to X \to B has the same length as A' \to X \to B (since |AX| = |A'X| by reflection). The shortest such path is the straight one.
Full solution Reflect A in \ell to A'. Then |AX| = |A'X|, so |AX| + |XB| = |A'X| + |XB| \ge |A'B| with equality iff X is on segment A'B. X = intersection of segment A'B with \ell. This is the Heron’s reflection problem (and the principle behind the law of reflection in optics).

Practice — Stretch

S1. A right triangle has hypotenuse c and legs a, b. Show that the perpendicular from the right-angle vertex to the hypotenuse has length \dfrac{ab}{c}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute the area two ways: \tfrac{1}{2} ab using the legs as base and height; \tfrac{1}{2} c \cdot h using the hypotenuse and the altitude h to it.
Full solution Area = \tfrac{1}{2} ab = \tfrac{1}{2} c h, so h = ab/c. (A staple right-triangle fact, used heavily in similarity in Ch 7.)

S2. Show that the three perpendicular bisectors of a triangle’s sides meet at one point — the circumcentre.

Hint 1 — a nudge Take any two of the bisectors; they meet at some O. Show O lies on the third.
Full solution Let \ell_{AB} be the perpendicular bisector of AB and \ell_{BC} of BC. They meet at some O (they are not parallel because AB and BC are not parallel — they share vertex B). On \ell_{AB}: |OA| = |OB| (locus theorem). On \ell_{BC}: |OB| = |OC|. So |OA| = |OB| = |OC|. The first and last give |OA| = |OC|, so O is on \ell_{CA} — the perpendicular bisector of CA. All three meet at O. (Planimetry §72; this O is the centre of the circumcircle through A, B, C.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Planimetry, §§60–74.

K1. From a point P off line \ell, two equal slants are drawn to feet G and H with |PG| = |PH|. Show that the perpendicular from P to \ell bisects segment GH.

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle PGH is isosceles with |PG| = |PH|. The foot of the perpendicular from P to \ell is…
Full solution \triangle PGH is isosceles with apex P. The altitude from P to GH bisects GH (from Ch 2). The altitude from P to line \ell — which contains G and H — is precisely the perpendicular from P to \ell. So its foot is the midpoint of GH. QED. (Planimetry §63.)

K2. Two perpendiculars from a point P are dropped onto two different lines \ell_1 and \ell_2. The two feet are F_1 on \ell_1 and F_2 on \ell_2. If |PF_1| = |PF_2|, show that P lies on a bisector of an angle formed by \ell_1 and \ell_2.

Hint 1 — a nudge The right triangles \triangle PF_1 X and \triangle PF_2 X — where X is the intersection of \ell_1 and \ell_2 — share hypotenuse PX and have equal legs.
Full solution Let X = \ell_1 \cap \ell_2. The right triangles \triangle PF_1 X (right angle at F_1) and \triangle PF_2 X (right angle at F_2) share hypotenuse PX and have equal legs |PF_1| = |PF_2|. By HL (Problem 5), they are congruent. Hence \angle F_1 X P = \angle F_2 X P — so P lies on the bisector of \angle F_1 X F_2, which is one of the two bisectors of the lines \ell_1, \ell_2. QED. (Planimetry §71 — locus theorem for angle bisectors.)

K3. A point P lies inside an angle. The perpendiculars from P to the two sides of the angle have equal length. Show that P lies on the bisector of the angle.

Full solution This is K2 with the angle’s sides as \ell_1, \ell_2 and the vertex X being the angle’s vertex. By the same HL argument, P lies on the angle bisector. (Planimetry §71.)

Source pointer. Adapted from Givental, Kiselev’s Geometry, Book I — Planimetry, §§60–74 (Sumizdat 2006). Free PDF on cimat.mx. K2 and K3 are the angle-bisector locus exercises that pair with Problem 6’s perpendicular-bisector locus.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. From a point off a line, the perpendicular is the unique shortest path to it. Existence, uniqueness, and shortness are one bundle.
  2. Equal slants ⇔ equal projections; longer slant ⇔ longer projection. The geometry of slants is monotone in the foot’s distance — and that monotonicity is what powers reflection- minimisation problems.
  3. A locus is a set of points, not a single point. The perpendicular bisector of AB is the locus of points equidistant from A and B — and locus thinking is the seed of analytic geometry.

Common confusions

  1. “The perpendicular and the shortest segment are different things.” They are the same thing — that is the content of Problem 2. Many students hold them as separate facts and miss the connection.
  2. “Hypotenuse-leg means SSA.” It does not. HL adds the constraint that the angle opposite the longer of the two given sides (the hypotenuse) is a right angle. That extra constraint eliminates the SSA ambiguity.
  3. “Locus = a single point.” A locus is the set of points satisfying a condition. It can be a line (perpendicular bisector in Problem 6), a circle (locus of points at fixed distance from a centre), or more exotic curves. Locus is plural in character.

Where this matters

Game development. Closest-point-on-a-line-segment is the single most-called geometric query in physics engines and pathfinding. Its answer is exactly the perpendicular foot — clipped to the segment when the foot falls outside its endpoints. Without this lesson’s core fact you cannot write a working collision detector.

Research. The locus characterisation of geometric objects — “the set of points satisfying property X is curve Y” — is the seed of analytic geometry, where curves are defined by equations (which encode the locus property). Descartes’ great move was to pair the synthetic locus with an algebraic equation; this lesson gives the synthetic side.
