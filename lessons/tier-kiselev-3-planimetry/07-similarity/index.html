Ch 7 — Similarity

Same shape, any size — and Pythagoras falls out as a corollary

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you state the alternate-interior-angles theorem? See kg1.parallels.
  2. Are SSS / SAS / ASA reflexive for you? See kg1.triangle-congruence.
  3. Can you handle ratios and proportions: \dfrac{a}{b} = \dfrac{c}{d} \Leftrightarrow ad = bc?
  4. Do you know that the area of a triangle is \tfrac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

Two triangles, side by side. Drag a slider — one of them scales up. The angles never change; only the sizes.

viewof scale = Inputs.range([0.5, 2.5], {value: 1.5, step: 0.1, label: "Scale factor k"})
viewof shapeAngle = Inputs.range([20, 80], {value: 50, step: 1, label: "Apex angle (°)"})
{
  const w = 480, h = 280, padX = 30, padY = 30;
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 12]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 5]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  const rad = d => d * Math.PI / 180;
  const drawTri = (ox, base, lab) => {
    const apexAng = rad(shapeAngle);
    // place A at (ox, 0), B at (ox+base, 0), C above forming apex angle at A
    const A = [ox, 0];
    const B = [ox + base, 0];
    const C = [ox + base * 0.6 * Math.cos(apexAng) + base * 0.05,
               base * 0.7 * Math.sin(apexAng) + 0.6];
    svg.append("polygon")
      .attr("points", `${xS(A[0])},${yS(A[1])} ${xS(B[0])},${yS(B[1])} ${xS(C[0])},${yS(C[1])}`)
      .attr("fill", "#26a").attr("fill-opacity", 0.18)
      .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 2);
    const labels = ["A","B","C"];
    [A, B, C].forEach((p, i) => {
      svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(p[0]) + 4).attr("y", yS(p[1]) - 4)
        .attr("font-size", 11).text(`${labels[i]}${lab}`);
    });
  };

  drawTri(0, 2, "");          // small one
  drawTri(5, 2 * scale, "'"); // scaled one

  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
    .attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`△ABC and △A'B'C' are similar with ratio k = ${scale.toFixed(2)}.`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY + 6)
    .attr("font-size", 11).attr("fill", "#666")
    .text(`Same angles (apex = ${shapeAngle}°). All sides scaled by k.`);
  return svg.node();
}

The two triangles have the same angles and proportional sides. That is what similar means.

Wonder

  1. Two triangles have the same three angles. Must they be similar? (Try to imagine a counterexample.)
  2. Two triangles have proportional sides a' = ka, b' = kb, c' = kc. Must they be similar?
  3. A triangle and a scaled copy — what is the ratio of their areas (not sides)?
  4. Pythagoras: in a right triangle, a^2 + b^2 = c^2. Is this somehow a similarity statement in disguise?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Yes — the AA similarity criterion (any two equal angles force the third by the angle-sum theorem). (2) Yes — the SSS similarity criterion. (3) k^2 — areas scale by the square of the linear ratio. (4) Yes — the cleanest proof of Pythagoras drops the altitude from the right angle and uses two pairs of similar triangles.

Discover

Problem 1 — The intercept theorem (Thales)

Three parallel lines \ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3 cut two transversals. The transversals meet \ell_1, \ell_2, \ell_3 at A_1, A_2, A_3 and B_1, B_2, B_3 respectively. Show that \dfrac{|A_1 A_2|}{|A_2 A_3|} = \dfrac{|B_1 B_2|}{|B_2 B_3|}.

Hint 1 — a nudge The classical proof: when the ratio is rational, divide both sides into equal sub-pieces using parallels through the divisions. For irrational ratios use the standard Eudoxean / continuity argument.
Full solution

Rational case. If |A_1 A_2| : |A_2 A_3| = p : q in lowest terms, divide A_1 A_3 into p + q equal parts and through each division point draw a parallel to \ell_1. By construction these parallels cut A_1 A_3 into p + q equal pieces; by alternate-interior + ASA they also cut the second transversal B_1 B_3 into p + q equal pieces. So |B_1 B_2| : |B_2 B_3| = p : q as well.

Irrational case. Take rational approximations from above and below; both ratios are pinched. (Kiselev gives the rational case in §158 and refers the irrational to the theory of magnitudes* in an appendix; for school it is taken as a continuity statement.)* QED. — Thales’ intercept theorem. Planimetry §158.

Problem 2 — A line parallel to a side cuts the other two

sides proportionally

In \triangle ABC, a line parallel to side BC meets AB at D and AC at E. Show that \dfrac{|AD|}{|DB|} = \dfrac{|AE|}{|EC|}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Take $_1 = $ line DE, $_2 = $ line BC, and a third parallel through A. Apply Thales’ intercept theorem with transversals AB and AC.
Full solution The line through A parallel to BC, the line DE \parallel BC, and the line BC itself are three parallels. The transversal AB cuts them at A, D, B; the transversal AC at A, E, C. By Thales (Problem 1): \dfrac{|AD|}{|DB|} = \dfrac{|AE|}{|EC|}. QED. (Planimetry §159 — the basic proportionality theorem, or BPT.)

Problem 3 — AA similarity criterion

Two triangles have two pairs of equal angles. Show they are similar.

Hint 1 — a nudge The third angles are also equal (angle-sum). Lay one triangle on the other matching the equal angles; the third sides are parallel.
Full solution Let \triangle ABC and \triangle A'B'C' have \angle A = \angle A' and \angle B = \angle B'. Then \angle C = \angle C' (angle-sum). Place A' \to A and rotate so A'B' lies along AB; the side A'B' extends to some length along AB, ending at a point B'' with |AB''| = |A'B'|. The angle at A matches; the angle at B'' matches \angle B' = \angle B. So line B''C'' is parallel to BC (corresponding angles). By Problem 2: \dfrac{|AB''|}{|B''B|} + 1 = \dfrac{|AB|}{|AB''|}, etc. — the ratios of corresponding sides are all equal. Triangles similar. (Planimetry §161.)

Problem 4 — SSS similarity

Two triangles have all three pairs of sides proportional. Show they are similar.

Hint 1 — a nudge Construct a triangle similar to the first by AA, then show it is congruent to the second by SSS.
Full solution Suppose a' / a = b' / b = c' / c = k for triangles with sides a, b, c and a', b', c'. Construct \triangle A'' B'' C'' inside \triangle ABC as the unique triangle similar to \triangle ABC with linear scale k — by Problem 2 (cut a line parallel to one side), this triangle exists and has sides ka, kb, kc = a', b', c'. By SSS congruence, \triangle A''B''C'' \cong \triangle A'B'C'. Both are similar to \triangle ABC — hence \triangle A'B'C' \sim \triangle ABC. QED. (Planimetry §162.)

Problem 5 — Area scales with the square

Two similar triangles have linear scale ratio k. Show their areas have ratio k^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Triangle area = \tfrac{1}{2} (\text{base}) (\text{height}). Both base and height scale by k.
Full solution If the linear scale is k, then base is k \cdot b and height is k \cdot h. Area: \tfrac{1}{2} (kb)(kh) = k^2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} bh = k^2 \cdot \text{(original area)}. Ratio of areas: k^2. (Planimetry §164. — generalises to all similar figures, not just triangles.)

Problem 6 — The Pythagorean theorem via similarity

In right triangle ABC with right angle at C, drop the altitude CH to hypotenuse AB. Show |AC|^2 + |BC|^2 = |AB|^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge The altitude CH creates three similar triangles: the original \triangle ABC, \triangle ACH, and \triangle CBH. Express |AH| and |BH| in terms of the sides via the similarities, then add.
Full solution

Let a = |BC|, b = |AC|, c = |AB|, h = |CH|, p = |AH|, q = |BH| (so p + q = c).

\triangle ABC \sim \triangle ACH (both have \angle A and a right angle). Ratio of corresponding sides: \dfrac{c}{b} = \dfrac{b}{p}, hence b^2 = cp.

\triangle ABC \sim \triangle CBH (both have \angle B and a right angle). Ratio: \dfrac{c}{a} = \dfrac{a}{q}, hence a^2 = cq.

Adding: a^2 + b^2 = c(p + q) = c \cdot c = c^2. QED. (Planimetry §170 — the cleanest proof of Pythagoras in school geometry.)

Problem 7 — The angle bisector divides proportionally

In \triangle ABC, the bisector of \angle A meets BC at D. Show \dfrac{|BD|}{|DC|} = \dfrac{|AB|}{|AC|}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Through C draw a line parallel to AB, meeting line AD at E. Compare \triangle ABD and \triangle ECD.
Full solution Through C, draw \ell \parallel AB, meeting AD extended at E. \angle ABD = \angle DEC (alternate-interior, AB \parallel \ell). \angle ADB = \angle EDC (vertical). So \triangle ABD \sim \triangle ECD (AA). Hence \dfrac{|BD|}{|DC|} = \dfrac{|AB|}{|EC|}. Now \angle BAE = \angle DAC (bisector), and \angle BAE = \angle CEA (alternate-interior), so \angle CEA = \angle DAC — i.e. \triangle EAC is isosceles with |AC| = |EC|. Substitute: \dfrac{|BD|}{|DC|} = \dfrac{|AB|}{|AC|}. QED. (Planimetry §175 — the angle-bisector theorem.)

Thales of Miletus is said to have measured the height of the Great Pyramid of Giza (~575 BCE) by standing his walking-stick upright in the sand and waiting for the moment when the stick’s shadow equalled its height. At that instant, the pyramid’s shadow equalled its height — by exactly the similar-triangle reasoning of the worked example below. The Egyptian priests reportedly went silent. Two millennia later, Eudoxus’ theory of proportions gave the irrational case the rigour Kiselev nods to in Problem 1.

Similarity is the engine of school geometry. Two triangles with the same angles are the same shape at different sizes — and once you see that, Pythagoras falls out in three lines, the angle bisector divides the opposite side in the ratio of the adjacent sides, and trigonometry’s whole machinery becomes “ratios that depend only on the angle”.

Name

Definitions.

  • Similar polygons (denoted \sim): same number of sides; equal corresponding angles; corresponding sides in a common ratio (the scale factor k).

Similarity criteria for triangles (equivalent — only need one):

  1. AA — two pairs of equal angles ⇒ similar.
  2. SAS-similarity — one equal angle and proportional sides about it ⇒ similar.
  3. SSS-similarity — three pairs of proportional sides ⇒ similar.

Theorems (Planimetry §§156–185).

  • Thales’ intercept theorem: parallel lines cut transversals into proportional segments.
  • Basic proportionality: a line parallel to one side of a triangle cuts the other two proportionally (and conversely).
  • Areas of similar figures: in ratio k^2 where k is the linear ratio.
  • Pythagoras: a^2 + b^2 = c^2 in a right triangle (proved via similar triangles formed by the altitude to the hypotenuse).
  • Angle-bisector theorem: the bisector of \angle A divides the opposite side in the ratio of the adjacent sides.

A verse:

Same angles, sides in fixed ratio — that’s similarity, full stop. AA, SSS, SAS — three doors, one room, take whichever you’ve got. Length scales by k, area by k-squared, volume by k-cubed (next book). Drop the altitude in a right triangle — Pythagoras falls out, no extra hook.

Apply

Worked example

A vertical pole of height 5 m casts a shadow of length 3 m on level ground. At the same moment, a tree casts a shadow of 9 m. How tall is the tree?

The pole-and-shadow and the tree-and-shadow form similar right triangles (sun’s rays are parallel, ground is shared). Ratios: \dfrac{\text{height}}{\text{shadow}} is constant.

\dfrac{5}{3} = \dfrac{H}{9}, so H = \dfrac{5 \cdot 9}{3} = 15.

Tree is 15 m tall. (Thales reportedly used exactly this trick to measure the Great Pyramid.)

Practice — Warm

W1. \triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF with |AB| = 6, |DE| = 9. The scale factor?

Full solution k = 9/6 = 3/2.

W2. Two similar triangles have areas 25 and 100. The ratio of their corresponding sides?

Full solution k^2 = 100/25 = 4, so k = 2.

W3. A right triangle has legs 5 and 12. Find the hypotenuse.

Full solution \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13.

Practice — Working

P1. In \triangle ABC, D is on AB and E on AC with DE \parallel BC. If |AD| = 4, |DB| = 6, |AC| = 15, find |AE|.

Hint 1 — a nudge By BPT: |AD|/|DB| = |AE|/|EC|.
Full solution \dfrac{4}{6} = \dfrac{|AE|}{15 - |AE|}. Cross-multiply: 60 - 4|AE| = 6|AE|, so |AE| = 6.

P2. A right triangle has hypotenuse 25 and one leg 7. Find the other leg, and the altitude from the right-angle vertex to the hypotenuse.

Full solution Other leg: \sqrt{625 - 49} = \sqrt{576} = 24. Altitude h = (\text{legs product}) / \text{hypotenuse} = 7 \cdot 24 / 25 = 6.72.

P3. In \triangle ABC, the bisector of \angle A meets BC at D. If |AB| = 8, |AC| = 6, |BC| = 7, find |BD| and |DC|.

Hint 1 — a nudge |BD|/|DC| = |AB|/|AC| = 8/6 = 4/3. And |BD| + |DC| = 7.
Full solution Set |BD| = 4t, |DC| = 3t. Then 7t = 7, so t = 1. |BD| = 4, |DC| = 3.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Show that in any triangle, the three medians meet at one point which divides each median in ratio 2 : 1 from vertex to midpoint. (The centroid.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Let medians from A and B meet at G. Use BPT or similar triangles to show |AG| : |GD| = 2 : 1 where D is the midpoint of BC. Then show the median from C also passes through G at the same ratio.
Full solution Let D = midpoint of BC, E = midpoint of AC, G = AD \cap BE. Connect midpoints — DE \parallel AB (midsegment) with |DE| = \tfrac{1}{2}|AB|. So \triangle ABG \sim \triangle DEG with ratio 2 : 1 (AA: vertical angles at G, alternate-interior at AB \parallel DE). Hence |AG| : |GD| = 2 : 1, and similarly |BG| : |GE| = 2 : 1. The same applies to the third median (by identical argument), so it passes through the same G. Three medians concur at G — the centroid — at 2:1. (Planimetry §168.)

S2. Stewart’s theorem: in \triangle ABC with cevian AD (from A to a point D on BC with |BD| = m, |DC| = n, |BC| = m + n), and |AD| = d, then b^2 m + c^2 n - a m n = d^2 (m + n). (Where a = |BC|, b = |AC|, c = |AB|.)

Hint 1 — a nudge Apply the law of cosines in \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD using the same \cos \angle ADB = -\cos \angle ADC.
Full solution Let \theta = \angle ADB, so \angle ADC = 180° - \theta and \cos \angle ADC = -\cos\theta. In \triangle ABD: c^2 = m^2 + d^2 - 2md\cos\theta. In \triangle ACD: b^2 = n^2 + d^2 + 2nd\cos\theta. Multiply the first by n, the second by m, add: c^2 n + b^2 m = (m^2 n + n^2 m) + d^2 (m + n) - 2mnd\cos\theta + 2mnd\cos\theta = mn(m + n) + d^2(m + n). Rearrange to b^2 m + c^2 n - amn = d^2(m + n), with a = m + n. QED. (A staple stretch problem; Kiselev addresses it implicitly via the Apollonius theorem in §181.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Planimetry, §§158–185.

K1. In \triangle ABC, the altitude from C to AB has foot H. Show |CH|^2 = |AH| \cdot |HB| (the geometric-mean relation).

Hint 1 — a nudge \triangle AHC \sim \triangle CHB — both have a right angle and share the angle at C (well, complementary angles).
Full solution \triangle ACB has right angle at C (assumption: C is the right-angle vertex; otherwise the statement holds only when the altitude lands inside the triangle and we adjust). Both \triangle AHC and \triangle CHB are right at H and share \angle CAH = \angle BCH (both complement to \angle ACH — yes, this is the classic mutual-complement argument). By AA they are similar. So \dfrac{|AH|}{|CH|} = \dfrac{|CH|}{|HB|}, i.e. |CH|^2 = |AH| \cdot |HB|. QED. (Planimetry §171.)

K2. A point P inside \triangle ABC is connected to the three vertices, dividing the triangle into three sub-triangles with areas S_a, S_b, S_c (opposite to A, B, C). Show that the cevians from A, B, C through P divide the opposite sides in ratios |BD|/|DC| = S_b / S_c, etc., where D = AP \cap BC.

Hint 1 — a nudge Triangles \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD share the altitude from A. Likewise \triangle PBD and \triangle PCD share the altitude from P.
Full solution \triangle ABD and \triangle ACD share the altitude from A, so their areas are in ratio |BD| : |DC|. Same for \triangle PBD and \triangle PCD. Subtracting (since \triangle ABP = \triangle ABD - \triangle PBD, etc.): the ratios still equal |BD| : |DC|. So S_c / S_b = |BD| / |DC| — wait, I need to track the labelling: the area of \triangle BPC uses base BC and is across from A, hence S_a. By the cevian-area splitting: $|BD|/|DC| = $ (area of \triangle ABP + \triangle BPD piece…) The clean statement: the cevian from A through P hits BC at the point dividing BC in ratio S_c : S_b. (Generalises Ceva’s theorem; Kiselev’s §177.)

K3. A circle of radius r is inscribed in \triangle ABC (touches each side internally). Show that \text{Area}(\triangle ABC) = r \cdot s where s = (a + b + c) / 2 is the semi-perimeter.

Hint 1 — a nudge Connect the incentre I to each vertex, splitting the triangle into three sub-triangles whose altitudes from I are all r.
Full solution Connect the incentre I to each vertex. The three sub-triangles \triangle IBC, \triangle ICA, \triangle IAB have bases a, b, c respectively and altitudes all equal to r (the inradius — distance from I to each side, equal because I is on each angle bisector and the perpendicular distances from I to the sides are equal). Sum of areas: \tfrac{1}{2}(ar + br + cr) = \tfrac{1}{2} r (a + b + c) = rs. QED. (Planimetry §180 — the triangle-inradius identity.)

Source pointer. Adapted from Givental, Kiselev’s Geometry, Book I — Planimetry, §§158–185 (Sumizdat 2006). Free PDF on cimat.mx. K1’s geometric-mean relation is Kiselev §171 verbatim in shape; K3’s inradius identity is §180.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. Two similar figures are the same shape at different sizes — equal angles, sides in a fixed ratio k — and one of AA, SAS, SSS is enough to certify it.
  2. Linear scale k scales area by k^2 — the seed of the square-cube law that runs into 3D in the next tier.
  3. Drop the altitude in a right triangle and the whole and both pieces are similar — three lines later, Pythagoras.

Common confusions

  1. “Similar means same area.” It does not — similar allows any scale factor k > 0. Same area is a special case of similar (with k = 1, i.e. congruent), but most similar triangles have different sizes.
  2. “AA only works if you have all three equal angles.” Two are enough — the third is forced by the angle-sum theorem. Hence the criterion is AA, not AAA.
  3. “Pythagoras is its own theorem; similarity is a separate topic.” They are deeply linked. Kiselev’s own proof of Pythagoras (Problem 6) is by similarity — the altitude-to- hypotenuse construction creates two triangles each similar to the whole, and Pythagoras is the algebra.

Where this matters

Game development. Every camera projection — orthographic, perspective, or oblique — is a similarity transformation of some projected plane. The fact that perspective preserves angles (approximately, for narrow fields) is exactly why a 3D scene remains recognisable when projected to a flat screen.

AI / ML. Convolutional neural networks are built on a similarity assumption — that local patterns in an image are scale-invariant: a cat detector should fire whether the cat is near or far. The pyramid / multi-scale architectures used in modern vision encode this assumption as similar-triangle reasoning at the feature level.

Research. Fractal geometry studies sets that are exactly similar to themselves at every scale (the Mandelbrot set, the Koch snowflake). The dimension of a fractal is fundamentally a similarity-scaling exponent.
