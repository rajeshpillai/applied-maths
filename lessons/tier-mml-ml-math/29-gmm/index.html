29 · Gaussian Mixture Models and MLE

A weighted average of Gaussians: simple to write, surprisingly hard to fit, and the workhorse of clustering / density estimation.

Pre-flight check

Before you start, you should be able to say yes to all four:

  1. Can you state the multivariate Gaussian density? — see MML — 18, Gaussian Summary Stats.
  2. Do you know what MLE is? — see MML — 17 / 19 / 24.
  3. Can you state the chain rule for graphical models? — MML — 23.
  4. Can you take a derivative of a sum of exponentials?

If any answer is no, follow the link before continuing.

Touch

A mixture of three 1D Gaussians. The widget plots the joint density and the contribution of each component.

Black is the mixture density; coloured are the components weighted by \pi_k. The total is a multimodal density — and a GMM’s MLE has to find these modes from data alone.

Wonder

  1. A GMM with K components can model arbitrarily complex (1D) densities by stacking enough Gaussians. Why doesn’t it overfit catastrophically?
  2. The MLE of a GMM doesn’t have a closed form (unlike Gaussian, linear regression, or PPCA). Why is this?
  3. The “responsibilities” — the posterior probability that point \boldsymbol{x}_n came from component k — are continuous. How does this relate to soft clustering?
  4. K-means is a special case of GMM. What does K-means assume that GMMs don’t?

Discover

Five problems building GMM.

D1. Write the GMM density: p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k), with \pi_k \ge 0, \sum \pi_k = 1.

Full solution A weighted sum of K Gaussians, with weights summing to 1 — i.e. a probability distribution. The model has K - 1 + KD + K \cdot D(D+1)/2 parameters for general covariance, or K - 1 + KD + K for shared isotropic. Plenty of flexibility.

D2. Show the GMM as a directed graphical model: latent z_n \in \{1, \dots, K\} with p(z_n = k) = \pi_k; conditional \boldsymbol{x}_n \mid z_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{z_n}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{z_n}).

Full solution p(\boldsymbol{x}_n) = \sum_k p(z_n = k) p(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid z_n = k) = \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k). Match. The latent indicator z_n tells us which component generated \boldsymbol{x}_n. Inferring z_n from \boldsymbol{x}_n is the clustering problem.

D3. The responsibility r_{nk} = p(z_n = k \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n) is given by Bayes: r_{nk} = \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_{j} \pi_j \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)}. Verify r_{nk} \in [0, 1] and \sum_k r_{nk} = 1.

Full solution Numerator and denominator are positive; ratio is in [0, 1]. Summing over k: numerators sum to denominator. Responsibilities are a posterior distribution over which component generated \boldsymbol{x}_n. Soft clustering: each point is fractionally assigned to clusters.

D4. Why doesn’t the MLE of GMM parameters have a closed form?

Hint 1 — a nudge Take \log of GMM density: \log \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\cdot). The log of a sum cannot be simplified — partials wrt \boldsymbol{\mu}_k involve responsibilities.
Full solution The log-likelihood involves \log \sum_k — the log of a sum is not the sum of logs. Setting \nabla = 0 gives equations where \boldsymbol{\mu}_k depends on responsibilities r_{nk}, which themselves depend on \boldsymbol{\mu}_k. A chicken-and-egg problem — closed form impossible, but EM (Lesson 30) solves it iteratively.

D5. Show that K-means is the limiting case of GMM-EM as \sigma \to 0 with shared isotropic covariance \sigma^2 \boldsymbol{I}.

Full solution As \sigma \to 0, the Gaussian becomes a “delta-spike.” Responsibilities r_{nk} \to 1 for the closest cluster centre, 0 for the others — hard assignment. The M-step for \boldsymbol{\mu}_k becomes the centroid of points assigned to k — K-means update. K-means is GMM with hard responsibilities and shared spherical covariance.

K-means clustering dates to MacQueen, 1967 and earlier (Steinhaus, 1956). Gaussian Mixture Models as a unifying probabilistic framework were synthesised by Dempster, Laird & Rubin, 1977 in their famous paper Maximum Likelihood from Incomplete Data via the EM Algorithm — which gave the EM algorithm its name and modern formulation. The DLR 1977 paper is one of the most-cited statistical papers ever, and the EM algorithm fits more models than just GMMs (HMMs, mixed-effect regression, mixture of experts, etc.).

A GMM models density as a sum of Gaussians. Latent indicators z_n “name” which Gaussian generated each point; responsibilities r_{nk} are the posterior over those names. The MLE has no closed form — the log-sum is intractable. EM solves it via alternating E (compute responsibilities) and M (re-fit Gaussians) — Lesson 30.

Name

Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) density: p(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x} \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k), with mixing coefficients \pi_k \ge 0, \sum_k \pi_k = 1, component means \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, and component covariances \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k.

Latent-variable representation: z_n \in \{1, \dots, K\} a discrete latent with p(z_n = k) = \pi_k; \boldsymbol{x}_n \mid z_n = k \sim \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k).

Responsibility of component k for point \boldsymbol{x}_n: r_{nk} = p(z_n = k \mid \boldsymbol{x}_n, \boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k)}{\sum_j \pi_j \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_j, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j)}.

MLE has no closed form — log-likelihood contains \log \sum_k. Standard fitting: EM algorithm (next lesson).

Soft clustering: r_{nk} is a fractional assignment. Hard clustering (K-means): r_{nk} \in \{0, 1\}, achieved as \sigma \to 0 in GMM with shared isotropic covariance.

Identifiability: GMM is non-identifiable up to label permutation — relabeling the components gives the same density.

Sūtra. GMM = weighted sum of Gaussians. Responsibilities = posterior over components. K-means = hard limit.

Watch out:

  1. “More components is always better.” — Each Gaussian is a flexible component; with K = N (one Gaussian per data point), training likelihood goes to infinity (singular covariance). Always regularise or cap K via BIC, cross-validation, or Bayesian model averaging.
  2. “K-means and GMM give the same answer.” — Only when clusters are spherical, well-separated, and same size. Otherwise GMM (with general covariance) handles elongated, overlapping, or unbalanced clusters much better.
  3. “Responsibilities equal class labels.” — They equal posterior probabilities. Hard label = argmax responsibility; but the posterior carries uncertainty information argmax discards.

Apply

Worked example

A 1D dataset \boldsymbol{x} = \{-2, -1.9, -1.1, 0.1, 0.0, 2.0, 2.1\} — visually 3 clusters. Initial GMM: \pi = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3), \mu = (-2, 0, 2), \sigma = 0.5.

Step 1. Compute initial responsibilities for \boldsymbol{x}_1 = -2: \mathcal{N}(-2 \mid -2, 0.25) \approx 0.798, \mathcal{N}(-2 \mid 0, 0.25) \approx 0, \mathcal{N}(-2 \mid 2, 0.25) \approx 0. r_{11} \approx 1, r_{12} \approx 0, r_{13} \approx 0. (Very confidently component 1.)

Step 2. Same for \boldsymbol{x}_3 = -1.1: \mathcal{N}(-1.1 \mid -2, 0.25) \approx 0.034, \mathcal{N}(-1.1 \mid 0, 0.25) \approx 0.087. r_{31} \approx 0.28, r_{32} \approx 0.72.

Step 3. The point \boldsymbol{x}_3 = -1.1 is uncertain between components 1 and 2 — closer to 0 than to -2 if we trust the standard deviations. Soft assignment captures this.

Conclusion: Even at initialisation, we can compute responsibilities. To improve the fit, we update \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k, \pi_k given responsibilities — the M-step of EM (Lesson 30).

Warm

W1. 2-component GMM in 1D. Identifiable under permutation?

Full solution \pi_1 \leftrightarrow \pi_2, \mu_1 \leftrightarrow \mu_2, \sigma_1 \leftrightarrow \sigma_2 gives the same density. Label-switching is the practical consequence — averages across runs become meaningless.

W2. Why is full covariance per component sometimes a bad idea?

Full solution K \cdot D(D+1)/2 parameters can explode. For D = 100, K = 10: 50,500 covariance params. With N = 200 data points, severe overfitting. Use diagonal or tied/shared covariance instead.

W3. A 1-component GMM is what?

Full solution A single Gaussian. MLE is sample mean and sample covariance.

Working

Wo1. Show the GMM is a universal density approximator: given enough components, any smooth density can be approximated arbitrarily well in L^1.

Full solution A smooth density can be approximated locally by Gaussians at many scales. Refining the partition makes each piece more Gaussian; summing reconstructs the original. Approximation theorem — Kostantinos, 2000. Conceptually: GMMs are dense in densities. In practice, you don’t need many components for reasonable approximations.

Wo2. Show why singularities arise in GMM MLE: a component shrinking to a single data point makes the likelihood infinite.

Full solution If \boldsymbol{\mu}_k = \boldsymbol{x}_n and \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k \to \boldsymbol{0} along some direction, \mathcal{N}(\boldsymbol{x}_n \mid \boldsymbol{\mu}_k, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_k) \to \infty. The likelihood explodes. MLE is ill-posed without regularisation. Fixes: prior on \boldsymbol{\Sigma}, lower bound on smallest eigenvalue, MAP rather than MLE.

Wo3. State the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) for GMM model selection.

Full solution \mathrm{BIC} = -2 \log L^* + k \log N, where k is the number of free parameters of the GMM with K components. For full-covariance K-component GMM in D dimensions: k = K(D + D(D+1)/2) + (K-1). BIC favours smaller models when their fit is comparable.

Stretch

S1. Variational Bayesian GMM (Bishop 2006 chapter 10): apply variational inference to a Bayesian GMM with priors on \pi, \boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma}. Argue why it can automatically determine the number of components.

Full solution With a Dirichlet prior on \pi (with concentration < 1), the variational posterior tends to prune unused components — their \pi_k collapses to 0, effectively removing them. Bayesian GMM does model selection inside inference, avoiding the manual BIC search.

S2. DPMM (Dirichlet Process Mixture Model) is a nonparametric GMM with possibly infinite components. Why is it useful?

Full solution A DPMM has a prior over partitions that grows the number of clusters with data — never has to choose K in advance. Inference (via Gibbs sampling, slice sampling, or variational methods) automatically discovers the “right” K for the observed data. Used in topic modeling, image segmentation, exploratory clustering when K is genuinely unknown.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A GMM is a weighted sum of Gaussians; latent z_n tells us which component generated each point.
  2. Responsibilities r_{nk} are posterior probabilities of component k given \boldsymbol{x}_n — soft cluster assignments.
  3. MLE has no closed form (log of a sum); EM solves it iteratively. K-means is the \sigma \to 0 hard limit.
