Ch 2 — Divisibility, Primes, GCD and LCM

Why every number factors uniquely into primes — and how Euclid’s algorithm finds the GCD in seconds

Pre-flight check

  1. Are the four operations on whole numbers automatic? See kar.whole-numbers.
  2. Can you do long division with remainder?
  3. Are you comfortable saying “15 is divisible by 5” and “5 is a factor of 15”?
  4. Can you list the divisors of 12 (1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12)?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

The number 30. List all its divisors: 1, 2, 3, 5, 6, 10, 15, 30. Eight numbers. The structure of those divisors is hidden in the prime factorisation 30 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5.

viewof n = Inputs.range([2, 100], {value: 30, step: 1, label: "n"})
{
  const divisors = [];
  for (let d = 1; d <= n; d++) if (n % d === 0) divisors.push(d);
  let m = n, factors = {};
  let p = 2;
  while (m > 1) {
    while (m % p === 0) { factors[p] = (factors[p] || 0) + 1; m /= p; }
    p++;
  }
  const fs = Object.entries(factors).map(([p, e]) => e === 1 ? p : `${p}^${e}`).join(" · ");
  return `**${n}**

Divisors (${divisors.length}): ${divisors.join(", ")}.

Prime factorisation: **${fs}**.`;
}

The number of divisors equals the product of (exponent + 1) over all primes — for 30 = 2^1 \cdot 3^1 \cdot 5^1, that’s 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 8.

Wonder

  1. Some numbers have only two divisors (1 and itself). What are they called?
  2. Every whole number > 1 can be written as a product of primes. Is the factorisation unique?
  3. The GCD (greatest common divisor) of 12 and 18 is 6. How would you find the GCD of a much larger pair, like 1071 and 462?
  4. The LCM (least common multiple) of 4 and 6 is 12. What is the relationship between GCD and LCM?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Primes: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, \ldots (2) Yes — up to ordering. This is the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.
  2. Euclid’s algorithm: repeatedly replace the larger by its remainder mod the smaller. (4) For any a, b: \gcd(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) = a \cdot b.

Discover

Problem 1 — Divisibility rules

State and verify: - n is divisible by 2 iff its last digit is even. - n is divisible by 3 iff its digit sum is divisible by 3. - n is divisible by 5 iff its last digit is 0 or 5. - n is divisible by 9 iff its digit sum is divisible by 9.

Full solution

Divisibility by 2 and 5 follows from 10 = 2 \cdot 5, so place- value contributions \ge 10 are always divisible by both. The last-digit test reduces n mod 10.

Divisibility by 3 and 9 follows from 10 \equiv 1 \pmod 9 (and mod 3), so n \equiv \text{(digit sum)} \pmod 9 — and hence mod 3.

(Algebra II §44 — modular arithmetic gives a cleaner proof.)

Problem 2 — Primes

A whole number > 1 is prime if its only positive divisors are 1 and itself. List all primes up to 20.

Full solution 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19. Eight primes up to 20. (The sieve of Eratosthenes finds primes by crossing out multiples of 2, 3, 5, … up to \sqrt n.)

Problem 3 — Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

State (and believe) the fundamental theorem: every whole number n \ge 2 factors uniquely into primes (up to ordering).

Hint 1 — a nudge Existence: iterative factoring (try smaller primes; if none, n is prime). Uniqueness: hardest to prove — uses Euclid’s lemma (if p \mid a b then p \mid a or p \mid b).
Full solution

Existence. Induction: if n has a prime factor p, then n/p is smaller and (by induction) factors into primes; combined with p, so does n.

Uniqueness. Suppose n = p_1 \cdots p_k = q_1 \cdots q_l with all p_i, q_j prime. Then p_1 \mid q_1 q_2 \cdots q_l. By Euclid’s lemma (a prime dividing a product divides one of the factors), p_1 \mid q_j for some j. Both prime, so p_1 = q_j. Cancel and recurse. The two factorisations match up to permutation. (Arithmetic §62 — Euclid’s Elements, Book IX, ~300 BCE.)

Application: 30 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 — and no other primes, in any order, multiply to 30.

Problem 4 — Euclid’s algorithm for GCD

Compute \gcd(1071, 462) by repeatedly replacing the larger by its remainder mod the smaller.

Hint 1 — a nudge 1071 = 462 \cdot 2 + 147. So \gcd(1071, 462) = \gcd(462, 147). Continue.
Full solution

1071 = 462 \cdot 2 + 147 → \gcd(462, 147). 462 = 147 \cdot 3 + 21 → \gcd(147, 21). 147 = 21 \cdot 7 + 0 → \gcd(21, 0) = 21.

\gcd(1071, 462) = 21.

(Verify: 1071 = 21 \cdot 51, 462 = 21 \cdot 22, and \gcd(51, 22) = 1 — confirming 21 is the greatest common divisor.) (Arithmetic §66 — Euclid’s algorithm, ~300 BCE.)

Problem 5 — Why Euclid’s algorithm works

Show that for a > b > 0: \gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b).

Full solution a = b q + r. Any common divisor d of a, b also divides r = a - b q. Conversely, any common divisor of b, r divides a = b q + r. So the set of common divisors of \{a, b\} equals the set of common divisors of \{b, r\}. Their greatest element is hence the same. QED. (Arithmetic §67.)

Problem 6 — LCM via prime factorisation

Compute \text{lcm}(12, 18) via prime factorisation.

Hint 1 — a nudge 12 = 2^2 \cdot 3, 18 = 2 \cdot 3^2. LCM = product of highest prime powers.
Full solution

LCM = 2^2 \cdot 3^2 = 4 \cdot 9 = 36. (Highest power of 2 in either: 2^2. Highest power of 3: 3^2.) \text{lcm}(12, 18) = 36.

GCD = product of lowest prime powers: 2^1 \cdot 3^1 = 6. Verify: \gcd \cdot \text{lcm} = 6 \cdot 36 = 216 = 12 \cdot 18. ✓

Problem 7 — GCD-LCM relation

For any a, b > 0: \gcd(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) = a \cdot b.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the prime-factorisation viewpoint: write a = \prod p_i^{a_i} and b = \prod p_i^{b_i}. GCD has exponent \min(a_i, b_i); LCM has \max. Sum: \min + \max = a_i + b_i.
Full solution For each prime p: \min(a_p, b_p) + \max(a_p, b_p) = a_p + b_p. So the prime factorisation of \gcd \cdot \text{lcm} matches that of a \cdot b exponent-by-exponent. QED. (Arithmetic §72.)

Euclid’s Elements (~300 BCE) contains both of this lesson’s big ideas: the algorithm for the GCD (Book VII, Propositions 1–2) and the proof that there are infinitely many primes (Book IX, Proposition 20). The same two pages have driven number theory for twenty-three centuries — straight through to RSA encryption, which uses Euclid’s algorithm millions of times a second to negotiate TLS keys. Few books in any field have a longer working life.

Every whole number greater than 1 has exactly one prime fingerprint. The factorisation 30 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 is the only way to write 30 as a product of primes (up to ordering). This uniqueness is what makes divisibility predictable — and what RSA encryption secretly bets your bank balance on.

Name

Definitions.

  • Divisor / factor. d \mid n means n = d k for some integer k.
  • Prime number. p > 1 with no divisors other than 1 and p. Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots
  • Composite number. n > 1 that is not prime — has a divisor strictly between 1 and n.
  • GCD (\gcd(a, b)): largest whole number dividing both.
  • LCM (\text{lcm}(a, b)): smallest whole number that is a multiple of both.
  • Coprime (relatively prime): \gcd(a, b) = 1.

Theorems.

  1. Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic. Every whole number n \ge 2 has a unique prime factorisation (up to ordering).
  2. Euclid’s algorithm. \gcd(a, b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b), terminating at \gcd(d, 0) = d.
  3. GCD-LCM relation. \gcd(a, b) \cdot \text{lcm}(a, b) = a \cdot b.
  4. Euclid’s lemma. If p is prime and p \mid a b, then p \mid a or p \mid b.

Divisibility shortcuts (base 10).

By Test
2 last digit even
3 digit sum divisible by 3
4 last two digits form a multiple of 4
5 last digit 0 or 5
6 divisible by both 2 and 3
9 digit sum divisible by 9
10 last digit 0
11 alternating digit sum divisible by 11

A verse:

Every number breaks into primes — that’s the fundamental rule. Euclid’s swap of larger for remainder finds the GCD as a tool. GCD times LCM is the product itself — remember the symmetry. And primes go on forever, by Euclid’s two-line eternity.

Apply

Worked example

Find \gcd(252, 105) and \text{lcm}(252, 105).

GCD by Euclid: 252 = 105 \cdot 2 + 42 105 = 42 \cdot 2 + 21 42 = 21 \cdot 2 + 0 \gcd(252, 105) = 21.

LCM: \dfrac{252 \cdot 105}{21} = \dfrac{26460}{21} = 1260.

Practice — Warm

W1. Is 2348 divisible by 4?

Full solution Last two digits: 48. 48 / 4 = 12. Yes.

W2. List the prime factorisation of 84.

Full solution 84 = 2 \cdot 42 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 21 = 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 7.

W3. \gcd(15, 25)?

Full solution 5.

Practice — Working

P1. Find all positive divisors of 36.

Hint 1 — a nudge 36 = 2^2 \cdot 3^2. Divisor count: 3 \cdot 3 = 9.
Full solution 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 9, 12, 18, 36. Nine divisors.

P2. Compute \gcd(360, 252).

Full solution 360 = 252 \cdot 1 + 108. 252 = 108 \cdot 2 + 36. 108 = 36 \cdot 3 + 0. \gcd = 36.

P3. Show that 7 is prime — i.e., it has no divisors other than 1 and 7.

Hint 1 — a nudge Test divisibility by 2, 3, 5 (you only need primes up to \sqrt 7 \approx 2.6, so just 2).
Full solution 7 is odd (not divisible by 2), digit sum 7 (not by 3), last digit 7 (not by 5). Since \sqrt 7 < 3, we only need to test up to 2. 7 is prime.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Show that there are infinitely many primes.

Hint 1 — a nudge Euclid’s proof (300 BCE). Suppose finitely many primes p_1, \ldots, p_n exist. Consider N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n + 1.
Full solution N = p_1 p_2 \cdots p_n + 1. By the fundamental theorem, N has a prime factor q. But q \ne p_i for any i (since p_i \mid p_1 \cdots p_n but p_i \nmid 1). So q is a new prime — contradicting the assumption that p_1, \ldots, p_n are all of them. Infinitely many primes exist. (Euclid’s Elements, Book IX, Proposition 20 — one of the most beautiful proofs in mathematics.)

S2. Show that \sqrt 2 is irrational using the fundamental theorem.

Hint 1 — a nudge Suppose \sqrt 2 = p/q in lowest terms. Then 2 q^2 = p^2. Look at the parity of the exponent of 2 in each side’s prime factorisation.
Full solution 2 q^2 = p^2. The exponent of 2 in the prime factorisation of p^2 is even (twice the exponent in p). The exponent in 2 q^2 is odd (one plus twice the exponent in q). Even \ne odd — contradiction. \sqrt 2 is irrational. (A clean alternative to Euclid’s parity proof from Algebra I Ch 8.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s terse style, adapted from Arithmetic, §§50–75.

K1. Find the smallest whole number divisible by all integers from 1 to 10.

Hint 1 — a nudge LCM of 1, 2, \ldots, 10. Highest prime powers \le 10: 2^3 (from 8), 3^2 (from 9), 5, 7.
Full solution \text{lcm}(1, 2, \ldots, 10) = 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 = 8 \cdot 9 \cdot 35 = 2520. 2520.

K2. Two bus routes leave a station — route A every 12 minutes, route B every 18 minutes. They start together at 8{:}00 am. At what time do they next leave together?

Full solution \text{lcm}(12, 18) = 36. 8{:}36 am.

K3. Show that of any n + 1 chosen integers from \{1, 2, \ldots, 2 n\}, at least two must be coprime.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pigeonhole — pair the integers \{1, 2\}, \{3, 4\}, \ldots, \{2n-1, 2n\} (i.e., consecutive pairs). With n + 1 choices in n pairs, two are in the same pair. Consecutive integers are coprime.
Full solution The 2 n numbers split into n pairs of consecutive integers \{1, 2\}, \{3, 4\}, \ldots, \{2n - 1, 2n\}. By pigeonhole, at least two of the n + 1 chosen numbers lie in the same pair — hence are consecutive. Consecutive integers k, k + 1 have \gcd = 1 (any common divisor divides their difference = 1). At least two chosen are coprime. QED. (A beautiful pigeonhole + GCD argument; classical Russian-school problem.)

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. The primes are the atoms of the whole numbers — every n \ge 2 breaks into them in exactly one way (the fundamental theorem of arithmetic).
  2. Euclid’s algorithm finds the GCD of any two whole numbers in a handful of steps — never thousands, even for thousand-digit inputs.
  3. There are infinitely many primes, by Euclid’s two-line proof — one of the oldest and shortest theorems in mathematics.

Common confusions

  1. “1 is prime.” No. 1 is the unit (multiplicative identity), not a prime. Including 1 as prime would break the uniqueness of factorisation.
  2. “GCD must be one of the two numbers.” Not generally — only if one divides the other. \gcd(12, 18) = 6, neither 12 nor 18.
  3. “All odd numbers are prime.” 9 = 3^2, 15 = 3 \cdot 5, 21 = 3 \cdot 7 — odd, composite. Primality requires having only 1 and itself as divisors.

Where this matters

Encryption. RSA generates two large primes p, q, computes n = p q, and uses \phi(n) = (p - 1)(q - 1) to find a public exponent coprime to \phi(n). Every step of RSA is a divisibility operation. The hardness of factoring n back into p, q is what secures the system.

Databases. Hash tables with prime-sized buckets distribute keys more evenly than non-prime sizes; this is a number-theoretic property used in production hash-map implementations.

Research. The Riemann hypothesis — about the distribution of primes — is one of the most famous open problems in mathematics. Every result on prime density traces back to fundamental theorem of arithmetic.
