14 · Lattice-Based Post-Quantum Cryptography

When quantum computers break ECC, lattices are humanity’s plan B.

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T5 L03 — Vector Spaces and T5 L04 — Linear Systems and Gaussian Elimination?
  2. Recall: bases of \mathbb{R}^n, modular arithmetic.
  3. Have you encountered the closest vector problem?

Touch

Pick a basis quality — clean, skewed, or worst-case ugly. Drop a target point near a grid built from that basis. The widget tells you whether finding the closest grid point is easy, hard, or hopeless.

viewof basisType = Inputs.select(["Orthogonal (easy)", "Skewed (hard)", "Cryptographic (very skewed)"], {value: "Skewed (hard)", label: "Basis quality:"})
{
  let info, hardness;
  switch(basisType) {
    case "Orthogonal (easy)": info = "Orthogonal basis: nearest neighbor by rounding each coord. CVP trivial."; hardness = "Easy."; break;
    case "Skewed (hard)": info = "Heavily-skewed basis: rounding gives wrong answer. CVP requires search."; hardness = "Hard."; break;
    case "Cryptographic (very skewed)": info = "Lattice with deliberately bad public basis. Decryption uses good private basis."; hardness = "Trapdoor!"; break;
  }
  return html`<div style="margin: 1rem 0;">
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:1.1rem; color:#1a237e;">${hardness}</div>
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:0.95rem; color:#444;">${info}</div>
  </div>`;
}

A skewed-enough basis makes this search hopeless — and even quantum computers can’t crack it. That stubborn hardness is the foundation of post-quantum cryptography.

Wonder

  1. Lattices generalise integer points; security comes from “good basis vs bad basis.”
  2. Learning With Errors (LWE): decoding noisy linear equations modulo a prime. Believed quantum-hard.
  3. Ring-LWE: structured variant for efficiency; basis of Kyber, NTRU, FALCON.

Discover

Problem 1

A lattice L = \{\sum a_i b_i : a_i \in \mathbb{Z}\} where b_1, \ldots, b_n form a basis.

Two bases of the same lattice can look very different. Show that \mathbb{Z}^2 has bases \{(1, 0), (0, 1)\} and \{(1, 0), (5, 1)\}.

Full solution

(5, 1) = 5 (1, 0) + 1 (0, 1) — integer combination of standard basis. And (1, 0), (0, 1) recoverable from the other: (0, 1) = (5, 1) - 5 (1, 0).

Both bases ⇒ the same lattice. But “good” basis (orthogonal) makes CVP easy; “bad” basis (skewed) makes CVP hard.

This basis-quality asymmetry is the heart of lattice crypto.

Problem 2

Closest Vector Problem (CVP). Given basis B and target t, find the closest lattice point.

For orthogonal basis: trivial (round each coordinate).

For skewed basis: NP-hard (in general). But “approx-CVP” with factor > n: efficient (LLL algorithm).

Why does this matter for crypto?

Full solution
  • Public: bad (skewed) basis B' — anyone can describe the lattice but CVP is hard.
  • Private: good (near-orthogonal) basis B — CVP becomes easy.

Encryption: encode message as a lattice point + small noise. Recipient uses good basis to round to closest lattice point.

LLL reduction (Lenstra-Lenstra-Lovász, 1982) approximates CVP — a lower bound on attack quality.

Problem 3

Learning With Errors (LWE) (Regev, 2005). Public:

  • Random A \in \mathbb{Z}_q^{m \times n}.
  • b = As + e \mod q where s \in \mathbb{Z}_q^n secret, e \in \mathbb{Z}_q^m small noise.

Goal: recover s.

Without noise: solvable (linear algebra). With small noise: believed hard.

What’s the connection to lattices?

Full solution

As + e \approx b — finding s ≈ solving CVP on a specific lattice.

Regev’s theorem: breaking LWE on average ⇔ solving lattice problems (GapSVP, SIVP) in worst case.

Worst-case → average-case reduction is unique to lattices among crypto candidates. Strong security guarantee.

Believed: LWE is quantum-hard. Best known quantum algorithms still exponential.

Problem 4

Ring-LWE. Replace \mathbb{Z}_q^n with the polynomial ring \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^n + 1).

Why is this faster than vanilla LWE?

Full solution

LWE: matrix A has m \cdot n entries; ciphertext is O(n).

Ring-LWE: A a single polynomial; multiplication via NTT (Number Theoretic Transform — analogue of FFT). O(n \log n) vs O(n^2).

Trade-off: structured assumption (Ring-LWE) is stronger than LWE (more attack surface) — but most attacks haven’t found significantly better algorithms.

Module-LWE (Kyber): a balance of efficiency and security.

Problem 5

Why post-quantum? Shor’s algorithm (1994) factors integers and solves discrete log on a quantum computer in polynomial time.

⇒ RSA broken, ECC broken, Diffie-Hellman broken (when large quantum computers exist).

Lattice problems: no known polynomial-time quantum algorithm.

What’s the NIST PQC standardization outcome?

Full solution

NIST PQC competition (2016–2024):

  • CRYSTALS-Kyber (Module-LWE) — primary KEM. FIPS 203, 2024.
  • CRYSTALS-Dilithium (Module-SIS / Module-LWE) — primary signature. FIPS 204, 2024.
  • FALCON (NTRU lattice) — alternate signature. Faster but more complex.
  • SPHINCS+ (hash-based) — backup signature, not lattice-based.
Web is migrating (Cloudflare, Google, Apple are deploying hybrid classical+PQC TLS).

The two papers that bracket post-quantum crypto. In 1994, Peter Shor — then at Bell Labs — published Algorithms for Quantum Computation: Discrete Logarithms and Factoring, a paper that showed a sufficiently large quantum computer could factor integers and break discrete-log in polynomial time, instantly threatening RSA, Diffie-Hellman, and ECC the moment such a machine existed. Two years later, in 1996, a young Hungarian mathematician named Miklós Ajtai quietly published Generating Hard Instances of Lattice Problems — which proved a previously unimaginable result: breaking lattice problems on average is at least as hard as solving them in the worst case. No other crypto candidate has anything like this guarantee. Oded Regev’s 2005 paper on Learning With Errors (LWE) then made the average-case problem look like noisy linear algebra, suitable for actual schemes. NIST’s Post-Quantum Cryptography competition (2016-2024) finished by standardising CRYSTALS-Kyber (FIPS 203) and CRYSTALS-Dilithium (FIPS 204) in 2024 — both lattice-based, both descendants of Ajtai’s worst-case-to-average-case reduction from 1996. The “harvest now, decrypt later” threat means every TLS connection today should already be migrating: adversaries are storing encrypted traffic in the bet that quantum will arrive sometime in the next decade.

Lattice crypto runs on a single asymmetry: a “good” basis makes finding the closest lattice point easy, a “bad” basis makes it hopeless — even though both bases describe the same lattice. The public key is a deliberately ugly basis; the private key is an orthogonal-ish one only you know. Add small noise (LWE) to a linear system and recovery becomes a CVP-style problem, and Regev’s worst-case-to-average-case reduction promises that every random LWE instance is at least as hard as the worst lattice problem in that dimension. This is the only crypto family with that kind of guarantee — and it’s why the post-quantum standards bet humanity’s encrypted future on geometry.

Name

Lattice L \subset \mathbb{R}^n: discrete subgroup of full rank. L = B \mathbb{Z}^n for basis B.

Hard problems:

  • SVP (Shortest Vector): find shortest non-zero vector. NP-hard (Ajtai 1998). \gamma-approx-SVP for poly \gamma: also believed hard.
  • CVP (Closest Vector): given target, find closest lattice point.
  • LWE (Learning With Errors): decode b = As + e \mod q, e small.
  • Ring-LWE / Module-LWE: structured.

Regev’s worst-case to average-case reduction: breaking LWE → solving worst-case lattice problems.

LLL algorithm: poly-time approx-SVP factor 2^{n/2}. Foundational.

Schemes:

  • NTRU (1996, Hoffstein-Pipher-Silverman). Trapdoor lattice.
  • GGH (1997). Broken (1999).
  • Regev’s LWE encryption (2005).
  • CRYSTALS-Kyber — NIST FIPS 203.
  • CRYSTALS-Dilithium — NIST FIPS 204.
  • FALCON, NTRU.
  • Fully Homomorphic Encryption (Gentry 2009): based on lattice assumptions.

Post-quantum: secure against quantum computers (no known polytime quantum algorithm for lattice problems).

Lattice = integer combinations of basis vectors; Good basis solves CVP, bad basis doesn’t; LWE / Ring-LWE: post-quantum hard, even quantum.

Apply

Worked example

Kyber-512 KEM. Module-LWE with n = 256, k = 2, modulus q = 3329.

Key gen:

  • Sample A \in R_q^{k \times k} where R_q = \mathbb{Z}_q[x]/(x^{256} + 1).
  • Sample small s, e \in R_q^k (small coefficients).
  • Public key: (A, t = As + e).

Encapsulate:

  • Sample small r, e_1, e_2.
  • u = A^T r + e_1.
  • v = t^T r + e_2 + \lfloor q/2 \rfloor m (encode message m).

Decapsulate: v - s^T u ≈ \lfloor q/2 \rfloor m + \text{noise} → round to recover m.

Security: ~128 quantum bits at small public-key size (~800 bytes).

Practice — Warm

W1. Is \mathbb{Z}^n \subset \mathbb{R}^n a lattice?

Full solution Yes — the standard integer lattice. Basis: standard basis vectors.

W2. What does LLL produce?

Full solution A “reduced” basis — short, near-orthogonal. Good enough for many applications, including some crypto attacks.

W3. What’s the role of noise in LWE?

Full solution Noise prevents linear-algebra recovery; turns a linear system into a hard decoding problem.

Practice — Working

P1. Why is NTRU older than LWE but still relevant?

Full solution

NTRU (1996) predates LWE (2005). Cryptosystem in \mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^n - 1).

Doesn’t have Regev’s worst-case reduction — security relies on specific hardness assumption. But: practical, fast, broadly studied.

NTRU and Falcon (lattice signatures) use NTRU lattices. Kyber is Module-LWE — distinct lineage.

P2. What’s Fully Homomorphic Encryption (FHE)?

Full solution

Operations on ciphertexts → ciphertexts of computed result.

\text{Enc}(a) + \text{Enc}(b) = \text{Enc}(a + b) (additive homomorphic).

FHE: arbitrary circuits. Enables computing on encrypted data without seeing it.

Gentry (2009): first construction. Modern: BGV, BFV, CKKS, TFHE — all based on (Ring)-LWE.

Applications: privacy-preserving ML inference, secure cloud computing. Slow today (1000s× overhead), but improving.

P3. Why is constant-time implementation essential for lattice crypto?

Full solution

Branching on secret data leaks info via timing side-channels.

Many proposed schemes break in practice due to timing leaks (e.g., the GGH scheme).

Constant-time NTT, modular reduction, sampling — all required. Implemented in Kyber and Dilithium reference code.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Why is LLL one of the most consequential algorithms?

Full solution

Lenstra-Lenstra-Lovász (1982): polynomial-time approx-SVP, factor 2^{n/2}.

Applications: factoring polynomials over \mathbb{Q}, knapsack cryptanalysis, Coppersmith’s method (RSA attacks), integer relation finding (PSLQ).

LLL turns “give up — too many vectors” into “search efficiently in the reduced lattice.” Central to algorithmic number theory.

S2. The post-quantum migration timeline.

Full solution
  • 2024: NIST FIPS 203/204/205 finalised. Major TLS libraries (Google, Cloudflare, Apple) deploy hybrid classical+PQC.
  • 2030 (target): US federal CNSA 2.0 deadline for PQC.
  • “Harvest now, decrypt later”: adversaries collecting encrypted traffic now to decrypt when quantum computers arrive. Hence: migrate before quantum is real.
  • Quantum estimate: breaking RSA-2048 needs ~20M physical qubits (Gidney-Ekerå 2021). Current systems: ~1000 qubits.
  • Window: maybe a decade. Migrate now.

Common confusions

  1. “Lattice crypto = ECC for quantum era.” Different math. ECC uses elliptic-curve groups; lattice crypto uses geometric / algebraic number-theoretic structures.
  2. “Bigger keys = better security.” True up to a point. PQC keys are larger than ECC (~1KB vs ~32 bytes); engineering trade-off.
  3. “Quantum already breaks crypto.” Not yet — Shor needs many logical qubits. But cryptographic agility now is essential.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A lattice is integer combinations of basis vectors, and a single lattice has many bases — some easy (orthogonal, CVP trivial), some hard (skewed, CVP NP-hard).
  2. LWE reduces “find s from b = As + e \pmod q” to a lattice problem, and Regev’s worst-case-to-average-case reduction means the average instance you receive is as hard as the worst lattice in that dimension — uniquely strong among crypto candidates.
  3. Post-quantum security comes from problems Shor’s algorithm doesn’t crack: lattice problems have no known polynomial-time quantum algorithm, which is why CRYSTALS-Kyber and CRYSTALS-Dilithium are now NIST’s post-quantum cryptography standards.
