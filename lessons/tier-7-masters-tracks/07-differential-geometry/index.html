07 · Differential Geometry — Manifolds, Curvature, Geodesics

The local geometry of curved spaces — and why it underpins relativity, robotics, and graphics.

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T5 L11 — Partial Derivatives?
  2. Comfortable with parametric curves \mathbf{r}(t) and surfaces \mathbf{r}(u, v)?
  3. Recall: dot product, cross product, Jacobian.

Touch

A sphere is curved; a flat sheet is not. How would you measure curvature without leaving the surface? (i.e., as an ant on the surface.)

viewof curveSurface = Inputs.select(["Plane (κ = 0)", "Sphere of radius R", "Saddle (κ < 0)", "Cylinder"], {value: "Sphere of radius R", label: "Surface:"})
{
  let info, curvature;
  switch(curveSurface) {
    case "Plane (κ = 0)": info = "Zero Gaussian curvature. Triangle angles sum to 180°."; curvature = "K = 0"; break;
    case "Sphere of radius R": info = "Constant positive curvature 1/R². Triangle angles sum to > 180°."; curvature = "K = 1/R²"; break;
    case "Saddle (κ < 0)": info = "Negative curvature. Triangle angles sum to < 180°."; curvature = "K < 0"; break;
    case "Cylinder": info = "Zero curvature (intrinsically flat) — you can unroll it!"; curvature = "K = 0"; break;
  }
  return html`<div style="margin: 1rem 0;">
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:1.1rem; color:#1a237e;">${curvature}</div>
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:0.95rem; color:#444;">${info}</div>
  </div>`;
}

A 2D being on a cylinder cannot tell they’re curved. On a sphere they can — by walking a closed triangle and measuring its angle sum.

Wonder

  1. A smooth manifold is a space that locally looks like \mathbb{R}^n but globally has shape.
  2. Tangent space at a point: the linear approximation. Vectors “live” here.
  3. Riemannian metric: how to measure distances and angles intrinsically.
  4. Curvature quantifies how much it deviates from flat.

Discover

Problem 1

A smooth manifold M of dimension n: a topological space with charts \phi_\alpha : U_\alpha \to \mathbb{R}^n that overlap smoothly.

For the sphere S^2, two charts suffice: stereographic projection from the north pole and from the south pole. Show their overlap is smooth.

Hint 1 — a nudge Stereographic from north: (x, y, z) \mapsto (X, Y) = (x, y) / (1 - z). Stereographic from south: (x, y, z) \mapsto (X', Y') = (x, y) / (1 + z).
Full solution

The overlap is S^2 \setminus \{N, S\}. Composition: (X', Y') \mapsto (x, y, z) \mapsto (X, Y) = \frac{(X', Y')}{X'^2 + Y'^2}. The “inversion” map — smooth on the punctured plane (no pole at 0 since X'^2 + Y'^2 > 0).

So S^2 is a smooth 2-manifold.

Problem 2

The tangent space T_p M at p \in M is the set of velocities of curves through p. It’s a vector space of dimension n.

For the sphere S^2: what’s T_p S^2 for p = (1, 0, 0)?

Full solution

T_{(1,0,0)} S^2 = \{v \in \mathbb{R}^3 : v \cdot (1, 0, 0) = 0\} = \{(0, v_2, v_3)\} — the yz-plane.

In general, T_p S^2 = plane perpendicular to p — what you’d intuitively call “tangent.”

Problem 3

A Riemannian metric g assigns to each tangent space an inner product. On \mathbb{R}^n: usually the standard dot product.

For the sphere S^2 with parameters (\theta, \varphi) (latitude, longitude), the induced metric is g = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta \, d\varphi^2. What’s the length of a curve \theta = \pi/2 (equator), from \varphi = 0 to \varphi = 2\pi?

Full solution

Length = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{g(\dot c, \dot c)} \, d\varphi = \int_0^{2\pi} \sin(\pi/2)\, d\varphi = 2\pi.

For unit-radius sphere: equator length is 2\pi — matches Euclidean intuition. For a parallel at \theta = \pi/4: length = 2\pi \sin(\pi/4) = \pi\sqrt{2}.

Problem 4

A geodesic is the curve that locally minimises length — straightest possible path on a manifold.

What are the geodesics on a sphere? On a flat plane?

Full solution

Sphere: great circles. Equator, meridians.

Plane: straight lines.

Geodesics solve the geodesic equation: \ddot{x}^k + \Gamma^k_{ij} \dot{x}^i \dot{x}^j = 0, where \Gamma are Christoffel symbols (encode the metric’s derivatives). Forms an ODE — local existence + uniqueness.

Problem 5

Gaussian curvature K of a surface is intrinsic — computable from the metric alone (Theorema Egregium, Gauss).

For a sphere of radius R: K = 1/R^2. For a plane: K = 0. For a saddle: K < 0.

Why is “intrinsic” a deep fact?

Full solution

Theorema Egregium (Gauss, 1827): An ant on the surface, never leaving it, can compute K from how distances and angles behave locally — without embedding into 3D space.

Hence: a flat sheet of paper rolled into a cylinder has K = 0 (rolling doesn’t stretch). But you can never wrap a sphere onto a plane without distortion (their K differ).

This is why all flat maps of the Earth distort. The deep cause of mapmaker’s frustration.

Gauss called it the “Remarkable Theorem” because it surprised even him. In the Disquisitiones generales circa superficies curvas of 1827, Carl Friedrich Gauss had been working through the geometry of curved surfaces — partly for the practical reason that he had spent the previous decade running the geodetic survey of the Kingdom of Hanover and needed honest formulas for the curvature of the actual Earth. The expected formula for what we now call Gaussian curvature K involved the way the surface bends inside the surrounding 3-space — its second fundamental form. Gauss derived it, manipulated it, and discovered to his evident astonishment that the extrinsic terms cancelled: K could be computed from the metric alone, by an ant on the surface, with no need to step outside. He called the result the theorema egregium — Latin for “remarkable theorem” — and it became the seed of what Riemann generalised in 1854 to higher-dimensional manifolds. The same fact explains why every flat world map distorts (sphere has K = 1/R^2, plane has K = 0, and you cannot match them without stretching), why a pizza folds along a straight line when you bite into the tip (cylinder has K = 0 in any direction, so curving in one direction forbids curving in another), and why general relativity could be written down at all — gravity is the curvature of spacetime as measured from inside, with no need to embed the universe in anything larger.

A manifold is “locally flat, globally not” — and that single phrase controls everything you do in differential geometry. Locally, you borrow the calculus of \mathbb{R}^n unchanged. Globally, the curvature accumulates — triangle angles deviate from \pi, parallel transport around a loop fails to return you home, and “straight lines” become geodesics. The Theorema Egregium is the assertion that all of this is intrinsic: an inhabitant of the surface, without ever leaving it, can in principle measure how curved their world is. That fact is what lets general relativity speak about the curvature of the spacetime — the only one we have, with nothing outside.

Name

Smooth manifold M^n: topological space with smooth charts to \mathbb{R}^n with smooth transition maps.

Tangent space T_p M: n-dim vector space of velocities at p.

Tangent bundle TM: disjoint union \bigsqcup_p T_p M.

Riemannian metric g: smoothly varying inner product on T_p M.

Length of curve: L(\gamma) = \int \sqrt{g(\dot\gamma, \dot\gamma)} \, dt.

Geodesic: locally length-minimising; solves \ddot x^k + \Gamma^k_{ij} \dot x^i \dot x^j = 0.

Christoffel symbols \Gamma^k_{ij}: derivatives of g.

Levi-Civita connection \nabla: unique torsion-free metric- compatible covariant derivative.

Riemann curvature tensor R^l_{ijk}: full curvature info.

Gaussian curvature (2D): K = R_{1212}/\det g. Intrinsic by Theorema Egregium.

Sectional / Ricci / scalar curvature: higher-dim refinements.

Gauss-Bonnet theorem: \int_M K \, dA = 2\pi \chi(M) — links geometry (curvature) to topology (Euler characteristic).

Manifold = locally flat globally curved; Metric measures lengths intrinsically; Geodesics are straight lines for the curved; Curvature: K from g alone (Gauss).

Apply

Worked example

Spherical pendulum. A pendulum swinging on a sphere S^2. Lagrangian: L = \tfrac{1}{2} g_{ij} \dot q^i \dot q^j - V(q).

Equations of motion = geodesics in (configuration manifold, metric = kinetic energy).

For V = 0: motion is along great circles. For V = -gz: pendulum oscillation perturbs the geodesic.

This is Lagrangian mechanics in geometric form — a workhorse of robotics, classical mechanics, and physics.

Practice — Warm

W1. Is the torus T^2 a smooth manifold?

Full solution Yes — 2-dim, locally flat. Globally has genus 1.

W2. What’s \dim TM for M = S^2?

Full solution 4 — 2D base + 2D fiber. (TM is a 4-manifold for \dim M = 2.)

W3. \chi(S^2) = ?

Full solution
  1. So \int_{S^2} K \, dA = 4\pi, by Gauss-Bonnet.

Practice — Working

P1. Why does the Earth flat-map distortion matter for navigation?

Full solution

Mercator projection preserves angles (conformal) but distorts areas; Gall-Peters preserves areas but distorts angles. There is no flat map preserving both — Theorema Egregium guarantees it.

Pilots and ship navigators use great circles (geodesics on sphere) for shortest paths. Mercator straight lines are not geodesics.

P2. What’s a Lie group and why does it matter for robotics?

Full solution

A Lie group is a smooth manifold with a smooth group operation. Examples: SO(3) (rotations in 3D), SE(3) (rigid motions).

In robotics: a robot’s pose lives in SE(3). Trajectory planning involves geodesics on this manifold (with appropriate metric).

Lie algebras (tangent space at identity) give angular velocity and twists.

P3. Compute the geodesics of the Poincaré half-plane \{(x, y) : y > 0\} with metric g = (dx^2 + dy^2)/y^2.

Full solution

Geodesics: vertical lines and semicircles centred on the x-axis.

This is the model of hyperbolic geometry (K = -1). Used in: hyperbolic embeddings (Tier 7 L05), automorphic forms (Tier 7 L15), relativity, and number theory.

Practice — Stretch

S1. General relativity in two sentences.

Full solution

Spacetime is a 4-dimensional Lorentzian manifold. Matter curves spacetime via Einstein’s equation R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2} g_{\mu\nu} R + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}, and free-falling objects follow geodesics.

Special relativity = flat metric (Minkowski). General relativity = curved.

Tested by GPS daily: clocks at altitude run faster (gravitational time dilation), corrected via the Schwarzschild metric.

S2. 3D graphics: smooth interpolation on SO(3).

Full solution

For a rotating object: interpolate between two orientations R_0, R_1 \in SO(3).

Naive Euclidean interpolation of matrix entries gives non-rotations. Correct: slerp (spherical linear interpolation) on the quaternions, which traces a geodesic in SO(3).

Quaternions (T7 L08) are the working representation; geodesics on S^3 (which double-covers SO(3)) are the math.

Common confusions

  1. “A cylinder is curved.” Extrinsically yes (in 3D); but intrinsically K = 0 — unrollable.
  2. “Curvature is global.” Gaussian and sectional curvatures are pointwise — they vary across the manifold.
  3. “Geodesics are unique shortest paths.” Locally yes; globally no — antipodal points on a sphere have infinitely many shortest geodesics (great circles).

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A smooth manifold is just “patched together from open pieces of \mathbb{R}^n” — and the tangent space at a point is the vector space of velocities of curves through it, the seat of all linear approximation.
  2. A Riemannian metric is a smoothly-varying dot product on each tangent space — that one piece of data tells you lengths, angles, geodesics, and curvature, all intrinsic to the manifold.
  3. Gauss’s Theorema Egregium says curvature is intrinsic — computable from the metric alone, with no embedding into a larger space — which is why general relativity can speak about the curvature of spacetime even though spacetime is all there is.
