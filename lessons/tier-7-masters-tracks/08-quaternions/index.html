08 · Quaternions and 3D Rotations

Hamilton’s invention — the right tool for rotating things smoothly in 3D.

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T6 L02 — Groups and Symmetry and T5 L02 — Linear Algebra Foundation?
  2. Recall: complex numbers a + bi, matrix multiplication, dot/cross products.
  3. Have you encountered Euler angles or rotation matrices?

Touch

Rotate a 3D object smoothly from orientation A to orientation B.

viewof rotMethod = Inputs.select(["Euler angles (gimbal lock!)", "Rotation matrix (overdetermined)", "Quaternion (smooth)"], {value: "Quaternion (smooth)", label: "Method:"})

Quaternions: invented Oct 16, 1843 by Hamilton on Brougham Bridge. The standard rotation representation in modern game engines.

Wonder

  1. Complex numbers a + bi encode 2D rotations. Could there be a 4D number system encoding 3D rotations?
  2. Quaternions \mathbb{H}: a + bi + cj + dk with i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.
  3. Multiplication is non-commutative — exactly what’s needed for 3D rotation (which is also non-commutative).

Discover

Problem 1

Define \mathbb{H} = \{a + bi + cj + dk : a, b, c, d \in \mathbb{R}\} with i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.

Derive ij and ji.

Full solution

From ijk = -1 multiply right by k^{-1} = -k: ij = -1 \cdot (-k) = k. Similarly jk = i, ki = j.

For ji: from ij = k, multiply left by j^{-1} = -j: i = -jk, so ji = -k.

So ij = -ji = k, jk = -kj = i, ki = -ik = j. Anti-commutative on imaginaries — like cross product.

This non-commutativity is what makes \mathbb{H} encode 3D rotations.

Problem 2

For q = a + bi + cj + dk, define conjugate \bar q = a - bi - cj - dk and norm |q| = \sqrt{q \bar q}.

Show |pq| = |p||q|.

Full solution

|pq|^2 = (pq)(\overline{pq}) = pq \bar q \bar p = p (q \bar q) \bar p = p |q|^2 \bar p = |q|^2 p \bar p = |q|^2 |p|^2.

(We used \overline{pq} = \bar q \bar p — order reverses!)

So |pq| = |p| |q|. Multiplicativity. (Hence non-zero quaternions form a group under multiplication.)

Problem 3

A unit quaternion has |q| = 1. Set q = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2)(u_1 i + u_2 j + u_3 k) where \hat{u} = (u_1, u_2, u_3) is a unit vector.

Define rotation: for a 3D vector \vec v = v_1 i + v_2 j + v_3 k, R_q(\vec v) = q \vec v \bar q. Verify this is a rotation by angle \theta around axis \hat u.

Hint 1 — a nudge Try \hat u = (1, 0, 0), so q = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2) i. Compute q (v_2 j + v_3 k) \bar q — should rotate the jk-plane.
Full solution

Yes — a careful computation shows R_q rotates \vec v by angle \theta around axis \hat u.

Verify: R_q(\hat u) = \hat u (axis is fixed); R_q preserves length; the rotation in the plane orthogonal to \hat u has the right angle.

Note: q and -q give the same rotation. So unit quaternions are a double cover of SO(3): S^3 \to SO(3), kernel \{\pm 1\}.

Problem 4

Composition of rotations. R_{q_2} \circ R_{q_1} = ?

Full solution

R_{q_2}(R_{q_1}(\vec v)) = q_2 (q_1 \vec v \bar q_1) \bar q_2 = (q_2 q_1) \vec v \overline{(q_2 q_1)} = R_{q_2 q_1}(\vec v).

So composition = quaternion product. To combine rotations, just multiply the quaternions.

Compare to rotation matrices: also composition by multiplication, but 9 entries vs 4 — quaternions are more compact and avoid singularities.

Problem 5

SLERP (spherical linear interpolation). For unit quaternions q_0, q_1, define \text{slerp}(q_0, q_1, t) = \frac{\sin((1-t)\Omega)}{\sin\Omega} q_0 + \frac{\sin(t \Omega)}{\sin\Omega} q_1 where \cos\Omega = q_0 \cdot q_1.

Why is slerp the right interpolation for rotations?

Full solution

Slerp traces a geodesic on S^3 (the unit-quaternion sphere) at constant angular velocity.

Naive linear interpolation ((1-t)q_0 + t q_1) gives non-unit quaternions and uneven angular speed.

Slerp gives:

  • Smooth motion.
  • Constant angular velocity.
  • Shortest path through orientation space.
Used in: every modern animation system, character rigging, IK solvers.

Hamilton spent fifteen years stuck on the same problem before the answer hit him on a walk. Since 1828 William Rowan Hamilton — Astronomer Royal of Ireland, fluent in thirteen languages by his teens, the man who recast classical mechanics as the Hamiltonian formalism — had been trying to extend the multiplication of complex numbers from the plane to space. Every attempt foundered: the triplets a + bi + cj refused to multiply consistently, because there was no way to define ij without breaking either norm or distributivity. On the morning of 16 October 1843, walking with his wife along the Royal Canal in Dublin towards a meeting of the Royal Irish Academy, the missing trick suddenly arrived: give up commutativity, add a fourth dimension. He stopped at Brougham Bridge (now Broom Bridge) and carved i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1 into the stonework with his penknife — the carving has long since worn away, but a plaque now marks the spot. Hamilton spent the rest of his life arguing — wrongly, it turned out — that quaternions would replace vectors in physics. Vectors won the textbook battle in the 1880s, and quaternions languished for nearly a century. Then in April 1970 the Apollo 13 service module suffered an attitude control crisis in which the spacecraft’s gimbal-mounted IMU came within a degree of gimbal lock — a singularity inherent to the three-Euler-angle representation, in which two rotation axes align and one degree of freedom vanishes. Modern flight software, every modern game engine (Unity, Unreal, Godot), every CGI animation pipeline, and the attitude control of every modern satellite stores rotations as quaternions for exactly this reason: S^3 has no singularities, so gimbal lock cannot happen. Hamilton’s dead-end notation became the working representation of three-dimensional rotation in the digital age.

A quaternion is a rotation written so that composition becomes multiplication and there are no singularities — at the price of non-commutativity (which is the right price, because rotations in 3D do not commute either) and a curious double cover: q and -q are the same rotation, so a 360° turn in space corresponds to travelling halfway around the quaternion sphere S^3, and only at 720° do you return to where you started. The half-angle in q = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2)\hat u is not a notational quirk — it is a record of that doubling, the same doubling that physics calls spin and animation calls the reason slerp works.

Name

Quaternions \mathbb{H} = \{a + bi + cj + dk\} with i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1.

Multiplication: non-commutative. ij = k, jk = i, ki = j.

Conjugate \bar q = a - bi - cj - dk. Norm |q|^2 = q\bar q. \overline{pq} = \bar q \bar p.

Inverse of unit q: q^{-1} = \bar q.

Unit quaternions \cong S^3 form a group, double-covering SO(3).

Rotation by \theta around unit axis \hat u: q = \cos(\theta/2) + \sin(\theta/2) (u_1 i + u_2 j + u_3 k); \qquad R_q(\vec v) = q \vec v \bar q.

Composition: R_{q_2} \circ R_{q_1} = R_{q_2 q_1}.

SLERP for smooth rotation interpolation. Geodesic on S^3.

Advantages over Euler angles: no gimbal lock. Over rotation matrices: more compact (4 vs 9 numbers); cheaper composition.

Hamilton’s i, j, k satisfy i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1; Unit quaternions \cong S^3 double-cover SO(3); Rotate by q\vec v\bar q, compose by multiplication, interpolate by slerp.

Apply

Worked example

Rotate (1, 0, 0) by 90° around the z-axis.

\hat u = (0, 0, 1), \theta = 90°. So q = \cos 45° + \sin 45° \cdot k = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(1 + k).

\vec v = i, \bar q = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(1 - k).

q \vec v = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(1 + k) i = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(i + ki) = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(i + j).

(q \vec v) \bar q = \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(i + j) \cdot \frac{\sqrt 2}{2}(1 - k) = \frac{1}{2}(i - ik + j - jk) = \frac{1}{2}(i + j + j - i) = j.

So i \mapsto j — exactly a 90° rotation around z. ✓

Practice — Warm

W1. Compute (1 + i)(1 + j).

Full solution 1 + j + i + ij = 1 + i + j + k.

W2. What’s |2 + i + j + k|?

Full solution \sqrt{4 + 1 + 1 + 1} = \sqrt{7}.

W3. What rotation does q = i encode?

Full solution q = i = \cos 90° + \sin 90° \cdot i, so 180° rotation around x-axis.

Practice — Working

P1. Why are quaternions a division ring but not a field?

Full solution

Every non-zero quaternion has an inverse: q^{-1} = \bar q / |q|^2.

But multiplication is non-commutative (ij \neq ji), so \mathbb{H} is not a field. It’s a division ring (or “skew field”).

Frobenius: the only finite-dimensional associative division algebras over \mathbb{R} are \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}.

P2. Why is the relation between quaternions and SO(3) a double cover?

Full solution

Both q and -q produce the same rotation (since (-q)\vec v(-\bar q) = q\vec v \bar q).

Topologically: S^3 (unit quaternions) is simply connected; SO(3) is not. The map q \mapsto R_q is 2-to-1.

Consequence: a 360° rotation in SO(3) corresponds to going from 1 to -1 in S^3 — not back to identity. 720° gets back. (“Belt trick” / “Dirac string”.)

P3. Why does the NLERP (normalised linear interpolation) often substitute for slerp in games?

Full solution

NLERP: ((1-t)q_0 + t q_1)/|...| — linear then renormalise.

Faster than slerp (no trig). Doesn’t have constant angular velocity, but the visual difference is tiny for small angles.

Trade-off: speed vs angular-velocity smoothness. Most games use NLERP when frame-rate matters more than physical correctness.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Octonions \mathbb{O}: 8-dim, non-associative.

Full solution

After \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}, the octonions \mathbb{O} are 8-dim with multiplicative norm — but non-associative: (ab)c \neq a(bc) in general.

Hurwitz: only normed division algebras are \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}, \mathbb{O} (dimensions 1, 2, 4, 8).

Used in: exceptional Lie groups, string theory, certain finite simple groups.

S2. Dual quaternions for rigid-body motion (rotation + translation).

Full solution

Dual quaternion: \hat q = q + \epsilon p where \epsilon^2 = 0.

Encodes both rotation (q) and translation (p) compactly. Composition is just dual-quaternion multiplication. Interpolation: “DLB” (dual quaternion linear blending) for character skinning.

Used in robotics, character animation. Avoids “candy wrapper” artefacts of separate rotation + translation interpolation.

Common confusions

  1. “Quaternions are 4D rotations.” No — they encode 3D rotations (using 4 numbers). Genuine 4D rotations need a different setup.
  2. “q\vec v \bar q vs \bar q \vec v q.” Both rotate, but in opposite directions (one is the inverse of the other). Pick a convention and stick to it.
  3. “Half-angle is a hack.” It’s deep — ties to the double cover S^3 \to SO(3). The half is necessary.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. A quaternion is four real numbers a + bi + cj + dk obeying i^2 = j^2 = k^2 = ijk = -1 — and the consequent non-commutativity (ij = -ji = k) is exactly what is needed to encode the non-commutativity of 3D rotation.
  2. Unit quaternions form S^3 and double-cover the rotation group SO(3) — both q and -q act the same way via \vec v \mapsto q\vec v\bar q, so a full 360° rotation lands on -1, and only 720° gets you home (the belt trick).
  3. Composition becomes multiplication and interpolation becomes slerp — quaternions store a rotation in 4 numbers instead of the 9 entries of a matrix, avoid the gimbal-lock singularities of Euler angles, and slide along geodesics of S^3 when you blend two orientations smoothly.
