03 · Galois Theory — Solvability of Polynomial Equations

When can a polynomial be solved by radicals? Symmetry decides.

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done T6 L02 — Groups and Symmetry and T6 L03 — Rings and Fields?
  2. Recall: a field is a ring where every non-zero element has a multiplicative inverse (\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{F}_p).
  3. Comfortable with permutation groups S_n and normal subgroups?

Touch

The quadratic formula: x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}. Cubic and quartic also have such “by-radicals” formulas (Cardano, Ferrari).

But the quintic x^5 + \cdots has no such formula. Why?

viewof galDeg = Inputs.range([2, 6], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "Polynomial degree n:"})
{
  const symGroupOrder = (n) => { let r = 1; for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) r *= i; return r; };
  const solvable = galDeg <= 4;
  return html`<div style="margin: 1rem 0;">
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:0.95rem; color:#4527a0;">
      Generic polynomial of degree ${galDeg} has Galois group ${galDeg > 0 ? `S<sub>${galDeg}</sub>` : ""} of order ${symGroupOrder(galDeg)}.
    </div>
    <div style="font-family:monospace; font-size:0.95rem; color:${solvable ? "#1b5e20" : "#b71c1c"};">
      ${solvable ? "✓ Solvable group → solvable by radicals." : "✗ S₅ is not solvable → no general radical formula!"}
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

The deepest fact: solvability of polynomials = solvability of a group.

Wonder

  1. What is a “field extension”? E.g. \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) = \{a + b\sqrt{2} : a, b \in \mathbb{Q}\}.
  2. The Galois group of a polynomial p(x) over a field K is the group of symmetries of its roots. Why does it carry information about solvability?
  3. The Galois correspondence: subgroups of the Galois group ↔︎ intermediate fields. A Rosetta stone.

Discover

Problem 1

Compute \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}). Show every element is uniquely expressible as a + b\sqrt{2} with a, b \in \mathbb{Q}.

Hint 1 — a nudge

Closure under multiplication: (a + b\sqrt{2})(c + d\sqrt{2}) = (ac + 2bd) + (ad + bc)\sqrt{2}.

Inverse: (a + b\sqrt{2})^{-1} = (a - b\sqrt{2}) / (a^2 - 2b^2) (rationalising).
Full solution

\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) is a field, with \mathbb{Q}-basis \{1, \sqrt{2}\}. Dimension over \mathbb{Q}: [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) : \mathbb{Q}] = 2.

This is a degree-2 field extension.

Problem 2

The Galois group \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) / \mathbb{Q}) consists of \mathbb{Q}-fixing automorphisms of \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}).

Find them.

Full solution

An automorphism \sigma fixes \mathbb{Q}, so it’s determined by where it sends \sqrt{2}. We need \sigma(\sqrt{2})^2 = \sigma(2) = 2, so \sigma(\sqrt{2}) = \pm\sqrt{2}.

Two automorphisms: identity, and \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}. So \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}) / \mathbb{Q}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}.

Problem 3

Compute the Galois group of x^3 - 2 over \mathbb{Q}.

Hint 1 — a nudge

Roots: \sqrt[3]{2}, \omega\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega^2\sqrt[3]{2} where \omega = e^{2\pi i/3}.

Splitting field: \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[3]{2}, \omega), degree 6 over \mathbb{Q}.
Full solution

Galois group has order 6 (since splitting field is degree-6 over \mathbb{Q}). It permutes the 3 cube roots — gives an embedding into S_3.

Since |S_3| = 6, \mathrm{Gal}(x^3 - 2 / \mathbb{Q}) \cong S_3.

S_3 is solvable: \{e\} \trianglelefteq A_3 \trianglelefteq S_3 with abelian quotients. So x^3 - 2 is solvable by radicals. \sqrt[3]{2} is the radical that does it.

Problem 4

The Galois Correspondence.

For a Galois extension L / K with Galois group G:

  • Subgroups H \leq G correspond to intermediate fields K \subseteq F \subseteq L via H \mapsto L^H (the fixed field) and F \mapsto \mathrm{Gal}(L/F).
  • Inclusion-reversing.
  • H is normal in G iff L^H / K is a Galois extension, with \mathrm{Gal}(L^H / K) \cong G/H.

Apply to \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) / \mathbb{Q}.

Full solution

Galois group \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^2 = \{e, \sigma_2, \sigma_3, \sigma_2\sigma_3\} (Klein 4-group), where \sigma_2: \sqrt{2} \mapsto -\sqrt{2}, \sigma_3: \sqrt{3} \mapsto -\sqrt{3}.

Subgroups: 5 total — \{e\}, \langle\sigma_2\rangle, \langle\sigma_3\rangle, \langle\sigma_2\sigma_3\rangle, the whole group.

Intermediate fields: 5 — \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}), \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{3}), \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}), \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{6}), \mathbb{Q}.

The lattice of subgroups (upside-down) is the lattice of intermediate fields. Group theory replaces field theory.

Problem 5

The big theorem. A polynomial p(x) \in K[x] is solvable by radicals iff its Galois group is a solvable group.

Why is the quintic not solvable by radicals?

Full solution

The Galois group of a generic quintic is S_5. Recall a group G is solvable iff there’s a chain \{e\} = G_0 \trianglelefteq G_1 \trianglelefteq \cdots \trianglelefteq G_n = G with each G_{i+1}/G_i abelian.

For S_5: A_5 is the only proper non-trivial normal subgroup. And A_5 is simple (only normal subgroups: \{e\} and A_5) — but non-abelian. So no chain to \{e\} with abelian quotients. S_5 is not solvable.

Hence the quintic x^5 - x - 1 (whose Galois group is indeed S_5) cannot be solved by radicals. Abel-Ruffini, 1824. Galois explained why.

The night before he died, Évariste Galois wrote the proof you just saw. On 29 May 1832, expecting to be killed in a duel at dawn — over politics, a woman, or both, the historical record is muddied — the 20-year-old sat up writing letters to his friend Auguste Chevalier, sketching the theory of what we now call Galois groups and the correspondence between subgroups and intermediate fields. The manuscript margins carry the famous note “Je n’ai pas le temps” — “I have not the time.” He was shot in the abdomen the next morning and died on 31 May. The papers sat unread for fourteen years until Joseph Liouville finally published them in 1846 and announced to the Academy that they contained “a solution as correct as it is profound” to the long-standing problem of when a polynomial can be solved by radicals. Abel had proved in 1824 that the general quintic cannot; Galois explained why — and, more deeply, told you exactly which specific quintics can. The whole apparatus of modern algebra, from class field theory to elliptic-curve cryptography, descends from papers written by a sleep-deprived teenager preparing to die.

Galois’s move is replacement of one hard problem by an easier one: the question “can this polynomial be solved by radicals?” is wild — infinite-dimensional, geometric, slippery — but the question “is its symmetry group built from abelian pieces?” is finite and checkable. The Galois correspondence is the dictionary that lets you translate between the two languages, and once you have it, the impossibility of the quintic formula becomes a single fact about A_5 being simple.

Name

Field extension L / K: K \subseteq L both fields. Degree [L : K] = \dim_K L.

Galois extension L / K: separable and normal — automorphisms of L fixing K are “as many as possible” (|\mathrm{Aut}(L/K)| = [L : K]).

Galois group \mathrm{Gal}(L / K): K-automorphisms of L.

Galois Correspondence (the main theorem): For finite Galois L/K, inclusion-reversing bijection: \{\text{subgroups of } G\} \leftrightarrow \{\text{intermediate fields}\}, \qquad H \mapsto L^H. Plus: H \trianglelefteq G \Leftrightarrow L^H / K Galois with group G/H.

Solvable group: subnormal chain to \{e\} with all factors abelian.

Solvability theorem (Galois): p(x) \in K[x] is solvable by radicals iff \mathrm{Gal}(p) is solvable.

Consequence: quintic isn’t generally solvable by radicals (S_5 isn’t solvable).

A field extension’s symmetries form a group; Subgroups mirror sub-fields; Solvable polynomial = solvable group.

Apply

Worked example

Show x^4 - 2 is solvable by radicals over \mathbb{Q}.

Roots: \pm\sqrt[4]{2}, \pm i\sqrt[4]{2}.

Splitting field: \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt[4]{2}, i), degree 8 over \mathbb{Q}.

Galois group: D_4 (dihedral of order 8). Subnormal chain: \{e\} \trianglelefteq \langle r^2 \rangle \trianglelefteq \langle r \rangle \trianglelefteq D_4 with each quotient \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 — abelian.

So D_4 is solvable. Hence x^4 - 2 is solvable by radicals — and indeed, \sqrt[4]{2} is the radical that does it.

Practice — Warm

W1. What’s the Galois group of \mathbb{Q}(i) / \mathbb{Q}?

Full solution \mathbb{Z}/2. Generator: complex conjugation.

W2. What’s [\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}) : \mathbb{Q}]?

Full solution 4. Basis: \{1, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{6}\}.

W3. Is \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} solvable?

Full solution Yes — abelian, so trivially solvable.

Practice — Working

P1. Why does the constructibility of regular polygons relate to Galois theory?

Full solution

A regular n-gon is constructible by ruler and compass iff \phi(n) is a power of 2 (Gauss-Wantzel). Equivalently, the Galois group of \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n) / \mathbb{Q} — which is (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^* — must be a 2-group.

That’s why the regular 17-gon is constructible (Gauss, age 19) but the regular 7-gon is not.

P2. Compute \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n} / \mathbb{F}_p).

Full solution

Cyclic of order n, generated by the Frobenius \phi: x \mapsto x^p.

Finite fields’ Galois theory is uniformly cyclic.

P3. Why is A_5 simple?

Hint 1 — a nudge Show every non-trivial normal subgroup must contain a 3-cycle, and 3- cycles generate A_5.
Full solution

A_5 is the smallest non-abelian simple group. This is the linchpin of Galois’s proof of quintic insolvability.

Proof: A_5 has order 60. Conjugacy classes have sizes \{1, 15, 20, 12, 12\}. Any normal subgroup is a union of conjugacy classes whose sizes divide 60. Check all unions including \{1\}: only \{1\} and \{1, 15, 20, 12, 12\} (order 60) work. So A_5 has no proper non-trivial normal subgroup.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Inverse Galois Problem. Given a finite group G, does there exist a polynomial over \mathbb{Q} whose Galois group is G?

Full solution

Open in general. Known for solvable groups (Shafarevich). Known for S_n, A_n, and many sporadic groups. The Monster group: yes.

But not yet known for every finite group.

This is one of the most concrete unsolved problems in modern algebra.

S2. Cryptographic relevance: finite-field Galois.

Full solution

\mathbb{F}_{p^n}’s Galois group is cyclic, generated by Frobenius. In AES, the multiplications happen in \mathbb{F}_{2^8}. The S-box involves an inverse in \mathbb{F}_{2^8} followed by an affine map.

In elliptic-curve cryptography over \mathbb{F}_{p^n} (Tier 7 L13), the Galois action constrains which curves are secure.

Galois theory is the algebraic backbone behind the cryptographic schemes that secure the modern internet.

Common confusions

  1. “Galois proved the quintic has no formula.” Specifically: no formula using arithmetic operations and radicals. Numerical roots exist; the Bring radical and modular forms also yield closed forms beyond radicals.
  2. “The Galois group depends only on the polynomial.” It depends on the polynomial and the base field. Same polynomial, different K → different group.
  3. “All quintics are insolvable by radicals.” Many are: x^5 - 1, x^5 - 2, etc. The general (generic-coefficient) quintic is not. Special ones can be.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Every polynomial has a hidden group — the symmetries of its roots — and that group remembers exactly which arithmetic feats are possible over the base field.
  2. The Galois correspondence is a dictionary between subgroups of that group and sub-fields of the splitting field, with inclusions reversed — group theory and field theory become two sides of the same lattice.
  3. “Solvable by radicals” means “the Galois group is solvable” — so the quintic has no general formula because A_5 is simple and non-abelian, and there is nothing about polynomials in this fact, only about groups.
