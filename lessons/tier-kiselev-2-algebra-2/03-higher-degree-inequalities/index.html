Ch 3 — Higher-Degree Inequalities

Sign-analysis on factorisation — and reading off intervals from a polynomial’s roots

Pre-flight check

  1. Are linear-inequality manipulations (especially the flip on negative multiplication) automatic? See ka1.linear-inequalities.
  2. Can you factor a polynomial into linear factors? See ka2.higher-degree-equations.
  3. Are you comfortable with interval notation — open and closed intervals, unions?
  4. Can you reason about the sign of a product? (Even number of negatives ⇒ positive product.)

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

The polynomial p(x) = (x - 1)(x - 3). It is zero at x = 1, 3, positive outside [1, 3], and negative between them.

viewof xtest = Inputs.range([-2, 6], {value: 2, step: 0.5, label: "x"})
{
  const p = (xtest - 1) * (xtest - 3);
  let region;
  if (p === 0) region = "zero (root)";
  else if (p > 0) region = "positive";
  else region = "negative";
  return `**p(${xtest}) = (${xtest} − 1)(${xtest} − 3) = ${p}** — ${region}.`;
}

The roots split the number line into intervals; the sign is constant within each interval and flips at each simple root.

Wonder

  1. Each simple root flips the sign. What about a double root — does the sign flip there too?
  2. The inequality p(x) > 0 — how do we describe its solution set in interval notation?
  3. The expression \dfrac{x - 1}{x + 2} — its sign depends on both numerator and denominator. Where is it undefined?
  4. The expression |x - 1| < 3 — what’s the geometric description?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. A double root does not flip the sign — both factors of (x - r) contribute, and even-power factors keep the sign.
  2. The solution is the union of intervals where p is positive.
  3. Undefined where the denominator is zero (x = -2); sign-flips at both numerator and denominator zeros (one each side of the “undefined” point). (4) An interval of half-width 3 centred at 1, namely (-2, 4).

Discover

Problem 1 — Sign-chart for a quadratic

Solve x^2 - 5 x + 6 < 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Factor: (x - 2)(x - 3). The product is negative when one factor is positive and the other negative.
Full solution (x - 2)(x - 3) < 0 iff 2 < x < 3. Solution: (2, 3).

Problem 2 — Sign-chart for a cubic

Solve (x + 1)(x - 2)(x - 4) > 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Three roots split \mathbb R into four intervals. Test one point in each.
Full solution

Roots: -1, 2, 4. Intervals: (-\infty, -1), (-1, 2), (2, 4), (4, \infty).

Sign at x = 0: (0 + 1)(0 - 2)(0 - 4) = 1 \cdot (-2) \cdot (-4) = 8 > 0. So in (-1, 2): positive.

Signs alternate across simple roots: (-\infty, -1) negative, (-1, 2) positive, (2, 4) negative, (4, \infty) positive.

Solution: (-1, 2) \cup (4, \infty).

Problem 3 — Double-root sign

Solve (x + 1)^2 (x - 2) > 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge (x + 1)^2 \ge 0 always, with equality only at x = -1. So the sign of the product is the sign of (x - 2) — except at x = -1, where the product is 0.
Full solution

For x \ne -1: (x + 1)^2 > 0, so the product has the sign of (x - 2) — positive when x > 2, negative when x < 2.

At x = -1: product is 0 (not > 0).

Solution: x > 2, i.e. (2, \infty).

(Notice: the double root at -1 does not create a sign-flip; the sign is the same on both sides of x = -1.)

Problem 4 — Rational inequality

Solve \dfrac{x - 1}{x + 2} > 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge A fraction is positive iff numerator and denominator have the same sign. Two roots / undefined points: x = 1 (numerator = 0) and x = -2 (denominator = 0).
Full solution

Both positive: x > 1. Both negative: x < -2. (Excluding the points where each is zero — particularly x = -2, where the fraction is undefined.)

Solution: (-\infty, -2) \cup (1, \infty).

Problem 5 — Combining strict and non-strict

Solve (x - 1)(x - 3) \le 0.

Full solution

The product is \le 0 in [1, 3] (negative inside; zero at endpoints). Solution: [1, 3].

(Strict < would have given (1, 3); the non-strict \le includes the endpoints where the product equals 0.)

Problem 6 — A biquadratic inequality

Solve x^4 - 5 x^2 + 4 \le 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Substitute u = x^2: u^2 - 5 u + 4 \le 0.
Full solution

u^2 - 5 u + 4 = (u - 1)(u - 4) \le 0 for u \in [1, 4].

Back-substitute: 1 \le x^2 \le 4, i.e. 1 \le |x| \le 2.

Solution: -2 \le x \le -1 or 1 \le x \le 2, i.e. [-2, -1] \cup [1, 2].

Problem 7 — AM-GM inequality (general form)

Show that for non-negative a, b: \dfrac{a + b}{2} \ge \sqrt{ab}, with equality iff a = b.

Hint 1 — a nudge (a - b)^2 \ge 0 always. Expand and rearrange.
Full solution

(a - b)^2 \ge 0 \Rightarrow a^2 - 2 a b + b^2 \ge 0 \Rightarrow a^2 + b^2 \ge 2 a b \Rightarrow (a + b)^2 \ge 4 a b \Rightarrow \dfrac{a + b}{2} \ge \sqrt{a b} (taking square roots; valid for a, b \ge 0).

Equality iff (a - b)^2 = 0 iff a = b. QED. (Algebra II §45 — arithmetic-geometric mean inequality in two variables. Generalises to n variables by induction or other arguments.)

Brahmagupta (598–668 CE), in his Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta of 628 CE, was the first to write down systematic rules for arithmetic involving negative quantities — what he called “debts” (ṛṇa) as opposed to “fortunes” (dhana). The product of two debts is a fortune; a debt subtracted from zero is a fortune; the sign of a product flips with the parity of negatives. These are the same rules underlying the sign-chart machinery in this chapter — when you say “two negatives multiply to a positive, so the product on this interval is positive”, you are applying Brahmagupta’s 7th-century laws. Europe didn’t accept negative numbers until the 17th century, almost a thousand years later.

A polynomial’s sign is read off its factorisation: the roots split the line, and parity decides each interval. Once P(x) is factored as a (x - r_1)^{m_1} (x - r_2)^{m_2} \cdots, the roots are the only places where the sign can change — and the sign actually flips only at odd-multiplicity roots. Build a sign chart and the linear inequality routine of Algebra I generalises to every polynomial and rational expression.

Name

Polynomial inequality: P(x) > 0, \ge 0, < 0, \le 0, where P is a polynomial.

Sign-chart method.

  1. Factor P(x) over the reals as far as possible.
  2. Find the real roots and their multiplicities.
  3. Split \mathbb R into intervals at the roots.
  4. Test one point in each interval (or note the alternation rule).
  5. Read off the solution set.

Sign-flip rule. A simple root flips the sign. A double root does not flip the sign. A triple root flips the sign. (More generally, an odd-multiplicity root flips; even-multiplicity does not.)

Rational-inequality method. \dfrac{P(x)}{Q(x)} > 0 — combine into one rational expression, find roots of P and zeros of Q, build the sign-chart with both numerator and denominator zeros (remembering the undefined points where Q = 0).

AM-GM (two variables): \dfrac{a + b}{2} \ge \sqrt{a b}, with equality iff a = b. (Generalises to n variables.)

A verse:

Factor first, find the roots, walk the line. Each simple root flips the sign; the doubled ones decline. Test one point per interval, or use the alternation pattern. And AM-GM bounds the geometric mean from above by the arithmetic Saturn.

Apply

Worked example

Solve x^3 - 6 x^2 + 11 x - 6 \le 0.

Factor (using the lesson’s earlier polynomial): (x - 1)(x - 2) (x - 3) \le 0.

Roots 1, 2, 3. Sign-chart: - x < 1: all three factors negative ⟹ product negative ✓ - 1 < x < 2: one positive, two negative ⟹ positive ✗ - 2 < x < 3: two positive, one negative ⟹ negative ✓ - x > 3: all positive ⟹ positive ✗

Including the boundary roots: Solution: (-\infty, 1] \cup [2, 3].

Practice — Warm

W1. Solve (x - 2)(x + 5) > 0.

Full solution Roots 2, -5. Outside the interval: x < -5 or x > 2. Solution: (-\infty, -5) \cup (2, \infty).

W2. Solve x^2 + 4 < 0.

Full solution x^2 \ge 0 always, so x^2 + 4 \ge 4 > 0 always. No solution.

W3. Solve x^2 \ge 9.

Full solution |x| \ge 3 ⟹ x \le -3 or x \ge 3. (-\infty, -3] \cup [3, \infty).

Practice — Working

P1. Solve \dfrac{2 x - 3}{x + 1} \ge 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Move everything to one side: \dfrac{2 x - 3}{x + 1} - 1 \ge 0. Combine.
Full solution

\dfrac{2 x - 3 - (x + 1)}{x + 1} \ge 0 \Rightarrow \dfrac{x - 4} {x + 1} \ge 0.

Sign-chart: zeros at x = 4 (numerator) and x = -1 (denominator, undefined).

  • x < -1: (x - 4) < 0, (x + 1) < 0, ratio positive ✓
  • -1 < x < 4: (x - 4) < 0, (x + 1) > 0, ratio negative ✗
  • x > 4: both positive ✓
Including x = 4 (numerator = 0, ratio = 0, OK) but excluding x = -1 (undefined). Solution: (-\infty, -1) \cup [4, \infty).

P2. Solve |2 x - 1| > 3.

Hint 1 — a nudge |y| > 3 \Leftrightarrow y > 3 or y < -3.
Full solution 2 x - 1 > 3 (x > 2) or 2 x - 1 < -3 (x < -1). Solution: (-\infty, -1) \cup (2, \infty).

P3. Solve x^4 - 16 \ge 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Factor: x^4 - 16 = (x^2 - 4)(x^2 + 4) = (x - 2)(x + 2)(x^2 + 4). (x^2 + 4) > 0 always, so sign depends on (x - 2)(x + 2).
Full solution (x - 2)(x + 2) \ge 0 outside (-2, 2). Solution: (-\infty, -2] \cup [2, \infty).

Practice — Stretch

S1. Solve \dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 - 4} \ge 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Factor: \dfrac{(x - 1)(x + 1)}{(x - 2)(x + 2)} \ge 0. Four critical points: x = -2, -1, 1, 2.
Full solution

Zeros: \pm 1. Undefined: \pm 2. Sign-chart on five intervals:

  • x < -2: positive
  • -2 < x < -1: negative
  • -1 < x < 1: positive
  • 1 < x < 2: negative
  • x > 2: positive
Including roots \pm 1 but excluding \pm 2: Solution: (-\infty, -2) \cup [-1, 1] \cup (2, \infty).

S2. Show that for any positive a, b, c: \dfrac{a + b + c}{3} \ge \sqrt[3]{a b c} (the three-variable AM-GM).

Hint 1 — a nudge Reduces to two applications of the two-variable case, by clever choice. Or: use the identity a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3 a b c = (a + b + c)(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 - ab - bc - ca) from Algebra I Ch 3 S2 — and note that the second factor is \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{(sum of squares of differences)}.
Full solution

Use the identity a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3 a b c = (a + b + c) \cdot \frac{1}{2} [(a - b)^2 + (b - c)^2 + (c - a)^2] \ge 0 (since positive sum of squares is non-negative).

So a^3 + b^3 + c^3 \ge 3 a b c. Substituting a = \alpha^{1/3}, b = \beta^{1/3}, c = \gamma^{1/3}: \alpha + \beta + \gamma \ge 3 (\alpha \beta \gamma)^{1/3}, i.e. \dfrac{\alpha + \beta + \gamma}{3} \ge \sqrt[3]{\alpha \beta \gamma}. QED.

(The general n-variable AM-GM follows by an induction or a similar substitution; the proof here is one of Cauchy’s classical arguments.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s terse style, adapted from Algebra II, §§40–50.

K1. Find all real x such that \dfrac{x^2 - x - 6}{x^2 - 5 x + 6} > 0.

Full solution

Factor: \dfrac{(x - 3)(x + 2)}{(x - 2)(x - 3)} > 0.

For x \ne 3: cancel (x - 3), getting \dfrac{x + 2}{x - 2} > 0. By P1’s logic: x < -2 or x > 2, excluding x = 3.

Solution: (-\infty, -2) \cup (2, 3) \cup (3, \infty).

(The cancelled (x - 3) creates a removable singularity* at x = 3 — undefined in the original, but otherwise sign-irrelevant.)*

K2. Show that for all real x: x^4 + 1 \ge 2 x^2.

Full solution x^4 + 1 - 2 x^2 = (x^2 - 1)^2 \ge 0, with equality iff x = \pm 1. QED.

K3. Solve \sqrt{x + 7} > x - 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two cases: (a) RHS negative (x < 1): inequality holds whenever LHS is defined; (b) RHS non-negative: square both sides, but be careful.
Full solution

Domain: x \ge -7.

Case (a): x < 1. RHS < 0 \le LHS. So inequality holds for -7 \le x < 1.

Case (b): x \ge 1. Both sides non-negative; square: x + 7 > (x - 1)^2 = x^2 - 2 x + 1. Rearrange: x^2 - 3 x - 6 < 0. Discriminant 9 + 24 = 33. Roots \dfrac{3 \pm \sqrt{33}}{2} \approx \dfrac{3 \pm 5.745}{2}, so \approx -1.37, 4.37. Strict inequality holds for -1.37 < x < 4.37. Combined with x \ge 1: 1 \le x < (3 + \sqrt{33})/2.

Combined: -7 \le x < (3 + \sqrt{33})/2, approximately [-7, 4.37).

Source pointer. Adapted from Kiselev, Алгебра, Часть вторая (Fizmatlit reprint 2005), §§36–50. Russian original: archive.org scan. The AM-GM inequality (Problem 7 and S2) is §45.

Common confusions

  1. “\dfrac{a}{b} > 0 \Leftrightarrow a > 0 and b > 0.” Wrong. Also when both are negative. The full statement: \dfrac{a}{b} > 0 iff a, b have the same sign.
  2. “Sign flips at every root.” Only at odd-multiplicity roots. At even-multiplicity roots, the sign does not flip (Problem 3).
  3. “Squaring an inequality preserves direction.” Only if both sides are non-negative. -3 < 1 but 9 \not< 1. Be careful.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. Factor, mark the roots, walk the number line. The sign-chart method reduces every polynomial inequality to a single procedure: factor, find roots, test one point per interval (or alternate signs across odd- multiplicity roots).
  2. Even multiplicity means no sign flip. A double root touches the axis without crossing; a triple root crosses; in general, only odd-multiplicity roots flip the sign. Forgetting this is the chapter’s most common error.
  3. AM-GM is the universal bound. The arithmetic-mean– geometric-mean inequality \tfrac{a + b}{2} \ge \sqrt{ab} is the simplest of an entire family (Cauchy-Schwarz, Hölder, Minkowski) — but knowing this one and equality condition a = b already cracks dozens of competition problems.

  2. Did the double-root case (Problem 3) feel like a special-case exception, or like a natural consequence of the structure?
  3. Can you prove the AM-GM in two variables (Problem 7) on a fresh sheet?

Where this matters

AI / ML. Loss landscapes are usually inequality-bounded: predictions must lie in a probability simplex (each p_i \ge 0, \sum p_i = 1), gradient updates clip to a constraint set. Sign- chart logic at the boundary is exactly this lesson’s machinery.

Databases. Range queries like WHERE age BETWEEN 18 AND 65 AND status != 'inactive' are systems of polynomial-style inequalities (literal boolean polynomials over \{\text{TRUE}, \text{FALSE}\}). Query planners exploit sign-chart-like logic when choosing index strategies.

Research. The AM-GM inequality (Problem 7 and S2) is one of the most-used inequalities in mathematics. It generalises to Hölder, Minkowski, Cauchy-Schwarz — a whole web of named inequalities.
