Ch 7 — Binomial Theorem

Expanding (a + b)^n without multiplying it out by hand

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you compute \binom{n}{r} from the formula?
  2. Do you remember the identity (a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2?
  3. Are you fluent with summation \sum notation?
  4. Can you expand (a+b)^3 without looking it up?

Three confident yeses — read on. If \binom{n}{r} feels uncertain, revisit Permutations and Combinations.

Touch

Write Pascal’s triangle. Start with a 1 at the top. Each row begins and ends with 1; every interior entry is the sum of the two entries above it.

                1
              1   1
            1   2   1
          1   3   3   1
        1   4   6   4   1
      1   5  10  10   5   1
    1   6  15  20  15   6   1

The n-th row is the list of \binom{n}{0}, \binom{n}{1}, \ldots, \binom{n}{n}. And those are exactly the coefficients in the expansion of (a+b)^n.

The widget below expands (a + b)^n for any n you choose.

viewof bn = Inputs.range([1, 8], {value: 4, step: 1, label: "n"})
{
  // build (a+b)^n
  function fact(n) { let r = 1; for (let i = 2; i <= n; i++) r *= i; return r; }
  function C(n, r) { return fact(n) / (fact(r) * fact(n - r)); }
  const terms = [];
  for (let r = 0; r <= bn; r++) {
    const c = C(bn, r);
    const aPow = bn - r === 0 ? "" : (bn - r === 1 ? "a" : `a^${bn - r}`);
    const bPow = r === 0 ? "" : (r === 1 ? "b" : `b^${r}`);
    const coef = c === 1 ? "" : `${c}`;
    let term = coef + aPow + bPow;
    if (term === "") term = "1";
    terms.push(term);
  }
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;line-height:1.7;">
    <div><b>(a + b)<sup>${bn}</sup></b> =</div>
    <div style="font-size:1.1em;margin-left:1em;">${terms.join(" + ")}</div>
  </div>`;
}

Note how the powers of a go from n down to 0, the powers of b go from 0 up to n, and the coefficients trace exactly Pascal’s n-th row.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. The expansion of (a+b)^n has n+1 terms. Why exactly n+1 — and not, say, n or 2^n?
  2. The total of the coefficients in the row is 1 + n + \binom{n}{2} + \cdots + 1. Is there a clean formula for this total?
  3. If you replace b by -b, what changes about the expansion?
  4. The middle of the row holds the largest entries. Where exactly is the middle?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. is solved by setting a = b = 1: the sum of any row of Pascal’s triangle is 2^n. (3) explains alternating signs in expansions like (1 - x)^n.

Discover

Problem 1 — Expand by hand

Expand (x + 2)^4 using Pascal’s row 1, 4, 6, 4, 1.

Full solution (x + 2)^4 = x^4 + 4 x^3(2) + 6 x^2 (2)^2 + 4 x (2)^3 + (2)^4 = x^4 + 8x^3 + 24x^2 + 32x + 16.

Problem 2 — Single term

Find the term containing x^5 in (x + 2)^7.

Hint 1 — a nudge General term: T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r. We need power of x to be 5.
Full solution

T_{r+1} = \binom{7}{r} x^{7-r} 2^r. Want 7 - r = 5, so r = 2.

T_3 = \binom{7}{2} x^5 \cdot 4 = 21 \cdot 4 x^5 = 84 x^5.

Problem 3 — Sum of coefficients

What is the sum of the coefficients in (2x + 3)^5?

Hint 1 — a nudge Set x = 1.
Full solution At x = 1: (2 + 3)^5 = 5^5 = 3125.

Problem 4 — Middle term

Find the middle term of (x + y)^{10}.

Hint 1 — a nudge 11 terms total (n + 1 when n = 10). Middle is the 6th, i.e. r = 5.
Full solution T_6 = \binom{10}{5} x^5 y^5 = 252 \, x^5 y^5.

Problem 5 — Negative inside

Expand (1 - 2x)^4.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the binomial theorem with b = -2x.
Full solution \binom{4}{0}(1)^4 + \binom{4}{1}(1)^3(-2x) + \binom{4}{2}(1)^2(-2x)^2 + \binom{4}{3}(1)(-2x)^3 + \binom{4}{4}(-2x)^4 = 1 - 8x + 24x^2 - 32x^3 + 16x^4.

Problem 6 — Term independent of x

Find the term independent of x in \left(x + \dfrac{1}{x}\right)^6.

Hint 1 — a nudge T_{r+1} = \binom{6}{r} x^{6-r} (1/x)^r = \binom{6}{r} x^{6 - 2r}. Power of x is zero when…
Full solution

6 - 2r = 0 \Rightarrow r = 3. T_4 = \binom{6}{3} = 20.

The constant term is \mathbf{20}.

The “indices add to n” check. In every term \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r, the exponents on a and b must sum to n. Use this as a free sanity check after every general-term calculation. In Problem 6, (6 - r) + r = 6 even after the 1/x became x^{-r} — the underlying b-power is still r, which is what matters.

You have just used the binomial theorem, the general term, the middle term, and the independent-of-x trick. Now we name them.

The triangle of combination numbers we call Pascal’s triangle was studied by the Chinese mathematician Yang Hui around 1261 CE — and Yang Hui himself attributed it to the earlier Jia Xian (~1100 CE). Persian mathematician al-Karaji (~1000 CE) also had it. The Indian prosodist Pingala (~200 BCE) listed binomial coefficients in his Chhandahshastra to count Sanskrit poetic meters of length n. Pascal’s Traité du Triangle Arithmétique (1665) was the first systematic Western treatment, but the triangle had been doing its job around the world for nearly two millennia. Newton went further the same year — extending the binomial theorem to non-integer powers — and that single generalisation opened up the calculus of power series.

The binomial theorem is just the multiplication principle in disguise. Expanding (a+b)^n = (a+b)(a+b)\cdots(a+b) asks: from each of the n brackets, pick a or b. The number of ways to pick b from exactly r brackets is \binom{n}{r} — and that is exactly the coefficient of a^{n-r} b^r. Every formula in this chapter — general term, middle term, sum of coefficients — is a different question asked of the same picking process.

Name

Binomial theorem for any positive integer n:

(a + b)^n = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r.

The general term (the (r+1)-th term) is T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r.

Number of terms: n + 1.

Sum of coefficients in (a+b)^n, evaluated at a = b = 1: \displaystyle \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} = 2^n.

Alternating sum: at a = 1, b = -1: \displaystyle \sum_{r=0}^{n} (-1)^r \binom{n}{r} = 0 for n \ge 1.

Middle term(s):

  • n even (n = 2m): one middle term, T_{m+1}.
  • n odd (n = 2m+1): two middle terms, T_{m+1} and T_{m+2}.

Pascal’s rule: \binom{n}{r-1} + \binom{n}{r} = \binom{n+1}{r} — the construction rule of the triangle.

A short verse:

Powers of a slide down from n; Powers of b climb up to greet them. Coefficients live in Pascal’s house — Add the two above, the next is born.

Apply

Worked example 1 — coefficient of a specific power

Find the coefficient of x^4 in the expansion of (1 + 2x)^7.

How a competent solver thinks:

  1. General term: T_{r+1} = \binom{7}{r} (1)^{7-r} (2x)^r = \binom{7}{r} 2^r x^r.
  2. Want the x^4 term: r = 4.
  3. T_5 = \binom{7}{4} 2^4 x^4 = 35 \cdot 16 \cdot x^4 = 560 x^4.

Coefficient: 560.

Worked example 2 — numerical evaluation by binomial

Evaluate (99)^5 by hand.

The trick: pick a base near a round number. Write 99 = 100 - 1 and apply (a - b)^n with a = 100, b = 1, n = 5:

(100 - 1)^5 = \sum_{r=0}^{5} (-1)^r \binom{5}{r} 100^{5-r}.

Term by term, \binom{5}{0} 100^5 - \binom{5}{1} 100^4 + \binom{5}{2} 100^3 - \binom{5}{3} 100^2 + \binom{5}{4} 100 - \binom{5}{5} = 10{,}000{,}000{,}000 - 500{,}000{,}000 + 10{,}000{,}000 - 100{,}000 + 500 - 1 = 9{,}509{,}900{,}499.

The same trick handles (101)^4 = (100 + 1)^4, (998)^3 = (1000 - 2)^3, and so on. Lead with the round number; let b be the small correction.

Worked example 3 — divisibility (the “1 + k” trick)

Show that 9^{n+1} - 8n - 9 is divisible by 64 for every positive integer n.

The structural idea: rewrite 9 = 1 + 8 so the binomial expansion exposes a factor of 8^2 = 64.

9^{n+1} = 9 \cdot 9^n = 9 \cdot (1 + 8)^n. Expand:

(1 + 8)^n = 1 + 8n + \binom{n}{2} 8^2 + \binom{n}{3} 8^3 + \cdots + 8^n.

So 9 \cdot 9^n = 9 + 72n + 9\!\left[\binom{n}{2} 8^2 + \binom{n}{3} 8^3 + \cdots\right].

Subtract 8n + 9: 9^{n+1} - 8n - 9 = (9 - 9) + (72n - 8n) + 9\!\left[\binom{n}{2} 8^2 + \cdots\right] = 64n + 9 \cdot 64 \left[\binom{n}{2} + \binom{n}{3} \cdot 8 + \cdots\right] = 64 \cdot k, for some positive integer k. Hence 64 \mid (9^{n+1} - 8n - 9). \blacksquare

The “1 + k” rewrite is the master move for divisibility. Want to show something is divisible by k^2? Rewrite the base as 1 + k (so 7 = 1 + 6, 11 = 1 + 10, 9 = 1 + 8), expand, and the first two terms are 1 and nk — everything beyond carries k^2 as a factor.

Practice — Warm

W1. Expand (1 + x)^5.

Full solution 1 + 5x + 10x^2 + 10x^3 + 5x^4 + x^5.

W2. Number of terms in (2x - 3y)^9?

Full solution n + 1 = 10.

W3. Sum of coefficients in (x + 1)^{12}.

Full solution At x = 1: 2^{12} = 4096.

Practice — Working

P1. Find the middle term of \left(\dfrac{x}{2} + \dfrac{2}{x}\right)^8.

Hint 1 — a nudge n = 8 is even, so one middle term at r = 4.
Full solution

T_5 = \binom{8}{4} \left(\dfrac{x}{2}\right)^4 \left(\dfrac{2}{x}\right)^4 = 70 \cdot \dfrac{x^4}{16} \cdot \dfrac{16}{x^4} = 70.

(Independent of x.)

P2. Find the coefficient of x^{10} in \left(2x^2 - \dfrac{1}{x}\right)^{11}.

Hint 1 — a nudge T_{r+1} = \binom{11}{r}(2x^2)^{11-r}(-1/x)^r. Find r that gives power 10.
Full solution

x power: 2(11 - r) - r = 22 - 3r = 10 \Rightarrow r = 4.

T_5 = \binom{11}{4} 2^7 (-1)^4 x^{10} = 330 \cdot 128 \cdot x^{10} = 42240 x^{10}.

Coefficient: 42{,}240.

P3. Use the binomial expansion of (1.01)^5 = (1 + 0.01)^5 to estimate (1.01)^5 to 4 decimal places.

Hint 1 — a nudge The first few terms dominate.
Full solution 1 + 5(0.01) + 10(0.01)^2 + 10(0.01)^3 + \cdots = 1 + 0.05 + 0.001 + 0.00001 + \cdots \approx 1.0510.

Practice — Stretch

S1. If the coefficients of three consecutive terms in (1 + x)^n are in the ratio 1 : 7 : 42, find n.

Hint 1 — a nudge Coefficients: \binom{n}{r-1}, \binom{n}{r}, \binom{n}{r+1}. Ratios: \binom{n}{r}/\binom{n}{r-1} = (n - r + 1)/r.
Full solution

\dfrac{\binom{n}{r}}{\binom{n}{r-1}} = \dfrac{n - r + 1}{r} = 7 gives n + 1 = 8r.

\dfrac{\binom{n}{r+1}}{\binom{n}{r}} = \dfrac{n - r}{r + 1} = 6 gives n - r = 6(r+1), i.e. n = 7r + 6.

From the first: n = 8r - 1. Equate: 8r - 1 = 7r + 6 \Rightarrow r = 7. Then n = 55.

n = 55.

S2. Show that \binom{2n}{n} is the largest binomial coefficient in (a + b)^{2n}, and use the binomial theorem to prove \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r}^2 = \binom{2n}{n}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare (1 + x)^n (1 + x)^n with (1 + x)^{2n}. Match the coefficient of x^n on both sides.
Full solution

LHS: (1+x)^n (1+x)^n. The coefficient of x^n is \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r} \binom{n}{n-r} = \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r}^2 (using \binom{n}{n-r} = \binom{n}{r}).

RHS: (1+x)^{2n}. The coefficient of x^n is \binom{2n}{n}.

Equating coefficients: \sum_{r=0}^{n} \binom{n}{r}^2 = \binom{2n}{n}. ✓

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). The number of terms in (1 + x)^n + (1 - x)^n, when n is even, equals (a) n (b) n + 1 (c) n/2 + 1 (d) n/2.

Full solution Terms with even powers of x survive (odd cancel). For n even, even powers are 0, 2, 4, \ldots, n, count n/2 + 1. Answer: (c).

2-mark (short answer). Expand (1 + x)^4 - (1 - x)^4.

Full solution

(1 + x)^4 = 1 + 4x + 6x^2 + 4x^3 + x^4.

(1 - x)^4 = 1 - 4x + 6x^2 - 4x^3 + x^4.

Difference: 8x + 8x^3.

3-mark (short answer II). Find the coefficient of x^6 y^3 in (x + 2y)^9.

Hint 1 — a nudge T_{r+1} = \binom{9}{r} x^{9-r}(2y)^r. Want 9 - r = 6, so r = 3.
Full solution

\binom{9}{3} \cdot 2^3 = 84 \cdot 8 = 672.

Coefficient: 672.

5-mark (long answer). (i) Find the term independent of x in \left(\dfrac{3}{2} x^2 - \dfrac{1}{3x}\right)^9. (ii) Verify by computing the same with \dfrac{3}{2} x^2 + \dfrac{1}{3x} (the “+” version) and contrasting the sign of the result.

Hint 1 — a nudge T_{r+1} = \binom{9}{r}\left(\tfrac{3}{2}\right)^{9-r} x^{2(9-r)} \left(-\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^r x^{-r}. The exponent of x is 2(9-r) - r = 18 - 3r.
Full solution

(i) 18 - 3r = 0 \Rightarrow r = 6.

T_7 = \binom{9}{6} \left(\dfrac{3}{2}\right)^3 \left(-\dfrac{1}{3}\right)^6 = 84 \cdot \dfrac{27}{8} \cdot \dfrac{1}{729} = \dfrac{84 \cdot 27}{8 \cdot 729} = \dfrac{2268}{5832} = \dfrac{7}{18}.

(ii) With “+”: same magnitude, with (+1/3)^6 = 1/729 — sign unchanged. Both versions give \dfrac{7}{18}. (For odd r, the sign would differ; for even r, it does not.)

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. One formula does everything: T_{r+1} = \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r. The general term is the only thing you have to remember; “middle term,” “term independent of x,” and “coefficient of x^k” are all the same problem with a different equation in r.
  2. Powers of a slide down, powers of b slide up, sum is always n. That’s the structural fingerprint of a binomial expansion. If your a- and b-powers don’t add to n, you’ve made an algebra error.
  3. The coefficients are the entries of Pascal’s triangle, and the row sums to 2^n. Setting a = b = 1 makes the entire row collapse into a single number — the cleanest sanity check there is.

Common confusions

  1. “Coefficient of x^k equals \binom{n}{k} in (a + bx)^n.” Only when a = 1. The general formula gives the term \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k x^k, so the coefficient is \binom{n}{k} a^{n-k} b^k, not just \binom{n}{k}.
  2. “Number of terms = n.” It’s n + 1. Always include the r = 0 case.
  3. “Middle term position.” For n even there is one middle term at r = n/2; for n odd there are two, at r = (n-1)/2 and r = (n+1)/2.
  4. Off-by-one on the term index. T_{r+1} — not T_r — equals \binom{n}{r} a^{n-r} b^r. So “find the 6th term” means r = 5, not r = 6. Mis-reading T_r vs T_{r+1} is the single most common error in JEE binomial questions.
  5. Dropping the sign in (a - b)^n. When the binomial is (a - b)^n, every odd-r term is negative. Always carry the (-1)^r explicitly, especially when only the coefficient (not the term) is asked for.
  6. Forgetting parentheses on negative bases. (-2x)^3 = -8x^3, but -2x^3 is something else entirely. Always bracket the negative base before raising to the power.
  7. “Term” vs “coefficient”. The 6th term of (2 + 3x)^{13} includes the x-power; the coefficient is just the numerical part.

What this unlocks

Where this matters

Probability / AI. A coin tossed n times produces k heads with probability \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}. This is the binomial distribution, the most common discrete distribution in statistics and machine learning.

Encryption. Cyclic codes and BCH codes rely on polynomial identities that follow from binomial-coefficient combinatorics in finite fields.

Research. Newton extended the binomial theorem to negative and fractional n, opening the path to power-series analysis. The identity in Stretch S2 — the Vandermonde identity — is one of combinatorics’ most reused tools.
