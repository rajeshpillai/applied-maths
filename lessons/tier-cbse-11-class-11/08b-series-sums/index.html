Ch 8b — Series and Special Sums

Sums in closed form: Σn, Σn², Σn³, and the arithmetic-geometric mix

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you write down the AP sum formula S_n = \dfrac{n}{2}(2a + (n-1)d)?
  2. Are you fluent with the GP sum S_n = \dfrac{a(r^n - 1)}{r - 1}?
  3. Are you comfortable with summation \sum notation?
  4. Can you compute 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 100 in your head with a trick?

Three confident yeses — read on.

Touch

Pile up unit cubes into a square of side n. The number of cubes is n^2. Now stack: a 1 \times 1 on top of 2 \times 2 on top of 3 \times 3 on top of \cdots on top of n \times n. The total cubes used is 1 + 4 + 9 + 16 + \cdots + n^2.

Is there a clean formula? Yes — and the widget below verifies it for each n.

viewof Nss = Inputs.range([1, 20], {value: 5, step: 1, label: "n"})
{
  const n = Nss;
  let s1 = 0, s2 = 0, s3 = 0;
  for (let k = 1; k <= n; k++) { s1 += k; s2 += k * k; s3 += k * k * k; }
  const f1 = n * (n + 1) / 2;
  const f2 = n * (n + 1) * (2 * n + 1) / 6;
  const f3 = (n * (n + 1) / 2) ** 2;
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;line-height:1.7;">
    <div><b>Σk</b> = 1 + 2 + ... + ${n} = <b>${s1}</b> &nbsp; (formula: n(n+1)/2 = ${f1})</div>
    <div><b>Σk²</b> = 1² + 2² + ... + ${n}² = <b>${s2}</b> &nbsp; (formula: n(n+1)(2n+1)/6 = ${f2})</div>
    <div><b>Σk³</b> = 1³ + 2³ + ... + ${n}³ = <b>${s3}</b> &nbsp; (formula: [n(n+1)/2]² = ${f3})</div>
    <div style="margin-top:0.4em;color:#666;">
      Notice: Σk³ = (Σk)². The cubes' sum is the square of the linear sum.
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

Three formulas; each is the closed form for one of the special sums of this lesson.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. The sum \sum k has a n^2-flavoured answer. The sum \sum k^2 has a n^3-flavoured answer. What pattern do you predict for \sum k^4?
  2. The sequence 1 \cdot 2, 2 \cdot 3, 3 \cdot 4, 4 \cdot 5, \ldots — what is its k-th term, and how would you sum it?
  3. The series 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + 4x^3 + \cdots — neither AP nor GP, but a blend. Does it sum to something clean for |x| < 1?
  4. Why does \sum k^3 = (\sum k)^2? Is that a coincidence?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. is the seed of summing \sum k(k+1) = \sum (k^2 + k) by linearity. (3) is the arithmetic-geometric series — the headline of this lesson.

Discover

Problem 1 — Sum of first n natural numbers

Compute 1 + 2 + 3 + \cdots + 100.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pair: (1 + 100), (2 + 99), \ldots — Gauss’s trick.
Full solution 50 pairs, each summing to 101: 50 \times 101 = 5050. Equivalently, \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{100 \cdot 101}{2} = 5050.

Problem 2 — Sum of squares

Compute 1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + \cdots + 10^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}.
Full solution \dfrac{10 \cdot 11 \cdot 21}{6} = \dfrac{2310}{6} = 385.

Problem 3 — Sum of cubes

Verify 1^3 + 2^3 + 3^3 + 4^3 = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4)^2.

Full solution

LHS: 1 + 8 + 27 + 64 = 100. RHS: 10^2 = 100. ✓

In general \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2.

Problem 4 — Linearity

Compute \sum_{k=1}^{20} (3k^2 + 2k - 5).

Hint 1 — a nudge Split: 3 \sum k^2 + 2 \sum k - 5 \sum 1.
Full solution 3 \cdot \dfrac{20 \cdot 21 \cdot 41}{6} + 2 \cdot \dfrac{20 \cdot 21}{2} - 5 \cdot 20 = 3 \cdot 2870 + 420 - 100 = 8610 + 420 - 100 = 8930.

Problem 5 — Sum of k(k+1)

Compute \sum_{k=1}^{n} k(k+1) in closed form.

Hint 1 — a nudge k(k+1) = k^2 + k.
Full solution \sum k^2 + \sum k = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{6}\left[(2n+1) + 3\right] = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+4)}{6} = \dfrac{n(n+1)(n+2)}{3}.

Problem 6 — Arithmetic-geometric series

Find S = 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + 4x^3 + \cdots for |x| < 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Multiply by x, subtract.
Full solution

S = 1 + 2x + 3x^2 + 4x^3 + \cdots

xS = \quad x + 2x^2 + 3x^3 + \cdots

Subtract: S - xS = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \cdots = \dfrac{1}{1 - x}.

So S(1 - x) = \dfrac{1}{1 - x}, giving S = \dfrac{1}{(1 - x)^2}.

You have just used the three special sums (\sum k, \sum k^2, \sum k^3), linearity of summation, and the multiply-and-subtract trick for arithmetic-geometric (AGP) series. Now we name them.

The Indian astronomer-mathematician Aryabhata wrote the formulas for \sum k, \sum k^2, and \sum k^3 in his Aryabhatiya (499 CE) — verses II.19 and II.22 — more than a thousand years before Faulhaber’s general 1631 formula and Bernoulli’s Ars Conjectandi (1713) gave the rest of the family. Aryabhata stated the cube-sum identity \sum k^3 = (\sum k)^2 as a single Sanskrit verse, the length of a tweet. The three formulas you are about to memorise have been used continuously by working mathematicians for over fifteen hundred years.

Three closed-form sums — for \sum k, \sum k^2, \sum k^3 — plus linearity (\sum (au_k + bv_k) = a\sum u_k + b\sum v_k) handles any polynomial \sum p(k). For sequences that are AP-times-GP the trick is multiply by r and subtract; for \sum 1/[k(k+1)] the trick is partial-fraction telescoping. Three formulas plus three tricks: that is the whole arsenal.

Name

The three special sums: \sum_{k=1}^{n} k = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^3 = \left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2

Linearity: \sum (a u_k + b v_k) = a \sum u_k + b \sum v_k.

Arithmetic-Geometric Progression (AGP). Term: T_k = (a + (k-1)d) r^{k-1}. Sum to n terms (multiply-and-subtract method): S_n = \dfrac{a - (a + (n-1)d) r^n}{1 - r} + \dfrac{d r (1 - r^{n-1})}{(1 - r)^2}.

For |r| < 1, sum to infinity: S_\infty = \dfrac{a}{1 - r} + \dfrac{d r}{(1 - r)^2}.

(In practice: derive the formula on the spot by multiplying by r and subtracting. That’s the Class-11 standard method.)

A short verse:

Sum the first n — the n^2 pattern shows; Sum the squares — a cubic surprise grows. Sum the cubes — the linear sum, squared; And linearity splits any combined.

Apply

Worked example

Find S = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (2k - 1)^2 in closed form.

How a competent solver thinks:

  1. Expand: (2k - 1)^2 = 4k^2 - 4k + 1.
  2. Sum: 4 \sum k^2 - 4 \sum k + \sum 1 = 4 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} - 4 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} + n.
  3. Common factor n: = n\left[\dfrac{2(n+1)(2n+1)}{3} - 2(n+1) + 1\right] = \dfrac{n}{3}\left[2(n+1)(2n+1) - 6(n+1) + 3\right] = \dfrac{n}{3}\left[(n+1)(4n - 4) + 3\right] = \dfrac{n}{3}\left[4(n+1)(n-1) + 3\right] = \dfrac{n(4n^2 - 1)}{3} = \dfrac{n(2n-1)(2n+1)}{3}.

Answer: \sum_{k=1}^{n}(2k-1)^2 = \dfrac{n(2n-1)(2n+1)}{3}.

Practice — Warm

W1. Find \sum_{k=1}^{15} k.

Full solution \dfrac{15 \cdot 16}{2} = 120.

W2. Find \sum_{k=1}^{8} k^2.

Full solution \dfrac{8 \cdot 9 \cdot 17}{6} = 204.

W3. Find \sum_{k=1}^{6} k^3.

Full solution \left(\dfrac{6 \cdot 7}{2}\right)^2 = 21^2 = 441.

Practice — Working

P1. Find the sum of the first n terms of the series whose k-th term is T_k = k(k+1)(k+2).

Hint 1 — a nudge Expand: T_k = k^3 + 3k^2 + 2k.
Full solution \sum k^3 + 3 \sum k^2 + 2 \sum k = \left(\dfrac{n(n+1)}{2}\right)^2 + 3 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + 2 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{n^2(n+1)^2}{4} + \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{2} + n(n+1) = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{4}\left[n(n+1) + 2(2n+1) + 4\right] = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{4}\left[n^2 + 5n + 6\right] = \dfrac{n(n+1)(n+2)(n+3)}{4}.

P2. Find the sum to n terms of 1 + 3x + 5x^2 + 7x^3 + \cdots.

Hint 1 — a nudge AGP: a = 1, d = 2, r = x. Multiply by x and subtract.
Full solution

S = 1 + 3x + 5x^2 + \cdots + (2n - 1) x^{n-1}.

xS = \quad x + 3x^2 + \cdots + (2n - 3) x^{n-1} + (2n - 1) x^n.

Subtract: S(1 - x) = 1 + 2x + 2x^2 + \cdots + 2x^{n-1} - (2n-1)x^n = 1 + 2 \cdot \dfrac{x(1 - x^{n-1})}{1 - x} - (2n-1)x^n (GP from the middle).

So S = \dfrac{1}{1-x} + \dfrac{2x(1 - x^{n-1})}{(1-x)^2} - \dfrac{(2n-1)x^n}{1-x} (for x \ne 1).

P3. Find \sum_{k=1}^{20} (3k - 2)^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Expand (3k - 2)^2 = 9k^2 - 12k + 4.
Full solution 9 \cdot \dfrac{20 \cdot 21 \cdot 41}{6} - 12 \cdot \dfrac{20 \cdot 21}{2} + 4 \cdot 20 = 9 \cdot 2870 - 12 \cdot 210 + 80 = 25830 - 2520 + 80 = 23390.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Find \sum_{k=1}^{n} \dfrac{1}{k(k+1)} in closed form.

Hint 1 — a nudge Partial fractions: \dfrac{1}{k(k+1)} = \dfrac{1}{k} - \dfrac{1}{k+1}. Telescoping sum.
Full solution

\sum_{k=1}^{n} \left(\dfrac{1}{k} - \dfrac{1}{k+1}\right) = 1 - \dfrac{1}{n+1} = \dfrac{n}{n+1}.

In particular, \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{1}{k(k+1)} = 1.

S2. Find S = 1 + \dfrac{2}{3} + \dfrac{3}{9} + \dfrac{4}{27} + \cdots to infinity.

Hint 1 — a nudge AGP with a = 1, d = 1, r = 1/3.
Full solution

S = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{k}{3^{k-1}}. Using \sum k x^{k-1} = 1/(1-x)^2 for |x|<1:

S = \dfrac{1}{(1 - 1/3)^2} = \dfrac{1}{(2/3)^2} = \dfrac{9}{4}.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). \sum_{k=1}^{10} k equals (a) 45 (b) 50 (c) 55 (d) 60.

Full solution \dfrac{10 \cdot 11}{2} = 55. Answer: (c).

2-mark (short answer). Find \sum_{k=1}^{n} (2k + 3).

Full solution 2 \sum k + 3n = 2 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} + 3n = n^2 + n + 3n = n^2 + 4n = n(n + 4).

3-mark (short answer II). Find the sum to n terms of the series whose k-th term is T_k = k(2k + 1).

Hint 1 — a nudge T_k = 2k^2 + k.
Full solution 2 \sum k^2 + \sum k = 2 \cdot \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6} + \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{3} + \dfrac{n(n+1)}{2} = \dfrac{n(n+1)}{6}\left[2(2n+1) + 3\right] = \dfrac{n(n+1)(4n + 5)}{6}.

5-mark (long answer). (i) Sum the AGP 1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} + 3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{4} + 4 \cdot \dfrac{1}{8} + \cdots to infinity. (ii) Sum the same series to n terms.

Hint 1 — a nudge T_k = k \cdot (1/2)^{k-1}. Use \sum k x^{k-1} = 1/(1-x)^2.
Full solution

(i) S_\infty = \dfrac{1}{(1 - 1/2)^2} = \dfrac{1}{1/4} = 4.

(ii) S_n = 1 + 2 \cdot \dfrac{1}{2} + 3 \cdot \dfrac{1}{4} + \cdots + n \cdot \dfrac{1}{2^{n-1}}.

\dfrac{1}{2} S_n = \dfrac{1}{2} + 2 \cdot \dfrac{1}{4} + \cdots + (n-1) \cdot \dfrac{1}{2^{n-1}} + n \cdot \dfrac{1}{2^n}.

Subtract: \dfrac{1}{2} S_n = 1 + \dfrac{1}{2} + \dfrac{1}{4} + \cdots + \dfrac{1}{2^{n-1}} - n \cdot \dfrac{1}{2^n} = \dfrac{1 - (1/2)^n}{1 - 1/2} - \dfrac{n}{2^n} = 2 - \dfrac{2}{2^n} - \dfrac{n}{2^n} = 2 - \dfrac{n + 2}{2^n}.

So S_n = 4 - \dfrac{n + 2}{2^{n-1}}.

Check: as n \to \infty, S_n \to 4. ✓

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Three closed forms — \sum k = n(n+1)/2, \sum k^2 = n(n+1)(2n+1)/6, \sum k^3 = [n(n+1)/2]^2 — and almost every polynomial \sum is a one-line linear combination of them. Memorise the three; let linearity do the rest.
  2. For an arithmetic-geometric series (AP \times GP), multiply by the common ratio r and subtract. What was an AGP becomes a plain GP plus a single boundary term. There is no “AGP formula” worth memorising — there is only the trick.
  3. For \sum 1/[k(k+1)] and its cousins, partial-fractions makes the sum telescope — almost every term cancels and only the boundary survives. This is the cheapest trick in series and gives the cleanest infinite sums (here, exactly 1).

Common confusions

  1. “\sum k^2 = (\sum k)^2.” No — that’s only true for cubes. Squares give a different polynomial in n.
  2. “\sum k^4 equals \dfrac{n(n+1)(2n+1)(3n^2 + 3n - 1)}{30}? Memorise it.” You don’t need this for boards. But know it exists, and that the pattern continues to higher powers.
  3. “AGP is just AP × GP.” It is the term-by-term product. There’s no “AGP sum formula” you can plug into; always derive by multiply-and-subtract.
