Ch 3a — Trigonometric Functions (Radians, Unit Circle, Graphs)

Trigonometry escapes the right triangle and runs along the whole real line

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you begin.

  1. Can you state \sin 30^\circ, \cos 60^\circ, \tan 45^\circ from memory?
  2. Do you remember the right-triangle definitions: opposite over hypotenuse, adjacent over hypotenuse, etc.?
  3. Have you drawn a unit circle (radius 1, centre at origin)?
  4. Can you identify the four quadrants of the coordinate plane?

Three confident yeses and you’re ready. Otherwise, refresh Class 10 Trigonometric Identities or Unit Circle.

Touch

Take a piece of string of length exactly the radius of a small circle you can draw. Pin one end at the centre. Lay the string along the circle’s circumference, starting from the point where the circle meets the positive x-axis. Mark where the string ends.

The angle from the positive x-axis to that mark is one radian — the angle that an arc of length one radius subtends at the centre. There is nothing special about degrees; radians are nature’s choice because the arc length and the angle measure agree.

The widget below lets you drag a point around the unit circle. Watch how the angle (in radians and degrees) and the coordinates change together.

viewof theta = Inputs.range([0, 2 * Math.PI], {value: Math.PI / 4, step: 0.01, label: "Angle θ (radians)"})
{
  const x = Math.cos(theta).toFixed(3);
  const y = Math.sin(theta).toFixed(3);
  const deg = (theta * 180 / Math.PI).toFixed(1);
  const tan = Math.abs(Math.cos(theta)) > 0.001 ? (Math.sin(theta) / Math.cos(theta)).toFixed(3) : "undefined";
  return html`<div style="font-family:system-ui;font-size:1.05rem;line-height:1.7;">
    <div><b>Angle:</b> ${theta.toFixed(3)} rad = ${deg}°</div>
    <div><b>Point on unit circle:</b> (cos θ, sin θ) = (${x}, ${y})</div>
    <div><b>tan θ = sin θ / cos θ =</b> ${tan}</div>
    <div style="margin-top:0.5em;color:#666;">
      Notice: cos θ is the x-coordinate, sin θ is the y-coordinate. Always.
    </div>
  </div>`;
}

This is the unit-circle definition. It is the definition for the rest of this curriculum.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. If \sin\theta is the y-coordinate of a point on the unit circle, what is the largest value it can take? The smallest? Can it equal 2?
  2. \sin 0 = 0. Where else on [0, 2\pi] is \sin\theta = 0? Why exactly there?
  3. As \theta grows past 2\pi, the point comes back to where it started. What does this say about the function \sin?
  4. The right-triangle definition seemed to require a triangle with a right angle inside it. Where does the triangle go when \theta is, say, 200^\circ?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. reveals that sine and cosine are bounded between -1 and 1 — a fact you’ll lean on for the rest of trigonometry. (3) is periodicity;
  2. is the whole point of switching to the unit circle.

Discover

Problem 1 — Radians and degrees

Convert 60° to radians, and \dfrac{3\pi}{4} radians to degrees.

Hint 1 — a nudge 180° = \pi radians. So 1° = \pi/180 rad and 1 rad = 180/\pi deg.
Full solution

60° = 60 \times \dfrac{\pi}{180} = \dfrac{\pi}{3} radians.

\dfrac{3\pi}{4} rad = \dfrac{3\pi}{4} \times \dfrac{180}{\pi} = 135°.

Problem 2 — Sin and cos for special angles

State the values of \sin\theta and \cos\theta for \theta = 0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2.

Hint 1 — a nudge The “1, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, 2 over 2” pattern.
Full solution
\theta 0 \pi/6 \pi/4 \pi/3 \pi/2
\sin\theta 0 1/2 \sqrt{2}/2 \sqrt{3}/2 1
\cos\theta 1 \sqrt{3}/2 \sqrt{2}/2 1/2 0
Memorise this table by Friday. It will save you minutes on every test for the next three years.

Problem 3 — Signs in each quadrant

In which quadrants is \sin\theta positive? In which is \cos\theta negative? In which is \tan\theta negative?

Hint 1 — a nudge \sin is the y-coordinate, \cos is the x-coordinate. Look at the signs of x and y in each quadrant.
Full solution
Quadrant \sin\theta \cos\theta \tan\theta
I + + +
II + − −
III − − +
IV − + −
Mnemonic: All, Silver, Tea, Cups (or “All Students Take Calculus”) — quadrants I, II, III, IV: All positive, Sin only, Tan only, Cos only.

Problem 4 — Out of range angles

What is \sin\left(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\right)?

Hint 1 — a nudge \dfrac{7\pi}{6} = \pi + \dfrac{\pi}{6}. The angle is in quadrant III.
Full solution Reference angle \dfrac{\pi}{6}, in quadrant III where sine is negative. So \sin\left(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\right) = -\dfrac{1}{2}.

Problem 5 — Periodicity

Compute \sin(2\pi + \pi/3) and \cos(4\pi - \pi/4).

Hint 1 — a nudge Both \sin and \cos have period 2\pi.
Full solution

\sin(2\pi + \pi/3) = \sin(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}/2.

\cos(4\pi - \pi/4) = \cos(-\pi/4) = \cos(\pi/4) = \sqrt{2}/2 (cosine is even).

Problem 6 — Pythagorean identity

Verify \sin^2(\pi/3) + \cos^2(\pi/3) = 1.

Full solution

\sin(\pi/3) = \sqrt{3}/2, \cos(\pi/3) = 1/2.

\left(\sqrt{3}/2\right)^2 + (1/2)^2 = 3/4 + 1/4 = 1. ✓

This is a special case of \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1, which is just x^2 + y^2 = 1 for points on the unit circle.

Problem 7 — Domain of tangent

For which real \theta is \tan\theta undefined? Why?

Hint 1 — a nudge \tan\theta = \sin\theta / \cos\theta.
Full solution Undefined when \cos\theta = 0, i.e. \theta = \dfrac{\pi}{2} + n\pi for any integer n. At those angles \sin\theta = \pm 1 and division blows up.

Problem 8 — Arc length on a circle

A circle has radius r = 21 cm. A central angle of 60° cuts off an arc. How long is the arc?

Hint 1 — a nudge The formula \ell = r\theta requires \theta in radians, not degrees. Convert 60° first.
Hint 2 — the technique 60° = \pi/3 rad. Then \ell = r\theta = 21 \cdot \pi/3.
Full solution

\theta = 60° = \pi/3 radians.

\ell = r\theta = 21 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{3} = 7\pi \approx 22 cm (using \pi \approx 22/7).

This is the second reason radians are the natural unit: the formula \ell = r\theta is false if \theta is in degrees, and there is no fix that doesn’t reintroduce \pi/180.

You have just used radians, the unit-circle definitions, quadrant signs, reference angles, periodicity, the Pythagorean identity, the domain of tangent, and the arc-length formula. Now we name them.

The Indian astronomer Āryabhaṭa (5th century CE) tabulated what he called jyā — half the chord of a doubled angle — for 24 angles spaced by 3°45' across the first quadrant. Those jyā values are exactly R\sin\theta for circle of radius R = 3438 (chosen so one minute of arc equals one unit of length). Five centuries later Persian astronomers transliterated jyā as jiba; Latin translators in 12th-century Toledo misread jiba as the unrelated Arabic word jaib (“bay” or “bosom”), and translated it into Latin as sinus — the Latin word for “bay”. The English sine is a thousand-year-old clerical mistranslation of an Indian word for chord.

The unit circle is the definition. Sine and cosine are not “ratios in a right triangle” — they are the y- and x-coordinates of a point running around a circle of radius 1. That is why they live for all real \theta, why they are periodic with period 2\pi, and why \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 is just the equation of the circle.

Name

For any real number \theta, let P(\theta) = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta) be the point on the unit circle reached by rotating the point (1, 0) through angle \theta counter-clockwise.

The six trigonometric functions are \sin\theta,\ \cos\theta,\ \tan\theta = \frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}, \csc\theta = \frac{1}{\sin\theta},\ \sec\theta = \frac{1}{\cos\theta},\ \cot\theta = \frac{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}.

Pythagorean identities: \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1, \quad 1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta, \quad 1 + \cot^2\theta = \csc^2\theta.

Periodicity: \sin, \cos, \sec, \csc have period 2\pi; \tan, \cot have period \pi.

Symmetry: \sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta (odd), \cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta (even).

Conversion: 180° = \pi rad. Multiply by \pi/180 to go deg → rad.

Arc length: for a central angle \theta (in radians) on a circle of radius r, the arc cut off has length \ell = r\theta.

A short verse:

Round the unit circle the angle does ride; Cosine is across, sine is up the side. Square them and add — you get one every time; Two pi is the song, then the tune starts to chime.

Graphs to memorise:

  • y = \sin x: smooth wave, range [-1, 1], zeros at n\pi, peaks at \pi/2 + 2n\pi.
  • y = \cos x: same wave shifted left by \pi/2.
  • y = \tan x: vertical asymptotes at \pi/2 + n\pi, zeros at n\pi, passes through (0, 0) with slope 1.

Apply

Worked example

Find \sin\theta and \tan\theta if \cos\theta = -3/5 and \theta lies in quadrant II.

How a competent solver thinks:

  1. Use the Pythagorean identity to find \sin\theta: \sin^2\theta = 1 - 9/25 = 16/25, so \sin\theta = \pm 4/5.
  2. Quadrant II: \sin > 0, so \sin\theta = 4/5.
  3. \tan\theta = \sin\theta / \cos\theta = (4/5) / (-3/5) = -4/3.

Answers: \sin\theta = 4/5, \tan\theta = -4/3.

TipThe two-rule system for \sin(90° \pm \theta), \cos(180° \pm \theta), …

These allied-angle identities (a.k.a. reduction formulas) come out of two independent rules — apply them in sequence:

  1. Function changes when the reference is an odd multiple of \pi/2 (i.e. 90°, 270°): \sin \leftrightarrow \cos, \tan \leftrightarrow \cot, \sec \leftrightarrow \csc. Function stays the same when the reference is a multiple of \pi (180°, 360°).
  2. Sign is fixed by the quadrant of the resulting angle, using ASTC.

Example. \cos(90° + \theta). Reference 90° is an odd multiple of \pi/2, so the function flips: \cos \to \sin. The angle 90° + \theta lives in Q II, where cosine is negative. Combine: \cos(90° + \theta) = -\sin\theta.

The two rules are independent — never collapse them into one step.

Practice — Warm

W1. Convert 225° to radians.

Full solution 225 \times \pi/180 = 5\pi/4.

W2. State \cos(7\pi/6).

Full solution Quadrant III, reference \pi/6, cosine negative: -\sqrt{3}/2.

W3. Find the period of \sin(3x).

Hint 1 — a nudge \sin(kx) has period 2\pi/k.
Full solution Period = 2\pi/3.

Practice — Working

P1. If \tan\theta = 4/3 and \theta is in quadrant III, find \sin\theta and \cos\theta.

Hint 1 — a nudge Build a 3-4-5 triangle, then assign signs by quadrant.
Full solution \tan = 4/3 → opposite 4, adjacent 3, hypotenuse 5. In Q III both sin and cos are negative: \sin\theta = -4/5, \cos\theta = -3/5.

P2. Prove \sin^4 x - \cos^4 x = \sin^2 x - \cos^2 x.

Hint 1 — a nudge Difference of squares: a^2 - b^2 = (a-b)(a+b). Then use \sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1.
Full solution LHS $= (^2 x)^2 - (^2 x)^2 = (^2 x - ^2 x)(^2 x + ^2 x) = (^2 x - ^2 x)(1) = $ RHS.

P3. Find all the trig values for \theta = -5\pi/6.

Hint 1 — a nudge -5\pi/6 is the same point as -5\pi/6 + 2\pi = 7\pi/6.
Full solution \sin(-5\pi/6) = -1/2, \cos(-5\pi/6) = -\sqrt{3}/2, \tan(-5\pi/6) = 1/\sqrt{3}, and reciprocals follow.

P4. Convert 47°\,30' to radians (exact form).

Hint 1 — a nudge The apostrophe is minutes of arc — angle subdivision, not time. 1' = (1/60)°, so 30' = 0.5°.
Hint 2 — the technique Convert the whole angle to a single decimal (or improper fraction) of degrees first, then multiply by \pi/180.
Full solution

47°\,30' = 47.5° = \dfrac{95}{2}°.

Multiply by \pi/180: \dfrac{95}{2} \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{180} = \dfrac{95\pi}{360} = \dfrac{19\pi}{72} rad.

General method (radian → D-M-S): multiply by 180/\pi. If the result is fractional, multiply the fractional part by 60 to get minutes; multiply the new fraction by 60 to get seconds.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Show that \dfrac{1 - \cos\theta}{\sin\theta} = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{1 + \cos\theta}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Multiply numerator and denominator on one side by 1 + \cos\theta.
Full solution \dfrac{1 - \cos\theta}{\sin\theta} \cdot \dfrac{1+\cos\theta}{1+\cos\theta} = \dfrac{1 - \cos^2\theta}{\sin\theta (1+\cos\theta)} = \dfrac{\sin^2\theta}{\sin\theta(1+\cos\theta)} = \dfrac{\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta}.

S2. Find the maximum and minimum of f(\theta) = 3\sin\theta + 4\cos\theta.

Hint 1 — a nudge Write a\sin\theta + b\cos\theta = R\sin(\theta + \phi) where R = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}.
Full solution R = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5. So f = 5\sin(\theta + \phi) for some \phi, giving max = 5, min = -5.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark (objective). \sin(\pi - x) equals (a) -\sin x (b) \sin x (c) \cos x (d) -\cos x.

Full solution \pi - x has the same y-coordinate as x (reflection across y-axis). Answer: (b) \sin x.

2-mark (short answer). Find the value of \sin\dfrac{31\pi}{3}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Subtract multiples of 2\pi until the angle is in [0, 2\pi).
Full solution

\dfrac{31\pi}{3} = \dfrac{31\pi}{3} - 10\pi = \dfrac{31\pi - 30\pi}{3} = \dfrac{\pi}{3}.

So \sin\dfrac{31\pi}{3} = \sin\dfrac{\pi}{3} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}.

3-mark (short answer II). If \cos x = -\dfrac{12}{13} and x is in quadrant II, find the values of all six trigonometric functions.

Hint 1 — a nudge Pythagorean identity for \sin x, then divide.
Full solution

\sin^2 x = 1 - 144/169 = 25/169. Q II: \sin x > 0, so \sin x = 5/13.

\tan x = -5/12, \csc x = 13/5, \sec x = -13/12, \cot x = -12/5.

5-mark (long answer). (i) Prove \dfrac{\cos\theta}{1-\sin\theta} + \dfrac{\cos\theta}{1+\sin\theta} = 2\sec\theta. (ii) Hence find \dfrac{\cos(\pi/3)}{1-\sin(\pi/3)} + \dfrac{\cos(\pi/3)}{1+\sin(\pi/3)}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Combine the fractions over common denominator 1 - \sin^2\theta.
Full solution

(i) LHS = \cos\theta \cdot \dfrac{(1+\sin\theta) + (1-\sin\theta)}{(1-\sin\theta)(1+\sin\theta)} = \cos\theta \cdot \dfrac{2}{1-\sin^2\theta} = \dfrac{2\cos\theta}{\cos^2\theta} = \dfrac{2}{\cos\theta} = 2\sec\theta. ✓

(ii) Using the identity, the value is 2\sec(\pi/3) = 2 \times 2 = 4.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Each trigonometric function reads a coordinate of a point on the unit circle — \cos\theta is the x, \sin\theta is the y, the rest are ratios.
  2. The Pythagorean identities \sin^2\theta + \cos^2\theta = 1 are just x^2 + y^2 = 1 in disguise — every other identity descends from this one.
  3. Radians are not “smaller degrees” — they are arc-length on the unit circle, and calculus only behaves nicely for trig in radians.

Common confusions

  1. “Radians are just smaller degrees.” They are a different unit. A radian is the natural angle measure; calculus only behaves nicely for trig functions if you use radians. The arc-length formula \ell = r\theta is false in degrees.
  2. “\sin^{-1}\theta means 1/\sin\theta.” No. \sin^{-1} is the inverse function (we meet it in Class 12). The reciprocal of \sin is \csc.
  3. “\sin(A + B) = \sin A + \sin B.” Definitely not. The compound angle formula is the topic of Lesson 3b.
  4. “In allied-angle problems, just apply one rule.” No — the function-change rule and the quadrant-sign rule are independent. Decide the function first (does it flip?), then the sign (which quadrant is the resulting angle in?). Skip either step and the answer is wrong half the time.
  5. “\sin 765° has no nice value.” Reduce mod 360° first: 765° = 2 \cdot 360° + 45°, so \sin 765° = \sin 45° = 1/\sqrt 2.

Where this matters

Game development. Every rotation in a 2D game uses (x, y) \mapsto (x\cos\theta - y\sin\theta,\ x\sin\theta + y\cos\theta). The unit circle is the tool for spinning sprites, aiming projectiles, and orbiting cameras.

Research / Signal processing. A sinusoid A\sin(\omega t + \phi) is the building block of every periodic signal. Fourier showed that any reasonable function is a sum of sines and cosines — a fact that powers audio compression, MRI imaging, and wifi.
