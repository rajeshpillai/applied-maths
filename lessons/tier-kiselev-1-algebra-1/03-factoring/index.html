Ch 3 — Factoring Polynomials

Reading the three classical identities backward — and a strategy for every quadratic

Pre-flight check

  1. Are the three classical identities — square-of-sum, square-of-difference, difference-of-squares — automatic for you? See ka1.polynomials.
  2. Can you do polynomial long division reliably?
  3. Are you comfortable with a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)?
  4. Can you spot a common factor across two terms — e.g. 6 x y + 9 x = 3 x (2 y + 3)?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

The polynomial x^2 - 5 x + 6. Try to write it as a product of two simpler polynomials. The slider hints at the candidates by checking (x - p)(x - q) for various (p, q).

viewof p = Inputs.range([-6, 6], {value: 2, step: 1, label: "guess p"})
viewof q = Inputs.range([-6, 6], {value: 3, step: 1, label: "guess q"})
{
  const sum = p + q;
  const prod = p * q;
  const ok = (sum === 5 && prod === 6);
  return `**(x − ${p})(x − ${q})** = x² − (${p} + ${q})x + (${p} · ${q}) = **x² − ${sum} x + ${prod}**.

Target: **x² − 5x + 6**.

Match? ${ok ? "✓ — *factored*" : "✗ — keep guessing"}.

Pattern (Vieta): the two roots add to **5** and multiply to **6**.
`;
}

To factor x^2 - 5x + 6 as (x - p)(x - q), find p + q = 5 and p q = 6. The pair (2, 3) works.

Wonder

  1. Every quadratic x^2 + bx + c factors as (x - p)(x - q) where p + q = -b and p q = c. Why?
  2. The polynomial x^4 - 1 factors. How many factors does it have? (In real polynomials, two? Three? Four?)
  3. The expression a^2 + b^2 — does it factor over the reals? What about a^2 + b^2 over the complex numbers?
  4. Polynomial division gives a factor when the remainder is zero. Is there a faster way to test “is this polynomial divisible by x - r?” without doing the division?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Vieta’s formulas for the quadratic — sum and product of roots. (2) Over the reals: x^4 - 1 = (x - 1)(x + 1)(x^2 + 1), three irreducible factors. Over \mathbb C: four linear factors (x - 1)(x + 1)(x - i)(x + i). (3) No over the reals (a^2 + b^2 \ge 0 always); yes over \mathbb C: a^2 + b^2 = (a + bi)(a - bi). (4) The factor theorem. (x - r) is a factor of P(x) iff P(r) = 0.

Discover

Problem 1 — Common-factor extraction

Factor 6 x^2 y - 9 x y^2 + 3 x y.

Hint 1 — a nudge Find the greatest common factor of all three monomials.
Full solution GCF: 3 x y. Factor: 6 x^2 y - 9 x y^2 + 3 x y = 3 x y \cdot (2 x - 3 y + 1).

Problem 2 — Difference of squares

Factor 25 x^2 - 49.

Full solution (5 x)^2 - 7^2 = (5 x - 7)(5 x + 7).

Problem 3 — Square trinomial

Factor 4 x^2 - 12 x + 9.

Hint 1 — a nudge Recognise as a^2 - 2ab + b^2 with a = 2x and b = 3.
Full solution (2 x - 3)^2.

Problem 4 — General quadratic

Factor x^2 - 7 x + 12.

Hint 1 — a nudge Vieta: find two numbers summing to 7 and multiplying to 12.
Full solution 3 and 4 work: 3 + 4 = 7, 3 \cdot 4 = 12. So x^2 - 7 x + 12 = (x - 3)(x - 4).

Problem 5 — Factoring by grouping

Factor x^3 + 2 x^2 - x - 2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Group: (x^3 + 2 x^2) + (- x - 2) = x^2 (x + 2) - (x + 2).
Full solution x^2 (x + 2) - (x + 2) = (x + 2)(x^2 - 1) = (x + 2)(x - 1)(x + 1). (Triple factorisation by grouping + difference of squares.)

Problem 6 — The factor theorem

Show that (x - r) is a factor of polynomial P(x) if and only if P(r) = 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Polynomial division: P(x) = (x - r) Q(x) + R where R is a constant (since \deg(x - r) = 1). Substitute x = r.
Full solution

By long division, P(x) = (x - r) Q(x) + R for some polynomial Q and constant R (the remainder, of degree < 1).

Substitute x = r: P(r) = (r - r) Q(r) + R = R. So R = P(r).

Therefore (x - r) divides P(x) — i.e. R = 0 — iff P(r) = 0. QED. (Algebra I §53 — the factor theorem, also called Bezout’s little theorem.)

Problem 7 — Vieta for quadratics

Show that the quadratic x^2 + b x + c factors as (x - p)(x - q) where p, q are real roots iff p + q = -b and pq = c.

Hint 1 — a nudge Expand (x - p)(x - q) and compare coefficients.
Full solution

(x - p)(x - q) = x^2 - (p + q) x + pq. Equating to x^2 + b x + c: - coefficient of x: -(p + q) = b, i.e. p + q = -b. - constant: pq = c.

The same factorisation works iff p, q exist as real numbers — which requires the discriminant b^2 - 4c \ge 0. QED. (Algebra I §59 — Vieta’s formulas, the simplest case of the celebrated symmetric-polynomial-equals-elementary-symmetric duality.)

Diophantus of Alexandria (c. 200–284 CE) wrote the Arithmetica — thirteen books of polynomial problems, six of which survive — and introduced the technique of equating coefficients that powers Vieta’s formulas in Problem 7. Eleven centuries later, Pierre de Fermat scrawled his famous “marvellous proof, too narrow for this margin” in the margin of his copy of Diophantus’s Book II — sparking three hundred years of attempts at Fermat’s Last Theorem, finally closed by Andrew Wiles in 1995. That entire saga began with someone factoring polynomials at the edge of a page.

Factoring is the reverse of expansion — you read the three classical identities from right to left. Combined with the factor theorem (a polynomial P(x) has (x - r) as a factor iff P(r) = 0), every polynomial you can solve becomes one you can take apart.

Name

Factoring techniques.

  1. Common-factor extraction: a x + a y = a (x + y).
  2. Difference of squares: a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b).
  3. Square trinomials: a^2 \pm 2ab + b^2 = (a \pm b)^2.
  4. Difference and sum of cubes: a^3 \mp b^3 = (a \mp b)(a^2 \pm ab + b^2).
  5. Grouping: group terms in pairs, factor each, and notice a common bracket.
  6. General quadratic: x^2 + bx + c = (x - p)(x - q) where p + q = -b, pq = c (Vieta).
  7. Factor theorem: (x - r) \mid P(x) \iff P(r) = 0.

A verse:

Common factor first — pull the GCF out before all else. Difference of squares — a-plus-b times a-minus-b, no further help. Square trinomial — spot the a, the b, and the doubled cross. Vieta finds the roots: their sum is minus-b, their product, c — no loss.

Apply

Worked example

Factor x^3 - 2 x^2 - 5 x + 6 completely.

Try small integer roots (factor theorem). P(1) = 1 - 2 - 5 + 6 = 0. So (x - 1) is a factor.

Long division: x^3 - 2 x^2 - 5 x + 6 \div (x - 1) = x^2 - x - 6.

Factor x^2 - x - 6: by Vieta, find p + q = 1, pq = -6 — gives p = 3, q = -2. So x^2 - x - 6 = (x - 3)(x + 2).

x^3 - 2 x^2 - 5 x + 6 = (x - 1)(x - 3)(x + 2).

Practice — Warm

W1. Factor 9 x^2 - 16.

Full solution (3 x - 4)(3 x + 4).

W2. Factor x^2 + 8 x + 16.

Full solution (x + 4)^2.

W3. Factor x^2 + 5 x + 6.

Full solution (x + 2)(x + 3) — Vieta: 2 + 3 = 5, 2 \cdot 3 = 6.

Practice — Working

P1. Factor a^4 - b^4.

Full solution (a^2)^2 - (b^2)^2 = (a^2 - b^2)(a^2 + b^2) = (a - b)(a + b)(a^2 + b^2).

P2. Factor x^4 - 5 x^2 + 4.

Hint 1 — a nudge Treat as a quadratic in u = x^2: u^2 - 5u + 4.
Full solution u^2 - 5u + 4 = (u - 1)(u - 4). Substitute back: (x^2 - 1) (x^2 - 4) = (x - 1)(x + 1)(x - 2)(x + 2). Four real linear factors.

P3. Factor x^3 + 8.

Full solution Sum of cubes: x^3 + 2^3 = (x + 2)(x^2 - 2 x + 4).

Practice — Stretch

S1. Factor x^4 + x^2 + 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Add and subtract x^2: x^4 + 2 x^2 + 1 - x^2 = (x^2 + 1)^2 - x^2.
Full solution (x^2 + 1)^2 - x^2 = (x^2 + 1 - x)(x^2 + 1 + x) = (x^2 - x + 1) (x^2 + x + 1). (Cyclotomic factoring; appeared as K1 in Ch 2.)

S2. Show that a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3 abc = (a + b + c)(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 - ab - bc - ca).

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the cube-of-sum identity from Ch 2 K2 to relate a^3 + b^3 + c^3 and abc.
Full solution

RHS expansion: (a + b + c)(a^2 + b^2 + c^2 - ab - bc - ca). Distribute carefully: = a^3 + ab^2 + ac^2 - a^2 b - abc - a^2 c + a^2 b + b^3 + b c^2 - ab^2 - b^2 c - abc + a^2 c + b^2 c + c^3 - abc - bc^2 - ac^2.

Collect: a^3 + b^3 + c^3 are clearly there. Cross terms: ab^2 - ab^2 = 0, ac^2 - ac^2 = 0, etc. The abc terms: -abc - abc - abc = -3abc.

Result: a^3 + b^3 + c^3 - 3abc. ✓ QED. (A famous symmetric- polynomial identity; vanishes when a + b + c = 0.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s terse style, adapted from Algebra I, §§49–62.

K1. Without computing, decide which is larger: 2024 \cdot 2026 or 2025^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge 2024 \cdot 2026 = (2025 - 1)(2025 + 1) = 2025^2 - 1.
Full solution 2024 \cdot 2026 = 2025^2 - 1 < 2025^2. 2025^2 is larger by exactly 1. (A classic “algebra-as-fast-arithmetic” problem.)

K2. Show that for every integer n \ge 1, the number n^4 + 4 factors over the integers as n^4 + 4 = (n^2 - 2n + 2)(n^2 + 2n + 2). Conclude that n^4 + 4 is prime only for n = 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Add and subtract 4 n^2: n^4 + 4 = n^4 + 4 n^2 + 4 - 4 n^2 = (n^2 + 2)^2 - (2n)^2 — difference of squares.
Full solution

n^4 + 4 = (n^2 + 2)^2 - (2n)^2 = (n^2 - 2n + 2)(n^2 + 2n + 2).

For n = 1: factors are 1 and 5, so n^4 + 4 = 5 is prime (only the trivial factorisation 1 \cdot 5). For n \ge 2: both factors \ge 2, so n^4 + 4 is composite.

n^4 + 4 is prime iff n = 1. (Sophie Germain* identity; a celebrated number-theoretic factorisation.)*

K3. Factor a^5 - 1 completely over the integers.

Hint 1 — a nudge a^5 - 1 = (a - 1)(a^4 + a^3 + a^2 + a + 1) — geometric-series factoring.
Full solution

a^5 - 1 = (a - 1)(a^4 + a^3 + a^2 + a + 1).

The second factor is irreducible over the integers (it’s the 5th cyclotomic polynomial; this is a classical result usually proved in undergraduate algebra). Over the reals it factors further, but only with irrational coefficients.

Final integer factorisation: (a - 1)(a^4 + a^3 + a^2 + a + 1). (Algebra I §60.)

Source pointer. Adapted from Kiselev, Алгебра, Ч.1 (Fizmatlit reprint 2006), §§49–62. Russian original: archive.org scan. The factor theorem (Problem 6) is §53; Vieta (Problem 7) is §59. K2 (the Sophie Germain factorisation) is a Kiselev favourite from the §60 exercises.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Factoring is the backward reading of the three classical identities — expansion in reverse.
  2. Vieta (Problem 7) reduces factoring a quadratic x^2 + bx + c to finding two numbers with sum -b and product c — the only quadratic technique you need by sight.
  3. The factor theorem lets you test candidate roots in seconds: if P(r) = 0, then (x - r) is a factor — divide it out and continue on the smaller polynomial.

Common confusions

  1. “a^2 + b^2 factors.” Over the reals, no — a^2 + b^2 is irreducible. Over \mathbb C, yes: (a + bi)(a - bi). The distinction matters for engineering (signal processing relies on complex factorisations).
  2. “Vieta works for any polynomial.” Vieta extends to higher degrees (sum of roots = -b/a, sum of pairs = c/a, product of roots = \pm constant / a), but for school it’s usually stated only for the quadratic.
  3. “Factor theorem says: if P(r) = 0 then r is the root.” It says more: (x - r) is a factor of P — which is the key to iterative factoring (find one root, divide out, repeat on the smaller polynomial).

Where this matters

Encryption. Polynomial factorisation over finite fields is a hard problem — and many cryptographic primitives (e.g. AES, BCH codes) are built on the difficulty of factoring polynomials in \mathbb F_p[x]. Factoring over \mathbb Z and \mathbb Q (this lesson) is easy; over \mathbb F_p it’s the wall that secures encryption.

Research. Galois theory asks: when can the roots of a polynomial be expressed in radicals? The answer involves the solvable group underlying the polynomial’s factorisation — and the chain of factorings in this lesson is the seed of the modern algebraic structure.
