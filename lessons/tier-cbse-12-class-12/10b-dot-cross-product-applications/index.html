Ch 10b — Dot Product, Cross Product, and Applications

Two products that turn geometry into arithmetic

Pre-flight check

  1. Comfortable computing magnitude and unit vectors?
  2. Can you read components a_1, a_2, a_3 off a vector?
  3. Have you done Ch 10a?

Touch

Two vectors \vec a and \vec b. There are two natural products:

Dot product (\vec a \cdot \vec b) — gives a number. Measures how aligned \vec a and \vec b are: \vec a \cdot \vec b = |\vec a||\vec b|\cos\theta = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3. Zero when perpendicular. Maximum (positive) when parallel.

Cross product (\vec a \times \vec b) — gives a vector. Measures how non-aligned they are, and points perpendicular to both: \vec a \times \vec b = \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix}, |\vec a \times \vec b| = |\vec a||\vec b|\sin\theta = \text{area of parallelogram}.

The dot product picks out alignment. The cross product picks out twist (and the perpendicular axis).

This lesson: definitions, computations, and the two big applications — angle between two vectors and area of a parallelogram / triangle.

Wonder

  1. Why does \vec a \cdot \vec a = |\vec a|^2?
  2. If \vec a \times \vec b = 0, what does that say about \vec a, \vec b?
  3. The cross product is not commutative — \vec b \times \vec a = -\vec a \times \vec b. Why?

Discover

Problem 1

Compute \vec a \cdot \vec b for \vec a = 2\hat\imath + \hat\jmath - \hat k, \vec b = \hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath + 3\hat k.

Full solution 2\cdot1 + 1\cdot2 + (-1)\cdot3 = 1.

Problem 2

Find the angle between the two vectors in Problem 1.

Full solution |\vec a| = \sqrt{6}, |\vec b| = \sqrt{14}. \cos\theta = 1/(\sqrt 6 \cdot \sqrt{14}) = 1/\sqrt{84}. \theta = \cos^{-1}(1/\sqrt{84}).

Problem 3

Compute \vec a \times \vec b for the same vectors.

Full solution \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{vmatrix} = \hat\imath(3 + 2) - \hat\jmath(6 + 1) + \hat k(4 - 1) = 5\hat\imath - 7\hat\jmath + 3\hat k.

Problem 4

Verify |\vec a \times \vec b|^2 + (\vec a \cdot \vec b)^2 = |\vec a|^2 |\vec b|^2 for the vectors above.

Full solution |\vec a \times \vec b|^2 = 25 + 49 + 9 = 83. (\vec a \cdot \vec b)^2 = 1. Sum: 84. |\vec a|^2 |\vec b|^2 = 6 \cdot 14 = 84. ✓ This is the Lagrange identity: \sin^2 + \cos^2 = 1 in disguise.

Problem 5

Find the area of the parallelogram with adjacent sides \vec a, \vec b above.

Full solution |\vec a \times \vec b| = \sqrt{83}. Area = \sqrt{83}.

Problem 6 — perpendicularity test

For what value of \lambda are \vec a = 2\hat\imath + \lambda \hat\jmath - 3\hat k and \vec b = 3\hat\imath + \hat\jmath + 2\hat k perpendicular?

Hint 1 — the technique Two non-zero vectors are perpendicular exactly when their dot product is zero. Set \vec a \cdot \vec b = 0 and solve.
Full solution \vec a \cdot \vec b = 6 + \lambda - 6 = \lambda. So \lambda = 0. (When \lambda = 0, \vec a = (2, 0, -3) which lies in the xz-plane while \vec b leans into the y-direction; they meet at a right angle.)

Problem 7 — volume from three vectors

Three edges of a parallelepiped meet at a corner: \vec a = \hat\imath, \vec b = \hat\jmath, \vec c = \hat k. Compute \vec a \cdot (\vec b \times \vec c) and interpret the answer geometrically. Then redo with \vec a = \hat\imath, \vec b = \hat\imath + \hat\jmath, \vec c = \hat\imath + \hat\jmath + \hat k.

Hint 1 — a nudge \vec b \times \vec c is a vector. Then dot it with \vec a to get a scalar. The unit-cube case should give a clue what that scalar measures.
Full solution Unit cube. \hat\jmath \times \hat k = \hat\imath, so \hat\imath \cdot \hat\imath = 1. The unit cube has volume 1 — the scalar matches. Slanted box. \vec b \times \vec c = (\hat\imath + \hat\jmath) \times (\hat\imath + \hat\jmath + \hat k) = \hat\imath \times \hat k + \hat\jmath \times \hat\imath + \hat\jmath \times \hat k = -\hat\jmath - \hat k + \hat\imath = (1, -1, -1). \vec a \cdot (\vec b \times \vec c) = 1 \cdot 1 = 1. Same volume — even though the box is sheared, no edge has stretched in its own height direction. Geometric meaning. \vec a \cdot (\vec b \times \vec c) is the signed volume of the parallelepiped spanned by \vec a, \vec b, \vec c. It is called the scalar triple product, written [\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c].

Hamilton scratches the quaternion rule onto Brougham Bridge (1843). For ten years William Rowan Hamilton tried to extend complex numbers a + bi to triples for 3D rotation — and failed, because triples have no consistent multiplication. On 16 October 1843, walking along the Royal Canal in Dublin with his wife, the answer struck him: drop the expectation of commutativity and use four components, not three. He carved \mathbf{i}^2 = \mathbf{j}^2 = \mathbf{k}^2 = \mathbf{ijk} = -1 into the stone of Brougham Bridge with his pocket-knife. From those relations Gibbs and Heaviside later extracted \hat\imath \cdot \hat\imath = 1, \hat\imath \times \hat\jmath = \hat k — the multiplication rules you used in Problem 3. Modern game engines use quaternions for rotation; you’ve been computing with their fragments all lesson.

Two products, two questions. The dot answers “how much do these two arrows agree?” — a single number, positive when aligned, zero when perpendicular. The cross answers “how much area do they sweep out, and on which side?” — a vector whose length is parallelogram area and whose direction is the third axis. Every Class 12 application is one or the other: angle, projection, work — dot. Area, normal, torque, moment — cross.

Name

Dot product (scalar product). \vec a \cdot \vec b = |\vec a||\vec b|\cos\theta = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3.

  • Commutative: \vec a \cdot \vec b = \vec b \cdot \vec a.
  • Distributive: \vec a \cdot (\vec b + \vec c) = \vec a \cdot \vec b + \vec a \cdot \vec c.
  • \vec a \cdot \vec a = |\vec a|^2.
  • \vec a \perp \vec b \iff \vec a \cdot \vec b = 0 (for non-zero vectors).

Cross product (vector product). \vec a \times \vec b = \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix}. |\vec a \times \vec b| = |\vec a||\vec b|\sin\theta.

  • Anticommutative: \vec a \times \vec b = -\vec b \times \vec a.
  • \vec a \times \vec a = \vec 0.
  • Direction follows the right-hand rule.

Applications.

  • Scalar projection of \vec a on \vec b: \dfrac{\vec a \cdot \vec b}{|\vec b|} — a signed length along \vec b’s direction.
  • Vector projection of \vec a on \vec b: \left(\dfrac{\vec a \cdot \vec b}{|\vec b|^2}\right)\vec b — the shadow itself, as a vector along \vec b.
  • Angle between vectors: \cos\theta = \dfrac{\vec a \cdot \vec b}{ |\vec a||\vec b|}.
  • Area of parallelogram with adjacent sides \vec a, \vec b: |\vec a \times \vec b|.
  • Area of triangle with sides \vec a, \vec b at one vertex: \frac{1}{2}|\vec a \times \vec b|.

Scalar triple product. [\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c] := \vec a \cdot (\vec b \times \vec c). Geometrically, |[\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c]| is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by \vec a, \vec b, \vec c. The sign is positive when (\vec a, \vec b, \vec c) form a right-handed triple, negative otherwise. Coplanarity test: three non-zero vectors are coplanar iff [\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c] = 0 — the parallelepiped is flat, so its volume vanishes.

Standard basis products (worth memorising).

Dot Cross
\hat\imath\cdot\hat\imath = \hat\jmath\cdot\hat\jmath = \hat k\cdot\hat k = 1 \hat\imath\times\hat\jmath = \hat k,\ \hat\jmath\times\hat k = \hat\imath,\ \hat k\times\hat\imath = \hat\jmath
\hat\imath\cdot\hat\jmath = \hat\jmath\cdot\hat k = \hat k\cdot\hat\imath = 0 \hat\imath\times\hat\imath = \hat\jmath\times\hat\jmath = \hat k\times\hat k = \vec 0

Cross-product mnemonic: cycle \hat\imath \to \hat\jmath \to \hat k \to \hat\imath. Going with the cycle gives the next basis vector; going against the cycle adds a minus sign.

A verse:

Dot — a number, alignment in disguise; Cross — a vector, twist and perpendicular rise. Zero dot — perpendicular; zero cross — parallel; |cross| is parallelogram area, no need to spell it.

Apply

Worked example

Find a unit vector perpendicular to both \vec a = 2\hat\imath + \hat\jmath + \hat k and \vec b = \hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath + \hat k.

A competent solver:

  1. Compute \vec a \times \vec b: \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = \hat\imath(1 - 2) - \hat\jmath(2 - 1) + \hat k(4 - 1) = -\hat\imath - \hat\jmath + 3\hat k.
  2. |\vec a \times \vec b| = \sqrt{1 + 1 + 9} = \sqrt{11}.
  3. Unit perpendicular: (-\hat\imath - \hat\jmath + 3\hat k)/\sqrt{11}.

Practice — Warm

W1. \vec a \cdot \vec b for \vec a = \hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath - \hat k, \vec b = 3\hat\imath - \hat\jmath + 2\hat k.

Full solution 3 - 2 - 2 = -1.

W2. Angle between \hat\imath and \hat\imath + \hat\jmath.

Full solution \cos\theta = 1/(1 \cdot \sqrt 2) = 1/\sqrt 2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/4.

W3. \vec a \times \vec b for \vec a = \hat\imath + \hat\jmath, \vec b = \hat\imath - \hat\jmath.

Full solution \begin{vmatrix} \hat\imath & \hat\jmath & \hat k \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = \hat k(-1 - 1) = -2\hat k.

Practice — Working

P1. Find both the scalar projection and the vector projection of \vec a = 2\hat\imath + 3\hat\jmath + 2\hat k on \vec b = \hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath + \hat k.

Hint 1 — a nudge Scalar projection is (\vec a \cdot \vec b)/|\vec b|. Vector projection puts that shadow back along \vec b — divide by |\vec b|^2 instead of |\vec b|, then multiply by \vec b.
Full solution \vec a \cdot \vec b = 2 + 6 + 2 = 10. |\vec b|^2 = 6, so |\vec b| = \sqrt 6. Scalar projection = 10/\sqrt 6. Vector projection = (10/6)\,\vec b = (5/3)(\hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath + \hat k).

P2. Area of triangle with vertices A(1, 1, 1), B(2, 3, 5), C(0, 1, 4).

Full solution \vec{AB} = (1, 2, 4), \vec{AC} = (-1, 0, 3). \vec{AB} \times \vec{AC} = (6, -7, 2). |.| = \sqrt{36 + 49 + 4} = \sqrt{89}. Area = \sqrt{89}/2.

P3. If \vec a + \vec b + \vec c = \vec 0 with |\vec a| = 3, |\vec b| = 5, |\vec c| = 7, find the angle between \vec a and \vec b.

Hint 1 — a nudge \vec c = -(\vec a + \vec b). Square magnitudes.
Full solution |\vec c|^2 = |\vec a|^2 + |\vec b|^2 + 2\vec a \cdot \vec b \Rightarrow 49 = 9 + 25 + 2\vec a \cdot \vec b \Rightarrow \vec a \cdot \vec b = 15/2. \cos\theta = (15/2)/(3 \cdot 5) = 1/2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/3.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Show that (\vec a - \vec b) \times (\vec a + \vec b) = 2(\vec a \times \vec b).

Hint 1 — a nudge Expand using distributivity. \vec a \times \vec a = 0.
Full solution (\vec a - \vec b)\times(\vec a + \vec b) = \vec a \times \vec a + \vec a \times \vec b - \vec b \times \vec a - \vec b \times \vec b = 0 + \vec a \times \vec b + \vec a \times \vec b - 0 = 2(\vec a \times \vec b). ✓

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark MCQ #1. \hat\imath \cdot (\hat\jmath \times \hat k) = (a) 0 (b) 1 (c) \hat\imath (d) \hat\jmath.

Full solution \hat\jmath \times \hat k = \hat\imath. \hat\imath \cdot \hat\imath = 1. (b).

1-mark MCQ #2. If \vec a \cdot \vec b = 0 and |\vec a||\vec b| \ne 0, then \theta = (a) 0 (b) \pi/4 (c) \pi/2 (d) \pi.

Full solution (c) \pi/2.

2-mark. Find \lambda if \vec a = 2\hat\imath + \lambda \hat\jmath + \hat k is perpendicular to \vec b = \hat\imath - 2\hat\jmath + 3\hat k.

Full solution \vec a \cdot \vec b = 2 - 2\lambda + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 5/2.

2-mark. Find a vector of magnitude 5 perpendicular to both \vec a = \hat\imath + \hat\jmath, \vec b = \hat\jmath + \hat k.

Full solution \vec a \times \vec b = (1, -1, 1), magnitude \sqrt 3. Required vector: 5/\sqrt 3 \cdot (1, -1, 1).

3-mark. Show that \vec a = \hat\imath + 2\hat\jmath, \vec b = -\hat\imath + 3\hat\jmath + \hat k, \vec c = 5\hat\jmath + \hat k are coplanar.

Hint 1 — a nudge Coplanarity test: [\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c] = 0.
Hint 2 — the technique The scalar triple product is the determinant of the 3\times3 matrix whose rows are the components of \vec a, \vec b, \vec c.
Full solution [\vec a\ \vec b\ \vec c] = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \\ -1 & 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 5 & 1 \end{vmatrix}. Expanding along row 1: 1(3 - 5) - 2(-1 - 0) + 0 = -2 + 2 = 0. ✓ Notice \vec c = \vec a + \vec b — that’s why the triple is zero; \vec c is already a combination of \vec a and \vec b, so it lies in their plane.

3-mark. Find the area of the parallelogram with diagonals \vec d_1 = 3\hat\imath + \hat\jmath - 2\hat k and \vec d_2 = \hat\imath - 3 \hat\jmath + 4\hat k.

Hint 1 — a nudge For diagonals: area = \frac{1}{2}|\vec d_1 \times \vec d_2|.
Full solution \vec d_1 \times \vec d_2 = \hat\imath(4 - 6) - \hat\jmath(12 + 2) + \hat k(-9 - 1) = (-2, -14, -10). |.| = \sqrt{4 + 196 + 100} = \sqrt{300} = 10\sqrt 3. Area = 5\sqrt 3.

5-mark long answer. If \vec a, \vec b, \vec c are unit vectors such that \vec a + \vec b + \vec c = \vec 0, find \vec a \cdot \vec b + \vec b \cdot \vec c + \vec c \cdot \vec a.

Hint 1 — a nudge Square both sides of \vec a + \vec b + \vec c = 0.
Full solution |\vec a + \vec b + \vec c|^2 = 0. = |\vec a|^2 + |\vec b|^2 + |\vec c|^2 + 2(\vec a \cdot \vec b + \vec b \cdot \vec c + \vec c \cdot \vec a) = 1 + 1 + 1 + 2 S = 0. S = -3/2.

6-mark Case Study. Forces on a robot arm.

A robot arm has end-effector at \vec r = (4, 0, 3) m, and a force \vec F = (0, 5, 0) N is applied. Torque is \vec\tau = \vec r \times \vec F.

  1. Compute \vec\tau and its magnitude. (3 marks)
  2. Find the angle between \vec r and \vec F. (2 marks)
  3. Compute the work done if \vec F acts along a displacement \vec d = (1, 1, 1). (1 mark)
Full solution (i) \vec\tau = (4, 0, 3) \times (0, 5, 0) = (0\cdot0 - 3\cdot5, 3 \cdot0 - 4\cdot0, 4\cdot5 - 0\cdot0) = (-15, 0, 20). |\vec\tau| = \sqrt{225 + 400} = 25 N·m. (ii) |\vec r| = 5, |\vec F| = 5. \vec r \cdot \vec F = 0. \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = 90°. (iii) W = \vec F \cdot \vec d = 5 J.

NCERT Exemplar pointer: Ex 10.3, 10.4, 10.5. Previous-year pointer: CBSE 2023 Set 1 Q28 (5-mark unit-vector identity); CBSE 2022 Q26.

Common confusions

  1. “\vec a \cdot \vec b = \vec a \times \vec b if vectors are parallel.” Different types — one is a scalar, the other a vector. They live in different spaces.
  2. “|\vec a \times \vec b| = area of triangle.” It’s the area of the parallelogram. Triangle is half of that.
  3. “Cross product is commutative.” Anticommutative: \vec a \times \vec b = -\vec b \times \vec a. The order matters; swapping flips the sign of every component.
  4. “\vec a \cdot \vec b = 0 means \vec a = \vec 0 or \vec b = \vec 0.” No. It also vanishes when \vec a \perp \vec b. Same trap with cross: \vec a \times \vec b = \vec 0 does not force either vector to be zero — they could simply be parallel.
  5. “Mixing \sin and \cos.” Dot uses cosine, cross uses sine. Both formulas have the same |\vec a||\vec b| in front, so the sin/cos is the only thing distinguishing them at a glance — get it wrong and the answer is silently wrong.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Dot is alignment; cross is twist. \vec a \cdot \vec b is the number |\vec a||\vec b|\cos\theta; |\vec a \times \vec b| is the number |\vec a||\vec b|\sin\theta — they are \cos and \sin of the same angle.
  2. Zero dot means perpendicular; zero cross means parallel. Most “find \lambda” board questions are one of these two conditions.
  3. The cross product’s magnitude is the parallelogram’s area. Halve it for the triangle. This is the most-asked |\vec a \times \vec b| application in board papers.
