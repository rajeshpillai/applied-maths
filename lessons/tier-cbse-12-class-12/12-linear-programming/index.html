Ch 12 — Linear Programming

Maximise (or minimise) a linear objective under linear constraints

Pre-flight check

  1. Can you graph 2x + 3y \le 12 as a half-plane?
  2. Comfortable finding intersection of two lines?
  3. Have you done Class 11 linear inequalities?

Touch

A potter makes mugs and bowls. A mug needs 1 hour of clay-prep and 2 hours of glazing; a bowl needs 2 hours of prep and 1 hour of glazing. She has at most 8 hours of clay-prep and 7 hours of glazing weekly. Profit: ₹40/mug, ₹30/bowl. How many of each should she make to maximise weekly profit?

Let x = mugs, y = bowls.

Constraints: \begin{align*} x + 2y &\le 8 \quad\text{(prep hours)}\\ 2x + y &\le 7 \quad\text{(glazing hours)}\\ x, y &\ge 0 \end{align*}

Objective: maximise Z = 40x + 30y.

The set of (x, y) satisfying all four inequalities is a polygon — the feasible region. The maximum of Z occurs at a corner (vertex) of this polygon.

You graph the polygon, list its vertices, evaluate Z at each, and pick the best.

That’s the entire chapter. The hardest part is the modelling — turning words into the constraint inequalities.

Wonder

  1. Why must the maximum lie at a vertex, not in the middle of the feasible region?
  2. What if the feasible region is unbounded — could Z be infinite?
  3. Could a problem have infinitely many optimal solutions?

Discover

Problem 1

Sketch the feasible region: x + y \le 4, x \ge 0, y \ge 0.

Full solution Triangle with vertices (0, 0), (4, 0), (0, 4).

Problem 2

For the region in Problem 1, list the vertices and evaluate Z = 3x + 4y at each. Where is the max?

Full solution Z(0,0) = 0, Z(4, 0) = 12, Z(0, 4) = 16. Max 16 at (0, 4).

Problem 3

Plot the region: x + 2y \le 8, 2x + y \le 7, x, y \ge 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Find intersection of x + 2y = 8 and 2x + y = 7.
Full solution Intersection: solve x + 2y = 8, 2x + y = 7. Multiply first by 2: 2x + 4y = 16. Subtract: 3y = 9 \Rightarrow y = 3, x = 2. Vertex (2, 3). Also (0, 0), (7/2, 0), (0, 4).

Problem 4

For the region in Problem 3, maximise Z = 40x + 30y.

Full solution Z(0, 0) = 0. Z(7/2, 0) = 140. Z(2, 3) = 80 + 90 = 170. Z(0, 4) = 120. Max 170 at (2, 3) — 2 mugs, 3 bowls.

Problem 5

Same region as Problem 3, but minimise Z = 5x + 10y.

Full solution Evaluate: Z(0, 0) = 0. Min 0 at (0, 0). (Trivial when origin is in feasible region and objective is non-negative.)

Kantorovich’s plywood factory (1939) — the birth of LP. A young Soviet mathematician, Leonid Kantorovich, was asked to schedule machines at a Leningrad plywood plant. He realised the problem was neither calculus nor combinatorics — it was a brand-new beast of linear constraints and a linear objective. He wrote up his method in a 1939 booklet that almost no one outside the USSR could read. Independently, in 1947 the American George Dantzig invented the simplex algorithm for US Air Force planning problems, and Tjalling Koopmans framed economic activity in the same language. Kantorovich and Koopmans shared the 1975 Nobel Prize in Economics; Dantzig never received one (the Nobel committee does not award posthumously or for software, depending on whom you ask). When you walk a feasible region vertex by vertex in Apply, you are running Dantzig’s simplex by hand.

The optimum of a linear function over a polygon always sits on a corner. A linear Z = ax + by has parallel level lines; sliding those lines across a feasible region bounded by linear inequalities, the last point of contact is a vertex (or a whole edge in the tie case). That single fact is why “sketch, list vertices, evaluate Z” is the entire CBSE method — and why the simplex algorithm only ever has to visit corners.

Name

A linear programming problem (LPP) maximises or minimises a linear objective function Z = c_1 x_1 + c_2 x_2 + \ldots + c_n x_n subject to linear constraints of the form a_{i1} x_1 + \ldots + a_{in} x_n \le b_i (or \ge or =), with x_i \ge 0.

The set of points satisfying all constraints is the feasible region. The optimum (if it exists, and the region is bounded) is attained at a vertex of the feasible region.

Graphical method (CBSE form):

  1. Translate the word problem into constraints.
  2. Sketch the feasible region.
  3. Find all vertices.
  4. Evaluate Z at each vertex.
  5. Pick the vertex with the best Z.

For unbounded feasible regions: check whether Z has a max/min. The max/min may not exist; if it does, an open half-line at the optimal direction must lie outside the feasible region.

A verse:

Linear targets, linear walls — that’s an LPP; Sketch the polygon, list its corners, evaluate, see. Maximum on a vertex — boundedness saves the day; Unbounded? Test if the objective can run away.

Apply

Worked example

A factory makes products A and B. A needs 2 units of resource X and 3 units of Y; B needs 4 of X and 1 of Y. The factory has 16 of X and 12 of Y. Profit ₹6/A, ₹5/B. Find the production plan maximising profit.

A competent solver:

  1. Variables $x = $ # of A, $y = $ # of B.
  2. Constraints: 2x + 4y \le 16, 3x + y \le 12, x, y \ge 0.
  3. Objective: max Z = 6x + 5y.
  4. Vertices: (0, 0), (4, 0), (4 - \text{compute}, ?). Solve 2x + 4y = 16 and 3x + y = 12. Multiply 2nd by 4: 12x + 4y = 48. Subtract: 10x = 32 \Rightarrow x = 16/5 = 3.2, y = 12 - 9.6 = 2.4. Vertex (3.2, 2.4). Also (0, 4) from 2x + 4y = 16 at x = 0.
  5. Vertices: (0, 0), (4, 0), (3.2, 2.4), (0, 4).
  6. Z: 0, 24, 19.2 + 12 = 31.2, 20. Max 31.2 at (3.2, 2.4).

(In practice, fractional production may need integer-LP, but the graphical method gives the exact LP optimum.)

Practice — Warm

W1. Sketch and list vertices: x + y \ge 2, x + y \le 5, x, y \ge 0.

Full solution A trapezoidal strip. Vertices: (2, 0), (5, 0), (0, 5), (0, 2).

W2. Find max of Z = x + y on \{(x, y): 2x + y \le 6, x + 3y \le 9, x, y \ge 0\}.

Full solution Vertices: (0, 0), (3, 0), (0, 3), intersection of two lines: 2x + y = 6, x + 3y = 9 \Rightarrow x = 9/5, y = 12/5. Z(9/5, 12/5) = 21/5 = 4.2. Also Z(3, 0) = 3, Z(0, 3) = 3. Max 4.2.

W3. A point in the unbounded region x + y \ge 5, x \ge 0, y \ge 0 — can Z = -x - y be unbounded below? Yes/no, why.

Full solution Z = -x - y moves to -\infty as x + y \to \infty. Unbounded below — no minimum.

Practice — Working

P1. Maximise Z = 5x + 3y subject to 3x + 5y \le 15, 5x + 2y \le 10, x, y \ge 0.

Full solution Vertices: $(0, 0), (2, 0), $ intersection: 3x + 5y = 15, 5x + 2y = 10. Multiply: 6x + 10y = 30, 25x + 10y = 50. Subtract: 19x = 20 \Rightarrow x = 20/19, y = 45/19. Vertex (20/19, 45/19). Also (0, 3). Z values: 0, 10, (100 + 135)/19 = 235/19 \approx 12.37, 9. Max 235/19.

P2. A small dietician’s mix uses food F1 (₹6/100 g, 4 g protein per 100 g) and F2 (₹10/100 g, 6 g protein per 100 g). She wants at least 8 g of protein at minimum cost.

Full solution $x = $ 100g units of F1, y of F2. Constraints: 4x + 6y \ge 8, x, y \ge 0. Objective: min Z = 6x + 10y. The line 4x + 6y = 8 passes through (2, 0), (0, 4/3). Region above. Vertices on boundary: (2, 0) and (0, 4/3). Z(2, 0) = 12, Z(0, 4/3) = 40/3 \approx 13.33. Min ₹12 at x = 2, y = 0 — 200 g of F1 only.

P3. A manufacturer makes shirts and trousers. Each shirt needs 2 m fabric and 3 hr labour; each trouser needs 3 m fabric and 2 hr labour. Available: 16 m fabric, 18 hr labour. Profit: ₹40/shirt, ₹50/trouser. Maximise profit.

Full solution $x = $ shirts, y = trousers. Constraints: 2x + 3y \le 16, 3x + 2y \le 18, x, y \ge 0. Objective: Z = 40x + 50y. Intersection of bounds: 2x + 3y = 16, 3x + 2y = 18. Multiply: 6x + 9y = 48, 6x + 4y = 36. Subtract: 5y = 12 \Rightarrow y = 12/5 = 2.4, x = (18 - 4.8)/3 = 4.4. Vertex (4.4, 2.4). Vertices: (0, 0), (6, 0), (4.4, 2.4), (0, 16/3 \approx 5.33). Z: 0, 240, 176 + 120 = 296, 266.67. Max ₹296 at 4.4 shirts, 2.4 trousers (round in practice).

Practice — Stretch

S1. A diet has two foods. Food I gives 200 cal, 20 mg vit C per unit; Food II gives 100 cal, 50 mg vit C. Minimum required: 800 cal, 180 mg vit C. Cost: ₹30/unit (I), ₹40/unit (II). Find min cost.

Full solution x, y \ge 0. 200x + 100y \ge 800, 20x + 50y \ge 180. Min Z = 30x + 40y. Boundary intersections: 200x + 100y = 800, 20x + 50y = 180. First: 2x + y = 8. Second: 2x + 5y = 18. Subtract: 4y = 10 \Rightarrow y = 5/2, x = 11/4. Vertex (11/4, 5/2). Other vertices on the boundary: (9, 0) from 2x + 5y = 18 at y = 0 — but need 200x \ge 800 \Rightarrow x \ge 4, which holds. And (0, 8) from 2x + y = 8 at x = 0 — need 50y \ge 180 \Rightarrow y \ge 3.6, holds. Z: Z(11/4, 5/2) = 82.5 + 100 = 182.5. Z(9, 0) = 270. Z(0, 8) = 320. Min ₹182.50 at (11/4, 5/2).

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark MCQ #1. Max of Z = 3x + 2y on the region with vertices (0, 0), (3, 0), (1, 2), (0, 2) is (a) 0 (b) 7 (c) 9 (d) 4.

Full solution Z(3, 0) = 9, Z(1, 2) = 7, Z(0, 2) = 4. (c) 9.

1-mark MCQ #2. Optimal solution of LP problem (a) is always at a vertex (b) is unique (c) lies on a constraint line (d) cannot be at the origin.

Full solution (a) is always at a vertex (when bounded; if multiple optimal points exist, an entire edge is optimal but vertices are still optimal).

2-mark. Sketch and find vertices of \{(x, y): x + y \le 4, x + y \ge 2, x, y \ge 0\}.

Full solution Trapezoid: (2, 0), (4, 0), (0, 4), (0, 2).

2-mark. Maximise Z = x + 2y on the region from previous part.

Full solution Z(2, 0) = 2, Z(4, 0) = 4, Z(0, 4) = 8, Z(0, 2) = 4. Max 8 at (0, 4).

3-mark. A medicine company makes pills A and B. A: 4 mg ingredient X, 6 mg ingredient Y per pill, profit ₹3. B: 6 mg X, 3 mg Y, profit ₹4. Available 60 mg X, 90 mg Y. Maximise profit.

Full solution x, y \ge 0. 4x + 6y \le 60, 6x + 3y \le 90. Max Z = 3x + 4y. Intersection: 4x + 6y = 60, 6x + 3y = 90. Multiply 2nd by 2: 12x + 6y = 180. Subtract: 8x = 120 \Rightarrow x = 15, y = 0. So (15, 0). Other vertices: (0, 0), (15, 0), (0, 10). Z: 0, 45, 40. Max ₹45 at (15, 0).

3-mark. Minimise Z = 4x + 5y on \{x + y \ge 6, 2x + y \ge 7, x, y \ge 0\}.

Full solution Intersection: x + y = 6, 2x + y = 7 \Rightarrow x = 1, y = 5. Other boundary points: (7/2, 0) from 2x + y = 7, satisfies x + y = 3.5 < 6 — not feasible. (6, 0) — feasible. (0, 7) feasible. Vertices of region: (1, 5), (6, 0), (0, 7). Z(1, 5) = 4 + 25 = 29, Z(6, 0) = 24, Z(0, 7) = 35. Min 24 at (6, 0).

5-mark long answer. A farmer wants to plant wheat and barley on his 50-hectare field. Wheat needs 2 days of labour per hectare, ₹100/ha fertilizer; barley needs 3 days/ha, ₹150/ha. He has at most 80 days of labour and ₹6000 budget. Profit: ₹600/ha wheat, ₹500/ha barley. Find the optimal allocation.

Full solution

$x = $ ha wheat, $y = $ ha barley. Constraints: - x + y \le 50 - 2x + 3y \le 80 - 100x + 150y \le 6000 \Rightarrow 2x + 3y \le 120 (looser than above, redundant) - x, y \ge 0.

Effective constraints: x + y \le 50, 2x + 3y \le 80. Intersection: x + y = 50, 2x + 3y = 80 \Rightarrow y = -20 — infeasible. So the constraints don’t both bind. Try x + y \le 50 and 2x + 3y \le 80: at (40, 0): 40 + 0 = 40 \le 50 ✓, 80 + 0 = 80 ✓. Vertex. At (0, 80/3 \approx 26.67): ok. Vertices in region: (0, 0), (40, 0), (0, 80/3). Z = 600x + 500y. Z(40, 0) = 24000, Z(0, 80/3) = 40000/3 \approx 13333. Max ₹24,000 at (40, 0) — all wheat.

6-mark Case Study. School transport optimisation.

A school needs to transport at least 400 students using two types of buses. Type A holds 40 students and costs ₹1500/trip; Type B holds 60 and costs ₹2000/trip. The school has at most 20 buses available, and no more than 8 buses can be Type A.

  1. Set up the LP problem. (2 marks)
  2. Sketch the feasible region and list vertices. (2 marks)
  3. Find the minimum-cost plan. (2 marks)
Full solution (i) $x = $ # type A, $y = $ # type B. 40x + 60y \ge 400 (i.e. 2x + 3y \ge 20), x + y \le 20, x \le 8, x, y \ge 0. Min Z = 1500x + 2000y. (ii) Vertices: from 2x + 3y = 20 at x = 0: y = 20/3 \approx 6.67. At x = 8: y = (20 - 16)/3 = 4/3 \approx 1.33. From x + y = 20: less likely binding here. Vertices: (0, 20/3), (8, 4/3), (8, 12) (where x + y = 20), (0, 20) — but check 40 \cdot 0 + 60 \cdot 20 = 1200 \ge 400 ✓. Region is bounded by 2x + 3y = 20 below, x = 8 right, x + y = 20 upper. Hard to draw without picture; key candidates: (0, 20/3) and (8, 4/3). (iii) Z(0, 20/3) = 40000/3 \approx 13333. Z(8, 4/3) = 12000 + 8000/3 \approx 14667. Min ₹13,333 at 0 type A, \approx 7 type B. (In integers: 0 A, 7 B carrying 420 students, cost ₹14,000.)

Common confusions

  1. “All vertices are equally optimal.” Only the vertex/vertices with the best Z. Tie can occur — then the entire edge is optimal.
  2. “The feasible region is always bounded.” It can be unbounded. Then max/min may not exist; verify by checking the direction of Z.
  3. “Forgetting the non-negativity constraints.” x, y \ge 0 is almost always implicit in CBSE problems.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Translate words into constraints, then optimise. Variables first; one inequality per resource; non-negativity always.
  2. Sketch, find vertices, evaluate. A bounded feasible region pins the optimum at one of finitely many corners — no calculus required.
  3. Unbounded regions need a sanity check. If Z improves indefinitely along a direction inside the region, the problem has no finite optimum.

Where this matters

AI/ML. Support vector machines solve a quadratic program, but its linear-relaxation cousin is an LP. Resource scheduling in clusters is modelled as integer LP.

Databases. Query optimisers express join-order selection as a combinatorial optimisation; LP relaxations give bounds.

Research. The simplex algorithm (Dantzig, 1947) is one of the most-used algorithms in the world; modern interior-point methods extend the same ideas to convex programs.
