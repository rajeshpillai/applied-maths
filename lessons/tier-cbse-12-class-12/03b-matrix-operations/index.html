Ch 3b — Matrix Operations and Transpose

Add, scalar-multiply, multiply, transpose — and watch what survives

Pre-flight check

  1. Have you done Ch 3a — Matrices Intro?
  2. Can you read a_{ij} off a matrix?
  3. Comfortable with summation \sum from Class 11?

Touch

You and a friend run two tea-stalls. Last week’s sales:

Stall A

Mon Tue Wed
Tea 30 25 28
Coffee 12 18 14

Stall B

Mon Tue Wed
Tea 22 30 26
Coffee 15 10 12

You merge the two stalls’ books for total weekly sales: \begin{pmatrix} 30 & 25 & 28 \\ 12 & 18 & 14 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 22 & 30 & 26 \\ 15 & 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 52 & 55 & 54 \\ 27 & 28 & 26 \end{pmatrix}.

That’s matrix addition. You add entry-by-entry.

Tea costs ₹15, Coffee ₹25. To get the daily revenue at Stall A, multiply each row by its price and sum down columns. The cleanest way to write that is matrix multiplication: \begin{pmatrix} 15 & 25 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 30 & 25 & 28 \\ 12 & 18 & 14 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 750 & 825 & 770 \end{pmatrix}.

Three numbers — Mon, Tue, Wed revenue. Multiplication is not entry-by-entry. It is row-meets-column, sum-the-products.

Now flip the daily-sales matrix on its diagonal: \begin{pmatrix} 30 & 25 & 28 \\ 12 & 18 & 14 \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} 30 & 12 \\ 25 & 18 \\ 28 & 14 \end{pmatrix}.

Rows became columns. That’s transpose.

These three operations — add, multiply, transpose — are the lesson.

Wonder

  1. Can you add a 2 \times 3 matrix to a 3 \times 2 matrix? Why or why not?
  2. When you multiply two matrices, why must the inner dimensions match?
  3. Try transposing the price row \begin{pmatrix} 15 & 25 \end{pmatrix} and multiplying differently — does the answer change?
  4. If you transpose, then transpose again, what do you get?

Discover

Problem 1

Compute \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix}.

Full solution \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 8 \\ 10 & 12 \end{pmatrix}. Entry-by-entry.

Problem 2

Compute 3 \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.

Full solution Multiply every entry by 3: \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \\ -3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.

Problem 3

Compute \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix}. Use row of left meets column of right.

Hint 1 — a nudge Entry (1,1): row 1 of left \cdot column 1 of right = 1\cdot 5 + 2\cdot 7.
Full solution (1,1) = 1\cdot5 + 2\cdot7 = 19. (1,2) = 1\cdot6 + 2\cdot8 = 22. (2,1) = 3\cdot5 + 4\cdot7 = 43. (2,2) = 3\cdot6 + 4\cdot8 = 50. \begin{pmatrix} 19 & 22 \\ 43 & 50 \end{pmatrix}.

Problem 4

Now compute \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 6 \\ 7 & 8 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}. Compare to Problem 3.

Full solution (1,1) = 5+18 = 23. (1,2) = 10+24 = 34. (2,1) = 7+24 = 31. (2,2) = 14+32 = 46. \begin{pmatrix} 23 & 34 \\ 31 & 46 \end{pmatrix}. Different from Problem 3. Matrix multiplication is not commutative: AB \ne BA in general.

Problem 5

Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}. Write A^T and check that (A^T)^T = A.

Full solution A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \\ 2 & 5 \\ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix}. Transpose again: rows of A^T become columns; we get back A. ✓

Problem 6

Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. Compute (AB)^T and B^T A^T.

Full solution AB = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 4 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. (AB)^T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. B^T = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}. B^T A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. They match. That is, (AB)^T = B^T A^T — note the swap.

Problem 7

Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} and B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. Compute AB.

Hint 1 — a nudge Row-meets-column. Most entries will involve a zero somewhere.
Full solution (1,1) = 1\cdot 0 + 0\cdot 0 = 0. (1,2) = 1\cdot 0 + 0\cdot 1 = 0. (2,1) = 0\cdot 0 + 0\cdot 0 = 0. (2,2) = 0\cdot 0 + 0\cdot 1 = 0. AB = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = O. But neither A nor B is the zero matrix! In ordinary algebra, xy = 0 forces x = 0 or y = 0. Matrix algebra has no such cancellation. AB = O does not imply that A = O or B = O. This is one of the strangest features of matrix multiplication — and the source of more than a few exam traps.

When Arthur Cayley wrote A Memoir on the Theory of Matrices in 1858, the first rule he had to insist on was the strange one you just discovered in Problem 4: AB \ne BA in general. Until then, every “multiplication” mathematicians had ever defined — for numbers, for polynomials, for complex numbers — was commutative. Cayley showed that the natural way to compose two linear transformations gives a multiplication that depends on order, and he made this the foundation of a new algebra. The non-commutativity that breaks your high-school instincts here is the defining feature of matrix algebra — and it is why “first rotate, then translate” gives a different result from “first translate, then rotate” in every game engine on Earth.

Addition and scalar multiplication are entry-by-entry — the matrix is just a container, and the operation runs in parallel across all slots. Multiplication is something else entirely: it is composition of linear transformations, encoded as “row of left meets column of right, sum the products.” That is why AB needs the inner dimensions to agree (the output of one transformation feeds the input of the other), and why swapping the order changes the answer. The transpose identity (AB)^T = B^T A^T is the algebraic shadow of “to undo a stack of socks-then-shoes you remove shoes-then-socks.”

Name

For matrices A, B of compatible orders and scalar k:

  • Addition: (A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}. Requires same order.
  • Scalar multiplication: (kA)_{ij} = k\cdot a_{ij}.
  • Matrix multiplication: if A is m \times p and B is p \times n, then AB is m \times n with (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{p} a_{ik}\, b_{kj}.
  • Transpose: A^T has (A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}.

A matrix is symmetric if A^T = A, skew-symmetric if A^T = -A.

Algebra: - (A+B)^T = A^T + B^T - (kA)^T = k\,A^T - (AB)^T = B^T A^T - (A^T)^T = A

Matrix multiplication is associative and distributive but not commutative.

A verse:

Add by entry, scale by entry — straight; Multiply: row meets column, accumulate. Transpose flips the indices in pair; (AB)^T swaps to B^T A^T — beware.

Apply

Worked example

Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}. Find A^2 - 3A + 2I.

A competent solver thinks:

  1. A^2 = AA = \begin{pmatrix} 4+3 & 6-3 \\ 2-1 & 3+1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 3 \\ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}.
  2. 3A = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 9 \\ 3 & -3 \end{pmatrix}.
  3. 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.
  4. A^2 - 3A + 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 7-6+2 & 3-9+0 \\ 1-3+0 & 4+3+2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -2 & 9 \end{pmatrix}.

Practice — Warm

W1. Let A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. Compute 2A + A^T.

Full solution 2A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ -2 & 6 \end{pmatrix}, A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}. Sum = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 3 \\ 0 & 9 \end{pmatrix}.

W2. Compute \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.

Full solution Inner dims: 2 \times 2 times 2 \times 1 → 2 \times 1. Entry 1: 1\cdot2 + (-1)\cdot1 = 1. Entry 2: 2\cdot2 + 3\cdot1 = 7. \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 7 \end{pmatrix}.

W3. Show that A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} is symmetric, and B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 5 \\ -5 & 0 \end{pmatrix} is skew-symmetric.

Full solution A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \\ -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = A ✓. B^T = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -5 \\ 5 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = -B ✓.

Practice — Working

P1. If A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 7 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, find \lambda, \mu such that A^2 = \lambda A + \mu I.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute A^2 first. Match the four entries to \lambda A + \mu I.
Full solution A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 16 & 8 \\ 56 & 32 \end{pmatrix}. \lambda A + \mu I = \begin{pmatrix} 3\lambda + \mu & \lambda \\ 7\lambda & 5\lambda + \mu \end{pmatrix}. Match (1,2): \lambda = 8. Match (2,1): 7\cdot8 = 56 ✓. Match (1,1): 24 + \mu = 16 \Rightarrow \mu = -8. Check (2,2): 40 - 8 = 32 ✓. \lambda = 8, \mu = -8.

P2. Show every square matrix can be expressed as the sum of a symmetric and a skew-symmetric matrix.

Hint 1 — a nudge Try A = \frac{A + A^T}{2} + \frac{A - A^T}{2}.
Full solution Let P = \frac{A + A^T}{2}, Q = \frac{A - A^T}{2}. Then P^T = P (symmetric) and Q^T = -Q (skew). P + Q = A. ✓

P3. Verify (AB)^T = B^T A^T for A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.

Full solution AB = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, (AB)^T = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. B^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. B^T A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} ✓.

P4. Expand (A + B)^2 for 2 \times 2 matrices A and B, without assuming commutativity. Then evaluate it for A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} and B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, and check explicitly that A^2 + 2AB + B^2 gives a different answer.

Hint 1 — a nudge (A+B)^2 = (A+B)(A+B). Distribute carefully — you cannot reorder AB and BA.
Hint 2 — the technique Distribute on the right first: (A+B)(A+B) = A(A+B) + B(A+B) = A^2 + AB + BA + B^2. The middle two terms only collapse to 2AB if AB = BA.
Full solution

(A+B)^2 = A^2 + AB + BA + B^2 — four terms.

For the given matrices: A + B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, so (A+B)^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 4 \\ 4 & 5 \end{pmatrix}.

Now check the wrong identity. A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. B^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. AB = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}. So A^2 + 2AB + B^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 4 \\ 4 & 4 \end{pmatrix} — different from (A+B)^2. The discrepancy is BA - AB, exactly the failure of commutativity.

Moral: matrix algebra does not obey the school binomial expansion. Always expand (A+B)^2 as A^2 + AB + BA + B^2, and only collapse if you have proved AB = BA.

P5. Is the product \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 6 & 7 \end{pmatrix} defined? If yes, compute it. If no, explain why, and add or remove a single column from the right-hand matrix to make it defined.

Hint 1 — a nudge The left matrix is 1 \times 3. The right matrix is 2 \times 2. Inner dimensions: 3 vs 2.
Full solution Not defined. For AB we need \text{cols}(A) = \text{rows}(B), but here 3 \ne 2. To fix it, the right matrix needs 3 rows. (Adding a row to the right matrix changes its row-count from 2 to 3 — that is the minimal repair.) For example, with \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 5 \\ 6 & 7 \\ 8 & 9 \end{pmatrix}, the product is \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot4 + 2\cdot6 + 3\cdot8 & 1\cdot5 + 2\cdot7 + 3\cdot9 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 40 & 46 \end{pmatrix}, of order 1 \times 2.

Practice — Stretch

S1. If A = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}, show A^n = \begin{pmatrix} \cos n\theta & -\sin n\theta \\ \sin n\theta & \cos n\theta \end{pmatrix} for all positive integers n.

Hint 1 — a nudge Induction: check n = 1, then assume for n and prove for n+1.
Full solution Base n=1: trivial. Step: A^{n+1} = A^n \cdot A. Multiply using angle-addition: \cos(n\theta)\cos\theta - \sin(n\theta) \sin\theta = \cos((n+1)\theta), etc. By induction the formula holds. A is the 2D rotation matrix by \theta; A^n rotates by n\theta.

Board-Pattern Practice

1-mark MCQ #1. If A is 3\times2 and B is 2\times4, then AB has order (a) 3\times4 (b) 4\times3 (c) 2\times2 (d) undefined.

Full solution Inner dims match. (a) 3\times4.

1-mark MCQ #2. If A is symmetric, then A^T is (a) skew-symmetric (b) zero (c) symmetric (d) identity.

Full solution A^T = A, which is symmetric. (c).

2-mark. If A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, find A^2 and A^3.

Full solution A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = O. So A^3 = O. A is nilpotent.

2-mark. Find X if X + \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ -1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 1 & 7 \end{pmatrix}.

Full solution X = \begin{pmatrix} 5-2 & 0-3 \\ 1+1 & 7-4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -3 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}.

3-mark. If A and B are symmetric matrices of the same order, prove AB - BA is skew-symmetric.

Hint 1 — a nudge Take the transpose of AB - BA and use (XY)^T = Y^T X^T together with A^T = A, B^T = B.
Full solution (AB - BA)^T = (AB)^T - (BA)^T = B^T A^T - A^T B^T = BA - AB = -(AB - BA). So AB - BA is skew-symmetric. ✓

3-mark. Express A = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & -2 \\ 4 & 0 \end{pmatrix} as the sum of a symmetric and a skew-symmetric matrix.

Full solution P = \frac{1}{2}(A + A^T) = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} 12 & 2 \\ 2 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. Q = \frac{1}{2}(A - A^T) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -3 \\ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}. A = P + Q. ✓

5-mark long answer. Find a matrix A such that A \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 4 & -6 \\ 1 & -2 & 3 \\ 4 & -8 & 12 \end{pmatrix}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Right side has order 3 \times 3. Left has the row 1\times3. So A must be 3 \times 1.
Full solution A is 3 \times 1, say A = \begin{pmatrix} a \\ b \\ c \end{pmatrix}. Then A \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a & -2a & 3a \\ b & -2b & 3b \\ c & -2c & 3c \end{pmatrix}. Match: a = -2, b = 1, c = 4. So A = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}.

6-mark Case Study. A daily revenue model.

A factory produces three products P, Q, R. Quantities (units) made on two days are M = \begin{pmatrix} 50 & 40 & 60 \\ 60 & 55 & 70 \end{pmatrix}, where rows are days. Selling prices per unit (₹) for P, Q, R are 100, 150, 200 respectively.

  1. Write the price column matrix P. (1 mark)
  2. Compute M \cdot P and interpret the result. (3 marks)
  3. If a 10% sales tax is added on day 2 only, what is the post-tax revenue on day 2? (2 marks)
Full solution (i) P = \begin{pmatrix} 100 \\ 150 \\ 200 \end{pmatrix}. (ii) MP = \begin{pmatrix} 5000 + 6000 + 12000 \\ 6000 + 8250 + 14000 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 23000 \\ 28250 \end{pmatrix}. Day 1 revenue ₹23000, Day 2 revenue ₹28250. (iii) Day 2 post-tax: 28250 \times 1.10 = ₹31075.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Add and scalar-multiply matrices entry-by-entry; multiply them row-meets-column — addition needs identical orders, multiplication needs only the inner dimensions to match.
  2. Matrix multiplication is non-commutative — AB \ne BA in general, and many proofs and bugs in real systems hinge on remembering this single fact.
  3. Transpose flips (i,j) \leftrightarrow (j,i) and obeys the order-swap rule (AB)^T = B^T A^T; symmetric means A^T = A, skew-symmetric means A^T = -A, and every square matrix is the sum of one of each.

Common confusions

  1. “AB = BA for all matrices.” Almost never. Matrix multiplication is non-commutative; many proofs hinge on remembering this.
  2. “(AB)^T = A^T B^T.” Wrong. The correct identity is (AB)^T = B^T A^T — order swaps.
  3. “You can multiply any two matrices.” Only when the inner dimensions match: m\times p times p\times n. Otherwise the product is undefined.
  4. “AB = O implies A = O or B = O.” False. Matrix algebra has zero divisors: two non-zero matrices can multiply to the zero matrix (see Discover Problem 7). The same failure means “cancellation” breaks: AB = AC does not imply B = C, even when A \ne O.
  5. “(A + B)^2 = A^2 + 2AB + B^2.” Wrong unless AB = BA. The correct expansion is (A+B)^2 = A^2 + AB + BA + B^2. Likewise (A-B)(A+B) = A^2 + AB - BA - B^2, not A^2 - B^2.

Where this matters

AI/ML. A linear layer y = Wx + b is matrix-vector multiplication plus a vector. Backpropagation differentiates a chain of these. The non-commutativity is why “transformer” multiplications matter in order.

Game development. Composing two transforms (rotate then translate) multiplies their matrices in order — and reversing the order rotates about a different point. The non-commutativity is the design constraint.

Research. Symmetric matrices represent quadratic forms, covariances, Hessians; skew-symmetric matrices represent infinitesimal rotations and angular velocities. The decomposition A = P + Q is foundational across physics and statistics.
