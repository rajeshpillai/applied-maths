09 · Optimal Policies and Optimal Values

Define optimality; existence of an optimal policy and uniqueness of the optimal value.

Pre-flight check

Be honest. Are you ready for this lesson?

  1. From Lesson 5, can you compute v_\pi(s) for a fixed policy on a small MDP?
  2. From Lesson 8, can you write q_\pi(s, a) and use it to identify the greedy action?
  3. Can you compare two functions point-wise: “f \le g means f(x) \le g(x) for every x”?

If a “no” appears, briefly revisit lessons 5 and 8.

Touch

You have two policies in mind for the same grid world. Call them \pi_A and \pi_B. You can compute v_{\pi_A}(s) and v_{\pi_B}(s) for every cell — two side-by-side colour maps.

When is one policy better than the other? You might say “\pi_A is better than \pi_B if v_{\pi_A}(s) \ge v_{\pi_B}(s) for every state s.” That’s strict — both colour maps must be at least as bright on \pi_A’s side as on \pi_B’s.

But what if \pi_A’s map is brighter in the top-left corner and \pi_B’s is brighter in the bottom-right? Then neither is “better than” the other. Does an optimal policy even exist?

viewof scenario = Inputs.radio(
  ["A dominates", "Neither dominates", "Tied everywhere"],
  {value: "Neither dominates", label: "Compare two policies"})
{
  let A, B;
  if (scenario.startsWith("A")) { A = [3, 4, 5]; B = [2, 3, 4]; }
  else if (scenario.startsWith("Neither")) { A = [3, 4, 1]; B = [2, 3, 5]; }
  else { A = [3, 4, 5]; B = [3, 4, 5]; }
  return html`<pre style="font-size:0.95rem;">
state    v(πA)   v(πB)   πA ≥ πB at this state?
1        ${A[0]}      ${B[0]}      ${A[0] >= B[0] ? "yes" : "no"}
2        ${A[1]}      ${B[1]}      ${A[1] >= B[1] ? "yes" : "no"}
3        ${A[2]}      ${B[2]}      ${A[2] >= B[2] ? "yes" : "no"}

πA ≽ πB (everywhere)?  ${(A[0]>=B[0] && A[1]>=B[1] && A[2]>=B[2]) ? "YES" : "NO"}
</pre>`;
}

Caption: three states, two policies. The relation “\pi_A \succeq \pi_B” demands v_{\pi_A}(s) \ge v_{\pi_B}(s) at every state, not just on average.

Wonder

  1. Could two policies tie on every state’s value? Could one be tied at some states and strictly better at others?
  2. If two policies don’t dominate each other, which one would you call “optimal”?
  3. Bellman’s optimality theorem says an optimal \pi^* always exists for finite MDPs. Why is that not obvious?

Discover

Problem 1 — Define a partial order on policies

Define \pi^* \succeq \pi to mean v_{\pi^*}(s) \ge v_\pi(s) for every s \in S. Show this is reflexive (\pi \succeq \pi) and transitive (if \pi_1 \succeq \pi_2 and \pi_2 \succeq \pi_3 then \pi_1 \succeq \pi_3).

Full solution

Reflexive: v_\pi(s) \ge v_\pi(s) for every s, trivially.

Transitive: v_{\pi_1}(s) \ge v_{\pi_2}(s) \ge v_{\pi_3}(s) for every s chains to v_{\pi_1}(s) \ge v_{\pi_3}(s).

But \succeq is not total: there exist policies that don’t satisfy \succeq in either direction. (Problem 2.)

Problem 2 — Two policies that don’t compare

Construct a 2-state, 2-action MDP and two deterministic policies \pi_1, \pi_2 such that neither \pi_1 \succeq \pi_2 nor \pi_2 \succeq \pi_1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Make the actions specialise to different states.
Full solution

S = \{X, Y\}, A = \{a, b\}. Self-loops: P(X \mid X, \cdot) = 1, P(Y \mid Y, \cdot) = 1. (Each state is absorbing.) Rewards: R(X, a, X) = 1, R(X, b, X) = 0, R(Y, a, Y) = 0, R(Y, b, Y) = 1. \gamma = 0.9.

\pi_1(X) = a, \pi_1(Y) = a: v_{\pi_1}(X) = 10, v_{\pi_1}(Y) = 0. \pi_2(X) = b, \pi_2(Y) = b: v_{\pi_2}(X) = 0, v_{\pi_2}(Y) = 10.

\pi_1 wins at X, \pi_2 wins at Y. Neither dominates.

But the policy \pi_3(X) = a, \pi_3(Y) = b dominates both: v_{\pi_3}(X) = 10, v_{\pi_3}(Y) = 10.

Problem 3 — Bellman’s optimality theorem (statement)

State the following theorem. (You will use it in Problem 4 — proof comes in lessons 11–12.)

Theorem (Bellman). For a finite MDP with \gamma \in [0, 1), there exists at least one policy \pi^* such that \pi^* \succeq \pi for every policy \pi. Moreover, \pi^* can be chosen deterministic and stationary.

Why is this surprising?

Full solution

Surprising because the partial order \succeq is not total — most pairs of policies don’t compare. Yet the theorem says one policy beats everyone simultaneously: a global maximum in a partial order. That is not obvious.

The proof uses the contraction property of the Bellman optimality operator (lessons 11–12) and the existence of a fixed point. Bellman proved it in 1957.

Problem 4 — Define v^*

Let \pi^* be a policy from Problem 3. Define v^*(s) = v_{\pi^*}(s) = \max_\pi v_\pi(s). Show that the right-hand “\max” is well-defined: the maximum is achieved by the same \pi^* at every state.

Full solution

Suppose \pi^* \succeq \pi for all \pi. Then for each s: v_{\pi^*}(s) \ge v_\pi(s) for every \pi, so v_{\pi^*}(s) = \sup_\pi v_\pi(s) = \max_\pi v_\pi(s) (supremum is attained by \pi^*).

Equivalently, the per-state maxima are all attained by the same \pi^* — a non-trivial claim, since you might worry that no single policy attains the maximum everywhere. The theorem says: it does.

Problem 5 — Optimal policies are not unique

Construct a small MDP with two distinct optimal policies. Use it to argue that while v^* is unique, \pi^* may not be.

Hint 1 — a nudge Make two actions tie in q^* at some state.
Full solution

S = \{X\}, A = \{a, b\}. Both actions self-loop with reward 1. v_\pi(X) = 1/(1 - \gamma) = 10 for any \pi, including \pi(a) = 1, \pi(b) = 1, or \pi(a) = 0.5. Many optimal policies, one optimal value.

Generally, ties in the greedy step (\arg\max_a q^*) produce multiple optimal policies. The optimal value v^* is always unique (it solves the BOE — lesson 10).

v^* is unique but \pi^* is generally not. Different \pi^*’s arise from ties in the optimal action at some state. This matters when comparing trained agents — two agents with identical v^* may take quite different actions in the wild, all of them optimal. Practical RL almost always tiebreaks deterministically (smallest index, etc.) so reproducible behaviour is recovered.

Name

The optimal state value is v^*(s) = \max_\pi v_\pi(s) \quad \forall s \in S. A policy \pi^* is optimal if v_{\pi^*}(s) = v^*(s) for every s. For a finite MDP with \gamma < 1, an optimal deterministic stationary policy exists; v^* is unique but \pi^* may not be.

A verse to remember it by:

Wins at every state — the gold standard. Such a \pi^* exists, says Bellman. The value is one, the policy may split, but every \pi^* scores the same hit.

Apply

Worked example — enumerate policies on a 2-state MDP

Take the 2-state MDP from Discover P2 (separate self-loops, rewards arranged so a likes X, b likes Y). Four deterministic policies:

Policy \pi(X) \pi(Y) v(X) v(Y)
\pi_1 a a 10 0
\pi_2 a b 10 10
\pi_3 b a 0 0
\pi_4 b b 0 10

Step 1 — find v^*. Per-state maximum: v^*(X) = \max(10, 10, 0, 0) = 10. v^*(Y) = \max(0, 10, 0, 10) = 10. So v^*(X) = v^*(Y) = 10.

Step 2 — find \pi^*. A policy attains v^* at every state iff v_\pi(X) = 10 and v_\pi(Y) = 10. Looking at the table: only \pi_2 qualifies. \pi^* is unique here: \pi^*(X) = a, \pi^*(Y) = b.

Step 3 — verify Bellman’s theorem. \pi_2 \succeq \pi_1, \pi_3, \pi_4 (check each: \pi_2 ties or beats them at every state). The optimal policy exists and is found by inspection.

Practice — Warm

W1. In the worked example, is \pi_1 \succeq \pi_3? \pi_3 \succeq \pi_4?

Full solution

\pi_1 vs \pi_3: v_{\pi_1}(X) = 10 \ge 0 = v_{\pi_3}(X), and v_{\pi_1}(Y) = 0 = v_{\pi_3}(Y). So \pi_1 \succeq \pi_3.

\pi_3 vs \pi_4: v_{\pi_3}(X) = 0 = v_{\pi_4}(X), but v_{\pi_3}(Y) = 0 < 10 = v_{\pi_4}(Y). So \pi_4 \succeq \pi_3 (not \pi_3 \succeq \pi_4).

W2. For a 1-state MDP with all actions giving R = 5, \gamma = 0.9, how many optimal policies are there?

Full solution Infinitely many — every policy has v_\pi(X) = 50. The optimal value is unique (v^*(X) = 50), the optimal policy is wide open.

W3. Given v^* and the model (P, R, \gamma), write a formula for the greedy action at s.

Full solution

a^*(s) = \arg\max_a \sum_{s'} P(s' \mid s, a) [R(s, a, s') + \gamma v^*(s')].

This is the one-step look-ahead greedy rule. Lesson 10 will show it defines an optimal policy.

Practice — Working

P1. A 2-state MDP \{X, Y\} with two actions \{a, b\}. Transitions deterministic: a at any state stays put; b at any state swaps to the other. Rewards: R(X, \cdot, X) = 0, R(X, b, Y) = 0, R(Y, \cdot, Y) = 1, R(Y, b, X) = 0. \gamma = 0.9. Enumerate all 4 deterministic policies and find \pi^*, v^*.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute v_\pi for each of the four policies.
Full solution
  • \pi_1: a, a. Both states absorbing. v(X) = 0, v(Y) = 1/(1-0.9) = 10.
  • \pi_2: a, b. X absorbs, Y swaps to X. From Y the trajectory is Y \to X \to X \to \ldots, rewards 1, 0, 0, \ldots, v(Y) = 1. v(X) = 0.
  • \pi_3: b, a. X swaps to Y, Y absorbs. From X: X \to Y \to Y \to \ldots, rewards 0, 1, 1, \ldots, v(X) = \gamma/(1-\gamma) = 9. v(Y) = 10.
  • \pi_4: b, b. Both swap. From X: X \to Y \to X \to Y \ldots, rewards 0, 1, 0, 1, \ldots, v(X) = \gamma/(1 - \gamma^2) = 0.9/0.19 \approx 4.737. From Y: Y \to X \to Y \to X, rewards 0, 0, 1, 0, 1, \ldots — wait, R(Y, b, X) = 0, then X \to Y gives R(X, b, Y) = 0, then Y \to X gives R(Y, b, X) = 0. Rewards stay 0! Recompute. v(Y) = 0, v(X) = 0.
Per-state max: v^*(X) = 9 (achieved by \pi_3), v^*(Y) = 10 (achieved by \pi_1 and \pi_3). Need a single policy hitting both: \pi_3. So \pi^* = \pi_3, v^* = (9, 10)^T. Unique optimal here.

P2. Tied actions. Modify P1 so that the optimal \pi^* is not unique. Sketch how.

Full solution Add a third action c that behaves identically to b at X (P(Y \mid X, c) = 1, R(X, c, Y) = 0). Then \pi(X) = b and \pi(X) = c both yield identical futures. Two distinct optimal policies, same v^*.

P3. Show that if v_{\pi_1}(s_0) > v_{\pi_2}(s_0) for some specific s_0, then \pi_2 \not\succeq \pi_1. (The relation \succeq is sensitive to every state.)

Full solution

\pi_2 \succeq \pi_1 would require v_{\pi_2}(s) \ge v_{\pi_1}(s) for every s, including s_0. But v_{\pi_1}(s_0) > v_{\pi_2}(s_0) contradicts that. So \pi_2 \not\succeq \pi_1.

Note this does not say \pi_1 \succeq \pi_2 — neither may dominate.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Argue that “comparing average values” is not the same as the \succeq relation. Give a small MDP where one policy wins on average but loses on \succeq.

Hint 1 — a nudge Re-use the 2-state MDP from Discover P2.
Full solution

\pi_1 from Discover P2 has v(X) = 10, v(Y) = 0, mean 5. \pi_2 has v(X) = 0, v(Y) = 10, mean 5. Equal averages, but neither \succeq the other.

Conversely, if you weight states by some distribution \rho, you can order policies totally by \sum_s \rho(s) v_\pi(s) — this is the objective used in policy-gradient methods (lessons 33–37). But the BOE (lesson 10) targets the stronger \succeq ordering.

S2. Stationary deterministic suffices. Sketch why, in a finite MDP with \gamma < 1, no time-varying or stochastic policy can do strictly better than the best stationary deterministic one.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the BOE (lesson 10): the optimal q^* is well-defined, and at every state there’s an action achieving the max.
Full solution

The BOE (lesson 10) gives v^*(s) = \max_a [R + \gamma \mathbb{E}[v^*(s')]] — a deterministic maximisation. The \arg\max is a deterministic action; choosing it at every visit to s defines a deterministic stationary policy that achieves v^*. So no policy can do better than v^*, and a deterministic stationary one always achieves v^*. Time-varying or stochastic policies might tie but cannot beat.

This is why the entire RL literature defaults to “find the best deterministic policy” without apology: the maths says nothing else is needed.

The existence of an optimal deterministic policy for finite MDPs is nontrivial — Bellman’s 1957 Dynamic Programming established it as a theorem, not a definition. He proved the Bellman optimality equation has a fixed point and that the policy “act greedily with respect to the fixed point” is optimal. The argument is reproduced essentially verbatim in modern textbooks (Puterman, Sutton & Barto). The non-obvious step is that the same \pi^* wins at every state simultaneously — Bellman’s genuine theorem, not a triviality.

Common confusions

  1. “Optimal means highest average value.” No — optimal in the RL sense means v_{\pi^*}(s) \ge v_\pi(s) at every state. Average value is a weaker objective.
  2. “\pi^* is unique.” v^* is unique; \pi^* generally is not. Ties in the greedy step produce multiple optimal policies.
  3. “Stochastic policies might be optimal in some MDPs.” In finite MDPs with \gamma < 1, a deterministic stationary \pi^* always exists. Stochastic optimality arises in two-player or partially observable settings, not in vanilla MDPs.

Takeaways

  1. The partial order \pi^* \succeq \pi requires v_{\pi^*}(s) \ge v_\pi(s) at every state — strict Pareto-domination on the value function.
  2. The optimal state value v^*(s) = \max_\pi v_\pi(s) is unique. The optimal policy \pi^* achieving v^* exists (Bellman’s theorem) but may not be unique.
  3. For finite MDPs, \pi^* can always be chosen stationary and deterministic. Time-variation and randomness are not needed.

Where this matters

AI / ML. Every RL algorithm targets either v^* or q^* — they are the goalposts. AlphaZero approximates v^* for board games; DQN approximates q^* for Atari; PPO climbs toward \pi^* via gradients. The framework of “optimal value, optimal policy” is what makes “RL” a single subject rather than a grab bag of algorithms.

Research / control. Optimal control problems (LQR, HJB equation in continuous time) are the continuous-state analogue: an optimal policy exists, the optimal value is unique. The discrete and continuous worlds use the same vocabulary.
