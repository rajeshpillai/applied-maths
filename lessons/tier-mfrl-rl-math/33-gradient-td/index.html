33 · Gradient-TD: GTD, GTD2, and TDC

What semi-gradient TD would be if it stopped cheating. Honest gradient on MSPBE — at the cost of a second parameter and a slower clock.

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you read on.

  1. Can you write MSPBE in terms of \Pi_\mu, T_\pi, and \hat v_w from Lesson 32?
  2. Can you state the semi-gradient TD update and explain which gradient term it discards?
  3. Are you comfortable with one step of SGD on an expectation \mathbb{E}[L(w, X)] using a single sample X?
  4. Can you describe Baird’s counterexample from Lesson 30 in one sentence?

If any answer is no, revisit the named lesson before continuing.

Touch

L29’s key insight was a confession: the error is full; the gradient is half. Semi-gradient TD took the gradient of the prediction \hat v(s; w), but pretended the target r + \gamma\, \hat v(s'; w) did not depend on w.

Take out two coloured pens. Sketch on paper:

  • Red: the term semi-gradient TD already differentiates, \nabla_w \hat v(s; w) = \phi(s).
  • Blue: the term it threw away, \gamma\,\nabla_w \hat v(s'; w) = \gamma\,\phi(s').

What does the full gradient of \tfrac{1}{2}\delta^2 look like if you keep both pens?

\nabla_w \tfrac{1}{2}\delta^2 \;=\; \delta \cdot \bigl(\,\underbrace{-\phi(s)}_{\text{red}} \;+\; \underbrace{\gamma\,\phi(s')}_{\text{blue}}\,\bigr).

Adding blue back is what residual gradient did — L29 warned that it finds the wrong fixed point in mis-specified models and learns slowly. Adding blue is not the right fix.

The right fix is different. It says: stop trying to descend \tfrac12 \delta^2 at all. Descend MSPBE instead.

Semi-gradient TD descends the right objective — MSPBE — but by an unsound update (it ignores the target’s w-dependence). Residual gradient descends the wrong objective (\tfrac12 \delta^2) by a sound update. Gradient-TD descends the right objective by a sound update. The price: a second parameter and a slower clock.

Wonder

Sit with these.

  1. What does it mean to “take the gradient of MSPBE”? MSPBE involves the projection \Pi_\mu, which involves \mu and \Phi — but does it depend on w?
  2. The gradient of MSPBE will turn out to be a product of expectations. Why is that a problem when you only have one sample per step?
  3. If the bottleneck is a product of expectations, what could you keep around between time steps to break it apart?
  4. Two parameters w and u, two updates per step. What goes wrong if you update them at the same rate?

Discover

Six problems. The first three set up the obstruction; the next three break it.

Problem 1 — MSPBE in expectation form

For linear FA with \hat v_w = \Phi w, the MSPBE from L32 is

\mathrm{MSPBE}(w) \;=\; \|\Phi w - \Pi_\mu T_\pi \Phi w\|_\mu^2.

Use the matrix form of \Pi_\mu from L32’s Name beat to show that

\mathrm{MSPBE}(w) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi]^\top \;\mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top]^{-1}\; \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi],

where \delta = r + \gamma\,\phi(s')^\top w - \phi(s)^\top w is the TD error along a sample transition (s, r, s'), and expectations are over s \sim \mu, transition (s, r, s') \sim p(\cdot \mid s, \pi).

Hint 1 — a nudge The Bellman residual is T_\pi \Phi w - \Phi w. In matrix form on samples, that’s \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi] — a d-vector, the expected TD update direction. The projected residual squared in \|\cdot\|_\mu contracts this d-vector through the inverse Gram matrix \mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top]^{-1}.
Full solution

Let A = \Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top] and b_w = \Phi^\top D_\mu (T_\pi \Phi w - \Phi w) = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi] (the w-dependence is in \delta). Using \Pi_\mu = \Phi A^{-1} \Phi^\top D_\mu:

\Pi_\mu (T_\pi \Phi w - \Phi w) \;=\; \Phi A^{-1} b_w.

Take the \mu-weighted norm of \Pi_\mu(T_\pi \Phi w - \Phi w):

\|\Phi A^{-1} b_w\|_\mu^2 \;=\; (A^{-1} b_w)^\top \Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi (A^{-1} b_w) \;=\; b_w^\top A^{-1} A A^{-1} b_w \;=\; b_w^\top A^{-1} b_w.

So \mathrm{MSPBE}(w) = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi]^\top \mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top]^{-1} \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi] — a weighted-norm form of the expected TD update direction.

Problem 2 — the true gradient is a product

Take \nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE}(w). You may use the fact that \mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top] does not depend on w. Show that

\nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE}(w) \;=\; -2\,\mathbb{E}_\mu\bigl[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top\bigr]\;\mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top]^{-1}\;\mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi].

(Here \phi = \phi(s) and \phi' = \phi(s').) Why is this an obstacle to one-step SGD?

Hint 1 — a nudge \delta depends on w through \delta = r + \gamma\,\phi'^\top w - \phi^\top w, so \nabla_w \delta = -(\phi - \gamma\,\phi'). Apply the chain rule to b_w^\top A^{-1} b_w, treating A^{-1} as w-independent.
Hint 2 — the technique \nabla_w b_w = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\nabla_w \delta\,\phi^\top]^\top = -\mathbb{E}_\mu[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]^\top. Apply the matrix calculus identity \nabla_w (b_w^\top M b_w) = 2 (\nabla_w b_w) M b_w for symmetric M.
Full solution

By the chain rule:

\nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE}(w) \;=\; 2 (\nabla_w b_w)\, A^{-1}\, b_w \;=\; -2\,\mathbb{E}_\mu[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]\;A^{-1}\;\mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi].

(The sign comes from \nabla_w \delta = -(\phi - \gamma\,\phi').)

This gradient is a product of two expectations: \mathbb{E}_\mu[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top] on the left, and \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi] inside the A^{-1} on the right. With one sample, you’d need to estimate both expectations independently — the double-sampling problem. If you used the same sample for both, the products of stochastic terms would not be an unbiased estimate of the product of expectations; in general \mathbb{E}[XY] \ne \mathbb{E}[X]\,\mathbb{E}[Y].

So plain SGD on MSPBE is unimplementable without two independent samples of the same transition — which off-policy data simply doesn’t give you.

Problem 3 — break the product with a second parameter

Introduce a new vector u \in \mathbb{R}^d that tracks A^{-1} \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi] — the right-hand expectation. Suppose we update u to minimise

L(u; w) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_\mu\bigl[(\delta - \phi^\top u)^2\bigr].

Show that the minimiser u^\star of L (for fixed w) is exactly A^{-1} \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi].

Hint 1 — a nudge Take \nabla_u L = 0 and solve.
Full solution

\nabla_u L = -2 \mathbb{E}_\mu[(\delta - \phi^\top u)\,\phi] = 0 gives

\mathbb{E}_\mu[\phi \phi^\top]\, u^\star = \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi], \qquad u^\star = A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta\,\phi].

So u^\star is exactly the inner expectation we needed. The trick: keep u around between time steps, and update it slowly with SGD on L(u; w). Now the gradient of MSPBE factors as \nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE} \;\approx\; -2\,\mathbb{E}_\mu[(\phi - \gamma \phi')\,\phi^\top]\, u, which is estimable from one sample as -2 (\phi - \gamma \phi')\,(\phi^\top u).

Problem 4 — the GTD2 update

Combine Problems 2 and 3. Write down the per-sample updates for w and u. Use step sizes \alpha (for w) and \beta (for u).

Hint 1 — a nudge w moves opposite the gradient of MSPBE (factor in step size, drop the -2). u moves opposite the gradient of L(u; w) at the current sample.
Full solution

GTD2 per-sample update on (s, r, s'):

\begin{aligned} \delta \;&\leftarrow\; r + \gamma\,\phi'^\top w - \phi^\top w \\ w \;&\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,(\phi^\top u) \\ u \;&\leftarrow\; u + \beta\,(\delta - \phi^\top u)\,\phi \end{aligned}

The w-update has the same shape as semi-gradient TD — TD-error-like quantity times a feature vector — but the “TD-error-like quantity” is now \phi^\top u, not \delta, and the feature it multiplies is (\phi - \gamma\,\phi'), not \phi.

(GTD1 used a slightly different update; GTD2 is the cleaner variant introduced by Sutton, Maei et al. in their 2009 sequel paper.)

Problem 5 — TDC: same fixed point, less variance

The GTD2 w-update can be re-written using \delta explicitly. Show that the equivalent algebraic form

w \;\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,\delta\,\phi \;-\; \alpha\,\gamma\,\phi'\,(\phi^\top u)

has the same fixed point as GTD2 but typically much lower variance.

Hint 1 — a nudge At the fixed point, \mathbb{E}[(\delta - \phi^\top u)\phi] = 0, so \mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] = A u. Substitute that into GTD2’s w-update and see what you get.
Hint 2 — the technique GTD2 update: w \leftarrow w + \alpha (\phi - \gamma \phi') (\phi^\top u). Expand: \alpha (\phi^\top u)\,\phi - \alpha\,\gamma\,(\phi^\top u)\,\phi'. On expectation this is \alpha\,(A u) - \alpha\,\gamma\,\mathbb{E}[\phi' \phi^\top]\,u. At the fixed point, A u = \mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi], so the first term becomes \alpha\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] — the semi-gradient TD direction. The second term is a correction.
Full solution

TDC (“TD with gradient correction”):

\begin{aligned} \delta \;&\leftarrow\; r + \gamma\,\phi'^\top w - \phi^\top w \\ w \;&\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,\delta\,\phi \;-\; \alpha\,\gamma\,\phi'\,(\phi^\top u) \\ u \;&\leftarrow\; u + \beta\,(\delta - \phi^\top u)\,\phi \end{aligned}

The first w-term is identical to semi-gradient TD. The second term is the correction that makes the update an honest gradient of MSPBE — paid for by carrying u.

In expectation: \mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi - \gamma\,\phi'\,(\phi^\top u)] = \mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] - \gamma\,\mathbb{E}[\phi'\,\phi^\top]\,u, and at the joint fixed point A u = \mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] this becomes A u - \gamma\,\mathbb{E}[\phi' \phi^\top] u = (A - \gamma\,\mathbb{E}[\phi' \phi^\top])\,u. Setting this to zero gives the same equation as GTD2’s fixed point.

Why TDC has lower variance. GTD2’s update is \alpha (\phi - \gamma \phi')(\phi^\top u) — a product of two random vectors, each variance-y. TDC replaces the first random vector with \delta\,\phi (whose mean is A u at the fixed point, with smaller variance than A u recomputed via \phi(\phi^\top u)). The correction term then subtracts only the bias, not the full estimator. In practice TDC needs fewer samples than GTD2 to reach the same accuracy.

Problem 6 — Baird’s counterexample: divergence vs convergence

Baird’s 7-state counterexample is a linear-FA MDP where semi-gradient TD with off-policy data diverges geometrically: weights grow without bound. Without redoing the construction here, argue (using Problems 4 and 5) why TDC converges on the same MDP, given the GTD/TDC convergence theorem (Sutton, Maei et al., 2009): under linear FA, off-policy data with bounded importance weights, and two-time-scale step sizes (\beta \to 0 slower than \alpha), (w_t, u_t) converges almost surely to a fixed point of MSPBE.

Full solution

The semi-gradient TD analysis of Baird’s example shows that the expected update is \mathbb{E}[\text{update}] = -\alpha\,M w for an unstable M (some eigenvalues with positive real part). That makes the iteration diverge.

The gradient-TD updates are designed so that the expected joint (w, u)-update descends MSPBE. As a function of w, MSPBE is lower-bounded by zero; as long as the gradient estimator is unbiased in expectation, the iteration cannot diverge to infinity. Two-time-scale analysis (Borkar 1997) then shows the slow timescale converges to a fixed point of MSPBE — exactly the projected Bellman fixed point we named in L32.

In short: TDC converges on Baird’s counterexample, where semi-gradient TD diverges. This is the headline result of the gradient-TD program. The deadly triad is mathematically defused — at the price of u, \beta, and slower wall-clock convergence.

The gradient-TD line was opened by Sutton, Maei, Precup, Bhatnagar, Silver, Szepesvári, and Wiewiora (2009) with the paper “A convergent O(n) temporal-difference algorithm for off-policy learning with linear function approximation” (NeurIPS). The original GTD update was clean but slow; Sutton, Maei et al. (2009b, ICML) released GTD2 and TDC the same year. Why didn’t gradient-TD take over deep RL? Variance was the main issue: u is itself noisy, so the correction can swing wildly with neural-network features. DQN’s engineering (target networks, replay buffers) gave more reliable practical performance, even without the convergence guarantee. The road-not-taken stayed not-taken.

Gradient-TD is what semi-gradient TD would be if it stopped cheating. The “cheat” was treating the target as w-independent. The fix is not to put the missing gradient back (that’s residual gradient, which targets the wrong objective). The fix is to descend MSPBE itself — and the cost of that honesty is a second parameter u tracking \mathbb{E}[\phi \phi^\top]^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi], and a slower clock for u than for w. The deadly triad is mathematically defused; the engineering trade-off is variance.

Name

The gradient-TD family consists of SGD-style algorithms that descend MSPBE by tracking a second parameter u \in \mathbb{R}^d on a slower timescale than the main weights w:

GTD2 per-sample updates:

\begin{aligned} w \;&\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,(\phi^\top u) \\ u \;&\leftarrow\; u + \beta\,(\delta - \phi^\top u)\,\phi \end{aligned}

TDC (TD with gradient correction) — equivalent fixed point, lower variance:

\begin{aligned} w \;&\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,\delta\,\phi \;-\; \alpha\,\gamma\,\phi'\,(\phi^\top u) \\ u \;&\leftarrow\; u + \beta\,(\delta - \phi^\top u)\,\phi \end{aligned}

Under linear FA, off-policy data with bounded importance weights, and two-time-scale Robbins–Monro step sizes (\alpha, \beta \to 0 with \beta / \alpha \to 0), (w_t, u_t) converges almost surely to a fixed point of MSPBE — the projected Bellman fixed point of L32 — even when semi-gradient TD diverges.

In one mnemonic line:

Two parameters, two clocks: w chases the projected Bellman point, u tracks the gradient correction.

Apply

Worked example — TDC on a 2-state off-policy chain

Two states \{1, 2\}. Target policy \pi goes 1 \to 1, 2 \to 1 (absorbing at 1). Behaviour policy b samples uniformly over the two states. Reward r = 0, \gamma = 0.9. Features \phi(1) = (1, 0)^\top, \phi(2) = (1, 2)^\top.

The on-policy stationary distribution of \pi puts all mass on state 1, so the true value function under \pi is v_\pi = (0, 0)^\top (absorbing at the zero-reward state). The TD fixed point of MSPBE under \mu_b = (\tfrac12, \tfrac12) is also w^\star = (0, 0) in this setup (the only w in the feature span that gives \hat v = 0).

Initialise w = (1, 1)^\top, u = (0, 0)^\top, \alpha = 0.1, \beta = 0.01 (so \beta < \alpha). Sample one transition (s = 2, r = 0, s' = 1) — behaviour visits state 2, target sends it to state 1.

  1. \delta = 0 + 0.9 \cdot \phi(1)^\top w - \phi(2)^\top w = 0.9 \cdot 1 - 3 = -2.1.
  2. TDC w-update. \phi^\top u = 0, so the correction term is 0 on the first step. w \leftarrow (1, 1) + 0.1 \cdot (-2.1) \cdot (1, 2) = (1 - 0.21,\, 1 - 0.42) = (0.79,\, 0.58).
  3. u-update. u \leftarrow (0, 0) + 0.01 \cdot (-2.1 - 0)\cdot (1, 2) = (-0.021,\, -0.042).

On the next step, u \ne 0, so the correction term becomes active and pulls the w-update toward the projected Bellman direction.

Run this for 10 000 steps and you find w_t \to (0, 0). Semi-gradient TD on this off-policy data tends to drift instead — the on-policy mismatch breaks the contraction (see L32 Stretch S1).

Practice — Warm

W1. Write the GTD2 update on a single transition (s, r, s') with features \phi(s) = (1, s)^\top, \gamma = 0.5, \alpha = 0.1, \beta = 0.01. Use s = 1, r = 1, s' = 2, initial w = (0, 0), u = (0, 0).

Hint 1 — a nudge Compute \delta first; then both updates.
Full solution

\delta = 1 + 0.5 \cdot \phi(2)^\top w - \phi(1)^\top w = 1 + 0 - 0 = 1. \phi^\top u = 0. \phi - \gamma\,\phi' = (1, 1) - 0.5 \cdot (1, 2) = (0.5, 0). GTD2 w: stays (0, 0) because \phi^\top u = 0. u: u \leftarrow (0, 0) + 0.01 \cdot (1 - 0) \cdot (1, 1) = (0.01, 0.01).

First step: u moves, w stays. The auxiliary parameter has to warm up before it can correct the main update.

W2. Same setup as W1 but use TDC instead of GTD2.

Hint 1 — a nudge TDC’s w-update has a semi-gradient TD piece plus a correction.
Full solution

\delta = 1, \phi^\top u = 0. w: w \leftarrow (0, 0) + 0.1 \cdot 1 \cdot (1, 1) - 0.1 \cdot 0.5 \cdot (1, 2) \cdot 0 = (0.1, 0.1). The correction term vanishes (because \phi^\top u = 0), so TDC reduces to semi-gradient TD on the first step. u: same as W1, (0.01, 0.01).

Key contrast with GTD2: TDC’s first step moves w immediately — it doesn’t have to wait for u to warm up. That’s part of why TDC is faster in practice.

W3. Why does TDC update u on the fast timescale and w on the slow timescale? (Some treatments invert this — flag what’s conventional.)

Hint 1 — a nudge The convergence proof requires u to track A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] as a function of w. For that tracking to be accurate, u must move faster than w, so it equilibrates between w-updates.
Full solution

Convention: u uses the larger step size \beta (fast), w uses the smaller \alpha (slow); typically \beta / \alpha \to \infty as t \to \infty, or equivalently \alpha_t / \beta_t \to 0.

The reason: the convergence proof uses two-time-scale stochastic approximation (Borkar 1997). From w’s perspective, u has already converged to its w-dependent optimum u^\star(w) = A^{-1}\, \mathbb{E}[\delta(w)\,\phi]; from u’s perspective, w is frozen. That separation requires u to equilibrate faster than w moves.

Some early gradient-TD papers swap the notation — the unique parameter is “the slow one” in the convergence-proof sense, whichever symbol you give it. Here we follow Sutton & Barto’s convention: \alpha on w, \beta on u, with \beta > \alpha. (Yes, that means the “slow” w uses the smaller step size — confusing because “slow” feels like “small” but in this context “slow” means “moves less per unit time,” which is exactly what a smaller step achieves.)

Practice — Working

P1. Show that, in expectation, the GTD2 w-update equals -\tfrac12 \nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE}(w) when u has reached its w-dependent optimum u^\star(w) = A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi].

Hint 1 — a nudge From Problem 2’s solution, \nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE} = -2\,\mathbb{E}[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]\,A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] = -2\,\mathbb{E}[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]\,u^\star(w).
Full solution

The GTD2 update is \Delta w = \alpha\,(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,(\phi^\top u). Take expectation and substitute u = u^\star(w):

\mathbb{E}[\Delta w] = \alpha\,\mathbb{E}[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]\,u^\star(w) = \alpha\,\mathbb{E}[(\phi - \gamma\,\phi')\,\phi^\top]\,A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] = -\tfrac{\alpha}{2}\,\nabla_w \mathrm{MSPBE}(w).

So GTD2 is honest stochastic gradient descent on MSPBE (modulo the constant factor 2 and the assumption that u has equilibrated). That is precisely the convergence guarantee Problem 6 cited.

P2. Verify TDC reduces to semi-gradient TD when u \equiv 0.

Hint 1 — a nudge Look at TDC’s w-update with u = 0 plugged in.
Full solution w \leftarrow w + \alpha\,\delta\,\phi - \alpha\,\gamma\,\phi'\,(\phi^\top \cdot 0) = w + \alpha\,\delta\,\phi — exactly the semi-gradient TD update from L29. So TDC is “semi-gradient TD plus a correction the auxiliary parameter learns.” When u is at its initial zero, TDC is semi-gradient TD. As u warms up the correction kicks in, and at the fixed point the correction exactly cancels the semi-gradient bias that would otherwise drag w to the wrong place under off-policy data.

P3. A student says: “Gradient-TD only matters off-policy. On-policy, semi-gradient TD is fine, so we should always use semi-gradient TD when we can.” Is this right?

Hint 1 — a nudge What does the deadly triad require for divergence to even be possible?
Full solution

Half right. On-policy linear FA semi-gradient TD converges (Tsitsiklis–Van Roy 1997), and TDC’s correction term equals zero in expectation in that regime — so TDC offers no theoretical advantage on-policy and gives up some speed. In practice, when you know you’re on-policy with linear FA, use semi-gradient TD.

But many practitioners aren’t sure if they’re on-policy. Experience replay (DQN) breaks the on-policy property by definition. Off-policy evaluation of one policy from another’s trajectories (recommender systems, medical RL) is off-policy by construction. In those settings, TDC’s convergence guarantee is meaningful. The cost (a second parameter, slower convergence) is sometimes worth paying.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Emphatic TD is a different fix for the deadly triad: instead of carrying an auxiliary parameter, it re-weights the per-sample TD update by a state-dependent factor F_t that compensates for the off-policy mismatch. Sketch (informally) why this might work, and state one practical disadvantage relative to TDC.

Hint 1 — a nudge The deadly triad arose because \mu_b \ne \mu_\pi. What if you could construct sample weights that recovered \mu_\pi-norm contraction behaviour?
Full solution

Emphatic TD (ETD) (Sutton, Mahmood & White, 2016) introduces a recursive weight F_t that essentially counts “how much would the target policy have visited this state, given the trajectory so far?”. Re-weighting the standard TD update by F_t restores a contraction property in the emphatic norm — and ETD converges with linear FA off-policy.

Practical disadvantage: F_t can have very high variance under off-policy data, especially when the target and behaviour policies differ a lot. Numerical instability is the usual symptom. TDC’s u is also noisy but is bounded by the geometry of \phi; F_t has no such bound and can blow up.

Both ETD and TDC are correct convergent algorithms; both have variance issues; neither has displaced the engineering DQN does. Sutton & Barto §11.7 give a clean comparison.

S2. Convert the TDC update to the off-policy setting using importance sampling. (Behaviour b generates data, target \pi is evaluated. Per-step importance ratio \rho_t = \pi(a_t \mid s_t) / b(a_t \mid s_t).) Where does \rho_t enter the TDC update?

Hint 1 — a nudge Every place an expectation under \mu_\pi is being approximated by an empirical average over \mu_b-data, weight the sample by \rho_t.
Full solution

Off-policy TDC:

\begin{aligned} \delta_t \;&\leftarrow\; r_t + \gamma\,\phi_{t+1}^\top w - \phi_t^\top w \\ w \;&\leftarrow\; w + \alpha\,\rho_t\,\bigl[\delta_t\,\phi_t - \gamma\,\phi_{t+1}\,(\phi_t^\top u)\bigr] \\ u \;&\leftarrow\; u + \beta\,\rho_t\,(\delta_t - \phi_t^\top u)\,\phi_t \end{aligned}

The importance ratio \rho_t multiplies the entire per-sample update — both for w and for u — to convert the \mu_b-empirical average into an estimator of the \mu_\pi-expectation. That extra \rho_t factor is exactly where the variance creeps in: \rho_t can be large when \pi(a \mid s) \gg b(a \mid s).

Common confusions

  1. “Gradient-TD is just TD with the missing gradient added back.” No. That’s residual gradient, which targets \tfrac12\,\delta^2 — the wrong objective when v_\pi \notin \mathcal{F}. Gradient-TD targets MSPBE — the right objective. The two algorithms find different fixed points; one is the projected Bellman point, one is not.
  2. “u is a value function.” It’s not. u has the same dimension as w (d features) and lives in feature-space, but it represents A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi] — a “gradient correction.” Calling it a value-function approximator is a category error.
  3. “Two-time-scale just means use different step sizes.” Stronger than that: the proof needs \beta_t / \alpha_t \to \infty asymptotically. In practice constant ratios like \beta = 10 \alpha work, but the asymptotic regime is what licenses the convergence theorem.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Gradient-TD descends MSPBE honestly. Semi-gradient TD’s “cheat” (ignore the target’s w-dependence) is replaced by a real gradient estimator, paid for with a second parameter u tracking A^{-1}\,\mathbb{E}[\delta\,\phi].
  2. TDC = semi-gradient TD + correction. When u = 0 TDC is semi-gradient TD. As u warms up the correction cancels the off-policy bias. At the fixed point, the corrected update has expectation zero — MSPBE is at a minimum.
  3. The deadly triad is defused, but variance is not. TDC converges where semi-gradient TD diverges (Baird’s counterexample). Why didn’t it take over? Variance from u is the practical cost, and DQN’s engineering tricks dominate when the workload is forgiving.

Nothing else in this track depends on gradient-TD. This is the road-not-taken branch of the FA arc — the mathematically clean answer to the deadly triad, which the rest of modern deep RL declined in favour of DQN’s engineering. Where this matters

Research. Off-policy policy evaluation under linear FA is a live research area — recommender-system A/B-testing, off-policy medical RL, counterfactual reasoning in causal inference. In those settings divergence is not an acceptable failure mode, and the gradient-TD family (along with emphatic TD and recent gradient-Q variants) are the convergent options.

AI / ML. Modern off-policy actor-critic (DDPG, SAC, TD3) descends neither MSPBE nor MSTDE cleanly — they use semi-gradient updates and trust engineering (target networks, twin critics, clipped double Q) to suppress the divergence the gradient-TD family solves theoretically. Understanding what the engineering is suppressing is the value of this lesson.
