32 · The Projected Bellman Operator and MSPBE

Geometrically: a wall, a target, and a Bellman push. On-policy the round-trip is a contraction; off-policy it need not be.

Pre-flight check

Be honest before you read on.

  1. Can you state the semi-gradient TD update from Lesson 29 and say what “semi” means?
  2. Can you compute a dot product, and weight it by a probability distribution \langle x, y \rangle_\mu = \sum_s \mu(s)\, x(s)\, y(s)?
  3. Can you name the three ingredients of the deadly triad from Lesson 30?
  4. Do you know what a \gamma-contraction in a norm is — i.e. an operator T with \|Tu - Tv\| \le \gamma\,\|u - v\|?

If any answer is no, revisit the named lesson or glossary entry before continuing.

Touch

There are three states: A, B, C. Their true values are

v_\pi(A) = 1,\quad v_\pi(B) = 1,\quad v_\pi(C) = 3.

You don’t have a table — you have two knobs w_1 and w_2, and your estimate is forced to obey

\hat v(s) \;=\; w_1 \;+\; w_2 \cdot s, \qquad s \in \{1, 2, 3\}\ \text{for}\ A, B, C.

Try a few settings of (w_1, w_2). For each, write down \hat v(A), \hat v(B), \hat v(C), and the squared error E = (1 - \hat v(A))^2 + (1 - \hat v(B))^2 + (3 - \hat v(C))^2.

w_1 w_2 \hat v(A) \hat v(B) \hat v(C) E
0 1 1 2 3 1
-1 \tfrac{4}{3} \tfrac{1}{3} \tfrac{5}{3} 3 \approx 0.89
-\tfrac{1}{3} 1 \tfrac{2}{3} \tfrac{5}{3} \tfrac{8}{3} \approx 0.67
\tfrac{1}{3} 1 \tfrac{4}{3} \tfrac{7}{3} \tfrac{10}{3} \approx 2.0

No setting hits all three targets exactly. The third row is the lowest error you can reach.

There is no (w_1, w_2) that makes \hat v equal v_\pi. With two knobs you can produce only a 2-D plane of possible \hat v values inside the 3-D space \mathbb{R}^3. The truth lives off that plane. You can get closest to it, but you cannot reach it.

Wonder

Sit with these. Don’t write yet.

  1. The set of all \hat v you can produce by varying (w_1, w_2) — what shape is it inside \mathbb{R}^3?
  2. If we said state B matters twice as much as A or C, would the “best” setting of the knobs change?
  3. The fitting rule is linear in (w_1, w_2). Does that make finding the best setting easy, or just possible?
  4. Suppose you don’t know v_\pi; you only see TD targets r + \gamma \hat v(s') — which themselves depend on (w_1, w_2). Does the “best fit” idea still make sense when the target moves?

Discover

Five problems. Each is small.

Problem 1 — the feature span is a plane

Write each state’s feature vector \phi(s) = (1, s)^\top. Stack them as rows of a matrix

\Phi \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}.

Then \hat v = \Phi w where w = (w_1, w_2)^\top. Describe the set \{\Phi w : w \in \mathbb{R}^2\} as a subset of \mathbb{R}^3.

Hint 1 — a nudge The two columns of \Phi are the two feature vectors viewed across all states. What does varying w do to combinations of those columns?
Full solution \Phi w is a linear combination of the two columns of \Phi. Varying w \in \mathbb{R}^2 traces out the span of those columns — a 2-D subspace of \mathbb{R}^3. We call this subspace the feature span and write it \mathcal{F} = \mathrm{span}(\Phi) \subset \mathbb{R}^{|S|}. Every realisable \hat v lives on \mathcal{F}. Any v off \mathcal{F} — the true v_\pi in the Touch — is not reachable by linear FA, no matter how you tune w.

Problem 2 — the closest point on the plane

Equip \mathbb{R}^{|S|} with the \mu-weighted inner product

\langle x, y \rangle_\mu \;=\; \sum_s \mu(s)\, x(s)\, y(s), \qquad \|x\|_\mu \;=\; \sqrt{\langle x, x \rangle_\mu}.

For any v \in \mathbb{R}^{|S|} there is a unique point \Pi_\mu v in \mathcal{F} that minimises \|v - \Pi_\mu v\|_\mu. State (without proof) why such a point exists and is unique, and write down what \Pi_\mu v is when v already lies in \mathcal{F}.

Hint 1 — a nudge You can pull two facts off the shelf from linear algebra: closed convex subsets of a finite-dimensional inner-product space contain a unique closest point to any external point; and projecting a point that’s already in the subspace gives the point itself.
Full solution \mathcal{F} is a finite-dimensional linear subspace; the \mu-weighted inner product gives \mathbb{R}^{|S|} a Hilbert-space structure (every \mu(s) > 0 on the support). The orthogonal-projection theorem then guarantees a unique \Pi_\mu v \in \mathcal{F} minimising \|v - \cdot\|_\mu. If v \in \mathcal{F} then \Pi_\mu v = v — the closest point in a subspace to a point already in the subspace is itself. \Pi_\mu is linear (it’s a composition of linear maps), and idempotent (\Pi_\mu^2 = \Pi_\mu).

Problem 3 — projection is non-expansive

Show that for any u, v \in \mathbb{R}^{|S|},

\|\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v\|_\mu \;\le\; \|u - v\|_\mu.

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the Pythagorean theorem. The residual v - \Pi_\mu v is orthogonal to \mathcal{F} in \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu. Drop the residual component and see what remains.
Hint 2 — the technique Decompose u - v = (\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v) + (\text{residual}) where the two parts are orthogonal in the \mu-norm. Pythagoras gives \|u - v\|_\mu^2 = \|\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v\|_\mu^2 + \|\text{residual}\|_\mu^2.
Full solution

\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v \in \mathcal{F} (the subspace is closed under subtraction). The residual (u - \Pi_\mu u) - (v - \Pi_\mu v) is the difference of two vectors each orthogonal to \mathcal{F}, so is itself orthogonal to \mathcal{F}. The two pieces are orthogonal in \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu. By Pythagoras

\|u - v\|_\mu^2 \;=\; \|\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v\|_\mu^2 \;+\; \|(u - \Pi_\mu u) - (v - \Pi_\mu v)\|_\mu^2,

so \|\Pi_\mu u - \Pi_\mu v\|_\mu^2 \le \|u - v\|_\mu^2. Projection never lengthens distances — it’s non-expansive (1-Lipschitz) in the norm whose inner product it’s orthogonal to.

Problem 4 — on-policy: the composition is a contraction

Recall from L29 Stretch S1 the result we cited without proof: when \mu is the on-policy stationary distribution of \pi, the Bellman operator T_\pi, defined by

(T_\pi v)(s) \;=\; \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s', r} p(s', r \mid s, a)\,\bigl[r + \gamma\, v(s')\bigr],

is a \gamma-contraction in \|\cdot\|_\mu: \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_\mu \le \gamma\, \|u - v\|_\mu. Use that, plus Problem 3, to show that the composed operator \Pi_\mu T_\pi is a \gamma-contraction in \|\cdot\|_\mu. Conclude using Banach’s fixed-point theorem (L11) that \Pi_\mu T_\pi has a unique fixed point v^\star \in \mathcal{F}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Chain the two Lipschitz bounds. Apply T_\pi first, then \Pi_\mu.
Full solution

For any u, v,

\|\Pi_\mu T_\pi u - \Pi_\mu T_\pi v\|_\mu \;\le\; \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_\mu \qquad \text{(Problem 3)}

\le\; \gamma\,\|u - v\|_\mu \qquad \text{(on-policy Bellman contraction)}.

So \Pi_\mu T_\pi is a \gamma-contraction. Banach (L11) gives a unique fixed point v^\star \in \mathcal{F} — the TD fixed point — and iteration v_{n+1} = \Pi_\mu T_\pi v_n converges geometrically from any starting point. This is what semi-gradient TD with linear FA on-policy actually converges to (Tsitsiklis–Van Roy 1997).

Problem 5 — off-policy: the contraction can break

Suppose the data is generated by a behaviour policy b, not \pi. The sampling distribution is then \mu_b, the stationary distribution of b, not the on-policy \mu_\pi. The Bellman operator we still want to apply is T_\pi (we’re evaluating \pi, not b). Argue informally — without a proof — why T_\pi need not be a \gamma-contraction in \|\cdot\|_{\mu_b}, and conclude what this means for \Pi_{\mu_b} T_\pi.

Hint 1 — a nudge The Bellman contraction on-policy uses the stationary property \sum_s \mu_\pi(s)\, p_\pi(s' \mid s) = \mu_\pi(s'). The same identity fails for \mu_b paired with T_\pi’s transition kernel.
Full solution

The on-policy proof of “T_\pi is a \gamma-contraction in \|\cdot\|_{\mu_\pi}” leans on the fact that \mu_\pi is stationary under \pi: pushing mass forward by one step of \pi does not change \mu_\pi. Off-policy, the data distribution is \mu_b, but T_\pi operates with \pi’s kernel. Those don’t match. The cancellation that made the contraction work no longer applies. Concretely you can construct counterexamples where \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_{\mu_b} exceeds \|u - v\|_{\mu_b} — i.e. T_\pi expands the off-policy norm.

When the Bellman half of the round-trip is expansive, the projection half can’t always pull things back: \Pi_{\mu_b} T_\pi need not be a contraction, the fixed point need not be unique (or even exist), and the iteration v_{n+1} = \Pi_{\mu_b} T_\pi v_n can diverge. This is the deadly triad in one picture: function approximation (the projection), bootstrapping (the Bellman operator), and off-policy data (the wrong weighting) together break the contraction argument.

The composition \Pi_\mu T_\pi is exactly what John Tsitsiklis and Benjamin Van Roy used in their 1997 IEEE Transactions on Automatic Control paper “An Analysis of Temporal-Difference Learning with Function Approximation” to give the first rigorous convergence proof for TD(0) with linear FA. The proof is essentially Problem 4 — the rest of the paper handles the stochastic-approximation bookkeeping that gets you from “fixed point of an operator” to “limit of the stochastic update”. Twenty-eight years later their conditions still define the boundary of safe.

The projection is a wall: \hat v can never leave the feature span. The Bellman update wants to push the value somewhere on the other side of the wall; the projection drags it back. On-policy weighting keeps that drag short enough — at most \gamma-shrink per round-trip — that the dance converges to a single point. Off-policy weighting lets the drag grow: the Bellman push can expand, and the projection can no longer fix it. Divergence becomes possible — not certain, but no longer ruled out.

Name

Let \Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{|S| \times d} be the feature matrix and \mathcal{F} = \{\Phi w : w \in \mathbb{R}^d\} the feature span. Let \mu be a probability distribution on S with \mu(s) > 0 for all s in the support.

The projection operator \Pi_\mu : \mathbb{R}^{|S|} \to \mathcal{F} sends each v to the unique closest point in \mathcal{F} under the \mu-weighted norm. In matrix form,

\Pi_\mu \;=\; \Phi\,(\Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi)^{-1}\,\Phi^\top D_\mu, \qquad D_\mu = \mathrm{diag}(\mu).

The projected Bellman operator is the composition \Pi_\mu T_\pi. Its fixed points in \mathcal{F} are the TD-style solutions.

The mean-square projected Bellman error (MSPBE) of a weight vector w is

\mathrm{MSPBE}(w) \;=\; \bigl\|\,\hat v_w \;-\; \Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w\,\bigr\|_\mu^2.

Its zeros are exactly the TD fixed points: \mathrm{MSPBE}(w^\star) = 0 iff \hat v_{w^\star} = \Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_{w^\star}.

In one mnemonic line:

The projection is the wall; the Bellman is the push; on-policy the round-trip shrinks.

Apply

Worked example — the 3-state chain from Touch

States A, B, C with s = 1, 2, 3; true values v_\pi = (1, 1, 3)^\top; features \phi(s) = (1, s)^\top; uniform weighting \mu = (\tfrac13, \tfrac13, \tfrac13).

Step 1: build \Phi and D_\mu.

\Phi = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \\ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix}, \qquad D_\mu = \tfrac{1}{3}\, I_3.

Step 2: compute the projection matrix.

\Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi = \tfrac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix}3 & 6 \\ 6 & 14\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 \\ 2 & \tfrac{14}{3}\end{pmatrix}.

Its inverse is \frac{1}{1 \cdot \tfrac{14}{3} - 4} \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{14}{3} & -2 \\ -2 & 1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{3}{2}\begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{14}{3} & -2 \\ -2 & 1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}7 & -3 \\ -3 & \tfrac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix}.

Step 3: project v_\pi.

The least-squares weights are w^\star = (\Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi)^{-1}\, \Phi^\top D_\mu\, v_\pi. Compute

\Phi^\top D_\mu\, v_\pi = \tfrac{1}{3}\begin{pmatrix}5 \\ 12\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{5}{3} \\ 4\end{pmatrix},

then

w^\star = \begin{pmatrix}7 & -3 \\ -3 & \tfrac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{5}{3} \\ 4\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{35}{3} - 12 \\ -5 + 6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-\tfrac{1}{3} \\ 1\end{pmatrix}.

So the projection is

\Pi_\mu v_\pi \;=\; \Phi w^\star = \begin{pmatrix}-\tfrac{1}{3} + 1 \\ -\tfrac{1}{3} + 2 \\ -\tfrac{1}{3} + 3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{2}{3} \\ \tfrac{5}{3} \\ \tfrac{8}{3}\end{pmatrix}.

Step 4: check the residual is orthogonal.

The residual is v_\pi - \Pi_\mu v_\pi = (\tfrac{1}{3}, -\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{1}{3})^\top. Its \mu-weighted inner product with (1, 1, 1)^\top (the first feature) is \tfrac{1}{3}(\tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{1}{3}) = 0. With (1, 2, 3)^\top (the second feature): \tfrac{1}{3}(\tfrac{1}{3} - \tfrac{4}{3} + 1) = 0. The residual is orthogonal to \mathcal{F} in \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu, as the theory promised.

Practice — Warm

W1. With features \phi(s) = (1, s)^\top on three states and uniform \mu, what is \Pi_\mu v for v = (1, 2, 3)^\top?

Hint 1 — a nudge v lies on the feature span: try w_1 = 0, w_2 = 1.
Full solution v = (1, 2, 3) = \Phi (0, 1)^\top, so v \in \mathcal{F} and \Pi_\mu v = v. Projecting a point already in the subspace returns the point itself (idempotency).

W2. Same features, same \mu. What is \Pi_\mu v for v = (1, 1, 1)^\top?

Hint 1 — a nudge v is the all-ones vector; that’s \Phi (1, 0)^\top.
Full solution v = \Phi (1, 0)^\top, so v \in \mathcal{F} and \Pi_\mu v = v. Constant vectors are always in the span when one feature is the bias \phi_1 = 1.

W3. Write the matrix \Pi_\mu in full for the worked example.

Hint 1 — a nudge \Pi_\mu = \Phi (\Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi)^{-1} \Phi^\top D_\mu.
Full solution

Using the pieces from the worked example:

\Pi_\mu = \Phi \begin{pmatrix}7 & -3 \\ -3 & \tfrac{3}{2}\end{pmatrix} \Phi^\top D_\mu.

Multiplying out:

\Pi_\mu = \tfrac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 2 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}.

Row sums are 1 (consistent with \Pi_\mu \mathbf 1 = \mathbf 1). The matrix is idempotent: \Pi_\mu^2 = \Pi_\mu — you can check by re-multiplying. It is not symmetric in the standard sense; it is self-adjoint with respect to \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu, which means D_\mu \Pi_\mu = (D_\mu \Pi_\mu)^\top. Verify: D_\mu \Pi_\mu = \tfrac{1}{18}\begin{pmatrix}5 & 2 & -1 \\ 2 & 2 & 2 \\ -1 & 2 & 5\end{pmatrix} is symmetric.

Practice — Working

P1. Re-do the worked example with weighting \mu = (\tfrac12, \tfrac14, \tfrac14). Does the best w change? Compute \Pi_\mu v_\pi.

Hint 1 — a nudge The least-squares normal equations are \Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi\, w = \Phi^\top D_\mu\, v_\pi. Rebuild both sides.
Hint 2 — the technique With D_\mu = \mathrm{diag}(\tfrac12, \tfrac14, \tfrac14): \Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi = \begin{pmatrix}1 & \tfrac{7}{4} \\ \tfrac{7}{4} & \tfrac{15}{4}\end{pmatrix}, \Phi^\top D_\mu v_\pi = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{3}{2} \\ \tfrac{11}{4}\end{pmatrix}.
Full solution Solving the 2 \times 2 system: \det = \tfrac{15}{4} - \tfrac{49}{16} = \tfrac{11}{16}. Then w^\star = \tfrac{16}{11}\begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{15}{4} & -\tfrac{7}{4} \\ -\tfrac{7}{4} & 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{3}{2} \\ \tfrac{11}{4}\end{pmatrix}. Working through: w_1^\star = \tfrac{16}{11} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4}(\tfrac{15 \cdot 3}{2} - \tfrac{7 \cdot 11}{4}) = \tfrac{16}{11} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot \tfrac{90 - 77}{4} = \tfrac{16 \cdot 13}{11 \cdot 16} = \tfrac{13}{11}. And w_2^\star = \tfrac{16}{11} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4}(-\tfrac{21}{2} + 11) = \tfrac{16}{11} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{2}{11}. Change of \mu changes the projection. Heavier weight on A pulls the fit closer to v_\pi(A) = 1, lighter weight on C lets the fit miss v_\pi(C) = 3 by more.

P2. A student writes: “The projection \Pi is the same matrix no matter what \mu we use; only the measure of error changes.” Refute in one short paragraph.

Hint 1 — a nudge Compare W3 and P1.
Full solution Different \mu give different projections — see P1 vs the worked example, where \Pi_\mu v_\pi changed from (\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{5}{3}, \tfrac{8}{3}) to (\Phi w^\star) with w^\star = (\tfrac{13}{11}, \tfrac{2}{11}). The formula \Pi_\mu = \Phi (\Phi^\top D_\mu \Phi)^{-1} \Phi^\top D_\mu literally has D_\mu in it. “Project onto the feature span” is incomplete as a recipe — in what norm? must be specified. The norm chooses which errors matter, and that choice is encoded in \mu.

P3. State the relationship between \Pi_\mu T_\pi and semi-gradient TD (Lesson 29). What does semi-gradient TD converge to in the on-policy linear-FA regime?

Hint 1 — a nudge Use Problem 4 (Discover) and Tsitsiklis–Van Roy (the fun-fact).
Full solution Tsitsiklis–Van Roy (1997) showed that, under linear FA and on-policy sampling with Robbins–Monro step sizes, semi-gradient TD’s parameter w_t converges (with probability 1) to the unique w^\star satisfying \hat v_{w^\star} = \Pi_{\mu_\pi} T_\pi \hat v_{w^\star}. In words: the algorithm finds the TD fixed point — the fixed point of the projected Bellman operator \Pi_\mu T_\pi — which is exactly where MSPBE(w) is zero. This names what we said in L29 was happening without naming.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Construct an off-policy weighting \mu_b on 2 states that makes the Bellman operator T_\pi expansive in \|\cdot\|_{\mu_b} — i.e. find u, v with \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_{\mu_b} > \|u - v\|_{\mu_b}. Use \gamma = 0.9 and any deterministic \pi. This is the off-policy half of the deadly-triad story.

Hint 1 — a nudge Take two states where \pi sends almost all mass to one state, but \mu_b puts almost all weight on the other. The Bellman operator pushes mass forward by \pi; if \mu_b doesn’t match, the norm can magnify the difference.
Hint 2 — the technique Two states \{1, 2\}. Policy \pi goes 1 \to 2 and 2 \to 2 (absorbing). On-policy \mu_\pi puts most mass on state 2. Choose \mu_b = (0.99, 0.01) — opposite of stationary. Take u = (1, 0), v = (0, 0).
Full solution

With \pi: T_\pi v(s) = r(s) + \gamma\, v(\pi(s)) on this deterministic setup (taking r = 0 for simplicity). So T_\pi u(s_1) = 0.9 \cdot u(2) = 0 and T_\pi u(s_2) = 0.9 \cdot u(2) = 0. Thus T_\pi u = (0, 0) = T_\pi v. That gives \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_{\mu_b} = 0 — a contraction of u to itself, no expansion. We need a different setup.

Try \pi: 1 \to 1, 2 \to 1. Then T_\pi u = (0.9 \cdot u_1, 0.9 \cdot u_1). Pick u = (1, 0), v = (0, 0). T_\pi u = (0.9, 0.9), T_\pi v = (0, 0). With \mu_b = (0.01, 0.99): \|u - v\|_{\mu_b}^2 = 0.01 \cdot 1 + 0.99 \cdot 0 = 0.01, so \|u-v\|_{\mu_b} = 0.1. \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_{\mu_b}^2 = 0.01 \cdot 0.81 + 0.99 \cdot 0.81 = 0.81, so \|T_\pi u - T_\pi v\|_{\mu_b} = 0.9.

Ratio 0.9 / 0.1 = 9 \gg \gamma. The Bellman operator expanded by a factor of 9 in this off-policy norm. Compose with projection and the fixed-point iteration can diverge.

The intuition: state 1 is rare under \mu_b, but it’s where \pi sends everything. The Bellman push amplifies “what does the value at state 1 look like” — and \mu_b has nearly stopped measuring state 1.

S2. Show that the Bellman error \|T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w\|_\mu^2 and the projected Bellman error \|\Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w\|_\mu^2 are different objectives. Which one does semi-gradient TD secretly descend?

Hint 1 — a nudge Decompose T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w = (T_\pi \hat v_w - \Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w) + (\Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w). The two pieces are orthogonal in \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu because \hat v_w and \Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w both live in \mathcal{F} while the first piece is in \mathcal{F}^\perp.
Full solution

Pythagoras in \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_\mu:

\|T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w\|_\mu^2 \;=\; \underbrace{\|T_\pi \hat v_w - \Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w\|_\mu^2}_{\text{distance to }\mathcal{F}} \;+\; \underbrace{\|\Pi_\mu T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w\|_\mu^2}_{\mathrm{MSPBE}(w)}.

The first term measures how far the Bellman image is from the feature span — a quantity that depends only on the model and the features, not on w. The second term is exactly MSPBE. So the Bellman error decomposes as a w-independent constant plus MSPBE. Semi-gradient TD descends MSPBE (when it converges), not the full Bellman error. That distinction is what makes semi-gradient TD work despite the moving target — it’s chasing a w-meaningful objective, not a fictitious one. It is also why residual gradient (full SGD on \|T_\pi \hat v_w - \hat v_w\|^2) finds the wrong fixed point in mis-specified models: the residual-gradient objective is contaminated by the w-independent first term.

Common confusions

  1. “\Pi is just the closest point geometrically.” No — geometry here depends on \mu. With Euclidean weighting you get one closest point; with \mu-weighted you get another. Two different “closest” answers from two different inner products. The same feature span; the same target v; different projections.
  2. “MSPBE is just the squared TD error.” No. The TD error is \delta; summing \mathbb{E}_\mu[\delta^2] gives the mean-square TD error (MSTDE), not MSPBE. MSPBE measures distance to the projected target, which is the only meaningful thing when the target lives off the feature span. The two objectives have different minimisers and different gradients — gradient-TD (L33) exists precisely to descend MSPBE rather than MSTDE.
  3. “Off-policy plus FA always diverges.” No. The deadly triad needs all three: FA, bootstrapping, off-policy data. Off-policy Monte Carlo (no bootstrapping) with FA converges. Off-policy bootstrapping with tabular (no FA) converges. Tabular plus bootstrapping plus off-policy (Q-learning) converges. Mix all three and the contraction argument breaks — but breaking is possible, not necessary. DQN runs the deadly triad daily and mostly works.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. The feature span is a wall. Your estimate \hat v_w can never leave it; the best you can hope for is the projection of the truth onto that wall.
  2. On-policy, the round-trip shrinks; off-policy, it can grow. The projection is always non-expansive, but the Bellman operator is only a contraction in the on-policy norm. Match the weighting to the policy and convergence follows; mismatch them and divergence becomes possible.
  3. MSPBE is the objective that knows about the wall. Semi-gradient TD secretly descends MSPBE — that’s what its fixed point is. The next lesson builds the algorithm that actually descends MSPBE by honest gradient.

Where this matters

AI / ML. Every modern value-based algorithm — DQN, Double-DQN, Rainbow, SAC’s critic — is implicitly minimising MSPBE-ish objectives in feature spaces defined by neural networks. The deadly-triad reasoning above is why those algorithms need engineering tricks (target networks, replay buffers, double Q) to stay stable: they live in the regime where the clean contraction argument no longer applies.

Research. The projected Bellman operator is the central object of approximate dynamic programming theory. Bertsekas’s two-volume Dynamic Programming and Optimal Control (2017) develops convergence theorems for ADP algorithms by analysing fixed points of \Pi T under various projections — including non-Euclidean ones (averagers, aggregators). Sutton & Barto Chapter 11 also organises the deadly-triad discussion around MSPBE.
