Ch 3 — Cylinder and Cone (Round Solids)

Unroll the lateral surface, count the volume — and meet Archimedes’ three friends

Pre-flight check

  1. Do you know the area of a circle is \pi r^2 and its circumference is 2 \pi r? See t1.area-perimeter-volume.
  2. Can you compute the volume of a prism as \text{base area} \times \text{height}?
  3. Are you comfortable with the formula \text{volume of pyramid} = \tfrac{1}{3} \times \text{base} \times \text{height}? See cbse9.surface-areas-and-volumes.
  4. Do you accept that a cylinder is a “circular prism” and a cone is a “circular pyramid” — i.e. the limits of regular prisms / pyramids as the number of sides n \to \infty?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

A cylinder. Slit it from top to bottom along one vertical line and unroll the curved surface flat. What shape do you get?

viewof radius = Inputs.range([0.5, 3], {value: 1.5, step: 0.1, label: "Radius r"})
viewof height = Inputs.range([0.5, 4], {value: 2.5, step: 0.1, label: "Height h"})
viewof unroll = Inputs.range([0, 1], {value: 0.6, step: 0.05, label: "Unroll progress"})
{
  const w = 520, h = 280, padX = 30, padY = 30;
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([0, 14]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-2, 5]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  const C = 2 * Math.PI * radius;
  const u = unroll;

  // Left: 3D-ish cylinder (ellipse top + bottom + sides)
  const cx = 2.5;
  // ellipse top at y=height
  for (const y of [0, height]) {
    svg.append("ellipse")
      .attr("cx", xS(cx)).attr("cy", yS(y))
      .attr("rx", xS(radius) - xS(0))
      .attr("ry", (xS(radius) - xS(0)) * 0.3)
      .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 1.5);
  }
  // sides
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(cx - radius)).attr("x2", xS(cx - radius))
    .attr("y1", yS(0)).attr("y2", yS(height))
    .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 1.5);
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", xS(cx + radius)).attr("x2", xS(cx + radius))
    .attr("y1", yS(0)).attr("y2", yS(height))
    .attr("stroke", "#26a").attr("stroke-width", 1.5);

  // Right: unrolled rectangle, fading in with u, drawn at x ≈ 7..(7 + C)
  const rx = 7;
  // partial unroll: width grows from 0 to C as u: 0..1
  const wu = u * C;
  svg.append("rect")
    .attr("x", xS(rx)).attr("y", yS(height))
    .attr("width", xS(rx + wu) - xS(rx))
    .attr("height", yS(0) - yS(height))
    .attr("fill", "#a26").attr("fill-opacity", 0.22)
    .attr("stroke", "#a26").attr("stroke-width", 1.5);

  // labels
  svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(cx)).attr("y", yS(height) - 12)
    .attr("font-size", 11).attr("text-anchor", "middle")
    .text(`cylinder: r = ${radius.toFixed(2)}, h = ${height.toFixed(2)}`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(rx + 0.1)).attr("y", yS(height) - 6)
    .attr("font-size", 11)
    .text(`unrolled rectangle:  width = 2πr = ${C.toFixed(2)},  height = h`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", xS(rx)).attr("y", yS(0) + 14)
    .attr("font-size", 11)
    .text(`lateral area = 2πr · h = ${(C * height).toFixed(2)}`);

  // hint arrow on partial unroll
  if (u > 0 && u < 1) {
    svg.append("path")
      .attr("d", `M ${xS(cx + radius + 0.2)} ${yS(height/2)} L ${xS(rx - 0.1)} ${yS(height/2)}`)
      .attr("stroke", "#888").attr("stroke-width", 1)
      .attr("marker-end", null).attr("fill", "none");
    svg.append("text").attr("x", xS((cx + radius + rx) / 2)).attr("y", yS(height/2) - 4)
      .attr("font-size", 10).attr("text-anchor", "middle").text("unroll →");
  }

  return svg.node();
}

The lateral surface of a cylinder is a rectangle of width 2\pi r (the circumference) and height h. Total lateral area = 2 \pi r h.

Wonder

  1. We just unrolled the cylinder. What about a cone — what shape do you get if you slit it from apex to base and unroll?
  2. A cylinder and a cone share the same circular base and the same height. By how much do their volumes differ? Eyeball it first; the answer is famously simple.
  3. Drop a sphere of radius r inside a cylinder that just contains it (radius r, height 2r). What fraction of the cylinder does the sphere fill?
  4. Can you imagine slicing a cone parallel to its base? At height y from the apex, what is the radius of the cross-section?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. A circular sector (pie-slice). (2) The cone is exactly one-third of the cylinder — the same factor that turned a prism into a pyramid. (3) Two-thirds — Archimedes’ favourite theorem.
  2. Linearly: at height y the radius is \frac{y}{h} r. This is why cones scale by y^3 in volume.

Discover

Problem 1 — The lateral surface of a cylinder

Show that the lateral surface area of a cylinder of base radius r and height h is 2 \pi r h.

Hint 1 — a nudge Cut the cylinder along one vertical line and unroll it.
Full solution The unrolled surface is a rectangle. Its width equals the circumference of the base (2 \pi r); its height equals h. So the lateral area is 2 \pi r \cdot h. Total surface (including two circular caps) is 2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^2. (Planimetry-style proof — unroll, measure, multiply.)

Problem 2 — Volume of a cylinder

Show that the volume of a cylinder of base radius r and height h is \pi r^2 h.

Hint 1 — a nudge Approximate the cylinder by a regular n-gon prism, then take n \to \infty.
Full solution For each n \ge 3, inscribe a regular n-gon in the base circle. The resulting prism has volume A_n \cdot h, where A_n is the area of the inscribed n-gon. As n \to \infty, A_n \to \pi r^2 (definition of \pi from area). So the cylinder’s volume — sandwiched between inscribed and circumscribed prisms — is \pi r^2 \cdot h. QED.

Problem 3 — Lateral surface of a cone

A cone has base radius r and slant height \ell (the distance from apex to base-circle along the surface). Unroll its lateral surface. What shape and what dimensions?

Hint 1 — a nudge Every point on the surface is at distance \ell from the apex — so the unrolled shape is part of a circle of radius \ell.
Full solution The unrolled shape is a circular sector of radius \ell. Its arc length must equal the base circumference 2 \pi r (the rim). A full circle of radius \ell has circumference 2 \pi \ell, so the sector is the fraction \frac{2 \pi r}{2 \pi \ell} = \frac{r}{\ell} of the disc. The disc has area \pi \ell^2, so the sector — the lateral area of the cone — is \pi \ell^2 \cdot \frac{r}{\ell} = \pi r \ell.

Problem 4 — Volume of a cone

Show that the volume of a cone of base radius r and height h is \dfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h.

Hint 1 — a nudge The cone is the limit of regular pyramids over regular n-gons. A pyramid has volume \tfrac{1}{3} \cdot \text{base} \cdot \text{height}.
Full solution Inscribe a regular n-gon in the base; build a regular pyramid over it sharing the cone’s apex. Its volume is \tfrac{1}{3} A_n \cdot h. As n \to \infty, A_n \to \pi r^2, so the cone’s volume is \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 \cdot h. Same trick as Problem 2 — inscribe, increase n, take the limit. Kiselev uses the prism / pyramid relation as the load-bearing axiom; the cone / cylinder relation falls out.

Problem 5 — Cone fits inside cylinder

Place a cone (base radius r, height h) inside a cylinder of the same base and height, sharing the base. What fraction of the cylinder does the cone occupy?

Full solution \dfrac{\frac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h}{\pi r^2 h} = \dfrac{1}{3}. One third. (The same one-third factor that took prism to pyramid.)

Problem 6 — Archimedes’ theorem (read-off)

Inside a cylinder of radius r and height 2r (so it just contains a sphere of radius r), place the sphere and a double cone (two cones apex-to-apex, each of height r, sharing the cylinder’s axis). Compute the three volumes and check Archimedes’ famous relation cylinder : sphere : double cone = 3 : 2 : 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Cylinder volume: \pi r^2 \cdot 2r = 2 \pi r^3. Sphere volume: take \tfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3 as known. Double cone: two cones of base r and height r.
Full solution Cylinder: 2 \pi r^3. Sphere: \tfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3. Double cone: 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 \cdot r = \tfrac{2}{3} \pi r^3. Ratios (divide each by \tfrac{2}{3} \pi r^3): \quad \dfrac{2}{2/3} : \dfrac{4/3}{2/3} : \dfrac{2/3}{2/3} = 3 : 2 : 1. Archimedes asked for this ratio to be carved on his tombstone, and it was.

Archimedes of Syracuse (~250 BCE) considered the cylinder–sphere ratio of Problem 6 — the simple 3 : 2 : 1 — his greatest result. In his treatise On the Sphere and the Cylinder, he proved both the volume and the surface formulas via the method of exhaustion (the ancestor of integral calculus). He requested that a sphere inscribed in a cylinder be carved on his tombstone. A century and a half later, the Roman politician Cicero, serving as quaestor in Sicily, hunted through the overgrown cemetery at Syracuse and found the gravestone — identifying it by the carved figure. The geometry had outlived the inscription.

A cylinder is a limit of regular prisms and a cone is a limit of regular pyramids — so the polygon-area limit that defined \pi r^2 in 2D becomes the volume formulas \pi r^2 h and \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h in 3D, for free. The same one-third factor that turned a parallelogram into a triangle turns a cylinder into a cone.

Name

A cylinder of base radius r and height h has:

\text{lateral area} = 2 \pi r h, \quad \text{total surface} = 2 \pi r h + 2 \pi r^2, \quad \text{volume} = \pi r^2 h.

A cone of base radius r, height h, slant height \ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2} has:

\text{lateral area} = \pi r \ell, \quad \text{total surface} = \pi r \ell + \pi r^2, \quad \text{volume} = \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h.

A sphere of radius r has:

\text{surface area} = 4 \pi r^2, \quad \text{volume} = \tfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3.

The Archimedes relation: for the smallest enclosing cylinder of a sphere of radius r (and the inscribed double cone of the same height), the volumes are in the ratio 3 : 2 : 1.

A verse:

Unroll the cylinder — a rectangle, two pi r and h. Unroll the cone — a sector, slant by arc, the rim’s path. One-third for the cone, like one-third for the pyramid before. And cylinder, sphere, cone — three, two, one — Archimedes’ law.

Apply

Worked example

A cone has base radius 6 cm and slant height 10 cm. Find its volume and total surface area.

Slant height \ell = 10, base radius r = 6, so the height is h = \sqrt{\ell^2 - r^2} = \sqrt{100 - 36} = \sqrt{64} = 8 cm.

Volume: V = \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h = \tfrac{1}{3} \pi \cdot 36 \cdot 8 = 96 \pi \approx 301.6 cm³.

Lateral area: \pi r \ell = 60 \pi. Plus base \pi r^2 = 36 \pi.

Total surface: 96 \pi \approx 301.6 cm². Volume: 96 \pi \approx 301.6 cm³.

(A pleasing accident: same number, same units of \pi — but different physical quantities.)

Practice — Warm

W1. A cylindrical can has base radius 3 cm and height 10 cm. Find its volume and lateral area.

Full solution V = \pi \cdot 9 \cdot 10 = 90 \pi cm³. Lateral = 2 \pi \cdot 3 \cdot 10 = 60 \pi cm².

W2. A cone has volume 48 \pi cm³ and base radius 4 cm. Find its height.

Full solution \tfrac{1}{3} \pi \cdot 16 \cdot h = 48 \pi, so h = 9 cm.

W3. A sphere has volume \tfrac{32}{3} \pi cm³. Find its radius.

Full solution \tfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3 = \tfrac{32}{3} \pi, so r^3 = 8, r = 2 cm.

Practice — Working

P1. A cone is cut by a plane parallel to its base, halfway up. What fraction of the original cone’s volume is the upper small cone?

Hint 1 — a nudge Linear scale halves; volume scales by the cube.
Full solution The upper cone is similar to the whole, with linear ratio \tfrac{1}{2}. So volume ratio = (\tfrac{1}{2})^3 = \tfrac{1}{8}. The frustum below has \tfrac{7}{8} of the original volume.

P2. A solid is composed of a cylinder (r = 3, h = 7) with a hemisphere on top (r = 3). Find its total volume.

Hint 1 — a nudge Volume = cylinder + half-sphere.
Full solution Cylinder = \pi \cdot 9 \cdot 7 = 63 \pi. Hemisphere = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{4}{3} \pi \cdot 27 = 18 \pi. Total: 81 \pi cubic units.

P3. An ice-cream cone (open cone, no base) has slant height 12 cm and lateral area 60 \pi cm². Find its base radius.

Full solution \pi r \ell = 60 \pi with \ell = 12, so r = 5 cm.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A cylinder, a sphere, and a cone share base circle (radius r) and height h where h = 2r. Show that the sum of the volumes of the cone and the sphere equals the cylinder’s volume.

Hint 1 — a nudge This is Problem 6 in disguise. Add cone + sphere; compare with cylinder.
Full solution Cone (height 2r): \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 \cdot 2r = \tfrac{2}{3} \pi r^3. Sphere: \tfrac{4}{3} \pi r^3. Sum = 2 \pi r^3 = cylinder. QED. (This is the equivalent statement of Archimedes’ 3:2:1 — Kiselev gives both.)

S2. A right circular cone has the property that its lateral surface area equals the area of its base. Show that its slant height is exactly the radius — and conclude that no such non-trivial cone exists (because then the height would be zero, i.e. the cone is degenerate).

Hint 1 — a nudge \pi r \ell = \pi r^2, so \ell = r. But \ell^2 = r^2 + h^2.
Full solution \pi r \ell = \pi r^2 \Rightarrow \ell = r (assuming r > 0). Then \ell^2 = r^2 + h^2 gives h^2 = 0, so h = 0. The “cone” collapses to a flat disc. Boundary case; no genuine cone satisfies the condition.

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Stereometry, §§430–460.

K1. A cone of slant height 10 and base radius 6 is unrolled flat into a sector. Find the angle at the apex of the unrolled sector.

Hint 1 — a nudge Sector arc = base circumference = 2\pi r. Sector full circle of radius \ell has circumference 2\pi\ell. The fraction is the angle as a fraction of 2\pi (or 360°).
Full solution Fraction = \dfrac{r}{\ell} = \dfrac{6}{10} = 0.6. So the angle is 0.6 \cdot 360° = 216°. (Or in radians, 0.6 \cdot 2\pi = 1.2\pi.)

K2. A sphere is inscribed in a cone whose axial cross-section is an equilateral triangle of side 2a. Express the sphere’s radius in terms of a.

Hint 1 — a nudge The axial cross-section of the inscribed sphere is the incircle of the equilateral triangle.
Full solution The incircle of an equilateral triangle of side s has radius \dfrac{s}{2\sqrt 3} = \dfrac{s\sqrt 3}{6}. With s = 2a, r_{\text{sphere}} = \dfrac{a\sqrt 3}{3} = \dfrac{a}{\sqrt 3}.

K3. Two right circular cones share the same axis and apex. The inner cone has base radius r_1 and height h_1; the outer cone has base radius r_2 > r_1 and height h_2 > h_1. Show that the frustum-like region between them — bounded by the inner cone’s lateral surface, the outer cone’s lateral surface, and the two base discs at heights h_1 and h_2 from the apex — has volume

V = \tfrac{\pi}{3}\bigl(r_2^2 h_2 - r_1^2 h_1\bigr).

Hint 1 — a nudge Subtract one cone’s volume from the other.
Full solution V = V_{\text{outer cone}} - V_{\text{inner cone}} = \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r_2^2 h_2 - \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r_1^2 h_1 = \tfrac{\pi}{3}(r_2^2 h_2 - r_1^2 h_1). (Kiselev uses this kind of difference-of-cones to derive the volume of a conical frustum; this is the simpler co-axial version.)

Source pointer. Adapted from Givental, Kiselev’s Geometry, Book II — Stereometry, §§430–460 (Sumizdat 2008). Free PDF on cimat.mx. K3 is the cleanest entry into Kiselev’s frustum chapter — adapt freely; do not paste Givental’s prose.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. The lateral surface of a cylinder is a rectangle — unroll it — of size 2\pi r \times h; its volume is \pi r^2 h.
  2. The lateral surface of a cone is a circular sector; its volume is \tfrac{1}{3} \pi r^2 h — the same one-third factor as in pyramids.
  3. Archimedes’ 3 : 2 : 1. Cylinder, sphere, double cone in the smallest enclosing cylinder — volumes split as 3 : 2 : 1. The fact carved on his tombstone.

Common confusions

  1. “Slant height equals height.” No. Height is the perpendicular from apex to base; slant height is the distance along the surface, \ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}. Use \ell for surface area, h for volume.
  2. “The lateral area of a cylinder is \pi r h.” Half the truth. The full circumference is 2 \pi r, so lateral area = 2 \pi r h. The factor of 2 comes from going around the circle.
  3. “Volume scales linearly with size.” It does not. Doubling every linear dimension multiplies volume by 2^3 = 8 — see Problem P1. This is why a giant ant cannot exist (its weight scales by 8, its leg cross-section only by 4).

Where this matters

Game development. Cone, cylinder, capsule, and sphere are the canonical collision primitives in physics engines. The volume and surface formulas here are exactly the ones used to compute mass, inertia tensors, and intersection volumes in real-time. The unroll-into-rectangle trick is also how UV mapping a cylindrical texture works.

Research. The “inscribe a polygon, take n \to \infty” argument is Eudoxus’ method of exhaustion, the historical seed of integral calculus. Archimedes’ 3:2:1 is the first non-trivial theorem proved this way — and the line from this problem to the fundamental theorem of calculus is short.
