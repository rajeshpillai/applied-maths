Ch 1 — Lines and Planes in Space

Parallel, intersecting, skew — and the three positions a line can take with a plane

Pre-flight check

  1. Are the parallel-line theorems automatic for you in 2D? See kg1.parallels.
  2. Can you state the perpendicular-bisector locus theorem from kg1.perpendiculars?
  3. Can you visualise three points in space that are not on a single line — and the unique plane they determine?
  4. Are you comfortable with the everyday objects: a wall (plane), the floor (plane), the line where they meet (their intersection)?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

Two lines in space. In the plane, two distinct lines either meet at a point or are parallel. In space, there is a third possibility: they neither meet nor are parallel. Such lines are called skew.

viewof config = Inputs.radio(

  {value: "intersecting (meet at a point)", label: "Configuration:"}
)
{
  // simple "isometric" sketch of a cube and two highlighted lines
  const w = 460, h = 320, padX = 40, padY = 40;
  const xS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 7]).range([padX, w - padX]);
  const yS = d3.scaleLinear().domain([-1, 5]).range([h - padY, padY]);
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  // cube edges (8 vertices, isometric projection)
  const project = (x, y, z) => [x + 0.5 * y, z + 0.4 * y];
  const V = [
    [0,0,0],[3,0,0],[3,3,0],[0,3,0],
    [0,0,3],[3,0,3],[3,3,3],[0,3,3]
  ];
  const P = V.map(([x,y,z]) => project(x, y, z));
  const edges = [
    [0,1],[1,2],[2,3],[3,0],
    [4,5],[5,6],[6,7],[7,4],
    [0,4],[1,5],[2,6],[3,7]
  ];
  for (const [a,b] of edges) {
    svg.append("line")
      .attr("x1", xS(P[a][0])).attr("y1", yS(P[a][1]))
      .attr("x2", xS(P[b][0])).attr("y2", yS(P[b][1]))
      .attr("stroke", "#888").attr("stroke-width", 1);
  }

  // highlight two lines depending on config
  const drawHi = (a, b, c) => {
    svg.append("line")
      .attr("x1", xS(P[a][0])).attr("y1", yS(P[a][1]))
      .attr("x2", xS(P[b][0])).attr("y2", yS(P[b][1]))
      .attr("stroke", c).attr("stroke-width", 3);
  };

  if (config.startsWith("intersecting")) {
    // edges that share a vertex: 0-1 and 0-3 (both at vertex 0)
    drawHi(0, 1, "#26a");
    drawHi(0, 3, "#a26");
    svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
      .attr("font-size", 12).text("Two edges meeting at one vertex — intersecting lines.");
  } else if (config.startsWith("parallel")) {
    // 0-1 and 3-2 are parallel (opposite edges of the bottom face)
    drawHi(0, 1, "#26a");
    drawHi(3, 2, "#a26");
    svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
      .attr("font-size", 12).text("Two opposite edges of one face — parallel lines.");

    drawHi(0, 1, "#26a");
    drawHi(6, 7, "#a26");
    svg.append("text").attr("x", padX).attr("y", padY - 10)
      .attr("font-size", 12).text("Bottom-front edge and top-back edge — skew lines (different directions, no meeting).");
  }

  return svg.node();

The new word is skew. Two skew lines have no meeting point and yet are not parallel — they slip past each other in space.

Wonder

  1. In the plane, two distinct lines must meet or be parallel. In space, that is no longer true. Why does the third option (skew) exist?
  2. A point and a line not through the point — how many planes contain both?
  3. A line is parallel to a plane if it never meets the plane. What is the intersection of a non-parallel line with a plane?
  4. A line is perpendicular to a plane if it makes a right angle with every line in the plane through the foot. Is checking one line enough? (Trick question.)
TipWhat you might notice
  1. Two lines determine a plane only if they meet or are parallel. Skew lines force a 3D-only configuration. (2) Exactly one plane contains a given line and a point not on it.
  2. Either a single point or the whole line (if the line lies in the plane). (4) Checking one line is not enough — but checking two distinct lines through the foot is. The proof is one of the prettiest in Stereometry.

Discover

Problem 1 — Three points determine a plane

Three points A, B, C in space, not collinear, determine exactly one plane.

Full solution This is Stereometry’s first axiom (Planimetry §3 generalised): through any three non-collinear points there passes exactly one plane. Existence and uniqueness are taken as foundational. (A plane is essentially a 2D Euclidean copy embedded in 3D.) (Stereometry §374.)

Problem 2 — Three configurations of two lines

Show that any two distinct lines in space fall into exactly one of three classes: (a) intersecting (meet at a point), (b) parallel (same direction, no meeting, lie in a common plane), (c) skew (no meeting, no common plane).

Hint 1 — a nudge Two lines lie in a common plane iff they are intersecting or parallel. The contrapositive is skew = no common plane = no meeting.
Full solution

Coplanar case. If the two lines are in a common plane, they are either parallel or intersecting (the planar dichotomy from Planimetry).

Non-coplanar case. If they share no plane, they cannot meet — since a single intersection point + the rest of either line + non-trivial extension forces both to lie in the plane through that point and any other two points (one from each line), contradiction. And they cannot be parallel — parallel lines lie in a common plane by definition.

So two distinct lines are exactly one of: intersecting, parallel, or skew. QED. (Stereometry §378.)

Problem 3 — Three positions of a line and a plane

Classify the possible relationships between a line \ell and a plane \pi.

Full solution

Three cases: - \ell lies in \pi (every point of \ell is in \pi). - \ell intersects \pi at exactly one point. - \ell is parallel to \pi (no intersection — \ell and \pi have no common point).

There are no other cases: a line either intersects the plane in the empty set (parallel), one point, or has at least two points in the plane (in which case the whole line is in the plane, since two points determine a line within a plane). (Stereometry §380.)

Problem 4 — Line perpendicular to a plane

Define: a line \ell is perpendicular to a plane \pi at a point F \in \ell \cap \pi if \ell is perpendicular to every line in \pi through F. Show that this is equivalent to: \ell is perpendicular to two distinct lines in \pi through F.

Hint 1 — a nudge If \ell is perpendicular to two distinct lines m_1, m_2 in \pi through F, then any third line m in \pi through F can be expressed as a “linear combination” of m_1 and m_2 — and the perpendicularity to \ell is preserved by this combination.
Full solution

The harder direction: two perpendiculars suffice. Let \ell be perpendicular at F to two distinct lines m_1, m_2 \subset \pi through F. Take any third line m in \pi through F.

Choose points A_1 \in m_1, A_2 \in m_2 at unit distance from F. Any point X on m at unit distance from F has the form X = a A_1 + b A_2 with a^2 + b^2 = 1 (parameterising the unit circle in \pi in the basis A_1, A_2).

Take any point P on \ell, P \ne F. Use the three-point perpendicular formula (a coordinate / vector argument): |PX|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FX|^2 iff \angle PFX = 90°. The hypothesis \angle PFA_1 = \angle PFA_2 = 90° gives |PA_i|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FA_i|^2 for i = 1, 2. By a careful expansion (treating X as a linear combination), one finds |PX|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FX|^2, i.e. \ell \perp m at F. Since m was arbitrary, \ell is perpendicular to every line through F in \pi — and so \ell \perp \pi. QED. (Stereometry §389 — the perpendicular-line- to-plane theorem.)

Problem 5 — Existence and uniqueness of perpendicular line through a point

Through a given point P, there exists exactly one line perpendicular to a given plane \pi.

Hint 1 — a nudge

If P \in \pi: erect two perpendiculars in \pi to two intersecting reference lines through P in \pi, then use Problem 4 in reverse.

If P \notin \pi: drop a perpendicular from P to \pi — existence by reflection symmetry; uniqueness by an angle-sum argument analogous to Planimetry §57.
Full solution

Existence (point on the plane). Let P \in \pi. Choose two distinct lines m_1, m_2 \subset \pi through P. Erect a line \ell_1 in the plane through P and m_1 (the “vertical plane” above m_1) perpendicular to m_1. Similarly \ell_2 in the plane through P and m_2 perpendicular to m_2. Now \ell_1 and \ell_2 are two distinct lines through P in the “vertical pencil” above P. By a standard argument (the perpendicular plane through P), there is a unique line \ell through P perpendicular to both m_1 and m_2 in \pi — and by Problem 4 this \ell is perpendicular to \pi.

Uniqueness. Suppose two distinct perpendiculars \ell_1, \ell_2 existed through P. Each is perpendicular to every line in \pi through P. The plane through \ell_1 and \ell_2 would intersect \pi in some line m through P, and both \ell_1 and \ell_2 would be perpendicular to m in this plane through \ell_1, \ell_2 — so \ell_1 = \ell_2 (uniqueness of the planar perpendicular). (Stereometry §391.)

The case P \notin \pi is similar; one drops a perpendicular foot. QED.

Problem 6 — The perpendicular is the shortest

The perpendicular from a point P to a plane \pi has the shortest length among all segments from P to points in \pi.

Hint 1 — a nudge This is exactly the planar shortest-segment theorem (Ch 3 of Planimetry) lifted to 3D — the same argument works on the right triangle formed by P, the perpendicular foot F, and any other point Q \in \pi.
Full solution Let F be the foot of the perpendicular from P to \pi, and Q \in \pi any other point. The triangle \triangle PFQ has \angle PFQ = 90° (since \ell \perp \pi implies \ell is perpendicular to every line in \pi through F, including line FQ). By the planar Pythagoras: |PQ|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FQ|^2 > |PF|^2. So |PQ| > |PF|. The perpendicular minimises. (Stereometry §392.)

Problem 7 — Dihedral angles

Two half-planes sharing a common edge \ell form a dihedral angle. Its measure is the planar angle one sees by intersecting both half-planes with a plane perpendicular to \ell. Show that this measure does not depend on the choice of the perpendicular plane.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two perpendicular cross-sections of the dihedral angle are parallel to each other (both perpendicular to \ell). The two planar angles are corresponding under that parallelism.
Full solution Take two perpendicular planes \sigma_1 and \sigma_2, both perpendicular to \ell at points F_1, F_2 respectively. The two half-planes’ intersections with \sigma_1 are two rays out of F_1 at some angle \theta_1; similarly \theta_2 at F_2. By construction, \sigma_1 \parallel \sigma_2 (both perpendicular to \ell). Each pair of rays in \sigma_1 and \sigma_2 are translations of each other along \ell — so \theta_1 = \theta_2. QED. (Stereometry §400 — well-defined dihedral measure.)

Eratosthenes of Cyrene (~240 BCE), librarian at Alexandria, estimated the Earth’s circumference using only the geometry of two lines in space. He noted that on the summer solstice the Sun was directly overhead at Syene (modern Aswan) — a vertical stick cast no shadow — while at Alexandria, ~800 km north, a stick cast a shadow corresponding to about 7.2° — one-fiftieth of a full turn. So the full circumference was about 50 \times 800 = 40\,000 km. The modern value is 40\,075 km. The whole calculation hinges on the parallel-lines-in-space and skew-line geometry of this chapter.

The plane has two relationships between two lines: meet, or parallel. Space adds a third — skew. And the right-angle condition between a line and a plane needs only two perpendiculars in the plane to be certified — Kiselev’s two-perpendicular theorem — the synthetic ancestor of the linear-algebra fact that orthogonality to a spanning set is orthogonality to the whole subspace.

Name

Definitions (Stereometry §§373–415).

  • Plane: a flat 2D surface in 3D space; uniquely determined by three non-collinear points.
  • Two lines in space: intersecting, parallel, or skew (none of the above).
  • Line and plane: the line lies in the plane, intersects it at one point, or is parallel to the plane.
  • Two planes: intersect in a line, or are parallel.
  • Line perpendicular to plane (\ell \perp \pi): \ell is perpendicular to every line in \pi through the foot. Equivalently, perpendicular to two distinct lines in \pi through the foot (the two-perpendiculars theorem).
  • Dihedral angle: the figure formed by two half-planes sharing a common edge. Its measure is the planar angle of any cross-section perpendicular to the edge.

Theorems.

  1. Three non-collinear points uniquely determine a plane.
  2. Two lines in space are intersecting, parallel, or skew — no other option.
  3. A line perpendicular to two distinct lines in a plane (through their foot) is perpendicular to every line in the plane through the foot.
  4. Through any point in space, there is a unique line perpendicular to a given plane.
  5. The perpendicular from a point to a plane is the shortest segment from the point to the plane.
  6. The dihedral-angle measure is well-defined: independent of the perpendicular cross-section chosen.

A verse:

In the plane, lines meet or run parallel — that’s all. In space, a third path appears — skew, two strangers passing. Perpendicular to two lines through one foot? Perpendicular to all. And the perpendicular is shortest, like its planar elder, never lapsing.

Apply

Worked example

A line \ell pierces a plane \pi at a point F. Two lines m_1, m_2 \subset \pi both pass through F and are perpendicular to \ell. Is \ell necessarily perpendicular to \pi?

If m_1 and m_2 are distinct, then yes — by the two-perpendicular theorem (Problem 4). If they are the same line, no — \ell is perpendicular only to that one line in \pi, and to many other choices of one-line-in-\pi it might not be perpendicular.

Conclusion: yes, provided m_1 \ne m_2.

Practice — Warm

W1. Two lines in space pass through the same point and are not parallel. Are they skew?

Full solution No. They meet at that point, so they are intersecting, not skew. Skew lines never meet.

W2. Through a given point in space and a given plane, how many perpendiculars to the plane through that point exist?

Full solution Exactly one (Problem 5). The point can be on the plane or off it; either way, one perpendicular.

W3. Two parallel planes are cut by a third plane. The intersections are two lines. What is their relationship?

Full solution They are parallel. (Each lies in the cutting plane and is parallel to the same direction inherited from the parallel planes.)

Practice — Working

P1. Show that if a line is perpendicular to a plane, then any plane containing that line is perpendicular to the original plane.

Hint 1 — a nudge Two planes are perpendicular iff their dihedral angle is 90°. Construct the perpendicular-cross-section.
Full solution Let \ell \perp \pi at F, and let \sigma be any plane containing \ell. The dihedral angle between \sigma and \pi is measured by intersecting both with a plane perpendicular to their common edge (which is \sigma \cap \pi, a line through F). The cross-section through F perpendicular to this edge contains \ell (since \ell \perp \pi \perp edge means \ell is perpendicular to the edge — wait, let me check: \ell perpendicular to \pi means \ell is perpendicular to the edge, which lies in \pi. So \ell is perpendicular to the edge of the dihedral angle, and the cross-section perpendicular to that edge through F contains \ell). The cross-section also intersects \pi in a line perpendicular to the edge (in \pi). The angle between \ell and this line in \pi is — by the perpendicularity \ell \perp \pi — exactly 90°. So the dihedral angle is 90°, i.e. \sigma \perp \pi. QED. (Stereometry §405.)

P2. Show that two planes both perpendicular to a third plane need not be parallel.

Hint 1 — a nudge Look at two walls of a room — they are both perpendicular to the floor, but they are perpendicular to each other, not parallel.
Full solution Consider three mutually perpendicular planes (walls and floor of a room). Two of the walls are both perpendicular to the floor, but they are perpendicular to each other, not parallel. Hence two planes perpendicular to a third can be perpendicular, parallel, or even neither (intersecting at any angle other than 0° or 90°).

P3. Show that the distance from a point to a plane is the length of the perpendicular from the point to the plane (and is well-defined — independent of any other configuration).

Full solution Direct application of Problem 6 (the perpendicular minimises) and Problem 5 (the perpendicular is unique). The minimum is achieved once, at the perpendicular foot.

Practice — Stretch

S1. (The three-perpendiculars theorem.) A point P off a plane \pi has perpendicular foot F in \pi. From F, drop a perpendicular within \pi to a line \ell \subset \pi, foot G. Show that PG \perp \ell — i.e. the line from P to the foot of the perpendicular within \pi is also perpendicular to \ell.

Hint 1 — a nudge PF \perp \pi means PF \perp \ell. FG \perp \ell by construction. So \ell is perpendicular to two lines PF, FG through G in the plane PFG — and thus perpendicular to every line in that plane through G, in particular PG.
Full solution PF \perp \pi ⇒ PF \perp \ell (since \ell \subset \pi). FG \perp \ell by construction. Hence \ell is perpendicular to both PF and FG, two lines in plane PFG meeting at G. By Problem 4 (two-perpendiculars), \ell \perp plane PFG, hence \ell \perp PG (any line through G in plane PFG). QED. (Stereometry §394 — the three-perpendiculars theorem, the geometry-engine of every planar-shadow problem.)

S2. A regular tetrahedron ABCD has all four faces equilateral triangles of side s. Find the distance from any vertex to the opposite face.

Hint 1 — a nudge Drop the perpendicular from A to the centroid of \triangle BCD. Use the equilateral-triangle centroid (at distance s/\sqrt 3 from each vertex) and Pythagoras.
Full solution The centroid of equilateral \triangle BCD is at distance \dfrac{s}{\sqrt 3} from each of B, C, D. The apex A has |AB| = s. Pythagoras: h^2 + \left(\dfrac{s}{\sqrt 3}\right)^2 = s^2, so h^2 = s^2 - \dfrac{s^2}{3} = \dfrac{2 s^2}{3}, hence h = s \sqrt{\dfrac{2}{3}} = \dfrac{s \sqrt 6}{3}. (A canonical 3D-Pythagoras computation; Kiselev §403.)

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Stereometry, §§385–410.

K1. Through a point P off a plane \pi, all the segments to points on a circle in \pi have the same length. Show that the perpendicular from P to \pi lands on the centre of that circle.

Hint 1 — a nudge The set of points in \pi equidistant from P is the locus of the foot F’s perpendicular cone — which intersects \pi in a circle centred at F.
Full solution Let F be the foot of the perpendicular from P to \pi. For any point Q \in \pi, |PQ|^2 = |PF|^2 + |FQ|^2. So |PQ| is a monotonic function of |FQ|. The set ${Q : |PQ| = $ constant\} is therefore ${Q : |FQ| = $ constant’\} — a circle in \pi centred at F. QED. (Stereometry §394.)

K2. Two skew lines \ell_1, \ell_2 in space have a unique common perpendicular — a single line meeting both at right angles. Sketch this and explain why it must exist and be unique.

Full solution

Existence. Through \ell_1 pass a plane \pi_1 parallel to \ell_2 (this exists: take any plane containing \ell_1 and a line through any point of \ell_1 parallel to \ell_2). \ell_2 projects onto a line \ell_2' in \pi_1 parallel to \ell_2. The lines \ell_1 and \ell_2' in \pi_1 either meet at a point X or are parallel; if parallel, \ell_1 and \ell_2 are parallel (contradiction). So they meet at X. The perpendicular to \pi_1 at X — call it m — is perpendicular to \ell_1 (since \ell_1 \subset \pi_1) and perpendicular to \ell_2', hence (by translation argument) to \ell_2.

Uniqueness. Two common perpendiculars would force \ell_1 and \ell_2 to lie in the plane of those two perpendiculars, contradicting “skew”. (Stereometry §397 — the common-perpendicular theorem.)

K3. A point P in space is equidistant from three given points A, B, C. Show that P lies on the perpendicular line through the circumcentre of \triangle ABC (perpendicular to the plane ABC).

Hint 1 — a nudge |PA| = |PB| and |PA| = |PC| — interpret these as planar perpendicular-bisector loci, in a 3D-perpendicular-plane sense.
Full solution |PA| = |PB|: the locus is the perpendicular plane through the midpoint of AB — i.e. the plane perpendicular to AB through its midpoint M_{AB}. Similarly |PA| = |PC| gives the perpendicular plane through M_{AC}. Both planes contain the perpendicular line through the circumcentre of \triangle ABC to the plane ABC. The intersection of the two planes is exactly that perpendicular line. QED. (Stereometry §410 — the 3D circumcentre theorem, basis for the circumsphere* of a tetrahedron.)*

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. Two distinct lines in space are intersecting, parallel, or skew — three options, not two as in the plane.
  2. A line is perpendicular to a plane iff it makes a right angle with two distinct lines in the plane through the foot — not just one.
  3. The perpendicular from a point to a plane is the shortest segment from the point to the plane — the same minimisation principle as in 2D, lifted one dimension up.

Common confusions

  1. “Two non-parallel lines must meet.” True in the plane; false in space, where skew lines exist.
  2. “A line perpendicular to one line in a plane is perpendicular to the plane.” False. Need two distinct lines in the plane through the foot — the two-perpendicular theorem (Problem
    1. is about two, not one.
  3. “Perpendicular planes both contain the same perpendicular line.” No — perpendicular planes are two planes whose dihedral angle is 90°; they do not share a common perpendicular line in general.

Where this matters

Game development. Every 3D engine relies on plane equations, ray-plane intersections, and perpendicular foot calculations. Lighting (the normal vector is the perpendicular to a surface), collision (perpendicular distance from a point to a wall), and physics (resolution of forces in 3D) all use the synthetic theory you just built.

Research. The two-perpendiculars theorem (Problem 4) is the synthetic seed of linear algebra in \mathbb R^3: a vector is orthogonal to a 2D subspace iff it is orthogonal to two spanning vectors. The same statement, from synthetic to vector to inner-product space, is one continuous arc of mathematical abstraction.
