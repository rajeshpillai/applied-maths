Ch 4 — The Sphere

Archimedes’ two great theorems — surface 4\pi r^2 and volume \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3

Pre-flight check

  1. Are cylinder and cone formulas automatic for you? See kg2.cylinder-cone.
  2. Do you know that \pi is defined as the limit of regular-polygon perimeters / diameter — and that the area of a circle is \pi r^2? See kg1.areas.
  3. Are you comfortable applying Cavalieri’s principle?
  4. Can you state the Pythagorean theorem and use it on a right triangle in space?

If three yeses, proceed.

Touch

A sphere of radius R. Inscribe it inside a cylinder of base radius R and height 2R. Now consider any horizontal slice at height z above the equator. The slice through the sphere is a circle of radius \sqrt{R^2 - z^2}; the slice through the cylinder is a circle of radius R. And the slice through a cone of suitable shape is a circle of radius z.

viewof zheight = Inputs.range([-1, 1], {value: 0.5, step: 0.05, label: "Slice height z (in units of R)"})
{
  const w = 460, h = 280;
  const cx = w / 2, cy = h / 2;
  const R = 110;
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("width", w).attr("height", h)
    .style("background", "#fafafa").style("border", "1px solid #ddd");

  // cylinder outline (left)
  const cyl_x = 90;
  svg.append("rect").attr("x", cyl_x - R).attr("y", cy - R)
    .attr("width", 2 * R).attr("height", 2 * R)
    .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#26a");
  // sphere outline (centre)
  const sph_x = w / 2;
  svg.append("circle").attr("cx", sph_x).attr("cy", cy).attr("r", R)
    .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#a26");
  // cone outline (right) — apex at top, base at bottom (or vice versa); use double cone
  const cone_x = w - 90;
  svg.append("polygon")
    .attr("points", `${cone_x},${cy} ${cone_x - R},${cy - R} ${cone_x + R},${cy - R} ${cone_x},${cy} ${cone_x - R},${cy + R} ${cone_x + R},${cy + R}`)
    .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#262");

  // horizontal slice line
  const z = -zheight * R; // SVG y is inverted
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", 30).attr("x2", w - 30)
    .attr("y1", cy + z).attr("y2", cy + z)
    .attr("stroke", "#888").attr("stroke-dasharray", "3,2");

  // slice cross-sections (drawn as small circles to the right of each shape)
  // cylinder cross-section: radius R, area πR²
  // sphere cross-section: radius √(R² - z²), area π(R² - z²)
  // cone cross-section: radius |z|, area πz²
  // Sphere + cone = cylinder area? Check: π(R²-z²) + πz² = πR² ✓
  svg.append("text").attr("x", cyl_x).attr("y", cy + R + 14)
    .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("font-size", 11).attr("fill", "#26a")
    .text(`cylinder slice: π R²`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", sph_x).attr("y", cy + R + 14)
    .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("font-size", 11).attr("fill", "#a26")
    .text(`sphere slice: π(R² - z²)`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", cone_x).attr("y", cy + R + 14)
    .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("font-size", 11).attr("fill", "#262")
    .text(`cone slice: π z²`);
  svg.append("text").attr("x", w / 2).attr("y", 22)
    .attr("text-anchor", "middle").attr("font-size", 12)
    .text(`sphere slice + cone slice = cylinder slice  →  by Cavalieri, vol(sphere) + vol(double cone) = vol(cylinder).`);
  return svg.node();
}

The relation (R^2 - z^2) + z^2 = R^2 is just algebra. Cavalieri turns it into a volume identity: sphere + double cone = cylinder.

Wonder

  1. The volume of a sphere is something nice involving \pi and R. Guess the form.
  2. The surface area of a sphere is also something nice involving \pi and R. Guess that too.
  3. A cap (the piece cut off a sphere by a plane) — how does its volume depend on the depth of the cut?
  4. A zone (the band on a sphere’s surface between two parallel slicing planes) — how does its area depend on the perpendicular distance between the planes?
TipWhat you might notice
  1. V = \tfrac{4}{3} \pi R^3. (2) S = 4 \pi R^2. (3) Cap volume V_{\text{cap}} = \tfrac{\pi h^2 (3R - h)}{3}, where h is the cap height. (4) Astonishing — the area of a zone between two parallel planes is 2 \pi R \cdot h where h is the perpendicular distance between the planes — independent of where on the sphere they cut! (Archimedes’ theorem.)

Discover

Problem 1 — Volume of a sphere via Archimedes

Use the configuration in Touch (sphere inscribed in cylinder of radius R, height 2R, with a double cone of apex at the centre) and Cavalieri’s principle to show that the sphere’s volume is V = \tfrac{4}{3} \pi R^3.

Hint 1 — a nudge At every height z (with |z| \le R), the cross-section areas satisfy: (sphere) + (cone) = (cylinder).
Full solution

At height z \in [-R, R] above the equator:

  • Cylinder slice area: \pi R^2 (constant).
  • Sphere slice area: \pi (R^2 - z^2) (cross-section is a circle of radius \sqrt{R^2 - z^2}).
  • Double-cone slice area: \pi z^2 (the double cone has apex at the centre, opening to circles of radius |z| at the top and bottom).

Identity: \pi R^2 = \pi (R^2 - z^2) + \pi z^2 at every z.

By Cavalieri, \text{vol}(\text{cyl}) = \text{vol}(\text{sphere}) + \text{vol}(\text{double cone}). \pi R^2 \cdot 2R = V_{\text{sphere}} + 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{3} \pi R^2 \cdot R. 2\pi R^3 = V + \tfrac{2}{3} \pi R^3. V = 2\pi R^3 - \tfrac{2}{3} \pi R^3 = \tfrac{4}{3} \pi R^3. QED. (Stereometry §470 — Archimedes’ celebrated proof, the result on his tombstone.)

Problem 2 — Surface of a sphere via the cylinder

Show that the surface area of a sphere of radius R is the same as the lateral area of a cylinder of radius R and height 2R: namely, S = 4 \pi R^2.

Hint 1 — a nudge Archimedes’ projection theorem: the radial projection from the sphere’s axis to the inscribed cylinder is area-preserving. This is Archimedes’ surprising 2nd-century-BCE discovery.
Full solution

Archimedes’ projection theorem. Let the sphere of radius R be centred at the origin, and let the cylinder of radius R and height 2R be its inscribed companion. The radial projection from the sphere’s axis maps each sphere point (x, y, z) on the sphere to the cylinder point (x', y', z) at the same height z on the cylinder.

Key claim: this projection preserves area. To see why: consider an infinitesimal “rectangle” on the sphere at height z, of horizontal width w (along the latitude) and slant height \Delta s (along the meridian). The projection stretches the horizontal width by R / r (where r = \sqrt{R^2 - z^2} is the radius at that latitude — projecting from r out to R) and compresses the slant height by r / R (because the meridian on the sphere is steeper than vertical at that height — by exactly the factor r/R). The two factors cancel, so area is preserved.

Therefore: sphere surface area = cylinder lateral surface area = 2\pi R \cdot 2R = 4 \pi R^2. QED. (Stereometry §475 — Archimedes’ theorem on the sphere and the cylinder.)

Problem 3 — The zone surface formula

Show that the surface area of a spherical zone between two parallel cuts at heights z_1 < z_2 is 2 \pi R \cdot (z_2 - z_1).

Full solution

By Archimedes’ projection (Problem 2), the zone’s surface corresponds to a cylindrical band of height z_2 - z_1 on the inscribed cylinder. The band area is 2\pi R \cdot (z_2 - z_1). QED. (Stereometry §476.)

Striking corollary: zones at different latitudes (same height difference) have the same surface area. A polar cap of height h has the same area as an equatorial band of height h.

Problem 4 — Volume of a spherical cap

Show that the volume of a spherical cap of height h (cut off a sphere of radius R by a plane at distance R - h from the centre) is V_{\text{cap}} = \tfrac{\pi h^2 (3R - h)}{3}.

Hint 1 — a nudge Integrate \pi (R^2 - z^2) from z = R - h to z = R (or use the slicing decomposition).
Full solution

Sliced into thin discs at height z, the cap is the integral \int_{R - h}^{R} \pi (R^2 - z^2) dz = \pi \left[R^2 z - \frac{z^3}{3}\right]_{R-h}^{R}. Evaluate: \pi \left[R^3 - \frac{R^3}{3} - R^2 (R - h) + \frac{(R-h)^3}{3} \right]. Algebraic simplification gives \dfrac{\pi h^2 (3R - h)}{3}. QED. (Stereometry §480 — spherical cap volume.)

Sanity check: h = 2R gives the full sphere, V = \tfrac{\pi \cdot 4R^2 (3R - 2R)}{3} = \tfrac{4 \pi R^3}{3}. ✓

Problem 5 — Surface of a spherical cap

Show that the surface area of a spherical cap of height h is 2 \pi R h.

Full solution A cap is a special zone with z_2 = R (the pole) and z_1 = R - h. By Problem 3, area = 2 \pi R \cdot h. QED. (Stereometry §477.)

Problem 6 — Sphere similarity scaling

Two spheres have radii R and kR. Show that their surface areas are in ratio k^2 and their volumes in ratio k^3.

Full solution S = 4\pi R^2 and S' = 4\pi (kR)^2 = k^2 \cdot 4\pi R^2 = k^2 S. Similarly V' = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi(kR)^3 = k^3 V. QED. (Stereometry §483 — sphere is the simplest example of 3D similarity scaling: linear k, area k^2, volume k^3.)

Pappus of Alexandria (~320 CE), one of the last great Greek geometers, generalised Archimedes’ sphere and cylinder relations into the Pappus centroid theorems: rotate any plane region about an external axis and the resulting solid’s volume equals (area of region) × (distance travelled by its centroid). For a sphere of radius R, rotate a half-disc of area \tfrac{1}{2}\pi R^2 about its diameter — the centroid travels 2\pi \cdot (4R/3\pi) — and you recover Archimedes’ \tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3 in one step. Sixteen centuries before Newton, calculus was already implicit in Greek geometry.

The whole chapter is one identity algebra-ically: at every height z, \pi(R^2 - z^2) + \pi z^2 = \pi R^2 — i.e., sphere slice + cone slice = cylinder slice. Cavalieri promotes this to a volume statement, and Archimedes’ projection turns the surfaces into the same equality. Two ancient theorems, one piece of school algebra.

Name

Sphere of radius R centred at O: the locus of points in space at distance R from O.

Theorems (Stereometry §§461–490).

Object Quantity
Sphere of radius R volume V = \tfrac{4}{3} \pi R^3
Sphere of radius R surface S = 4 \pi R^2
Spherical cap, height h, sphere radius R volume \tfrac{\pi h^2 (3R - h)}{3}
Spherical cap, height h curved surface 2\pi R h
Spherical zone between parallel planes at heights z_1, z_2 curved surface 2\pi R \cdot (z_2 - z_1)
Sphere similarity, linear ratio k S' = k^2 S, V' = k^3 V

Archimedes’ theorem. The sphere of radius R, the inscribed double cone (apex at centre), and the circumscribed cylinder (radius R, height 2R) have volumes in ratio 2 : 1 : 3.

Archimedes’ projection theorem. The radial projection of the sphere onto the inscribed cylinder is area-preserving — sphere and cylinder lateral surface have the same area (4 \pi R^2).

A verse:

Sphere meets cylinder in a contest both ancient and grand — Volume two parts to three — Archimedes’ last command. Surface stretched as a cylinder — that’s how four π R squared appears. Zones, caps, and slices: every height of h gives a 2 \pi R h tier.

Apply

Worked example

A sphere has volume 36\pi cubic units. Find its radius and surface area.

\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3 = 36\pi \Rightarrow R^3 = 27 \Rightarrow R = 3.

S = 4\pi \cdot 9 = 36\pi. (Pleasing accident: same number for volume and surface in these units, but different physical quantities.)

R = 3, S = 36\pi.

Practice — Warm

W1. A sphere has radius 7. Find its surface and volume.

Full solution S = 4\pi \cdot 49 = 196\pi. V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi \cdot 343 = \tfrac{1372}{3}\pi.

W2. A sphere has surface 100\pi. Find its radius.

Full solution 4\pi R^2 = 100\pi \Rightarrow R^2 = 25 \Rightarrow R = 5.

W3. A spherical cap of height 1 on a sphere of radius 4. Find its curved surface area.

Full solution 2\pi R h = 2\pi \cdot 4 \cdot 1 = 8\pi.

Practice — Working

P1. A hemisphere has the same diameter as a cylinder, with the hemisphere’s flat face on the cylinder’s top. The cylinder has height h. The total volume of the combined solid is twice that of the cylinder alone. Find h in terms of the radius r.

Hint 1 — a nudge Hemisphere volume \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \tfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3. Total = cylinder + hemisphere = 2 × cylinder.
Full solution \pi r^2 h + \tfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3 = 2 \pi r^2 h, so \pi r^2 h = \tfrac{2}{3}\pi r^3, giving h = \tfrac{2}{3} r.

P2. A sphere of radius R is inscribed in a cube. Find the ratio of their volumes.

Full solution Cube side: 2R, volume 8R^3. Sphere volume: \tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3. Ratio: \dfrac{(4/3)\pi R^3}{8 R^3} = \dfrac{\pi}{6} \approx 0.524. The inscribed sphere fills about 52\% of the cube.

P3. A spherical cap is one-half a hemisphere by volume. Find the cap’s height in terms of the sphere’s radius R.

Hint 1 — a nudge Half a hemisphere = \tfrac{1}{4} of the full sphere = \tfrac{1} {3}\pi R^3. Set the cap-volume formula equal to this and solve for h.
Full solution \tfrac{\pi h^2 (3R - h)}{3} = \tfrac{\pi R^3}{3}, so h^2 (3R - h) = R^3, i.e. h^3 - 3R h^2 + R^3 = 0. Numerical root: h \approx 0.6527 R. (No clean closed form — this is Cardano cubic territory.) Approximately h = 0.653 R.

Practice — Stretch

S1. A spherical lune is the region of a sphere’s surface between two great-circle arcs meeting at antipodal poles. Show that the area of a lune of dihedral angle \theta (radians) is 2 R^2 \theta.

Hint 1 — a nudge The full sphere is the lune of dihedral angle 2\pi (going all the way around). Lune area scales linearly with \theta.
Full solution By rotational symmetry, the area of a lune is proportional to its dihedral angle. Total sphere is the lune of \theta = 2\pi with area 4\pi R^2. So area-per-radian is 4\pi R^2 / (2\pi) = 2 R^2, hence lune area = 2 R^2 \theta. QED. (Used in the area formula for spherical triangles: area = R^2 (\alpha + \beta + \gamma - \pi), the spherical excess.)

S2. Two spheres intersect in a circle. The two sphere radii are R_1 and R_2, and the distance between their centres is d. Find the radius of the intersection circle.

Hint 1 — a nudge The intersection circle is perpendicular to the line of centres. Use Pythagoras twice — for each sphere, the radius, the distance from the centre to the intersection plane, and the intersection- circle radius form a right triangle.
Full solution Let r be the intersection-circle radius and d_1, d_2 the distances from each sphere’s centre to the intersection plane, with d_1 + d_2 = d. Pythagoras: R_1^2 = d_1^2 + r^2 and R_2^2 = d_2^2 + r^2. Subtract: R_1^2 - R_2^2 = d_1^2 - d_2^2 = (d_1 - d_2) d. So d_1 - d_2 = (R_1^2 - R_2^2)/d, and combined with d_1 + d_2 = d: d_1 = (d^2 + R_1^2 - R_2^2)/(2d). Then r^2 = R_1^2 - d_1^2, computable explicitly. (Generalises the intersecting-spheres* problem; Stereometry §488.)*

Kiselev-style problems

Three problems in Kiselev’s classical style, adapted from Givental, Stereometry, §§470–490.

K1. Show that a cylinder and a sphere of the same radius R have the same lateral surface area when the cylinder’s height equals the sphere’s diameter.

Full solution Cylinder lateral area: 2\pi R \cdot h. With h = 2R: 2\pi R \cdot 2R = 4\pi R^2. Sphere surface: 4\pi R^2. Equal. (This is Archimedes’ projection theorem* in surface form; Stereometry §475.)*

K2. Two spheres are inscribed in a cylinder of radius R — one at each end, each touching the cylinder’s bottom or top and the other sphere. Find the minimum height of the cylinder.

Hint 1 — a nudge Each sphere has radius R (since they fit inside the cylinder of radius R and touch the curved wall). They sit at the two ends and touch each other in the middle.
Full solution Each sphere has radius R. The bottom sphere’s centre is at height R from the bottom; the top sphere’s centre is at height h - R. For the spheres to touch each other, the distance between centres equals 2R: h - R - R = 2R \Rightarrow h = 4R. Minimum cylinder height = 4R.

K3. Archimedes’ tombstone problem. A cone, a sphere, and a cylinder all have the same height 2R and the same maximum radius R (the cone is inscribed in the cylinder, the sphere is too). Verify Archimedes’ celebrated 3 : 2 : 1 ratio of volumes for the cylinder, sphere, and cone.

Full solution

Cylinder: \pi R^2 \cdot 2R = 2\pi R^3. Sphere: \tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3. Cone (single cone of height 2R, base R, not the double cone): \tfrac{1}{3}\pi R^2 \cdot 2R = \tfrac{2}{3}\pi R^3.

Ratios (divide by \tfrac{2}{3}\pi R^3): \dfrac{2\pi R^3}{(2/3)\pi R^3} : \dfrac{(4/3)\pi R^3}{(2/3)\pi R^3} : 1 = 3 : 2 : 1. QED. (Stereometry §471 — the ratio Archimedes asked to be inscribed on his gravestone, found by Cicero a century later.)

Source pointer. Adapted from Givental, Kiselev’s Geometry, Book II — Stereometry, §§461–490 (Sumizdat 2008). Free PDF on cimat.mx. The Archimedes-projection-theorem proof of the sphere surface (Problem 2) is Kiselev’s §475 — and the canonical proof of one of the most beautiful theorems in classical geometry.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this chapter, remember these:

  1. A sphere of radius R has volume \tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3 and surface 4\pi R^2 — both proved by Archimedes via Cavalieri-and-projection, two and a half millennia ago.
  2. Archimedes’ projection maps the sphere onto the inscribed cylinder area-preservingly — the ancestor of every equal-area map projection in cartography.
  3. Spherical zones between parallel planes have the same surface 2\pi R h regardless of latitude — a polar cap of height h and an equatorial band of height h have identical area.

Common confusions

  1. “Sphere volume is \pi R^3.” No — it’s \tfrac{4}{3} \pi R^3. The factor \tfrac{4}{3} comes from the integration / Cavalieri argument and is exact, not an approximation.
  2. “Sphere surface is \pi R^2.” No — that’s a circle’s area. Sphere surface is four times that: 4 \pi R^2. (Hint to remember: sphere is “four circles wrapped”.)
  3. “Spherical zones at high latitudes are smaller in area than at the equator.” They are not. Archimedes’ projection theorem says zones of equal height have equal area, regardless of latitude. Counter-intuitive but true.

Where this matters

Game development. Bounding spheres are the simplest collision primitive in 3D — and 4 / 3 * π * r * r * r is run millions of times per second. Sphere-sphere intersection (S2) is the literal sqrt((dx)² + (dy)² + (dz)²) < r1 + r2 test.

AI / ML. Spherical embeddings — vectors normalised to unit length on the unit sphere — are the standard preprocessing step for cosine-similarity search and modern transformer architectures. The sphere’s geometry is what makes the dot product a meaningful angular measure.

Research. Archimedes’ projection theorem (Problem 2) is a measure-preserving map from sphere to cylinder — the historical seed of symplectic geometry (volume-preserving Hamiltonian flows) and the foundation of all map projections in cartography that preserve area.
