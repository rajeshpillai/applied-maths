04 · Addition and Subtraction

Two operations, one picture: walking along the number line

Pre-flight check

Are you ready for this lesson?

  1. Can you locate any number — say 7 — on the number line, from Lesson 2?
  2. Can you read 243 as “two hundred and forty-three” and say what each digit is worth, from Lesson 3?
  3. Have you done addition and subtraction by hand at some point — even if you found it mechanical?

If yes to (1) and (2) you are ready. The point of this lesson is not to teach you to add and subtract — it is to re-ground what you already do.

Touch

Use the slider to set a starting position. Then press the buttons to walk right (add) or left (subtract). Watch the number-line marker hop, and watch the running total update.

Adding hops you to the right. Subtracting hops you to the left. That is all. Everything else — carrying, borrowing, columns — is bookkeeping for when the hops are large.

Wonder

  1. If you start at 12 and hop right by 5, where do you land? If you then hop left by 5, where are you?
  2. Is “12 + 5” the same as “5 + 12”? What about “12 − 5” and “5 − 12”?
  3. When you do addition by hand on paper, why do you “carry the 1”? Have you ever asked?

Discover

Problem 1

Compute these in your head, by walking the number line: 8 + 6, \quad 13 + 4, \quad 15 - 7

Hint 1 — a nudge Picture the marker. Hop right for plus, left for minus.
Full solution 8 + 6 = 14, \ 13 + 4 = 17, \ 15 - 7 = 8. Each was just a walk along the line — a sequence of hops. The arithmetic and the picture are the same thing.

Problem 2

Try both orders: 12 + 5 \ \text{vs.} \ 5 + 12; \qquad 12 - 5 \ \text{vs.} \ 5 - 12.

What do you notice?

Hint 1 — a nudge For addition: hops are hops; the order doesn’t matter. For subtraction: it might.
Full solution

12 + 5 = 17 and 5 + 12 = 17 — the same. For addition, the order doesn’t matter (this is called the commutative property — but you discovered it without the word).

12 - 5 = 7 but 5 - 12 = -7. They are not the same. Subtraction is sensitive to which number you start from. Hopping left from 12 lands you at 7. Hopping left from 5 the same distance overshoots 0 — you land at -7 (the negative numbers we met informally in Lesson 2).

Problem 3

Now a big one — without a calculator: 47 + 35

Hint 1 — a nudge Hopping by 1 thirty-five times is silly. Use place value — Lesson 3 — to hop in tens and ones.
Hint 2 — the technique Add tens to tens (4 + 3 = 7 → 70), add ones to ones (7 + 5 = 12). Then combine — but watch what happens: 12 ones is more than 9, so the ones column overflows.
Full solution

Tens: 40 + 30 = 70. Ones: 7 + 5 = 12. Combine: 70 + 12 = 82.

When you write the algorithm vertically: \begin{array}{r} \!\!^{1}\,4 \, 7 \\ +\ 3 \, 5 \\ \hline 8 \, 2 \end{array} the “carry the 1” is exactly this: 12 ones don’t fit in the ones column (only digits 0–9 are allowed there), so the one ten of overflow gets written above the tens column. It’s pure place-value bookkeeping.

Problem 4

Now subtract: 52 - 17. What goes wrong if you try column-by-column naively?

Hint 1 — a nudge Ones first: 2 - 7. The ones column doesn’t have enough.
Full solution

2 - 7 would go negative — but the ones column can only hold digits 0–9. So we borrow: take one ten from the tens column (turning it into ten ones) and add those to the ones column.

After borrowing: ones column is 12, tens column has 4 (was 5). Now 12 - 7 = 5 in ones, 4 - 1 = 3 in tens. Result: 35.

Both “carrying” and “borrowing” are the same idea: when a column overflows or underflows, you re-arrange the place-value bookkeeping. The arithmetic itself is just walking along the number line.

Problem 5

Compute 999 + 1. Then compute 1000 - 1.

Hint 1 — a nudge Each one is a single hop along the line. But notice what happens to the digits.
Full solution

999 + 1 = 1000. The ones column overflows (9+1=10), carries 1 to the tens, which also overflows (9+1=10), carries to hundreds, also overflows, carries to thousands. Three carries in a single addition.

1000 - 1 = 999. Mirror image: borrow from the thousands → hundreds → tens → ones, three borrows in cascade.

This is what makes large-number arithmetic feel “tricky” — but it is the same tiny rule (overflow goes left, underflow borrows from the left) applied repeatedly.

The “carrying” algorithm you just used was popularised in Europe by Fibonacci in his 1202 Liber Abaci (“Book of Calculation”), which introduced Hindu–Arabic numerals to Western merchants. Before that book, European bookkeeping was done with Roman numerals on counting boards; addition meant sliding pebbles around. Within two centuries Fibonacci’s column-arithmetic had quietly displaced the abacus across European counting houses — and it is still exactly what you do on paper today.

Carrying and borrowing aren’t separate tricks — they are the same idea: when a column overflows, the surplus walks left; when a column runs short, it borrows from the left. The hops along the line never changed; place value just chunks them so you don’t have to make them one at a time.

Name

Addition is “hop right”. a + b means: start at a, take b hops to the right.

Subtraction is “hop left”. a - b means: start at a, take b hops to the left.

When numbers are large, we hop in place-value chunks (ones, tens, hundreds) rather than one at a time. Carrying records overflow from one column into the next column on the left. Borrowing records that we took some value from the column on the left to cover an underflow.

Addition is commutative: a + b = b + a. Subtraction is not: a - b \neq b - a in general.

To add is to walk right; to subtract, to walk left. Hop in tens and hundreds when the journey is long. When a column overflows, carry the overflow leftward; When a column runs short, borrow from the left.

Apply

Worked example

Compute 284 + 567.

A competent solver works column by column, right to left:

  1. Ones: 4 + 7 = 11. Write 1, carry 1.
  2. Tens: 8 + 6 + 1 \,(\text{carried}) = 15. Write 5, carry 1.
  3. Hundreds: 2 + 5 + 1 \,(\text{carried}) = 8. Write 8.

\begin{array}{r} \!^{1}\!^{1}\,2 \, 8 \, 4 \\ +\ 5 \, 6 \, 7 \\ \hline 8 \, 5 \, 1 \end{array}

Answer: 851. Check on the line: starting at 284, walking 567 to the right, you land at 851. Same operation, different picture.

Practice — Warm

W1. Compute by hopping (in your head): 14 + 6, \ 25 - 8, \ 9 + 11.

Full solution 14 + 6 = 20, \ 25 - 8 = 17, \ 9 + 11 = 20.

W2. Without computing fully, which is bigger: 200 + 350 or 300 + 240?

Hint 1 — a nudge Estimate by hundreds first.
Full solution 200 + 350 = 550 and 300 + 240 = 540. The first is bigger by 10. Estimating by hundreds (200+300=500 vs 300+200=500) was a tie; the tens broke it.

W3. Compute 73 - 28 by hand, showing the borrow.

Full solution Ones: 3 - 8 underflows. Borrow 1 ten from the tens column, leaving 6 tens; ones become 13. 13 - 8 = 5. Tens: 6 - 2 = 4. Answer: 45.

Practice — Working

P1. A bus has 47 passengers. At the next stop, 19 get off and 12 get on. How many passengers are on the bus now?

Hint 1 — a nudge Two hops: left 19, then right 12. Or compute the net hop first.
Full solution 47 - 19 + 12. Method 1: 47 - 19 = 28, then 28 + 12 = 40. Method 2: net change is -19 + 12 = -7, so 47 - 7 = 40. Either way, 40 passengers. Notice that the order of operations didn’t matter for the net result.

P2. Find the missing number: \ ? + 36 = 100.

Hint 1 — a nudge “What plus 36 gives 100?” is the same question as “100 minus 36 is what?” Subtraction undoes addition.
Full solution

100 - 36 = 64. Check: 64 + 36 = 100. ✓

The deeper idea: addition and subtraction are inverse operations — each undoes the other. We will use this constantly when we meet Variables and start solving equations.

P3. A child has Rs. 1000. She spends Rs. 247 on a book and Rs. 158 on a notebook. How much is left?

Hint 1 — a nudge Two subtractions, in either order — or one big addition followed by one subtraction.
Full solution

Total spent: 247 + 158 = 405. Money left: 1000 - 405 = 595. Rs. 595 remain.

Check: 1000 - 247 = 753, then 753 - 158 = 595. Same answer; addition before subtraction is more efficient because we collapse two hops into one.

Practice — Stretch

S1. Without computing the right side, decide whether this is true or false: 48 + 73 = 73 + 48

Then decide for: 48 - 73 = 73 - 48

Full solution True for the first; false for the second. Addition’s commutative property says order does not matter (a + b = b + a for all numbers a, b). Subtraction is not commutative — in fact a - b and b - a have opposite signs. (We will return to this when we name the negation operation in Lesson 10: Negative Numbers.)

S2. A computer adds two numbers stored in 8 binary “columns” (an 8-bit number — recall binary from Lesson 3). The columns are worth, right to left: 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128. The largest 8-bit number is 11111111 (all 1s).

What number is that, in our usual base-10? What happens if you try to add 1 to it?

Hint 1 — a nudge Sum the column values: 1 + 2 + 4 + ... + 128.
Full solution

Sum: 128 + 64 + 32 + 16 + 8 + 4 + 2 + 1 = 255. So the largest 8-bit number is 255.

Adding 1 to 11111111 would set the ones column to 10 — overflow! The carry propagates all the way left, just like our 999 + 1 in Discover P5. But there is no 9th column to receive the final carry. The result is either: a 9-bit number 100000000 (= 256) if you have room, or — in fixed 8-bit storage — the number wraps around to 00000000 (= 0). This is called integer overflow, and it is the source of many famous bugs in real software (the Y2K-style flaws, the 2038 problem, and a particularly funny one in Civilization where Gandhi’s aggression rolled over to maximum). Place-value arithmetic is the same; the storage limit is what bites.

Common confusions

  1. “Subtraction always gives a smaller number.” Only when you start with a positive number and subtract a positive number. As soon as negative numbers enter — Lesson 10 — subtracting a negative grows the result.
  2. “Carrying / borrowing are different ideas.” They are mirror images of the same idea: a column has overflowed (or underflowed), so the excess (or deficit) gets recorded in the column to the left.
  3. “Addition and subtraction must be done in the order written.” For only-addition expressions, you can rearrange freely (3 + 5 + 7 = 7 + 3 + 5). For mixed addition and subtraction, you can rearrange if you carry the sign with each number — but that’s most easily handled after we name negative numbers in Lesson 10.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. Addition is a hop right; subtraction is a hop left — both happen on the same number line.
  2. Carrying and borrowing are the same idea seen from opposite sides: a column overflows or underflows, and the difference moves to the column on its left.
  3. Addition’s order is free (a+b = b+a); subtraction’s is not (a-b \ne b-a unless the numbers are equal).

Connect

Prerequisites used

  • Lesson 2 — The Number Line — addition is “step right”, subtraction is “step left”. The whole lesson lives on this picture.
  • Lesson 3 — Place Value — carrying and borrowing are place-value bookkeeping.

What this unlocks

  • Multiplication as Area — multiplication is repeated addition done in two dimensions.
  • Negative Numbers — subtracting more than you have forces the line to extend left of zero.
  • Variables — equations like x + 36 = 100 are solved by inverting the addition.

Where this matters

Systems. Every CPU has an “ALU” (arithmetic logic unit) whose primary job is binary addition with carry. Subtraction is implemented as addition of a negation — the same trick you saw informally in Discover P2. Integer overflow (Stretch S2) is one of the most common sources of serious software bugs.

Mathematical research. The pair (addition, subtraction) is the first example of a group — an algebraic structure with an operation, an identity (here, 0), and inverses (here, “negation”). The whole subject of abstract algebra in Tier 6 grows from generalising what you just did.

Everywhere else. Banking, accounting, inventory, scoring in games, measuring time differences, calculating change at a shop — addition and subtraction are the workhorse operations of daily numerical life. The algorithms you internalise here run quietly in your head millions of times across a lifetime.
