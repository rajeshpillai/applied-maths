12 · Shapes and Angles

Lines that meet, corners that turn — the alphabet of geometry

Pre-flight check

This lesson stands alone — it has no maths prerequisites in this curriculum. You only need:

  1. To know what a straight line looks like (a taut string, the edge of a ruler).
  2. The willingness to think about shape and direction rather than numbers for a while.
  3. (Optional) Pencil and paper to sketch.

Touch

Drag the slider to set the angle between two lines meeting at a point. Watch the corner open and close.

viewof angleDeg = Inputs.range([0, 180], {value: 60, step: 5, label: "Angle (degrees):"})
{
  const W = 480, H = 280, cx = W/2, cy = H - 40, len = 180;
  const a = angleDeg * Math.PI / 180;
  const svg = d3.create("svg").attr("viewBox", `0 0 ${W} ${H}`)
    .attr("style", "width:100%; max-width:520px; display:block; margin: 0 auto;");

  // Reference line (horizontal right)
  const x1 = cx + len, y1 = cy;
  // Rotating line
  const x2 = cx + len * Math.cos(-a), y2 = cy + len * Math.sin(-a);

  svg.append("line").attr("x1", cx).attr("y1", cy).attr("x2", x1).attr("y2", y1)
    .attr("stroke", "#888").attr("stroke-width", 2);
  svg.append("line").attr("x1", cx).attr("y1", cy).attr("x2", x2).attr("y2", y2)
    .attr("stroke", "#2c5f7c").attr("stroke-width", 2.5);

  // Arc showing the angle
  const arcR = 50;
  const arcEndX = cx + arcR * Math.cos(-a), arcEndY = cy + arcR * Math.sin(-a);
  const large = a > Math.PI ? 1 : 0;
  svg.append("path")
    .attr("d", `M ${cx + arcR},${cy} A ${arcR},${arcR} 0 ${large} 0 ${arcEndX},${arcEndY}`)
    .attr("fill", "none").attr("stroke", "#c2185b").attr("stroke-width", 2);
  // Angle label
  const labA = -a / 2;
  svg.append("text")
    .attr("x", cx + (arcR + 20) * Math.cos(labA))
    .attr("y", cy + (arcR + 20) * Math.sin(labA))
    .attr("font-size", "16").attr("fill", "#c2185b").attr("font-weight", "bold")
    .text(`${angleDeg}°`);
  // Vertex
  svg.append("circle").attr("cx", cx).attr("cy", cy).attr("r", 4).attr("fill", "#1f2933");

  // Name annotations
  let name;
  if (angleDeg === 0) name = "(zero — lines coincide)";
  else if (angleDeg < 90) name = "acute (less than 90°)";
  else if (angleDeg === 90) name = "right (exactly 90°)";
  else if (angleDeg < 180) name = "obtuse (between 90° and 180°)";
  else name = "straight (exactly 180°)";

  svg.append("text").attr("x", cx).attr("y", 28).attr("text-anchor", "middle")
    .attr("font-size", "16").attr("fill", "#1f2933").attr("font-weight", "bold")
    .text(name);

  return svg.node();
}

An angle measures how much one line turns away from another, in degrees. A full turn (back to the start) is 360°. A half-turn is 180°. A quarter-turn is 90° — the right angle.

Wonder

  1. Open and close the angle. Is the length of the lines part of the angle? Or only the spread between them?
  2. What angles do you see in the corners of a book, a window, a phone screen?
  3. A triangle has three corners. If you measured the angles at each corner and added them up, do you think the answer would always be the same?

Discover

Problem 1

Sketch (or imagine) these shapes and name them:

  1. Three straight sides.
  2. Four straight sides, all the same length, all corners right-angled.
  3. Four straight sides, opposite sides equal, all corners right-angled.
  4. A flat curve where every point is the same distance from one fixed centre.
Full solution
  1. Triangle. (b) Square. (c) Rectangle. (d) Circle.
Notice the structure: shapes are described by number of sides, side lengths, and angles. These three pieces of data determine almost every elementary shape.

Problem 2

Sketch a triangle. Cut it out (or just imagine cutting it). Tear off the three corners. Place them next to each other along a straight line.

What do you find?

Hint 1 — a nudge A straight line corresponds to an angle of 180°.
Full solution

The three corners exactly fill a straight line — i.e. they sum to 180°.

This is the most famous fact in elementary geometry:

The three angles of any triangle sum to 180°.

True for any triangle — pointy, flat, equilateral, scalene. The classroom experiment with torn corners is a physical proof. Tier 3 will give a careful proof using parallel lines.

Problem 3

A triangle has angles 40° and 75°. What is the third angle?

Hint 1 — a nudge Use the rule from P2: total = 180°.
Full solution 180 - 40 - 75 = 65°. 65°. Algebra (Lesson 11) makes the calculation trivial: if the third angle is x, then 40 + 75 + x = 180, so x = 65.

Problem 4

Imagine walking around the boundary of a square. Each corner, you turn by 90° (a right angle). After four corners, you’ve turned through how much in total — and back to your starting direction?

Hint 1 — a nudge Four right angles, each 90°. Sum?
Full solution

4 \times 90° = 360° — a full turn. After walking around any convex shape, you turn through 360° in total.

This generalises beautifully: a triangle’s exterior angles (the turn at each corner if you walked along it) also sum to 360°. So do a pentagon’s, a hexagon’s, and any convex shape. The interior angles add up differently for each shape, but the exterior angles always sum to 360°. Geometry is full of such conserved totals.

Problem 5

Two lines meet, forming an angle of 53° on one side. What is the angle on the other side of the same line?

Hint 1 — a nudge The two angles together fill a straight line.
Full solution

180 - 53 = 127°. 127°. Two angles forming a straight line are called supplementary, and they sum to 180° by definition. Two angles forming a right angle are called complementary and sum to 90°.

The two angles on opposite sides of a single straight line are called vertical angles when two lines cross — and an important Tier 3 fact is that they are always equal.

Euclid’s Elements (c. 300 BCE) opens with twenty-three definitions — point, line, angle, circle, triangle — and five postulates, from which every theorem of plane geometry follows. The triangle angle-sum (Discover P2) appears as Book I, Proposition 32. The Elements was the second-most printed book in Western history (after the Bible) and was the standard geometry textbook in European schools for over 2,000 years. Why 360° for a full turn? That’s older still — inherited from the Babylonians (c. 2000 BCE) and their base-60 number system, which also gave us 60 minutes in an hour.

Length is one kind of measurement; turn is another. An angle is what you measure when length doesn’t matter — only how much one direction swings away from another.

Name

A point has position but no size. A line is the path traced by moving in one fixed direction. A line segment is a finite piece of a line, with two endpoints.

An angle measures the turn between two lines (or segments) that meet at a common point — the vertex. We measure angles in degrees: a full turn is 360°, a half-turn 180°, a quarter-turn 90° (a right angle).

Special angle names:

  • Acute: less than 90°.
  • Right: exactly 90°.
  • Obtuse: between 90° and 180°.
  • Straight: exactly 180°.

A polygon is a closed shape with straight sides. Common ones:

  • Triangle (3 sides), quadrilateral (4), pentagon (5), hexagon (6), …
  • A square has 4 equal sides and 4 right angles.
  • A rectangle has opposite sides equal and 4 right angles.
  • A regular polygon has all sides equal and all angles equal.

A circle is the set of points at a fixed radius from a centre.

The triangle angle-sum: the three interior angles of any triangle sum to 180°.

Two lines meeting — the angle between them; Three lines closing — a triangle, whose corners always sum to 180°; A walk around a closed shape returns you 360° to the start.

Apply

Worked example

A triangle has two equal sides and a vertex angle (between the equal sides) of 50°. Find the other two angles.

A competent solver uses a small fact about isoceles triangles:

In an isoceles triangle, the angles opposite the two equal sides are equal.

So the other two angles are equal — call each x. Sum to 180°: 50 + x + x = 180 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad 2x = 130 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad x = 65°.

Answer: the other two angles are 65° each. Verify: 50 + 65 + 65 = 180° ✓.

The “equal sides imply equal opposite angles” fact will be proved properly in Tier 2 / Tier 3 geometry.

Practice — Warm

W1. Classify each: 30°, 90°, 130°, 180°.

Full solution acute, right, obtuse, straight.

W2. A triangle has two angles of 50° and 70°. Find the third.

Full solution 180 - 50 - 70 = 60°.

W3. A square’s four angles are all equal and sum to… ? Hence each is… ?

Full solution Sum = 360° (the four corners). Each = 90° — confirming squares are right-angled.

Practice — Working

P1. A triangle has all three angles equal. What is each angle?

Hint 1 — a nudge Three equal angles summing to 180°. So each is…
Full solution 180 \div 3 = 60°. This is the equilateral triangle: all three sides equal, all three angles equal to 60°. The most symmetric triangle.

P2. A regular pentagon has 5 equal interior angles. The interior angles of any pentagon sum to (5 - 2) \times 180° = 540°. What is each interior angle of a regular pentagon?

Hint 1 — a nudge Total ÷ 5.
Full solution

540 \div 5 = 108°. (Each angle of a regular pentagon is 108°.)

The general formula for the interior-angle sum of a polygon with n sides is (n - 2) \times 180°. You can derive it by noticing that any n-sided polygon can be sliced into (n - 2) triangles from a single vertex.

P3. Two angles of a triangle are equal, and the third is twice each of them. Find all three angles.

Hint 1 — a nudge Let each equal angle be x. Then the third is 2x. Use Lesson 11.
Full solution x + x + 2x = 180, so 4x = 180 and x = 45°. The angles are 45°, 45°, 90°. A right-angled isoceles triangle. This shape is the diagonal of a square — a recurring shape in geometry.

Practice — Stretch

S1. What angle do the hands of a clock make at exactly 3:00?

Hint 1 — a nudge The clock face is a circle (360°) divided into 12 equal segments.
Full solution

At 3:00, the minute hand is at 12 and the hour hand is at 3. They are 3 hours apart, which is \tfrac{3}{12} = \tfrac{1}{4} of the full circle. \tfrac{1}{4} \times 360 = 90°.

90° — a right angle. That’s why a clock at 3:00 (or 9:00) makes a visually familiar L-shape.

S2. A 3D triangle in a video game is described by three points (vertices). The game engine needs to compute the angles between pairs of edges to do lighting (which way is the surface facing?). The angle between two vectors \vec{u} and \vec{v} is given by \cos\theta = \frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{v}}{|\vec{u}|\,|\vec{v}|}. You don’t need to compute this now — but recognise: even 3D shading is a question about angles. What does this say about the importance of angles?

Full solution

Every modern game renders 3D scenes by decomposing surfaces into millions of tiny triangles, and computing the angle between each triangle’s surface direction and the direction of the light. The result controls the brightness of each pixel.

The simple “three corners of a triangle add to 180°” you met today generalises into the trigonometry of Tier 3 and the vector algebra of Tier 5. From a child’s protractor to real-time 3D graphics, the same idea — angle between two directions — is the through line.

Angles are not a small topic. They are one of the most reused ideas in all of applied mathematics.

Common confusions

  1. “Bigger lines mean a bigger angle.” No — angle measures only the turn between the lines, not their length. Two short pencils at a wide angle, and two long swords at a narrow angle, can have very different angles regardless of length.
  2. “All four-sided shapes are squares or rectangles.” No — there are also rhombuses (equal sides, no right angles), parallelograms, trapeziums, and irregular quadrilaterals.
  3. “360° is special — it’s the only full turn.” It’s the convention for full turn (chosen by the Babylonians for their base-60 number system). Mathematicians often use radians instead (a full turn is 2\pi), which we’ll meet in Tier 3.

Takeaways

If you remember nothing else from this lesson, remember these:

  1. An angle measures turn between two directions, not length — short pencils and long swords can meet at the same angle.
  2. The three angles of any triangle sum to 180°, and walking the boundary of any closed convex shape turns you through 360° in total.
  3. Shapes get their names from sides and angles — and these two pieces of data carry every elementary geometric fact, from a square to a 3D game model.

Connect

Prerequisites used

None within this curriculum. Geometry has its own logical foundation — this lesson sits at the start of the geometric strand.

What this unlocks

  • Area, Perimeter, Volume — with shapes and angles named, we can measure them.
  • Tier 3 — Trigonometry — the ratios of triangle sides as functions of the angles. Every angle calculation in physics or graphics ultimately uses trigonometry.

Where this matters

Game development & graphics. Every 3D model is a mesh of triangles. Every lighting calculation is an angle calculation. The angle between the camera and a surface determines what you see. Stretch S2 gives the high-level view.

Mathematical research. Geometry is one of mathematics’ two oldest branches (with arithmetic). Euclid’s Elements (c. 300 BCE) systematised plane geometry from a few axioms — its method (axiom → theorem → proof) is the model for all subsequent rigorous mathematics. Kiselev’s Geometry (Russia, 19th century) is the modern reincarnation.

Everywhere else. Architecture, navigation (compass bearings), astronomy (positions of stars), surveying, engineering, art and design, sports (camera angles in cricket replays), and traffic engineering (intersection angles) all depend on the angle vocabulary.
